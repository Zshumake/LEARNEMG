## 6.1 Introduction to Radiculopathy 

Radiculopathy refers to dysfunction of a spinal nerve root resulting in pain, sensory disturbance, weakness, or reflex changes in the distribution of the affected root. It is among the most common indications for electrodiagnostic testing and represents a diagnosis where needle EMG provides information that no other test can offer -- objective evidence of motor axon loss in a specific myotomal distribution.

The electrodiagnostic evaluation of radiculopathy has both remarkable strengths and important limitations. Its specificity is high: when fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves are found in muscles sharing the same root innervation but supplied by different peripheral nerves, the diagnosis is compelling. However, its sensitivity is moderate at best, estimated at 50 to 85% for cervical radiculopathy and somewhat higher for lumbosacral radiculopathy, depending on the study protocol and timing. The electromyographer must understand these limitations and communicate them clearly to referring physicians and patients.

Several principles govern the electrodiagnostic approach to radiculopathy:

1. **Radiculopathy cannot be diagnosed by nerve conduction studies alone.** Needle EMG must be performed.
2. **The study must be timed appropriately** relative to symptom onset to maximize sensitivity.
3. **A sufficient number of muscles must be examined**, including paraspinals and muscles from different peripheral nerves sharing the suspected root.
4. **The SNAP is expected to be normal** in pure radiculopathy because the lesion is proximal to the dorsal root ganglion.
5. **EMG detects only the motor axon loss component of radiculopathy.** Purely sensory or demyelinating radiculopathies may produce normal EMG findings.

## 6.2 The Dorsal Root Ganglion Principle 

The dorsal root ganglion (DRG) principle is the single most important concept in the electrodiagnostic evaluation of radiculopathy. It explains why sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) remain normal in radiculopathy despite the presence of clinical sensory deficits, and it forms the basis for distinguishing radiculopathy from plexopathy and peripheral neuropathy.

### 6.2.1 Anatomical Basis

The DRG is located in the intervertebral foramen, outside the spinal canal in most cervical and lumbar levels. It contains the cell bodies of primary sensory neurons. The peripheral process of each sensory neuron extends distally through the plexus and peripheral nerve to the sensory receptor, while the central process extends proximally through the dorsal root into the spinal cord.

In radiculopathy, the nerve root is compressed or injured at a site that is typically proximal to the DRG (i.e., within the spinal canal or at the foraminal entrance). Because the DRG cell body is intact and the peripheral sensory axon remains in continuity with its cell body, Wallerian degeneration does not occur in the peripheral sensory nerve. The sensory axon continues to conduct action potentials normally, and the SNAP recorded over the peripheral nerve remains normal.

### 6.2.2 Clinical Implication

The patient with a radiculopathy may experience significant sensory symptoms -- numbness, tingling, burning pain -- in the dermatome of the affected root. These symptoms arise from dysfunction at the root level, disrupting sensory signal transmission into the spinal cord. However, the peripheral sensory nerve is physiologically intact, so the SNAP is preserved.

This creates an apparent paradox: the patient has clinical sensory loss, but the SNAP is normal. Understanding this paradox is essential for correct electrodiagnostic interpretation.

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** The combination of **normal SNAPs + abnormal needle EMG** in a myotomal distribution is the electrodiagnostic signature of radiculopathy. If the SNAP is abnormal in the distribution of the clinical deficit, the lesion must be at or distal to the DRG -- consider plexopathy, peripheral neuropathy, or a combined lesion.

### 6.2.3 Exceptions and Caveats

Several clinical scenarios can complicate the DRG principle:

- **Concurrent peripheral neuropathy:** Patients with diabetic polyneuropathy or other pre-existing neuropathies may have abnormal SNAPs that are unrelated to the radiculopathy. This is one of the most common diagnostic challenges in electrodiagnostic medicine.
- **L5 DRG position:** The L5 DRG sits within or very near the intervertebral foramen, and in some individuals it may be intraspinal. A large L4-5 disc herniation could potentially damage the L5 DRG directly, producing an abnormal superficial fibular SNAP. This is uncommon but is worth considering in atypical cases.
- **S1 DRG position:** Similarly, the S1 DRG may be located more proximally than other lumbar levels, though clinically significant involvement is rare.

## 6.3 Clinical Features by Root Level 

### 6.3.1 Cervical Radiculopathy

| Root | Motor Deficit | Sensory Distribution | Reflex | Key Muscles for EMG |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C5 | Shoulder abduction, elbow flexion | Lateral arm (regimental badge area) | Biceps | Deltoid, infraspinatus, biceps, rhomboids |
| C6 | Elbow flexion, wrist extension | Lateral forearm, thumb, index finger | Brachioradialis | Biceps, brachioradialis, pronator teres, ECR |
| C7 | Elbow extension, wrist flexion, finger extension | Middle finger | Triceps | Triceps, pronator teres, FCR, EDC |
| C8 | Finger flexion, hand intrinsics | Medial forearm, ring and small fingers | Finger flexors | FDP, FPL, abductor pollicis brevis, FDI |
| T1 | Hand intrinsics (abduction, adduction) | Medial arm | None reliable | First dorsal interosseous, abductor digiti minimi |

**C5 Radiculopathy:**

C5 radiculopathy typically presents with weakness of shoulder abduction and external rotation. Patients have difficulty raising the arm overhead and may note weakness brushing their hair. The biceps reflex may be diminished. On needle EMG, the deltoid and infraspinatus are the most commonly affected muscles. The rhomboids (dorsal scapular nerve, C5) and supraspinatus provide additional localizing information but are technically more difficult to examine.

**C6 Radiculopathy:**

C6 is the second most common cervical radiculopathy (approximately 25% of cases). It produces weakness of elbow flexion and wrist extension. The brachioradialis reflex is the most specific reflex for C6 (the biceps reflex receives contribution from both C5 and C6). The pronator teres is an important muscle for C6 localization because it is innervated by the median nerve -- finding abnormalities in both the brachioradialis (radial nerve, C6) and pronator teres (median nerve, C6) confirms a root-level rather than peripheral nerve lesion.

**C7 Radiculopathy:**

C7 is the most common cervical radiculopathy (approximately 60% of cases) and produces weakness of elbow extension, wrist flexion, and finger extension. The triceps reflex is diminished. The C7 root has the broadest myotomal representation of any cervical root, innervating muscles through the radial, median, and ulnar nerves. Key EMG muscles include the triceps (radial nerve), pronator teres (median nerve, shared C6-C7), flexor carpi radialis (median nerve), and extensor digitorum communis (posterior interosseous nerve).

**C8 Radiculopathy:**

C8 radiculopathy produces weakness of finger flexion and hand grip, along with intrinsic hand muscle weakness. There is no reliable deep tendon reflex for C8, though the finger flexor reflex may be diminished. C8 has the **least myotomal overlap** of any cervical root, making it the most reliably identified on needle EMG. Key muscles include the flexor digitorum profundus (median and ulnar nerve branches), flexor pollicis longus (AIN), first dorsal interosseous (ulnar nerve), and abductor pollicis brevis (median nerve).

> **Clinical Pearl:** C8 radiculopathy is the cervical root most accurately localized by needle EMG because of its limited myotomal overlap. C5-C6 and C6-C7 share extensive overlap, making single-root localization at these levels more challenging.

**T1 Radiculopathy:**

Isolated T1 radiculopathy is rare. It predominantly affects the hand intrinsic muscles (interossei, hypothenar muscles). T1 must be distinguished from lower trunk brachial plexopathy and true neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome, both of which also produce hand intrinsic weakness. The presence of normal SNAPs with abnormal needle EMG and paraspinal abnormalities supports T1 radiculopathy over plexopathy.

### 6.3.2 Lumbosacral Radiculopathy

| Root | Motor Deficit | Sensory Distribution | Reflex | Key Muscles for EMG |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| L2 | Hip flexion | Anterior thigh (proximal) | Cremasteric | Iliopsoas, hip adductors |
| L3 | Hip flexion, knee extension | Anterior thigh (middle) | Patellar (partial) | Iliopsoas, vastus lateralis, hip adductors |
| L4 | Knee extension, ankle dorsiflexion (partial) | Medial leg | Patellar | Vastus medialis, tibialis anterior, hip adductors |
| L5 | Ankle dorsiflexion, great toe extension, hip abduction | Lateral leg, dorsum of foot | None reliable (medial hamstring in some) | Tibialis anterior, peroneus longus, tibialis posterior, gluteus medius, tensor fasciae latae |
| S1 | Ankle plantar flexion, foot eversion | Lateral foot, sole | Achilles | Gastrocnemius (medial and lateral), soleus, gluteus maximus, biceps femoris (short head) |

**L3-L4 Radiculopathy:**

The upper lumbar roots are challenging to evaluate electrodiagnostically due to broad myotomal overlap among L2, L3, and L4 in the anterior thigh muscles. The quadriceps femoris receives innervation from L2-L4 through the femoral nerve. Distinguishing L3 from L4 radiculopathy often relies on clinical features and the pattern of muscle involvement: L4 tends to produce more tibialis anterior weakness (early foot drop) while L3 produces more hip flexion and knee extension weakness.

**L5 Radiculopathy:**

L5 is the most common lumbosacral radiculopathy and one of the most important to diagnose electrodiagnostically. It presents with ankle dorsiflexion weakness (foot drop), great toe extension weakness, and hip abduction weakness. There is no reliable deep tendon reflex for L5, which means the electrodiagnostic study carries particular importance in confirming the diagnosis.

The key diagnostic challenge with L5 radiculopathy is distinguishing it from common fibular (peroneal) neuropathy at the fibular head, as both produce foot drop. Several electrodiagnostic strategies help make this distinction:

1. **SNAP evaluation:** The superficial fibular SNAP should be normal in L5 radiculopathy (DRG principle) but may be abnormal in fibular neuropathy
2. **Non-fibular L5 muscles:** Tibialis posterior (tibial nerve, L5), tensor fasciae latae (superior gluteal nerve, L5), and gluteus medius (superior gluteal nerve, L5) are affected in L5 radiculopathy but spared in fibular neuropathy
3. **Short head of biceps femoris:** This muscle is innervated by the fibular division of the sciatic nerve (L5, S1). It is abnormal in sciatic neuropathy proximal to the knee but normal in fibular neuropathy at the fibular head. It may be abnormal in L5 radiculopathy.
4. **Paraspinal muscles:** Abnormal in radiculopathy, normal in fibular neuropathy

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** The tibialis posterior is the most important muscle for distinguishing L5 radiculopathy from fibular neuropathy. It is innervated by the tibial nerve (L5) and is spared in fibular neuropathy but affected in L5 radiculopathy. A foot drop with a normal superficial fibular SNAP and abnormal tibialis posterior strongly suggests L5 radiculopathy.

**S1 Radiculopathy:**

S1 radiculopathy produces weakness of ankle plantar flexion (often subtle, as the gastrocnemius-soleus complex is very powerful) and foot eversion. The Achilles reflex is diminished or absent. The H-reflex is the most sensitive electrodiagnostic test for S1 radiculopathy, as it traverses the S1 root and is affected even when needle EMG findings are minimal.

Key EMG muscles for S1 include the medial and lateral gastrocnemius, soleus, gluteus maximus, and the short head of the biceps femoris. The abductor hallucis (medial plantar nerve, S1-S2) may also be examined.

## 6.4 EDX Approach and Screening Protocols 

### 6.4.1 General Principles

The electrodiagnostic evaluation for radiculopathy combines nerve conduction studies and needle EMG. The NCS component serves primarily to exclude other diagnoses (peripheral neuropathy, plexopathy, entrapment neuropathy) and to confirm that SNAPs are normal in the distribution of the clinical deficit. The needle EMG is the primary diagnostic tool.

### 6.4.2 Nerve Conduction Study Protocol

**For suspected cervical radiculopathy:**

- Median motor nerve conduction study (recording APB)
- Ulnar motor nerve conduction study (recording ADM)
- Median sensory nerve conduction study (recording digit 2 or 3)
- Ulnar sensory nerve conduction study (recording digit 5)
- Radial sensory nerve conduction study (recording anatomical snuffbox)
- Additional studies as clinically indicated (medial antebrachial cutaneous, lateral antebrachial cutaneous)

**For suspected lumbosacral radiculopathy:**

- Fibular (peroneal) motor nerve conduction study (recording EDB)
- Tibial motor nerve conduction study (recording AH)
- Sural sensory nerve conduction study
- Superficial fibular sensory nerve conduction study
- H-reflex (recording soleus with tibial nerve stimulation)
- Additional studies as indicated (femoral motor for upper lumbar, lateral femoral cutaneous sensory)

### 6.4.3 Needle EMG Screening Protocol

**Minimum requirements for diagnosis:**

The AANEM recommends examining a sufficient number of muscles to establish that abnormalities follow a myotomal pattern (same root) but cross peripheral nerve boundaries (different nerves). The minimum diagnostic criteria require:

- Abnormalities in at least **one paraspinal muscle** at the appropriate level
- Abnormalities in at least **two limb muscles** innervated by the same root through **different peripheral nerves**

The sensitivity of the needle examination increases with the number of muscles tested. A screen of five or more muscles yields a sensitivity of approximately 80 to 95%. A minimum of six muscles, including paraspinals, is the standard recommendation for a thorough evaluation.

**Cervical radiculopathy EMG screen:**

### 6.4.4 The Role of Paraspinal Muscles 

The paraspinal muscles are innervated by the posterior primary rami, which branch from the spinal nerve before the formation of the plexus. This anatomical fact makes paraspinal muscle examination uniquely valuable:

**Diagnostic value:**

- Fibrillation potentials in the paraspinals confirm that the lesion is at or proximal to the root level -- not in the plexus or peripheral nerve
- Paraspinal abnormalities appear earliest after acute radiculopathy (7 to 10 days), before limb muscle abnormalities
- Specificity of paraspinal fibrillations for radiculopathy is approximately 90%

**Limitations:**

- Sensitivity is only approximately 33% -- many radiculopathies do not produce paraspinal fibrillations
- Paraspinal fibrillations may be found in up to 12% of asymptomatic individuals (especially in cervical paraspinals)
- Prior spinal surgery disrupts paraspinal muscle architecture, making interpretation unreliable
- Individual root levels cannot be reliably identified by paraspinal examination alone (the multifidus is technically difficult to isolate by level)
- Paraspinal fibrillations are nonspecific: they can be seen in motor neuron disease, inflammatory myopathy, and other conditions

> **Clinical Pearl:** In a post-surgical spine, paraspinal fibrillation potentials are unreliable markers of radiculopathy. Surgical disruption of the muscle produces chronic denervation changes that persist indefinitely. In this setting, the diagnosis must rely entirely on limb muscle findings.

## 6.5 Chronology of EDX Findings After Nerve Root Injury 

Understanding the temporal sequence of electrodiagnostic abnormalities after acute nerve root injury is critical for both study timing and interpretation. The following timeline assumes a complete axonal lesion at the root level:

| Time After Injury | Finding | Location |
|---|---|---|
| 0-3 days | Reduced recruitment | All affected muscles |
| 5-10 days | Fibrillation potentials appear | Paraspinal muscles |
| 2-3 weeks | Fibrillation potentials appear | Proximal limb muscles |
| 3-6 weeks | Fibrillation potentials appear | Distal limb muscles |
| 6-12 weeks | Nascent MUAPs (polyphasic, unstable) | Early reinnervation |
| 3-6 months | MUAP duration and amplitude increasing | Progressive reinnervation |
| 6-12+ months | Large, long-duration, polyphasic MUAPs | Chronic reinnervation |
| Months to years | Fibrillations diminish or disappear | Complete reinnervation |

### 6.5.1 Why Paraspinals Show Changes First 

The paraspinal muscles are located closest to the site of root injury. Wallerian degeneration must propagate distally along the axon from the point of injury to the muscle's neuromuscular junction before denervation supersensitivity develops. Because the paraspinal muscles are only centimeters from the root, degeneration reaches them first (5 to 10 days). Proximal limb muscles are next (2 to 3 weeks), and distal limb muscles -- which may be 50 to 100 centimeters from the root -- are last (3 to 6 weeks).

### 6.5.2 Optimal Timing of the Study

For maximal sensitivity, the electrodiagnostic study should be performed **at least three weeks** after the onset of motor symptoms. This timing allows fibrillations to develop in most proximal limb muscles and in some distal muscles. Studies performed earlier may show only reduced recruitment, which, while suggestive, does not permit the same degree of localization as fibrillation potentials in a myotomal distribution.

If the study is performed too late -- months to years after injury -- reinnervation may have replaced fibrillation potentials with chronic MUAP changes, and recruitment may have normalized. In this setting, the only findings may be large, long-duration MUAPs, which confirm a previous neurogenic process but do not prove that it is ongoing.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The "sweet spot" for EMG timing in radiculopathy is **3 to 8 weeks** after symptom onset. Earlier studies lack sensitivity for fibrillation potentials. Later studies may miss active denervation if reinnervation has occurred.

## 6.7 Dual-Innervated Muscles and Their Diagnostic Utility 

Dual-innervated muscles -- those receiving innervation from the same root through two different peripheral nerves -- are the cornerstone of electrodiagnostic localization in radiculopathy. Finding denervation in muscles that share a root but have different peripheral nerve supplies proves that the lesion is at the root level, not in a single peripheral nerve.

**Critical dual-innervated pairs by root level:**

| Root | Muscle 1 (Nerve 1) | Muscle 2 (Nerve 2) |
|---|---|---|
| C5 | Deltoid (axillary) | Infraspinatus (suprascapular) |
| C6 | Brachioradialis (radial) | Pronator teres (median) |
| C7 | Triceps (radial) | Flexor carpi radialis (median) |
| C8 | First dorsal interosseous (ulnar) | Flexor pollicis longus (AIN/median) |
| L4 | Vastus medialis (femoral) | Tibialis anterior (deep fibular) |
| L5 | Tibialis anterior (deep fibular) | Tibialis posterior (tibial) |
| S1 | Medial gastrocnemius (tibial) | Gluteus maximus (inferior gluteal) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** When constructing a needle EMG screen for radiculopathy, always include at least two muscles innervated by different peripheral nerves at the suspected root level. Without this cross-nerve confirmation, the findings could represent a single peripheral nerve lesion rather than a radiculopathy.

## 6.8 H-Reflex in Radiculopathy Evaluation 

### 6.8.1 Physiology of the H-Reflex

The H-reflex is the electrophysiological analog of the monosynaptic stretch reflex. It is elicited by submaximal stimulation of the tibial nerve at the popliteal fossa, which preferentially activates large-diameter Ia afferent fibers. The afferent impulse travels proximally through the tibial nerve, sciatic nerve, and S1 root to the spinal cord, where it synapses on alpha motor neurons. The efferent response returns through the S1 root, sciatic nerve, and tibial nerve to produce a compound muscle action potential in the soleus muscle.

The H-reflex therefore traverses the entire S1 reflex arc -- peripheral sensory nerve, dorsal root, spinal cord, ventral root, and peripheral motor nerve. Any lesion along this pathway can prolong or abolish the H-reflex.

### 6.8.2 Application in S1 Radiculopathy

The H-reflex is the **most useful late response** in the evaluation of radiculopathy. Its clinical utility is primarily in the assessment of S1 radiculopathy:

- A unilaterally absent or prolonged H-reflex (side-to-side latency difference greater than 1.5 to 2.0 milliseconds) supports S1 radiculopathy
- Sensitivity: Approximately 50 to 60% for S1 radiculopathy (higher in acute cases)
- The H-reflex may remain abnormal after clinical recovery, serving as evidence of prior S1 root injury

**Limitations:**

- The standard tibial-soleus H-reflex primarily evaluates the **S1 root only**
- It is not specific for radiculopathy -- any lesion in the S1 reflex arc (peripheral neuropathy, sciatic neuropathy, plexopathy) can produce an abnormal H-reflex
- The H-reflex is absent bilaterally in many elderly patients (over age 60 to 65), reducing its diagnostic utility in this population
- Bilateral S1 radiculopathy (e.g., from central disc herniation) produces bilaterally absent H-reflexes, making side-to-side comparison impossible

> **Clinical Pearl:** The H-reflex is most diagnostically useful when it is **unilaterally** absent or prolonged. Bilateral absence should be interpreted cautiously, especially in older patients, and may reflect age-related changes, polyneuropathy, or bilateral pathology.

## 6.9 F-Waves in Radiculopathy Evaluation 

### 6.9.1 F-Wave Physiology

F-waves are late motor responses generated by antidromic activation of a small percentage of alpha motor neurons in the anterior horn. When a peripheral motor nerve is stimulated supramaximally, the action potential travels both distally (producing the CMAP) and proximally (antidromic conduction). The antidromic impulse reaches the anterior horn cell, and in approximately 1 to 5% of motor neurons, it triggers a new action potential that travels orthodromically back to the muscle, producing a small, late potential.

### 6.9.2 Utility and Limitations in Radiculopathy

F-waves evaluate the proximal motor nerve pathway, including the nerve root. In theory, a lesion at the root level should prolong or abolish F-wave responses. In practice, the diagnostic utility of F-waves in radiculopathy is **limited**:

- **Low sensitivity:** F-waves are often normal in mild to moderate radiculopathy because the majority of motor axons may still conduct normally
- **Poor localizing value:** F-waves cannot identify which specific root level is affected, as they represent the response of all motor neurons in the nerve (which span multiple root levels)
- **Nonspecific:** Prolonged F-wave latencies can result from any process affecting the proximal motor pathway -- polyneuropathy, plexopathy, proximal nerve lesion
- **Fibular and tibial F-waves** have particularly low sensitivity for detecting lumbar nerve root compromise

F-waves have greater utility in conditions that produce diffuse proximal conduction abnormalities, such as Guillain-Barre syndrome or chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy, than in focal radiculopathy.

## 6.10 NCS Findings in Radiculopathy 

### 6.10.1 Sensory NCS

**SNAPs are expected to be normal** in pure radiculopathy. This is the direct application of the DRG principle discussed in Section 6.2. If a SNAP is abnormal in the territory of the clinical deficit, the differential must expand to include plexopathy, peripheral neuropathy, or a concurrent condition.

### 6.10.2 Motor NCS

CMAPs are often normal in radiculopathy due to the multi-root innervation of most muscles. Each peripheral nerve derives its motor axons from two to four root levels, so loss of a single root's contribution may not produce a detectable amplitude decrease in the CMAP. In severe radiculopathy with extensive axonal loss, the CMAP amplitude may be reduced, but this is the exception rather than the rule.

### 6.10.3 Summary of Expected NCS Findings

| NCS Parameter | Expected Finding | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| SNAP amplitude | Normal | DRG principle; preganglionic lesion |
| SNAP latency/velocity | Normal | No peripheral sensory nerve involvement |
| CMAP amplitude | Normal (usually) | May be reduced in severe axonal loss |
| CMAP latency/velocity | Normal | No demyelination of peripheral motor nerve |
| H-reflex | May be prolonged/absent (S1) | Most sensitive late response |
| F-wave | Usually normal | Low sensitivity, nonlocalizing |

## 6.11 The HI MADAM Mnemonic 

The mnemonic **HI MADAM** is a board-relevant memory aid for the typical EDX findings in radiculopathy:

- **H** -- H-reflex may be abnormal (S1 radiculopathy)
- **I** -- Insertional activity is increased (acute denervation)
- **M** -- Motor NCS may show reduced CMAP amplitude (severe cases)
- **A** -- Abnormal spontaneous activity (fibrillation potentials, PSWs)
- **D** -- Decreased recruitment
- **A** -- Absent or abnormal paraspinal activity (fibrillations in paraspinals)
- **M** -- Myotomal distribution of needle EMG abnormalities

This mnemonic captures the key electrodiagnostic features that, when present in combination, support a diagnosis of radiculopathy on EDX testing.

## 6.12 Chronic Radiculopathy: EDX Pitfalls 

The electrodiagnostic evaluation of chronic radiculopathy is fraught with interpretive challenges. As time passes after the initial nerve root injury, the electrodiagnostic picture evolves in ways that can obscure the diagnosis:

### 6.12.1 Resolution of Fibrillation Potentials

Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves gradually diminish over time as reinnervation occurs. Collateral sprouts from surviving motor neurons reinnervate the denervated muscle fibers, restoring neural input and eliminating the denervation supersensitivity that drives spontaneous activity. In a fully reinnervated muscle, fibrillation potentials may be entirely absent.

### 6.12.2 Chronic Reinnervation MUAP Changes

As collateral sprouting matures, MUAPs evolve into large-amplitude, long-duration, polyphasic potentials. These "giant MUAPs" may be the **only** residual finding in chronic radiculopathy. They confirm that a previous neurogenic process occurred but cannot establish whether it is ongoing or remote.

### 6.12.3 Normalization of Recruitment

With successful reinnervation, motor unit numbers may partially or fully recover. Recruitment that was initially reduced may improve to normal or near-normal levels, further reducing the sensitivity of the study.

### 6.12.4 Clinical Implications

A negative EMG in a patient with chronic radiculopathy symptoms does not exclude the diagnosis. It may indicate:

- Complete reinnervation with resolution of all acute changes
- A predominantly sensory or demyelinating radiculopathy that never produced significant axonal loss
- Symptom origin from structures not assessed by EMG (facet joints, ligaments, disc annulus)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In chronic radiculopathy, the only EMG abnormality may be large, long-duration, polyphasic MUAPs with no fibrillation potentials and normal recruitment. This finding confirms prior denervation and reinnervation but does not prove ongoing root compression. Correlation with imaging and clinical findings is essential.

## 6.13 Limitations of EMG in Radiculopathy 

The electrodiagnostic evaluation, despite its value, has well-recognized limitations in the assessment of radiculopathy:

1. **EMG detects only motor axon loss.** Purely sensory radiculopathies, demyelinating radiculopathies (neurapraxia), and radiculopathies producing only pain without motor fiber damage will yield normal EMG results.

2. **Timing dependency.** Studies performed before fibrillation potentials have had time to develop (less than 2 to 3 weeks) or after complete reinnervation (months to years) will have reduced sensitivity.

3. **Myotomal overlap.** The extensive overlap of root innervation at certain levels (L2-L4, C5-C6, C6-C7) makes precise single-root localization difficult.

4. **No correlation with pain.** EMG cannot assess the sensory nerve root contribution, and many patients' primary complaint -- radicular pain -- has no EMG correlate.

5. **Pre-existing conditions.** Concurrent peripheral neuropathy, prior radiculopathy, or previous spine surgery can confound interpretation by producing SNAP abnormalities or paraspinal changes unrelated to the current episode.

6. **Sampling error.** Not every muscle in a myotome can be examined, and the muscles sampled may not be the ones most affected by the radiculopathy.

7. **Patient cooperation.** Assessment of recruitment and MUAP morphology requires the patient's active participation. Pain, anxiety, or central nervous system conditions may limit the quality of voluntary activation.

| CMAP | Normal or reduced | Reduced | Reduced |

## Review Questions 

1. A 45-year-old man presents with three weeks of right foot drop. NCS reveal a normal superficial fibular SNAP on the right. Needle EMG shows fibrillation potentials in the tibialis anterior, peroneus longus, tibialis posterior, and tensor fasciae latae. Lumbar paraspinals show 2+ fibrillations. What is the diagnosis?

   **Answer:** Right L5 radiculopathy. The normal superficial fibular SNAP is consistent with a preganglionic (root level) lesion. Fibrillations in muscles from multiple peripheral nerves (deep fibular, superficial fibular, tibial, superior gluteal) that all share L5 innervation confirm a myotomal pattern. Paraspinal fibrillations further support a root-level localization.

2. A patient has right S1 radiculopathy symptoms. The H-reflex is absent on the right and normal on the left. The sural SNAP is normal bilaterally. What do these findings indicate?

   **Answer:** The unilaterally absent right H-reflex supports right S1 radiculopathy. The normal sural SNAP is consistent with a preganglionic (root level) lesion, as expected by the DRG principle. The absent H-reflex indicates disruption somewhere along the S1 reflex arc. Combined with the normal SNAP, the most likely localization is the S1 root.

3. A patient had lumbar disc surgery six months ago and now presents with new leg symptoms. Needle EMG shows fibrillation potentials in the lumbar paraspinal muscles. Should this finding be attributed to new radiculopathy?

   **Answer:** Not necessarily. Paraspinal fibrillation potentials after lumbar spine surgery are unreliable markers of new radiculopathy because surgical disruption of the paraspinal muscles produces chronic denervation changes that may persist indefinitely. The diagnosis of new radiculopathy must be based on limb muscle findings in a myotomal distribution.
