## 10.1 Introduction: The Motor Neuron Disease Spectrum 

Motor neuron diseases (MNDs) encompass a group of progressive neurodegenerative disorders characterized by selective loss of upper motor neurons (UMNs), lower motor neurons (LMNs), or both. The electrodiagnostic evaluation plays an essential role in confirming the presence of widespread LMN involvement, excluding alternative diagnoses, and quantifying the extent of disease.

The MND spectrum includes:

| Disease | UMN Involvement | LMN Involvement | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| ALS (classic) | Yes | Yes | Combined UMN + LMN in multiple regions |
| Primary lateral sclerosis (PLS) | Yes | No (early) | Pure UMN for at least 4 years |
| Progressive muscular atrophy (PMA) | No (clinically) | Yes | Pure LMN; may represent ALS variant |
| Progressive bulbar palsy | Yes (pseudobulbar) | Yes (bulbar) | Bulbar-onset ALS |
| Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) | No | Yes | Hereditary; SMN1 gene |
| Kennedy disease (SBMA) | No | Yes | X-linked; androgen receptor; sensory changes |
| Post-polio syndrome | No | Yes (prior polio) | Remote polio history; new weakness decades later |

## 10.2 Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis: Diagnostic Criteria 

### 10.2.1 El Escorial Criteria (1994, revised 2000)

The El Escorial criteria, originally published in 1994 and revised in 2000, established the first standardized framework for ALS diagnosis. They define four body regions (bulbar, cervical, thoracic, lumbosacral) and require evidence of combined UMN and LMN degeneration in progressively more regions for increasing diagnostic certainty.

**Revised El Escorial Classification:**

| Category | Requirements |
|---|---|
| Clinically Definite ALS | UMN + LMN signs in 3 body regions |
| Clinically Probable ALS | UMN + LMN signs in 2 body regions, with UMN signs rostral to LMN signs |
| Clinically Probable -- Laboratory Supported | UMN + LMN signs in 1 region, OR UMN signs in 1 region with LMN signs by EMG in at least 2 regions |
| Clinically Possible ALS | UMN + LMN signs in 1 region, OR UMN signs in 2 or more regions |

**Limitations:** The El Escorial criteria were developed primarily for clinical research enrollment and are criticized for poor sensitivity, particularly early in the disease course. Many patients never achieve "clinically definite" status during their lifetime. The criteria also undervalue EMG evidence, relegating it to the "laboratory supported" category.

### 10.2.2 Awaji-Shima Criteria (2008) 

The Awaji-Shima criteria represent a critical revision that elevated the diagnostic value of electrodiagnostic findings to the same level as clinical examination. The key innovation was recognizing that fasciculation potentials, in the clinical context of suspected motor neuron disease, carry the same diagnostic weight as fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves for documenting LMN involvement.

**Key Awaji modifications:**

1. **Fasciculation potentials are equivalent to fibrillation potentials** for recognizing active denervation, provided they occur in a muscle with chronic neurogenic MUP changes in the clinical context of a progressive motor disorder
2. **EMG evidence of LMN degeneration is equivalent to clinical evidence** (eliminates the "laboratory supported" category -- all EMG-positive regions count as clinically positive)
3. Maintains the El Escorial requirement for demonstrating UMN and LMN involvement in the same region

The practical impact of the Awaji criteria was to increase diagnostic sensitivity for early ALS, particularly in patients with fasciculations but limited fibrillation potentials. In a muscle showing fasciculation potentials alongside chronic neurogenic MUP changes (increased duration and amplitude, reduced recruitment), the electromyographer can now count that region as showing active LMN involvement.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Not all fasciculation potentials are equal. Benign fasciculations tend to be simple in morphology (similar to normal MUAPs) and stable on repeated firing. Fasciculations in ALS tend to be complex (polyphasic, unstable morphology) and are often accompanied by other signs of denervation. The clinical context is paramount.

### 10.2.3 Gold Coast Criteria (2019/2020) 

The Gold Coast criteria (GCC), developed at a consensus meeting in Gold Coast, Australia in 2019 and published by Shefner et al. in 2020, represent the most recent simplification of ALS diagnosis. They replace the multi-tiered El Escorial/Awaji classification with a dichotomous system: "ALS" or "not ALS."

**Gold Coast Criteria Requirements:**

1. **Progressive motor impairment** documented by history or repeated clinical assessment, preceded by normal motor function
2. **Upper AND lower motor neuron dysfunction** in at least one body region, OR **lower motor neuron dysfunction** in at least two body regions
3. **Investigations excluding alternative diagnoses**

**Performance data (2024-2025 meta-analysis):**

| Criteria | Sensitivity | Specificity | AUC | Diagnostic Odds Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gold Coast | ~95% | ~66% | 0.95 | 36.1 |
| Revised El Escorial (Probable+) | ~59% | ~94% | -- | -- |
| Awaji (Probable+) | ~54% | ~94% | -- | -- |

The GCC achieve dramatically improved sensitivity (95% vs. 54-59%) at the cost of reduced specificity (66% vs. 94%). This trade-off is considered acceptable because the consequences of delayed ALS diagnosis (lost treatment time, delayed supportive care, ineligibility for clinical trials) outweigh the risks of temporary misdiagnosis, which is ultimately corrected through follow-up.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Gold Coast criteria permit ALS diagnosis with pure LMN findings in at least 2 body regions, provided alternative diagnoses are excluded. This is a major departure from El Escorial/Awaji, which required UMN signs. This change captures PMA patients who likely have underlying ALS pathology.

### 10.2.4 Comparison of Diagnostic Criteria

| Feature | El Escorial | Awaji | Gold Coast |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classification system | 4-tier | 4-tier (modified) | Dichotomous |
| Role of fasciculations | Not equivalent to fibs | Equivalent to fibs/PSWs | Clinical judgment |
| EMG vs. clinical | EMG subordinate | EMG equivalent | EMG equivalent |
| Pure LMN permitted | No | No | Yes (2+ regions) |
| Emphasis | Research enrollment | Earlier diagnosis | Clinical practice |

## 10.3 Split Hand Syndrome and Other Clinical EDX Correlations in ALS 

### 10.3.1 The Split Hand Phenomenon

The "split hand" is one of the most distinctive clinical and electrophysiological features of ALS. It refers to the preferential wasting and weakness of the thenar (lateral hand) muscles (abductor pollicis brevis and first dorsal interosseous) compared to the hypothenar (medial hand) muscles (abductor digiti minimi). This dissociation produces a characteristic pattern of hand atrophy.

**EDX manifestation of split hand:**
- CMAP amplitude recorded from APB (median nerve) is disproportionately reduced compared to CMAP amplitude recorded from ADM (ulnar nerve)
- The Split Hand Index (SHI) = (APB CMAP x FDI CMAP) / ADM CMAP. Values below the normal threshold suggest ALS
- This index has been proposed as an early diagnostic biomarker for ALS

**Pathophysiology:** The split hand is thought to reflect the preferential vulnerability of corticomotoneuronal projections to the thenar muscles. The APB and FDI receive dense direct corticospinal input (important for fine manipulative movements), making their motor neurons more susceptible to the excitotoxic cascade in ALS. The hypothenar muscles receive relatively less direct cortical input.

### 10.3.2 The Split Leg Phenomenon

An analogous "split leg" pattern has been described in ALS, with preferential weakness of ankle dorsiflexion (tibialis anterior, deep fibular nerve) compared to ankle plantarflexion (gastrocnemius-soleus, tibial nerve). The ratio of fibular CMAP to tibial CMAP is disproportionately reduced.

### 10.3.3 Fasciculation Potentials: Benign vs. Pathological

Distinguishing benign fasciculations from those associated with ALS is a common clinical challenge. Several features help differentiate them:

| Feature | Benign Fasciculations | ALS Fasciculations |
|---|---|---|
| MUP morphology | Simple (resembles normal MUAP) | Complex (polyphasic, unstable) |
| Firing pattern | Semi-rhythmic; may be repetitive | Irregular |
| Associated denervation | Absent (no fibs/PSWs) | Present (fibs/PSWs, chronic neurogenic changes) |
| MUP changes | Normal duration and amplitude | Increased duration and amplitude |
| Distribution | Often limited to calves | Widespread across multiple regions |
| Progression | Stable over months to years | Progressive weakness develops |
| Patient age | Often younger (20s-40s) | More common in older adults (50s-70s) |
| Associated symptoms | Cramps without weakness | Progressive weakness and atrophy |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most important distinguishing feature is NOT the fasciculation itself but the company it keeps. Fasciculations with a normal EMG (no fibrillation potentials, normal MUP morphology, normal recruitment) are benign. Fasciculations accompanied by active denervation and chronic neurogenic changes in a muscle suggest motor neuron disease.

## 10.4 Electrodiagnostic Findings in ALS 

### 10.3.1 Nerve Conduction Studies

The NCS findings in ALS reflect pure motor neuron loss without primary myelin or sensory nerve involvement.

**Motor NCS:**
- CMAP amplitudes: May be reduced (reflecting LMN loss and muscle atrophy), but degree varies by disease stage and body region
- Conduction velocities: Normal or mildly reduced (remaining axons conduct normally; mild slowing reflects loss of the largest, fastest-conducting motor axons)
- Distal motor latencies: Normal or mildly prolonged
- F-wave latencies: Normal or mildly prolonged; F-wave persistence may be reduced
- No conduction block (critical for excluding MMNCB)
- No temporal dispersion

**Sensory NCS:**
- **Normal** -- this is the single most important NCS finding in ALS

> **Clinical Pearl:** Normal sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) in the setting of progressive motor weakness and widespread denervation is the electrodiagnostic hallmark of motor neuron disease. Abnormal SNAPs should prompt consideration of alternative diagnoses: Kennedy disease (SBMA), multifocal motor neuropathy, cervical myelopathy with superimposed neuropathy, or polyradiculopathy.

**AANEM minimum study for suspected MND:**
- Up to 4 motor NCS and 2 sensory NCS
- Needle EMG of up to 4 extremities (or 3 limbs plus bulbar muscles)

### 10.3.2 Needle EMG Findings

The EMG in ALS demonstrates the coexistence of active (ongoing) denervation and chronic (compensated) reinnervation, reflecting the progressive but relentless nature of motor neuron loss.

**Active denervation:**
- Fibrillation potentials: Present in denervated muscle fibers that have lost their motor neuron
- Positive sharp waves: Present alongside fibrillations
- Fasciculation potentials: Spontaneous firing of entire motor units; often complex and unstable in ALS

**Chronic reinnervation:**
- Increased MUP duration: Surviving motor neurons sprout to reinnervate denervated fibers, enlarging the motor unit territory
- Increased MUP amplitude: More muscle fibers per motor unit produce larger potentials ("giant MUAPs" may exceed 10-20 mV)
- Increased polyphasia: Reinnervated motor units have less synchronized fiber firing
- Decreased recruitment: Fewer motor units are available, so remaining units fire at faster rates to generate force
- Unstable MUPs: Newly reinnervated fibers have immature neuromuscular junctions, producing variability in MUP morphology on successive firings

**Key diagnostic requirement:** BOTH acute denervation (fibrillations, PSWs, or fasciculation potentials per Awaji) AND chronic neurogenic changes (large MUPs, reduced recruitment) must coexist in the same muscle. Chronic neurogenic changes alone may reflect a remote, static process; acute denervation alone may reflect a different acute process.

### 10.3.3 Regional Distribution

For diagnostic purposes, the body is divided into four regions. EMG abnormalities should be documented in as many regions as possible:

| Region | Representative Muscles |
|---|---|
| Bulbar | Tongue (genioglossus), masseter, orbicularis oris, mentalis |
| Cervical | Deltoid, biceps, triceps, first dorsal interosseous, APB |
| Thoracic | Paraspinal muscles (T6-T10) |
| Lumbosacral | Vastus lateralis, tibialis anterior, gastrocnemius, EDB |

**Thoracic paraspinal EMG** is particularly useful in ALS diagnosis because the thoracic region is rarely affected by other common conditions (radiculopathy, entrapment neuropathy) and provides an additional body region for diagnostic criteria fulfillment.

> **Clinical Pearl:** When evaluating a patient with suspected ALS who has only cervical and lumbosacral EMG abnormalities, adding thoracic paraspinal examination can provide evidence of a third body region, potentially upgrading the diagnostic certainty.

## 10.4 Spinal Muscular Atrophy 

Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) is an autosomal recessive disorder caused by homozygous deletion or mutation of the survival motor neuron 1 (SMN1) gene on chromosome 5q13. The SMN protein is essential for motor neuron survival. Disease severity is modulated by the copy number of SMN2, a nearly identical gene that produces predominantly a truncated, less functional protein but can compensate partially for SMN1 loss.

### 10.4.1 SMA Classification

| Type | Onset | Motor Milestones | SMN2 Copies | Survival | Alternate Name |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| SMA I | 0-6 months | Never sit independently | 1-2 | <2 years (untreated) | Werdnig-Hoffmann |
| SMA II | 6-18 months | Sit but never walk independently | 2-3 | Adolescence to adult | Dubowitz |
| SMA III | >18 months | Walk independently (may lose later) | 3-4 | Normal or near-normal | Kugelberg-Welander |
| SMA IV | >21 years | Normal early milestones | 4+ | Normal | Adult-onset SMA |

### 10.4.2 EDX Findings in SMA

- **Motor NCS:** Reduced CMAP amplitudes reflecting motor neuron loss; conduction velocities are normal or mildly reduced
- **Sensory NCS:** Normal (anterior horn cell disease spares sensory neurons)
- **EMG:** Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves (active denervation); large-amplitude, long-duration, polyphasic MUAPs (chronic reinnervation); reduced recruitment
- **SMA I distinguishing feature:** Tongue fasciculations are common; generalized hypotonia with severe weakness

### 10.4.3 SMA Treatment Revolution 

Three disease-modifying therapies have transformed SMA outcomes:

| Drug | Mechanism | Route | Indication | Key Details |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nusinersen (Spinraza) | Antisense oligonucleotide; modifies SMN2 splicing to increase full-length SMN protein | Intrathecal | All SMA types | FDA 2016; loading + maintenance doses |
| Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma) | AAV9 gene therapy delivering functional SMN1 gene | IV (one-time) | <2 years old | FDA 2019; most expensive single-dose drug |
| Risdiplam (Evrysdi) | Small molecule; modifies SMN2 splicing | Oral (daily) | All ages, all types | FDA 2020; first oral SMA therapy |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Presymptomatic treatment of SMA (identified by newborn screening) produces dramatically better outcomes than treatment initiated after symptom onset. All 50 US states now include SMA in their newborn screening panels.

### 10.5.4 EDX Findings Across SMA Types 

| Feature | SMA I | SMA II | SMA III | SMA IV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CMAP amplitude | Markedly reduced or absent | Reduced | Mildly reduced or normal | Normal or mildly reduced |
| Conduction velocity | Normal or mildly slow | Normal | Normal | Normal |
| SNAP | Normal | Normal | Normal | Normal |
| Fibrillation potentials | Prominent, diffuse | Present, widespread | Variable; may be mild | Mild or absent |
| Fasciculations | Prominent (including tongue) | Present | Variable | Variable |
| MUP changes | Limited reinnervation (severe loss) | Large MUAPs (chronic) | Large MUAPs | Large MUAPs |
| Tongue EMG | Denervation + fasciculations | Variable | Usually normal | Normal |

### 10.5.5 SMN2 Copy Number and Phenotype Correlation 

The number of SMN2 gene copies is the primary modifier of SMA severity:

- **1 copy:** Almost always SMA type I (severe)
- **2 copies:** Usually SMA type I or II
- **3 copies:** Usually SMA type II or III
- **4+ copies:** Usually SMA type III or IV (mild)

This correlation is imperfect; modifier genes and epigenetic factors also influence phenotype. However, SMN2 copy number is used clinically for prognosis and treatment planning.

### 10.5.6 Hexosaminidase A Deficiency (Adult Tay-Sachs) 

Late-onset hexosaminidase A (Hex-A) deficiency is a rare but important motor neuron disease mimic that presents in adolescence or early adulthood with progressive proximal weakness, ataxia, and sometimes psychiatric symptoms. EDX findings resemble SMA with chronic denervation and neurogenic MUP changes. The key to diagnosis is recognizing the clinical context (particularly Ashkenazi Jewish heritage) and testing serum hexosaminidase enzyme levels. Unlike ALS, UMN signs are typically absent, and the course is very slowly progressive.

## 10.5 Kennedy Disease (Spinobulbar Muscular Atrophy, SBMA) 

### 10.5.1 Genetics and Pathophysiology

Kennedy disease is an X-linked recessive trinucleotide repeat expansion disorder affecting the androgen receptor (AR) gene on chromosome Xq11-12. The expansion consists of CAG repeats (normally 10-36; pathogenic when >=38) within exon 1 of the AR gene. The mutant androgen receptor protein accumulates in motor neurons, causing selective toxicity.

Because the disease is X-linked, it overwhelmingly affects males. Female carriers are rarely symptomatic (mild symptoms may occur due to skewed X-inactivation).

### 10.5.2 Clinical Features

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Age of onset | Typically 30-50 years |
| Weakness pattern | Proximal > distal; bulbar involvement prominent |
| Fasciculations | Prominent, especially perioral (chin quivering) |
| Gynecomastia | Present in >50% (androgen insensitivity) |
| Testicular atrophy | Common |
| Sensory symptoms | Numbness, paresthesias in extremities |
| Progression | Very slow (decades); life expectancy near-normal |
| Cramps | Prominent early symptom |

### 10.5.3 EDX Findings -- Key Board Differentiators from ALS 

| Feature | Kennedy Disease (SBMA) | ALS |
|---|---|---|
| Inheritance | X-linked recessive | Usually sporadic (10% familial) |
| SNAPs | **Abnormal** (reduced or absent) | **Normal** |
| CMAP amplitudes | Reduced | Reduced |
| Conduction velocities | Normal | Normal |
| Fibrillation potentials | Present | Present |
| Fasciculation potentials | Prominent | Prominent |
| MUP changes | Large, chronic neurogenic | Large, chronic neurogenic |
| UMN signs | Absent | Present |
| Gynecomastia | Present | Absent |
| Sensory symptoms | Present | Absent |
| Progression rate | Very slow (decades) | Rapid (2-5 years) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The combination of LMN-predominant weakness with abnormal SNAPs, gynecomastia, and absence of UMN signs should immediately suggest Kennedy disease rather than ALS. Genetic testing for CAG repeat expansion in the AR gene confirms the diagnosis. This distinction is critical because Kennedy disease has a much more favorable prognosis.

## 10.6 Kennedy Disease: Detailed Clinical and EDX Analysis 

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

Kennedy disease (SBMA) remains one of the most important conditions to distinguish from ALS because of the dramatically different prognosis. Several additional features aid in this distinction.

**Laboratory findings in Kennedy disease:**
- Elevated CK (often 3-10x normal, reflecting chronic muscle damage)
- Elevated testosterone (or normal) with clinical androgen insensitivity
- Glucose intolerance and dyslipidemia (metabolic features of androgen receptor dysfunction)
- Liver enzyme elevation (may be seen)

**Genetic testing:**
The definitive diagnostic test is measurement of CAG repeat length in the androgen receptor gene. Normal individuals have 10-36 CAG repeats; pathogenic expansion is >=38 repeats. Longer repeat lengths generally correlate with earlier onset and greater disease severity (anticipation is variable).

**Natural history:**
Kennedy disease progresses much more slowly than ALS, with a mean disease duration of 20-30 years from symptom onset. Respiratory failure is uncommon. The primary sources of morbidity are progressive weakness (particularly bulbar), aspiration risk, and metabolic complications.

**EMG tip for distinguishing Kennedy disease from ALS:**
In Kennedy disease, the combination of low-amplitude SNAPs with neurogenic motor changes should immediately raise suspicion. While ALS always shows normal SNAPs, Kennedy disease shows reduced SNAP amplitudes (often diffusely) reflecting the sensory neuronopathy component. Additionally, Kennedy disease tends to show chronic neurogenic changes with relatively less acute denervation (fewer fibrillation potentials proportionally) compared to ALS, reflecting the slower rate of motor neuron loss.

## 10.7 Primary Lateral Sclerosis (PLS) 

PLS is a rare motor neuron disease characterized by progressive, symmetric UMN dysfunction without clinical evidence of LMN involvement for at least 4 years from symptom onset. If LMN signs develop before the 4-year threshold, the diagnosis is reclassified as UMN-dominant ALS.

**EDX findings in PLS:**
- Motor NCS: Typically normal CMAP amplitudes (no significant LMN loss)
- Sensory NCS: Normal
- EMG: Normal or minimal chronic neurogenic changes (no active denervation)
- Importantly, the EMG may be entirely normal in PLS, which is the expected finding

**Prognosis:** PLS has a significantly better prognosis than ALS, with median survival exceeding 10-20 years. However, approximately 10-20% of patients initially diagnosed with PLS eventually develop LMN signs and are reclassified as ALS.

---

## 10.7 Progressive Muscular Atrophy (PMA) 

PMA presents with pure LMN signs (weakness, atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia) without clinical UMN involvement. The relationship between PMA and ALS is debated, as autopsy studies demonstrate UMN pathology (corticospinal tract degeneration) in approximately 50% of clinically diagnosed PMA patients, suggesting PMA may represent a LMN-predominant variant of ALS rather than a distinct entity.

**EDX findings:**
- Motor NCS: Reduced CMAP amplitudes
- Sensory NCS: Normal
- EMG: Widespread active denervation with chronic neurogenic changes, identical to ALS

Under the Gold Coast criteria, PMA patients meeting the requirement of LMN dysfunction in at least 2 body regions (with exclusion of alternatives) can now be diagnosed as ALS.

## 10.8 Flail Arm and Flail Leg Syndromes 

### Flail Arm Syndrome (Vulpian-Bernhart Syndrome)

Flail arm syndrome is a regional variant of ALS characterized by progressive bilateral proximal upper limb weakness and wasting with relative sparing of other body regions for an extended period (typically >12-18 months). Patients present with bilateral arm weakness, often with prominent deltoid and biceps wasting, creating a "man-in-a-barrel" appearance.

**EDX findings:**
- Active and chronic denervation predominantly in cervical myotomes bilaterally
- Relative sparing of lumbosacral and bulbar regions early in the course
- Motor NCS show reduced CMAP amplitudes in upper extremity nerves
- Sensory NCS are normal

**Prognosis:** Flail arm syndrome has a significantly better prognosis than classic ALS, with median survival of approximately 4-5 years compared to 2-3 years for limb-onset ALS.

### Flail Leg Syndrome (Pseudopolyneuritic ALS)

Flail leg syndrome presents with progressive bilateral distal lower extremity weakness and wasting, often mimicking a polyneuropathy or bilateral L5 radiculopathy. Foot drop is a common presenting symptom.

**EDX findings:**
- Active and chronic denervation predominantly in lumbosacral myotomes
- The pattern may initially be mistaken for a distal polyneuropathy
- Key differentiator: normal SNAPs despite motor denervation (polyneuropathy would show abnormal SNAPs)

**Prognosis:** Similar to flail arm syndrome, flail leg syndrome has a more favorable prognosis than classic ALS.

## 10.9 ALS Treatment 

### 10.8.1 FDA-Approved Disease-Modifying Therapies

| Drug | Year Approved | Mechanism | Benefit | Key Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Riluzole (Rilutek) | 1995 | Glutamate release inhibitor; reduces excitotoxicity | Extends median survival by ~2-3 months | Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs), fatigue, GI symptoms, dizziness |
| Edaravone (Radicava) | 2017 | Free radical scavenger (neuroprotective) | Slows ALSFRS-R decline in select patients | Infusion reactions; oral formulation available |
| Tofersen (Qalsody) | 2023 | Antisense oligonucleotide targeting SOD1 mRNA | Reduces neurofilament levels; stabilizes symptoms in SOD1-ALS | Intrathecal injection; injection site reactions; myelitis risk |

**Riluzole** remains the only medication demonstrated to extend survival in ALS and should be initiated at the time of diagnosis. The mechanism involves inhibition of presynaptic glutamate release, blocking sodium channels, and interfering with intracellular events that follow binding of excitatory amino acids to glutamate receptors. Liver function tests should be monitored regularly during treatment.

**Edaravone** was approved based on a study showing slower functional decline (measured by ALSFRS-R) in a selected subgroup of early ALS patients with preserved respiratory function. The oral formulation has improved accessibility compared to the original intravenous administration.

**Tofersen** represents the first gene-targeted therapy for ALS. It is an antisense oligonucleotide that binds to SOD1 messenger RNA, promoting its degradation and reducing production of the toxic mutant SOD1 protein. It is indicated only for patients with SOD1 mutations (approximately 2% of all ALS and 12-20% of familial ALS). Long-term data (approximately 3 years) suggest that approximately 25% of treated patients show stabilization of symptoms with functional improvement in grip strength and respiratory function. The drug is administered intrathecally.

### 10.8.2 Withdrawn Therapy

**Relyvrio (AMX0035 -- sodium phenylbutyrate/taurursodiol)** was initially approved via accelerated approval in 2022 based on a small Phase 2 trial (CENTAUR) showing slowed ALSFRS-R decline. The confirmatory Phase 3 PHOENIX trial failed to demonstrate efficacy, and the manufacturer announced voluntary withdrawal from the market on April 4, 2024, with formal discontinuation effective October 31, 2024.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Board examinations may test knowledge of all three currently approved ALS medications (riluzole, edaravone, tofersen) as well as the withdrawal of Relyvrio. Remember that tofersen is specific to SOD1-mutant ALS only.

### 10.8.3 Emerging Biomarkers 

Neurofilament light chain (NfL) measured in serum or cerebrospinal fluid is emerging as a valuable biomarker in ALS. Elevated NfL levels correlate with disease activity and rate of progression. NfL is used as a pharmacodynamic biomarker in tofersen trials (reduction in NfL suggests therapeutic effect on neurodegeneration). Future integration of biomarkers into diagnostic criteria may improve the sensitivity and specificity of the Gold Coast criteria.

## 10.9 Nutritional Management and Gastrostomy Timing in ALS 

### Weight Loss as a Prognostic Factor

Weight loss in ALS is a negative prognostic factor, independent of respiratory function. Hypermetabolism occurs in approximately 50% of ALS patients, contributing to muscle wasting beyond what is explained by denervation alone. Maintaining caloric intake is a priority.

### Gastrostomy Timing

Percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) tube placement should be considered when:
- Oral intake becomes inadequate to maintain weight (>5% weight loss)
- Meal duration exceeds 45-60 minutes due to dysphagia
- Recurrent aspiration events

**Critical timing consideration:** PEG placement carries increased procedural risk when FVC falls below 50% predicted due to the sedation requirements. The recommendation is to place PEG proactively while FVC is still above 50%, even if the patient is still managing oral intake. Radiologically inserted gastrostomy (RIG) may be a safer alternative when FVC is below 50%.

## 10.10 Respiratory Management in ALS 

Respiratory failure is the most common cause of death in ALS. Monitoring respiratory function and timely intervention with noninvasive ventilation (NIV) are critical components of ALS care.

### 10.9.1 Respiratory Monitoring

| Measure | Threshold for Action | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Forced vital capacity (FVC) | <50% predicted | Initiate NIV; discuss advanced directives |
| Maximum inspiratory pressure (MIP) | <-60 cmH2O | Indicates diaphragmatic weakness |
| Sniff nasal inspiratory pressure (SNIP) | <40 cmH2O | More sensitive than FVC for diaphragm weakness |
| Nocturnal oximetry | SpO2 <90% for >5% of recording time | Indicates nocturnal hypoventilation |
| Arterial blood gas | pCO2 >45 mmHg | Indicates ventilatory failure |

### 10.9.2 Noninvasive Ventilation (NIV)

NIV (bilevel positive airway pressure, BiPAP) is the most important intervention for respiratory management in ALS and has been shown to extend survival by a median of approximately 7-11 months while simultaneously improving quality of life.

**Indications for initiating NIV:**
- FVC <50% predicted
- MIP <-60 cmH2O
- SNIP <40 cmH2O
- Symptoms of nocturnal hypoventilation (morning headaches, excessive daytime somnolence, orthopnea)
- Nocturnal desaturation

### 10.9.3 Mechanical Insufflation-Exsufflation (MIE)

MIE (CoughAssist) is an airway clearance device that delivers positive pressure followed by rapid negative pressure to simulate a cough. It is indicated when peak cough flow falls below 270 L/min and is essential for preventing respiratory infections in patients with weakened cough.

### 10.9.4 Invasive Ventilation

Tracheostomy and invasive mechanical ventilation may be considered when NIV is no longer adequate. This decision requires careful discussion of goals of care, as invasive ventilation may extend survival by years but does not halt disease progression. The locked-in state (preserved cognition with complete paralysis) is a potential outcome that must be discussed in advance.

## 10.10 ALSFRS-R: Disease Monitoring 

The ALS Functional Rating Scale-Revised (ALSFRS-R) is the standard clinical outcome measure used to track disease progression and is the primary endpoint in most ALS clinical trials.

**Structure:**
- 12 items assessing function in 4 domains: bulbar (speech, salivation, swallowing), fine motor (handwriting, cutting food, dressing/hygiene), gross motor (turning in bed, walking, climbing stairs), and respiratory (dyspnea, orthopnea, respiratory insufficiency)
- Each item scored 0-4 (4 = normal function, 0 = complete loss)
- Total score range: 0-48 (48 = normal)
- Average decline rate in ALS: approximately 0.9-1.1 points per month

The ALSFRS-R rate of decline is also a prognostic indicator: patients losing more than 1.5 points per month have a more aggressive disease course.

## 10.11 Motor Unit Number Estimation (MUNE) in Motor Neuron Disease 

### 10.11.1 Principles

Motor unit number estimation (MUNE) is a specialized electrodiagnostic technique that estimates the number of functioning motor units in a given muscle. While not routinely performed in clinical practice, MUNE has important research applications in ALS, providing a quantitative measure of motor neuron loss that can detect subclinical decline before clinical weakness becomes apparent.

**Methods of MUNE:**

| Method | Principle | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Incremental stimulation | Gradually increasing stimulus intensity recruits individual motor units; average single motor unit potential (SMUP) amplitude is divided into maximum CMAP | Simple concept | Alternation artifact; operator-dependent |
| Multiple point stimulation | Stimulate at slightly different sites to activate different subsets of motor units | Avoids alternation | Time-consuming |
| Statistical (Poisson) | Uses variability in CMAP amplitude with submaximal stimulation | Automated; reproducible | Assumptions may not hold in diseased nerve |
| Spike-triggered averaging | Voluntary activation of individual motor units; average their contribution | Physiological activation | Requires patient cooperation |
| Decomposition-based | Automated decomposition of interference pattern | Rapid; automated | Technical complexity |

### 10.11.2 MUNE in ALS Monitoring

In ALS, MUNE values decline progressively, often before clinical weakness is detectable. A normal thenar muscle contains approximately 200-300 motor units. In early ALS, MUNE may show a 50% reduction before the patient notices meaningful weakness, because collateral reinnervation compensates for motor neuron loss. Only when compensatory capacity is exhausted does clinical weakness become apparent.

MUNE is used as an outcome measure in ALS clinical trials because it provides a quantitative, objective measure of motor neuron number that is more sensitive to change than clinical assessments such as the ALSFRS-R.

### 10.11.3 Electrical Impedance Myography (EIM) 

Electrical impedance myography is an emerging non-invasive technique that measures the impedance characteristics of muscle tissue. It is painless, rapid, and can detect both myopathic and neurogenic changes in muscle. In ALS, EIM values correlate with disease progression and may complement MUNE and ALSFRS-R as a biomarker. While not yet standard clinical practice, EIM is increasingly used in ALS clinical trials.

## 10.12 Poliomyelitis and Post-Polio Syndrome 

### 10.11.1 Acute Poliomyelitis

Acute poliomyelitis is caused by the poliovirus, which selectively destroys anterior horn cells in the spinal cord and motor nuclei in the brainstem. The acute phase produces an asymmetric, pure LMN paralysis (flaccid paralysis with areflexia) without sensory involvement. While acute polio has been nearly eradicated globally, the late effects of prior polio infection remain clinically relevant.

**EDX findings in remote (prior) polio:**
- Motor NCS: Reduced CMAP amplitudes in affected muscles
- Sensory NCS: Normal
- EMG: Chronic neurogenic changes -- large-amplitude, long-duration MUAPs ("giant MUAPs" reflecting extensive collateral reinnervation), stable recruitment pattern, usually no active denervation (fibrillation potentials absent if the disease is stable)

### 10.11.2 Post-Polio Syndrome 

Post-polio syndrome (PPS) occurs decades (typically 15-40 years) after acute poliomyelitis and is characterized by new onset of weakness, fatigue, pain, and functional decline in previously affected and sometimes clinically unaffected muscles.

**Diagnostic criteria for PPS:**
1. Prior episode of paralytic poliomyelitis with residual motor neuron loss (confirmed by history, neurological examination, or EDX)
2. Period of partial or complete functional recovery followed by a stable interval of at least 15 years
3. Gradual onset of new weakness, with or without fatigue, muscle atrophy, or pain
4. Exclusion of alternative diagnoses

**Pathophysiology:** During recovery from acute polio, surviving motor neurons sprout extensively to reinnervate denervated muscle fibers, producing greatly enlarged motor units. Over decades, these overextended motor neurons undergo distal degeneration of terminal axonal sprouts, leading to progressive denervation of muscle fibers at the periphery of the enlarged motor unit. This is metabolic exhaustion of the motor neuron, not reactivation of poliovirus.

**EDX findings in PPS:**
- Giant MUAPs (amplitudes often >8-10 mV, sometimes >20 mV) reflecting the extensive reinnervation from the initial recovery
- Increased MUP instability (jiggle) on consecutive firings, reflecting failing distal terminals
- New fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves in muscles with new weakness (indicating active denervation superimposed on chronic changes)
- Reduced motor unit number estimation (MUNE) compared to stable post-polio patients
- Increased SFEMG jitter and blocking (neuromuscular junction instability at the enlarged motor unit's distal terminals)

### 10.11.3 The Halstead-Ross Staging System 

Halstead and Ross described the temporal stages of post-polio, which provide a framework for understanding the natural history:

| Stage | Period | Description |
|---|---|---|
| I | Acute poliomyelitis | Viral infection with anterior horn cell destruction |
| II | Recovery | Axonal sprouting and reinnervation (months to years) |
| III | Stability | Functional plateau; compensated enlarged motor units (years to decades) |
| IV | Post-polio syndrome | New weakness, fatigue, pain from decompensation of enlarged motor units |

**Management of PPS:**
- Activity modification and energy conservation
- Targeted strengthening (low-intensity, avoiding overwork weakness)
- Assistive devices and orthotics
- Weight management
- Treatment of sleep-disordered breathing
- No disease-modifying pharmacotherapy is currently available (IVIg trials have shown mixed results)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Giant MUAPs (>10 mV) are a hallmark of prior polio. If you encounter giant MUAPs in a patient without a known polio history, consider: (1) remote polio (subclinical infection or forgotten childhood illness), (2) advanced ALS with extensive reinnervation, or (3) chronic severe radiculopathy with long-standing reinnervation.

## 10.12 ALSFRS-R: Detailed Domain Analysis 

### Domain Structure

The ALSFRS-R contains 12 items across 4 domains, each scored 0-4 (total 0-48, where 48 = normal):

**Bulbar Domain (Items 1-3):** Speech, salivation, swallowing
**Fine Motor Domain (Items 4-6):** Handwriting, cutting food/handling utensils, dressing and hygiene
**Gross Motor Domain (Items 7-9):** Turning in bed, walking, climbing stairs
**Respiratory Domain (Items 10-12):** Dyspnea, orthopnea, respiratory insufficiency

### Rate of Decline and Prognosis

Average decline is approximately 0.9-1.1 points per month. This rate is relatively linear but varies significantly between patients:

- **Slow progressors:** <0.5 points/month -- more favorable prognosis
- **Average progressors:** 0.5-1.5 points/month -- median ALS trajectory
- **Rapid progressors:** >1.5 points/month -- aggressive disease, shorter survival

The respiratory domain is particularly important as respiratory decline is the most direct predictor of mortality. The ALSFRS-R slope (rate of change over time) is used as a primary or secondary endpoint in virtually all ALS clinical trials and provides an integrated measure of functional decline across all motor domains.

The King's Clinical Staging System provides an alternative framework, staging ALS based on the number of body regions involved: Stage 1 (symptom onset, 1 region), Stage 2A/2B (2 regions, diagnosis), Stage 3 (3 regions), Stage 4A (gastrostomy), Stage 4B (NIV), Stage 5 (death). This system correlates well with ALSFRS-R and provides a more intuitive disease trajectory framework.

## 10.13 Differential Diagnosis: EDX Mimics of ALS 

Several conditions can mimic ALS clinically and/or electrodiagnostically. Exclusion of these treatable conditions is essential before confirming an ALS diagnosis.

### Table 10.1: EDX Differential Diagnosis of ALS

| Condition | Key Differentiating EDX Feature | Key Clinical Feature |
|---|---|---|
| MMNCB | Motor conduction block; normal SNAPs | No UMN signs; nerve territory weakness pattern |
| Kennedy disease (SBMA) | **Abnormal SNAPs** (low-amplitude) | Gynecomastia; X-linked; very slow progression |
| Cervical spondylotic myelopathy | Abnormalities limited to cervical myotomes; UMN signs below lesion only | MRI shows cord compression |
| CIDP | Demyelinating NCS pattern (conduction block, slow velocity) | Sensory involvement; CSF protein elevated |
| Inclusion body myositis | Mixed myopathic + neurogenic EMG pattern | Finger flexor and quadriceps weakness; age >50 |
| Benign fasciculation syndrome | Normal EMG (no denervation, normal MUPs) | Isolated fasciculations without weakness or atrophy |
| Multifocal acquired motor axonopathy | Normal motor conduction (no block) | Anti-GM1 antibodies; does not respond to IVIg |
| Hirayama disease | Cervical myotomal denervation only; unilateral | Young males; oblique amyotrophy of hand/forearm |

> **Board Mnemonic -- "THICK CIMs" for ALS mimics to exclude:** **T**hyroid disease, **H**exosaminidase A deficiency (adult Tay-Sachs), **I**nclusion body myositis, **C**ervical myelopathy, **K**ennedy disease, **C**IDP, **I**nfectious (HIV, HTLV-1), **M**MNCB, **s**yringomyelia.

## 10.13 ALS Genetic Considerations 

Approximately 5-10% of ALS cases are familial (fALS), defined by a first- or second-degree relative with ALS. The remaining 90-95% are classified as sporadic (sALS), though up to 10% of "sporadic" cases harbor identifiable genetic mutations.

### Table 10.2: Major ALS Genes

| Gene | Chromosome | Protein | Frequency (fALS) | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C9orf72 | 9p21 | Hexanucleotide repeat expansion | 30-40% | Most common; associated with FTD; GGGGCC repeat |
| SOD1 | 21q22 | Superoxide dismutase 1 | 12-20% | First ALS gene identified (1993); target of tofersen |
| TARDBP | 1p36 | TDP-43 | 4-5% | TDP-43 is the major pathological protein in most ALS |
| FUS | 16p11 | Fused in sarcoma | 4-5% | Younger onset; aggressive course |
| NEK1 | 4q33 | NIMA-related kinase 1 | 2-3% | Risk factor; DNA damage response |

The C9orf72 hexanucleotide repeat expansion is the most common genetic cause of both ALS and frontotemporal dementia (FTD). The overlap between ALS and FTD is significant: approximately 15% of ALS patients develop FTD, and up to 50% show some degree of cognitive or behavioral impairment on formal testing.

## 10.14 Hirayama Disease (Monomelic Amyotrophy) 

Hirayama disease is a benign, self-limited form of motor neuron disease that predominantly affects young males (typically 15-25 years old) of Asian descent, although it is reported worldwide. It is characterized by unilateral or asymmetric distal upper extremity weakness and atrophy, particularly affecting the C7-T1 myotomes.

### Pathophysiology

The mechanism is thought to involve anterior horn cell ischemia caused by forward displacement of the posterior dural sac during neck flexion, leading to compression of the lower cervical spinal cord against the vertebral bodies. This "dynamic" compression preferentially damages the anterior horn cells at C7-T1 levels.

### Clinical Features

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Demographics | Young males, 15-25 years; Asian predominance |
| Weakness | Unilateral or asymmetric hand and forearm muscle wasting |
| Pattern | "Oblique amyotrophy" -- C7-T1 myotomes affected; C6-innervated muscles (brachioradialis) spared |
| "Cold paresis" | Worsening weakness in cold environments (characteristic) |
| Progression | Self-limited; stabilizes after 2-5 years of slow progression |
| UMN signs | Absent |

### EDX Findings

- Active and chronic denervation in C7-T1 myotomes (FDI, ADM, FDP)
- Relative sparing of brachioradialis (C5-C6) -- the "oblique amyotrophy" pattern
- Normal sensory NCS
- Contralateral subclinical EMG abnormalities in many cases
- No UMN findings (normal central conduction time on transcranial magnetic stimulation)

### Imaging

Flexion MRI of the cervical spine showing anterior displacement of the posterior dura and lower cervical cord flattening is the key diagnostic study. A cervical collar to limit neck flexion may prevent progression.

> **Clinical Pearl:** A young male with unilateral hand atrophy and normal sensory NCS should not automatically receive an ALS diagnosis. Hirayama disease is self-limited and has a benign prognosis. Flexion MRI of the cervical spine is the diagnostic study of choice.

## 10.15 Multidisciplinary Care and Rehabilitation 

ALS management requires a multidisciplinary team approach. Care at specialized ALS centers has been associated with improved survival and quality of life.

**Key team members and roles:**
- Neurologist/physiatrist: Diagnosis, disease monitoring, medication management
- Pulmonologist/respiratory therapist: NIV, airway clearance, ventilator management
- Speech-language pathologist: Dysphagia management, augmentative communication
- Occupational therapist: Adaptive equipment, home modifications
- Physical therapist: Mobility, fall prevention, gentle exercise programs
- Nutritionist: Weight maintenance, PEG tube timing
- Social worker/psychologist: Psychosocial support, advance care planning
- Palliative care: Symptom management throughout disease course

**Rehabilitation principles in ALS:**
- Avoid overwork weakness (exercise at submaximal levels)
- Range of motion to prevent contractures
- Assistive devices provided proactively (before complete loss of function)
- PEG tube placement when oral intake becomes inadequate and FVC is still >50% (to reduce procedural risk)
- Communication devices introduced while patient can still participate in training

## 10.15 ALS-Frontotemporal Dementia Spectrum 

### The ALS-FTD Continuum

The recognition that ALS and frontotemporal dementia (FTD) exist on a clinical and pathological continuum has been one of the most important advances in motor neuron disease understanding. Up to 50% of ALS patients demonstrate some degree of cognitive or behavioral impairment on formal testing, and approximately 15% meet full criteria for FTD.

TDP-43 proteinopathy is the unifying pathological feature: aggregates of the TAR DNA-binding protein 43 (TDP-43) are found in both the motor neurons of ALS patients and the frontotemporal cortical neurons of FTD patients. The C9orf72 hexanucleotide repeat expansion is the most common genetic cause of both diseases.

### Cognitive Testing in ALS

The Edinburgh Cognitive and Behavioural ALS Screen (ECAS) is designed specifically for ALS patients, accounting for motor limitations that may impair performance on standard cognitive tests. Identifying cognitive impairment in ALS has practical implications: patients with FTD may have reduced decision-making capacity for advance directives and may have more difficulty complying with NIV and other supportive interventions. ALS-FTD carries a worse prognosis than either condition alone.

## 10.16 Practical Approach: The EDX Examination for Suspected ALS 

A systematic approach to the EDX evaluation of suspected ALS ensures thorough documentation across all four body regions while efficiently excluding alternative diagnoses.

### Recommended NCS Protocol

| Test | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Median motor (wrist, elbow) | Assess for CTS; baseline CMAP amplitude |
| Ulnar motor (wrist, below/above elbow) | Exclude cubital tunnel; assess for conduction block (MMNCB) |
| Fibular motor (ankle, below/above fibular head) | Assess for fibular neuropathy; baseline CMAP |
| Tibial motor (ankle) | Additional motor nerve; baseline CMAP |
| Median sensory | Must be NORMAL for ALS diagnosis |
| Ulnar sensory | Must be NORMAL for ALS diagnosis |
| Sural sensory | Must be NORMAL for ALS diagnosis |

**Key NCS findings supporting ALS:**
- Normal sensory responses (all SNAPs within normal limits)
- Reduced CMAP amplitudes in affected muscles (reflecting motor neuron loss)
- Normal conduction velocities (no demyelination)
- No conduction block (excludes MMNCB)

### Recommended EMG Protocol: 4-Region Documentation

| Region | Muscles to Sample | Target Findings |
|---|---|---|
| Bulbar | Tongue (genioglossus) or mentalis | Fibrillation potentials + chronic neurogenic MUPs |
| Cervical | Deltoid, biceps, triceps, FDI, APB | Active + chronic denervation in multiple myotomes |
| Thoracic | Paraspinal (T6-T10) | Fibrillation potentials (valuable for adding a 3rd region) |
| Lumbosacral | Vastus lateralis, tibialis anterior, gastrocnemius, EDB | Active + chronic denervation in multiple myotomes |

### Documentation Checklist for ALS Diagnosis

The report should explicitly document:
- Both acute denervation (fibs/PSWs/fasciculations) AND chronic changes (large MUPs, reduced recruitment) in each positive muscle
- Multiple myotomes affected within each region (not just one root level)
- Normal sensory NCS explicitly stated
- No conduction block on motor NCS
- Number of body regions with confirmed LMN involvement
- Clinical correlation with UMN signs documented

> **Clinical Pearl:** Thoracic paraspinal EMG is an underutilized but highly valuable test in ALS evaluation. Denervation in thoracic paraspinals provides evidence of a third or fourth body region involvement that is rarely affected by other conditions (radiculopathy, entrapment neuropathy). It can upgrade diagnostic certainty with minimal additional patient discomfort.

## 10.17 Summary: Board-High-Yield Review Points

1. ALS diagnostic criteria have evolved from El Escorial (multi-tier, insensitive) to Awaji (fasciculations equivalent to fibs) to Gold Coast (dichotomous, 95% sensitivity).

2. Normal SNAPs are the critical NCS finding in ALS. Abnormal SNAPs should prompt consideration of Kennedy disease, MMNCB, or neuropathy.

3. EMG in ALS must show BOTH acute denervation (fibs, PSWs, fasciculations) AND chronic reinnervation (large MUPs, reduced recruitment) in multiple body regions.

4. Kennedy disease (SBMA): X-linked, androgen receptor CAG repeat, gynecomastia, ABNORMAL SNAPs, very slow progression. This is the most important mimic to distinguish from ALS.

5. SMA: autosomal recessive, SMN1 gene deletion, four types based on onset/severity. Treatment revolution with nusinersen, onasemnogene, and risdiplam.

6. ALS medications: riluzole (survival benefit), edaravone (slows decline in select patients), tofersen (SOD1-specific only). Relyvrio withdrawn April 2024.

7. Respiratory management: FVC <50% triggers NIV initiation. NIV extends survival 7-11 months. MIE for airway clearance when peak cough flow <270 L/min.

8. ALSFRS-R: 48-point scale, 12 items, 4 domains. Average decline ~1 point/month.

9. Post-polio syndrome: new weakness 15-40 years after acute polio. Giant MUAPs from extensive prior reinnervation. New fibrillation potentials indicate active denervation of decompensating motor units.

10. MMNCB: motor conduction block, normal SNAPs, treatable with IVIg. Must be excluded before diagnosing ALS.
