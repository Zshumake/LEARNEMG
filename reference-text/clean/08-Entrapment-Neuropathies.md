## 8.1 Carpal Tunnel Syndrome 

### 8.1.1 Anatomy of the Carpal Tunnel

The carpal tunnel is an osteofibrous canal at the wrist formed by the carpal bones (floor and walls) and the transverse carpal ligament (flexor retinaculum) as its roof. The tunnel contains ten structures: the median nerve and nine flexor tendons (four flexor digitorum superficialis tendons, four flexor digitorum profundus tendons, and the flexor pollicis longus tendon).

The median nerve is the most superficial structure in the tunnel, lying immediately beneath the transverse carpal ligament. It is therefore the most vulnerable to compression. At the distal edge of the carpal tunnel, the median nerve divides into its terminal branches: the recurrent motor branch (innervating the thenar muscles) and the digital sensory branches (innervating the palmar surface of digits 1 through 3 and the radial half of digit 4).

### 8.1.2 The LOAF Muscles 

The LOAF mnemonic identifies the four muscles innervated by the median nerve in the hand, distal to the carpal tunnel:

- **L** -- Lumbricals 1 and 2 (first and second lumbricals)
- **O** -- Opponens pollicis
- **A** -- Abductor pollicis brevis (APB)
- **F** -- Flexor pollicis brevis (superficial head)

The APB is the most commonly examined muscle for CTS on needle EMG because it is exclusively innervated by the recurrent motor branch of the median nerve and is technically easy to examine. The standard median motor study records from the APB.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The LOAF muscles are innervated by the recurrent branch of the median nerve, which arises at or just distal to the carpal tunnel. In severe CTS with thenar atrophy, all four LOAF muscles may be affected. The "ape hand" deformity results from loss of thumb opposition and abduction.

### 8.1.3 Electrodiagnostic Evaluation of CTS 

**Standard NCS Protocol:**

The AANEM recommends a systematic approach to CTS electrodiagnosis:

1. Median sensory NCS (recording digit 2 or 3)
2. Median motor NCS (recording APB)
3. At least one comparison study (see below)
4. Ulnar and/or radial NCS for comparison (to exclude polyneuropathy)
5. Needle EMG of APB and selected C5-T1 muscles (to exclude radiculopathy, plexopathy)

**Absolute Median Nerve Values:**

- **Median sensory distal latency:** Abnormal if greater than 3.5 milliseconds (recording digit 2, 14 cm distance) or greater than 3.7 ms (recording digit 3)
- **Median motor distal latency:** Abnormal if greater than 4.2 milliseconds (recording APB, 8 cm distance)
- **Median sensory conduction velocity across the wrist:** Abnormal if less than 50 m/s

These absolute values have a sensitivity of approximately 75% for CTS. Comparative studies increase sensitivity to 85 to 95%.

### 8.1.4 Comparison (Differential Latency) Studies 

Comparison studies are the most sensitive electrodiagnostic tests for CTS. They compare the median nerve conduction across the wrist segment with the conduction of an adjacent nerve (ulnar or radial) traversing the same anatomical region. Because both nerves are subject to the same temperature and tissue conditions, the comparison controls for systemic factors and isolates focal median nerve slowing.

**Key comparison studies (in order of sensitivity):**

| Study | Technique | Abnormal Value | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Median-ulnar palmar mixed comparison (palmdiff) | Stimulate palm, record median and ulnar at wrist | >0.3-0.4 ms difference | Highest (~95%) |
| Median-ulnar ring finger sensory comparison (ringdiff) | Record ring finger (digit 4), stimulate median and ulnar at wrist | >0.4-0.5 ms difference | Very high (~90%) |
| Median-radial thumb sensory comparison (thumbdiff) | Record thumb (digit 1), stimulate median and radial at wrist | >0.4-0.5 ms difference | High (~85%) |
| Second lumbrical-interossei motor comparison (2L-INT) | Record 2nd lumbrical (median) and interosseous (ulnar) | >0.4 ms difference | Very high; useful in severe CTS |

**The second lumbrical-interossei comparison** deserves special mention because it is the most useful study in **severe CTS** when median sensory responses are absent. Because both the second lumbrical (median nerve) and the interossei (ulnar nerve) are recorded from the hand, the motor latency comparison remains valid even when sensory studies are unobtainable.

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** When the median SNAP is absent in severe CTS, the second lumbrical-interossei motor comparison is the most reliable study for confirming the diagnosis. A latency difference of greater than 0.4 ms between the lumbrical and interossei motor responses supports CTS.

### 8.1.5 CTS Severity Grading: The CSI/Robinson Classification 

The Combined Sensory Index (CSI), also known as the Robinson classification, stratifies CTS severity based on the sum of three comparison study latency differences:

**CSI = (median-ulnar palmdiff) + (median-ulnar ringdiff) + (median-radial thumbdiff)**

| Severity | CSI Value | Additional Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | CSI >= 0.9 ms | Sensory latency prolonged; motor latency normal |
| Moderate | Variable | Both sensory and motor latencies prolonged; SNAP still present |
| Severe | Variable | Absent median SNAP; prolonged DML; CMAP may be low |

### 8.1.6 The Bland Severity Scale 

The Bland classification is a seven-grade scale (0-6) that provides a more granular severity assessment than most other systems:

| Grade | Severity | Electrodiagnostic Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Normal | No neurophysiological abnormality |
| 1 | Very mild | Abnormality detected only by most sensitive tests (comparison studies, inching) |
| 2 | Mild | Sensory conduction velocity (SCV) slowed (<40 m/s) with normal distal motor latency (DML <4.5 ms) |
| 3 | Moderate | DML prolonged (4.5-6.5 ms) with SCV <40 m/s |
| 4 | Severe | DML prolonged (4.5-6.5 ms) with absent SNAP |
| 5 | Very severe | DML >6.5 ms |
| 6 | Extremely severe | CMAP amplitude <0.2 mV (surface recording from APB) |

The Bland scale is notable for incorporating CMAP amplitude (Grade 6), which most other classification systems neglect. A CMAP less than 0.2 mV indicates near-complete loss of motor axons to the thenar muscles, carrying a poor prognosis for recovery after carpal tunnel release.

### 8.1.7 Needle EMG in CTS 

Needle EMG of the APB is indicated in moderate to severe CTS to assess the degree of motor axon loss:

- **Fibrillation potentials and PSWs in APB:** Indicate active denervation; present in severe CTS
- **Chronic neurogenic MUAP changes in APB:** Long-duration, high-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs indicate chronic denervation with reinnervation
- **Reduced recruitment in APB:** Indicates significant motor axon loss

The needle examination also serves to **exclude** other diagnoses. Examining muscles innervated by C6-T1 roots through different peripheral nerves helps exclude cervical radiculopathy, brachial plexopathy, or proximal median neuropathy (pronator syndrome, AIN syndrome).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Temperature is critical in CTS evaluation. Cool hand temperature slows nerve conduction and may produce falsely prolonged median nerve latencies. The AANEM requires that hand temperature be measured and maintained at or above 32 degrees Celsius during testing. If the hand is cool, it should be warmed before testing.

## 8.2 Pronator Syndrome 

### 8.2.1 Anatomy and Mechanism

Pronator syndrome is a proximal median nerve entrapment in the forearm, occurring at one of several potential compression sites:

1. **Ligament of Struthers:** A fibrous band extending from the supracondylar process of the humerus to the medial epicondyle (present in approximately 1% of the population)
2. **Lacertus fibrosus (bicipital aponeurosis):** The fascial expansion of the biceps tendon crossing over the median nerve
3. **Between the two heads of the pronator teres:** The most common site
4. **Fibrous arch of the FDS:** The proximal edge of the flexor digitorum superficialis

### 8.2.2 Clinical Features

- Aching pain in the proximal forearm, often worse with repetitive pronation
- Numbness in the median nerve sensory distribution (digits 1-3 and radial digit 4)
- Weakness may involve both thenar muscles AND forearm median-innervated muscles (FDS, pronator teres, FCR) -- a feature that distinguishes pronator syndrome from CTS
- **Tinel sign at the pronator teres** (not at the wrist)
- A positive pronator compression test (reproduction of symptoms with resisted pronation)

### 8.2.3 Electrodiagnostic Findings

Pronator syndrome is notoriously difficult to confirm electrodiagnostically:

- NCS may be **entirely normal** because the compression is proximal and the standard wrist-to-digit distance may not capture the slowing
- The median nerve conduction velocity may be slowed in the **forearm segment** (elbow to wrist), but this finding is inconsistent
- **Needle EMG** may show denervation in forearm median-innervated muscles (pronator teres, FCR, FDS, FPL) -- muscles that should be normal in CTS
- The primary EDX utility is in **excluding CTS** and **excluding AIN syndrome** as the cause of symptoms

### 8.2.4 Distinguishing Pronator Syndrome from CTS 

| Feature | CTS | Pronator Syndrome |
|---|---|---|
| Sensory loss | Digits 1-3, radial digit 4 | Same, plus thenar eminence (palmar cutaneous branch involved) |
| Forearm muscle weakness | Absent | Present (FDS, PT, FCR) |
| Night symptoms | Common | Uncommon |
| Median NCS at wrist | Abnormal | Normal |
| Median forearm CV | Normal | May be slowed |
| Tinel sign location | Wrist | Proximal forearm |

## 8.3 Anterior Interosseous Nerve Syndrome 

### 8.3.1 Anatomy

The anterior interosseous nerve (AIN) is a purely motor branch of the median nerve, arising approximately 5 to 8 cm distal to the lateral epicondyle. It innervates three muscles:

- **Flexor pollicis longus (FPL):** Flexion of the thumb IP joint
- **Flexor digitorum profundus to digits 2 and 3 (FDP 2,3):** Flexion of the DIP joints of the index and middle fingers
- **Pronator quadratus (PQ):** Pronation of the forearm

### 8.3.2 Clinical Features

AIN syndrome presents as a **pure motor deficit** with no sensory loss (because the AIN has no cutaneous sensory fibers):

- Inability to form a circle or "OK" sign (weakness of FPL and FDP to index)
- Weakness of thumb IP flexion (FPL)
- Weakness of index and middle finger DIP flexion (FDP 2,3)
- Weakness of forearm pronation (pronator quadratus -- may be subtle because the pronator teres, innervated by the main median nerve, compensates)
- **No sensory deficits** -- this is the defining feature that distinguishes AIN syndrome from other median neuropathies

### 8.3.3 EDX Findings 

- **All standard sensory NCS are normal** (AIN is purely motor)
- **Median motor DML recording APB is normal** (APB is innervated by the recurrent branch, not the AIN)
- **Needle EMG:** Fibrillation potentials and reduced recruitment in FPL, FDP to digits 2-3, and pronator quadratus. These three muscles and only these three muscles should be affected
- **Side-to-side comparison** of motor latency to the pronator quadratus may show prolongation on the affected side

> **Clinical Pearl:** AIN syndrome is a purely motor neuropathy. Any patient with median nerve territory weakness AND sensory loss does not have isolated AIN syndrome -- consider pronator syndrome, brachial plexopathy, or Parsonage-Turner syndrome (which has a predilection for the AIN).

## 8.4 Ulnar Neuropathy at the Elbow (UNE) 

### 8.4.1 Anatomy of the Cubital Tunnel

The ulnar nerve passes through several anatomical zones at the elbow, each of which can serve as a site of compression:

1. **Retroepicondylar groove:** The bony channel between the medial epicondyle and the olecranon. The nerve lies superficially here and is vulnerable to direct external compression ("funny bone" area)
2. **Cubital tunnel:** A fibro-osseous channel formed by the medial epicondyle, olecranon, and the cubital tunnel retinaculum (Osborne ligament). This is the most common site of compression
3. **Humeral-ulnar aponeurosis (arcuate ligament):** A fascial band between the two heads of the flexor carpi ulnaris, approximately 1 to 2 cm distal to the medial epicondyle

The ulnar nerve is subject to both compressive and traction forces at the elbow. During elbow flexion, the cubital tunnel narrows by up to 55%, increasing intraneural pressure. The nerve also elongates and slides anteriorly, subjecting it to traction. These dynamic factors explain why symptoms are often worse with prolonged elbow flexion (e.g., sleeping with the elbow bent, holding a phone).

### 8.4.2 Electrodiagnostic Criteria 

The AANEM practice parameter defines the following electrodiagnostic criteria for UNE:

**Motor NCS:**

- **Across-elbow motor conduction velocity (MCV) less than 50 m/s** (recording ADM or FDI): The most commonly applied criterion
- **Conduction velocity decrease of greater than 10 m/s** across the elbow compared to the forearm segment: A commonly used alternative criterion. Using a threshold of greater than 13 m/s slowing across the elbow compared to the forearm detected 84% of UNE cases in the original validation studies (Hawley and Capobianco)
- **CMAP amplitude drop greater than 20%** between above-elbow and below-elbow stimulation: Indicates focal demyelination (conduction block) or axonal loss at the elbow
- **Conduction block:** A CMAP amplitude drop of greater than 50% with less than 15% change in duration (no temporal dispersion) is diagnostic of focal demyelination

**Technical considerations:**

- The elbow must be positioned at **70 to 90 degrees of flexion** during testing. An extended elbow underestimates the conduction velocity because the nerve follows a longer course around the extended epicondyle than is measured by the surface distance
- Distances less than 10 cm across the elbow may introduce significant measurement error
- Stimulation above and below the elbow should use standardized distances (typically 10-12 cm segment)

### 8.4.3 Sensory NCS in UNE 

- **Ulnar SNAP (recording digit 5):** May be reduced or absent in axonal UNE
- **Dorsal ulnar cutaneous (DUC) SNAP:** Critically important for localization. The DUC branches from the ulnar nerve approximately 5 to 8 cm proximal to the ulnar styloid. In UNE at the elbow, the DUC SNAP is abnormal. In ulnar neuropathy at Guyon canal (wrist), the DUC SNAP is **normal** because the nerve branches before the canal

### 8.4.4 Inching Technique 

The inching technique involves short-segment incremental stimulation at **2 cm intervals** across the elbow to precisely localize the site of entrapment. The motor response (recording FDI or ADM) is obtained at each stimulation point, and the latency change between consecutive points is measured.

- Normal: Latency increase of approximately 0.4 ms per 2 cm segment (conduction velocity approximately 50 m/s)
- Abnormal: A latency increase of greater than 0.5 ms across a 2 cm segment indicates focal slowing at that site (some laboratories use a more conservative threshold of 0.7 to 0.8 ms)
- The inching technique is particularly useful when standard across-elbow studies are equivocal or when precise surgical localization is required

### 8.4.5 Needle EMG in UNE 

Needle EMG helps assess severity and provides additional localizing information:

- **FDI (first dorsal interosseous):** The most sensitive muscle for UNE detection. It may show denervation before the ADM
- **ADM (abductor digiti minimi):** Standard ulnar-innervated hypothenar muscle
- **FCU (flexor carpi ulnaris):** Innervated by the ulnar nerve in the forearm. If denervated, the lesion is at or above the elbow. If normal, the lesion may be more distal
- **FDP to digits 4-5:** Also innervated by the ulnar nerve in the proximal forearm

Important: In axonal UNE, NCS may fail to precisely localize the lesion because the amplitude may be diffusely reduced without focal slowing. Needle EMG becomes the primary tool for confirming ulnar motor axon loss in this setting.

## 8.5 Ulnar Neuropathy at Guyon Canal 

### 8.5.1 Anatomy of Guyon Canal

Guyon canal (the ulnar tunnel) is a fibro-osseous channel at the wrist formed by the pisiform bone and the hook of the hamate, spanned by the volar carpal ligament. Within the canal, the ulnar nerve divides into a superficial (sensory) branch and a deep (motor) branch.

### 8.5.2 Shea Classification 

The Shea classification categorizes ulnar neuropathy at Guyon canal into three zones based on the level of injury and the clinical pattern:

| Zone | Location | Motor | Sensory | Key EDX Finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 1 | Proximal Guyon canal (before bifurcation) | All ulnar hand muscles weak | Digits 4-5 numbness | Low ulnar CMAP + low ulnar SNAP (digit 5) |
| Zone 2 | Deep branch (motor only) | All ulnar hand muscles weak | **Normal sensation** | Low ulnar CMAP, normal ulnar SNAP (digit 5) |
| Zone 3 | Superficial branch (sensory only) | **Normal motor** | Digits 4-5 numbness | Normal ulnar CMAP, low ulnar SNAP (digit 5) |

### 8.5.3 Key Differentiator: DUC SNAP 

The dorsal ulnar cutaneous (DUC) nerve branches from the main ulnar nerve trunk in the distal forearm, approximately 5 to 8 cm proximal to the ulnar styloid -- well proximal to Guyon canal. Therefore:

- **UNE at the elbow:** DUC SNAP is **abnormal** (the lesion is proximal to the DUC branching point)
- **Ulnar neuropathy at Guyon canal:** DUC SNAP is **normal** (the lesion is distal to the DUC branching point)

This simple distinction is one of the most commonly tested electrodiagnostic concepts on board examinations.

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** A normal DUC SNAP with ulnar motor and/or sensory abnormalities at the wrist localizes the lesion to Guyon canal, not the elbow. The DUC branches proximal to Guyon canal, so it is spared in wrist-level lesions. Common causes of Guyon canal compression include ganglion cysts, handlebar palsy (cycling), and hook of hamate fractures.

## 8.6 Radial Nerve Entrapment 

### 8.6.1 Saturday Night Palsy (Spiral Groove Compression) 

**Anatomy and Mechanism:**

The radial nerve courses around the posterior humerus in the spiral (radial) groove, where it lies directly against the bone with minimal soft tissue protection. It is vulnerable to compression against the humerus by sustained external pressure -- classically from draping the arm over a chair back while intoxicated ("Saturday night palsy") or from positioning during anesthesia.

**Clinical Features:**

- **Wrist drop** (weakness of wrist extension: ECRL, ECRB, ECU)
- **Finger drop** (weakness of finger extension at the MCP joints: EDC, EIP, EDM)
- Weakness of forearm supination (supinator)
- **Preserved triceps function:** The radial nerve branches to the triceps arise proximal to the spiral groove in most individuals, so a spiral groove lesion spares the triceps
- **Preserved brachioradialis function is variable** depending on the exact lesion site
- **Sensory loss:** Over the dorsal hand (radial sensory nerve territory) and the posterior forearm (posterior antebrachial cutaneous)

**EDX Findings:**

- **Radial SNAP:** May be reduced or absent (though the superficial branch emerges distally, the lesion at the spiral groove affects the main trunk)
- **Radial motor NCS:** Recording from the extensor indicis proprius (EIP). Conduction block or slowing may be demonstrated by stimulating above and below the spiral groove. CMAP amplitude may be reduced distally in axonal lesions
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in radial-innervated muscles below the spiral groove (brachioradialis -- variable, supinator, wrist extensors, finger extensors). Triceps is spared. Paraspinals normal.

### 8.6.2 Posterior Interosseous Nerve (PIN) Syndrome 

**Anatomy:**

The posterior interosseous nerve is the deep motor branch of the radial nerve. After the radial nerve bifurcates in the region of the lateral epicondyle, the PIN dives through the supinator muscle, passing beneath the **arcade of Frohse** (the fibrous proximal edge of the supinator). This arcade is the most common site of PIN compression.

**Clinical Features:**

- Weakness of finger extension at the MCP joints (EDC, EIP, EDM)
- Weakness of wrist extension with **radial deviation** during extension (the ECRL is innervated by the radial nerve proper, proximal to the PIN takeoff, so it is spared; the ECU, which ulnar deviates the wrist, is affected because it is PIN-innervated)
- **No sensory loss** -- the PIN is a pure motor nerve. The superficial branch of the radial nerve (purely sensory) is unaffected
- No wrist drop per se -- the ECRL preserves some wrist extension, but the wrist deviates radially

**EDX Findings:**

- **Radial sensory SNAP is normal** (superficial branch is unaffected)
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in PIN-innervated muscles: supinator, EDC, ECU, APL, EPL, EPB, EIP. Sparing of ECRL, brachioradialis, and triceps (innervated proximal to the PIN)
- Motor NCS recording from EIP may show prolonged latency or low amplitude when stimulating at the elbow

### 8.6.3 Wartenberg Syndrome (Superficial Radial Sensory Neuropathy) 

**Anatomy and Mechanism:**

Wartenberg syndrome is an entrapment of the superficial branch of the radial nerve as it emerges between the brachioradialis and extensor carpi radialis longus tendons in the distal forearm. Compression may occur from watchbands, handcuffs, tight casts, or repetitive forearm pronation-supination.

**Clinical Features:**

- Pain and numbness over the dorsal radial hand and the dorsum of the first web space
- **No motor deficit** -- the superficial branch is purely sensory
- Positive Finkelstein test (may mimic de Quervain tendinopathy)
- Tinel sign over the distal radial forearm

**EDX Findings:**

- **Radial sensory SNAP reduced or absent** on the affected side; side-to-side comparison is essential (a 50% reduction compared to the contralateral side is considered significant)
- **All motor studies normal** -- no motor involvement
- **Needle EMG normal** -- no denervation

## 8.7 Musculocutaneous Neuropathy 

### 8.7.1 Anatomy and Clinical Features

The musculocutaneous nerve arises from the lateral cord (C5, C6) and innervates the coracobrachialis, biceps brachii, and brachialis. Its terminal sensory branch, the lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve, provides sensation to the lateral forearm.

Isolated musculocutaneous neuropathy is uncommon. It may result from shoulder surgery, strenuous exercise, direct trauma, or compression. Clinical features include weakness of elbow flexion (biceps, brachialis) and forearm supination (biceps), with sensory loss over the lateral forearm.

### 8.7.2 EDX Findings

- **Lateral antebrachial cutaneous SNAP:** Reduced or absent (comparing side-to-side)
- **Musculocutaneous motor study:** Recording from the biceps with stimulation at Erb point and the axilla. CMAP amplitude may be reduced
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in biceps, brachialis, and coracobrachialis. Sparing of other C5-C6 muscles (deltoid, infraspinatus) helps distinguish from upper trunk plexopathy or C5-C6 radiculopathy

## 8.8 Axillary Neuropathy 

### 8.8.1 Anatomy and Clinical Features

The axillary nerve arises from the posterior cord (C5, C6) and passes through the quadrilateral space (bordered by the teres minor superiorly, teres major inferiorly, long head of triceps medially, and surgical neck of humerus laterally). It innervates the deltoid and teres minor, and provides sensation to the lateral shoulder ("regimental badge" area).

Common causes include anterior shoulder dislocation (the most common), humeral neck fracture, and quadrilateral space syndrome.

### 8.8.2 EDX Findings

- **Axillary motor NCS:** Recording from the deltoid with stimulation at Erb point. Low CMAP amplitude indicates axonal loss
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in deltoid and teres minor. Sparing of other posterior cord muscles (latissimus dorsi, triceps) helps confirm an isolated axillary nerve lesion
- **Normal paraspinals and infraspinatus:** Distinguishes from C5-C6 radiculopathy and upper trunk plexopathy

## 8.9 Suprascapular Neuropathy 

### 8.9.1 Anatomy

The suprascapular nerve arises from the upper trunk (C5, C6) and passes through the suprascapular notch beneath the transverse scapular ligament to innervate the supraspinatus. It then continues through the spinoglenoid notch to innervate the infraspinatus.

Two patterns of entrapment occur:

- **Suprascapular notch entrapment:** Both supraspinatus and infraspinatus are affected
- **Spinoglenoid notch entrapment:** Only infraspinatus is affected (supraspinatus is spared because it is innervated before the nerve reaches the spinoglenoid notch)

### 8.9.2 Clinical Features and EDX Findings

- Shoulder pain (often deep and posterior) with weakness of external rotation (infraspinatus) and/or shoulder abduction initiation (supraspinatus)
- **No sensory deficits** (the suprascapular nerve is predominantly motor; its small articular branch provides no cutaneous innervation)
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in supraspinatus and/or infraspinatus depending on entrapment site
- NCS: Comparison of CMAP amplitude recording from the infraspinatus bilaterally; side-to-side difference greater than 50% is abnormal

Common causes include ganglion cysts (especially at the spinoglenoid notch), repetitive overhead activity (volleyball players), and scapular fractures.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Isolated infraspinatus denervation with a normal supraspinatus localizes the lesion to the spinoglenoid notch. Denervation of both muscles localizes it to the suprascapular notch.

## 8.10 Long Thoracic Neuropathy 

### 8.10.1 Anatomy and Clinical Features

The long thoracic nerve arises from the C5, C6, and C7 roots and descends along the chest wall to innervate the serratus anterior. It is one of the longest motor nerves in the body and is vulnerable to injury from trauma, surgical positioning, carrying heavy loads on the shoulder, and inflammatory processes (Parsonage-Turner syndrome).

Clinical features include **scapular winging** -- the medial border of the scapula protrudes when the patient pushes against a wall with outstretched arms.

### 8.10.2 EDX Findings

- **Needle EMG of serratus anterior:** Fibrillation potentials and/or chronic neurogenic changes in the serratus anterior. The muscle is examined at the anterior axillary line at the level of the fifth to seventh ribs
- NCS of the long thoracic nerve are technically possible but rarely performed in clinical practice
- Isolated serratus anterior denervation with normal rhomboids and trapezius confirms a long thoracic nerve lesion

## 8.11 Fibular (Peroneal) Neuropathy 

### 8.11.1 Anatomy and Sites of Compression

The common fibular (peroneal) nerve is the lateral branch of the sciatic nerve. After separating from the tibial nerve in the distal thigh or popliteal fossa, it winds around the **fibular head and neck**, where it is extremely superficial and vulnerable to compression. At the fibular neck, it divides into:

- **Deep fibular (peroneal) nerve:** Innervates the tibialis anterior, extensor hallucis longus, extensor digitorum longus, and extensor digitorum brevis (EDB). Provides sensory innervation to the first web space
- **Superficial fibular (peroneal) nerve:** Innervates the peroneus longus and peroneus brevis. Provides sensory innervation to the lateral leg and dorsum of the foot

### 8.11.2 Clinical Features

Common fibular neuropathy at the fibular head is the most common cause of **foot drop** and one of the most common entrapment neuropathies of the lower extremity. Causes include:

- Leg crossing (habitual compression against the opposite knee)
- Prolonged bed rest, coma, or anesthesia (pressure against the bed or table)
- Weight loss with loss of protective subcutaneous fat
- Knee surgery (arthroscopy, total knee arthroplasty)
- Casts, splints, or tight dressings
- Fibular head fractures

### 8.11.3 EDX Evaluation 

**NCS Protocol:**

- **Deep fibular motor study (recording EDB):** Stimulate at the ankle and below the fibular head. May show conduction block (CMAP amplitude drop >50%) or slowing across the fibular head in demyelinating lesions, or diffusely low CMAP amplitude in axonal lesions
- **Superficial fibular sensory study:** May be reduced or absent if the superficial fibular nerve is involved at or proximal to the fibular head
- **Tibial motor study (recording AH):** Should be **normal** -- this is essential to exclude sciatic neuropathy or L5-S1 radiculopathy
- **Sural sensory study:** Should be **normal** -- helps exclude peripheral polyneuropathy and sciatic neuropathy

**Needle EMG:**

- Denervation in fibular-innervated muscles: tibialis anterior, peroneus longus, extensor hallucis longus, EDB
- **Normal tibialis posterior** (tibial nerve, L5) -- essential to exclude L5 radiculopathy
- **Normal gluteus medius** (superior gluteal nerve, L5) -- excludes L5 radiculopathy or sciatic neuropathy
- **Short head of biceps femoris** -- this is the key discriminating muscle (see Section 8.11.4)

### 8.11.4 The Foot Drop Differential and the Short Head of Biceps Femoris 

The differential diagnosis of foot drop is one of the most commonly tested topics in electrodiagnostic medicine. Three conditions produce ankle dorsiflexion weakness: common fibular neuropathy at the fibular head, sciatic neuropathy, and L5 radiculopathy. The short head of the biceps femoris (SHBF) is the key muscle for distinguishing these conditions.

**Why the SHBF is so important:**

The short head of the biceps femoris is the only muscle in the thigh innervated by the **fibular division of the sciatic nerve** (L5, S1). All other hamstring muscles are innervated by the tibial division. Therefore:

- In **common fibular neuropathy at the fibular head:** SHBF is **normal** (the fibular nerve is injured distal to the branching point for the SHBF, which occurs in the proximal thigh)
- In **sciatic neuropathy with fibular predominance:** SHBF is **abnormal** (the fibular division is injured proximal to the SHBF branching point)
- In **L5 radiculopathy:** SHBF may be abnormal (L5 root innervation)

**Complete Foot Drop Differential Algorithm:**

| Feature | Fibular Neuropathy (fibular head) | Sciatic Neuropathy | L5 Radiculopathy |
|---|---|---|---|
| SNAP (superficial fibular) | Abnormal | Abnormal | **Normal** (DRG principle) |
| Tibialis posterior | Normal | Abnormal | Abnormal |
| Gluteus medius / TFL | Normal | Variable | Abnormal |
| Short head biceps femoris | **Normal** | **Abnormal** | Variable (may be abnormal) |
| Medial gastrocnemius | Normal | Abnormal (tibial division) | Normal (S1, not L5) |
| Paraspinals | Normal | Normal | Abnormal |
| Conduction block at fibular head | May be present | Absent | Absent |

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** The short head of the biceps femoris is the single most important muscle for the foot drop differential. Normal SHBF = fibular neuropathy at the knee. Abnormal SHBF = sciatic neuropathy (or possibly radiculopathy). Always examine the SHBF in any patient with foot drop.

## 8.12 Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome 

### 8.12.1 Anatomy

The tarsal tunnel is a fibro-osseous channel on the medial side of the ankle, formed by the medial malleolus, the calcaneus, and the flexor retinaculum (laciniate ligament). The posterior tibial nerve passes through this tunnel and divides into its terminal branches:

- **Medial plantar nerve:** Motor to abductor hallucis, FDB, FHB, first lumbrical; sensory to medial three and a half toes
- **Lateral plantar nerve:** Motor to remaining intrinsic foot muscles; sensory to lateral one and a half toes
- **Medial calcaneal branch:** Sensory to the heel (may branch before entering the tunnel)

### 8.12.2 Clinical Features

- Burning pain, numbness, and tingling in the sole of the foot
- Symptoms often worse at night or with prolonged standing
- Tinel sign at the tarsal tunnel
- Sensory loss over the plantar surface (may spare the heel if the medial calcaneal branch exits before the tunnel)

### 8.12.3 EDX Findings 

- **Medial and lateral plantar nerve studies:** Motor and/or sensory responses from the medial and lateral plantar nerves. Prolonged distal latencies, reduced amplitudes, or absent responses support the diagnosis
- **Mixed nerve study:** Stimulation of the medial or lateral plantar nerves at the toe with recording at the tibial nerve at the ankle
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in abductor hallucis (medial plantar) and/or abductor digiti quinti pedis (lateral plantar)
- **Normal sural SNAP:** Helps exclude peripheral polyneuropathy
- **Normal tibial motor study (recording AH from above ankle):** The tibial nerve itself is normal proximal to the tarsal tunnel

Important caveat: Tarsal tunnel syndrome is one of the most diagnostically challenging entrapment neuropathies because plantar nerve studies have significant technical limitations. Responses may be absent in normal individuals, particularly in older patients, making interpretation difficult. Clinical correlation is essential.

## 8.13 Sciatic Neuropathy and Piriformis Syndrome 

### 8.13.1 Sciatic Nerve Anatomy

The sciatic nerve (L4-S3) is the largest nerve in the body. It exits the pelvis through the greater sciatic foramen, typically passing inferior to the piriformis muscle. The nerve consists of two distinct divisions -- the tibial division (medially) and the fibular (peroneal) division (laterally) -- that are enveloped in a common connective tissue sheath throughout the thigh. They separate into the tibial and common fibular nerves in the distal thigh or popliteal fossa.

The fibular division is more vulnerable to injury than the tibial division because:

- It contains fewer and larger fascicles with less supportive connective tissue
- It is more laterally positioned and tethered at the fibular head
- It has less redundant blood supply

This selective vulnerability explains why sciatic neuropathy often presents with a **fibular-predominant pattern** that can mimic isolated fibular neuropathy at the knee.

### 8.13.2 Common Causes

- **Total hip arthroplasty** (most common iatrogenic cause)
- **Hip fracture or posterior hip dislocation**
- **Gluteal injection injury** (intramuscular injection in the buttock)
- **Prolonged bed rest or coma** (compression in the buttock)
- **Mass lesions** (tumors, hematomas, endometriosis)
- **Piriformis syndrome** (controversial; see below)

### 8.13.3 EDX Findings in Sciatic Neuropathy 

- **Fibular motor and sensory studies:** CMAP (recording EDB) reduced or absent; superficial fibular SNAP reduced or absent
- **Tibial motor study:** CMAP (recording AH) may be reduced (tibial division involvement)
- **Sural SNAP:** May be reduced (tibial division sensory involvement)
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in both fibular AND tibial nerve-innervated muscles below the knee
- **Short head of biceps femoris:** ABNORMAL -- the key finding distinguishing sciatic neuropathy from fibular neuropathy at the knee
- **Other hamstring muscles** (long head of biceps, semitendinosus, semimembranosus): May show denervation
- **Gluteal muscles:** Normal (they are innervated by the superior and inferior gluteal nerves, not the sciatic nerve)
- **Paraspinals:** Normal

### 8.13.4 Piriformis Syndrome 

Piriformis syndrome is a controversial clinical entity in which the sciatic nerve is purportedly compressed or irritated by the piriformis muscle as the nerve passes through or beneath the muscle in the buttock.

**Arguments for its existence:**

- Anatomical variants exist in which the fibular division passes through the piriformis muscle (present in approximately 10 to 15% of the population)
- Some patients improve with piriformis injection or surgical release

**Arguments against (or for extreme rarity):**

- No validated electrodiagnostic criteria exist
- Imaging findings are often nonspecific
- Clinical symptoms overlap extensively with other conditions (lumbar radiculopathy, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, hip pathology)
- Most EDX studies in clinically suspected piriformis syndrome are normal

**EDX approach in suspected piriformis syndrome:**

- The primary role of EDX is to **exclude** other diagnoses (L5-S1 radiculopathy, fibular neuropathy, true sciatic neuropathy from other causes)
- H-reflex prolongation on the affected side (prolonged FAIR -- flexion, adduction, internal rotation -- test) has been proposed but is not widely validated
- Needle EMG of the piriformis itself has been described but is technically demanding and of uncertain diagnostic utility

> **Clinical Pearl:** Piriformis syndrome remains a diagnosis of exclusion. The electrodiagnostic study is most useful for ruling out radiculopathy, fibular neuropathy, and other causes of sciatic-distribution symptoms. If the electrodiagnostic study is entirely normal, the diagnosis cannot be confirmed or excluded by EDX alone.

## 8.14 General Principles: Demyelinating vs. Axonal Entrapment Patterns 

Understanding the distinction between demyelinating and axonal patterns in entrapment neuropathies is fundamental to electrodiagnostic interpretation:

| Feature | Focal Demyelination | Axonal Loss |
|---|---|---|
| Conduction velocity across entrapment site | Significantly slowed | Normal or mildly slow |
| Distal latency | Prolonged (if entrapment is distal) | Normal |
| CMAP amplitude (distal stimulation) | Preserved (unless conduction block) | Reduced |
| Conduction block | May be present | Absent |
| Temporal dispersion | May be present | Absent |
| SNAP amplitude | May be preserved | Reduced |
| Needle EMG (acute) | May be normal | Fibrillations, PSWs |
| Needle EMG (chronic) | Normal MUAPs | Long-duration, high-amplitude MUAPs |
| Prognosis | Generally good (weeks to months) | Variable (months; depends on severity and distance) |

Most entrapment neuropathies produce a **mixed** pattern with both demyelinating and axonal components. Pure demyelinating entrapments (neurapraxia) carry the best prognosis, as remyelination can occur rapidly. Pure axonal lesions require axonal regrowth at the rate of approximately 1 mm/day (1 inch/month), and the prognosis depends on the distance from the lesion to the target muscle.

## Review Questions 

1. A 45-year-old woman presents with bilateral hand numbness and is referred for EDX evaluation of suspected carpal tunnel syndrome. Median sensory NCS shows a distal latency of 4.0 ms with normal amplitude. The median motor distal latency is 4.0 ms. The median-ulnar ring finger comparison shows a latency difference of 0.8 ms. What is the severity classification and what study would you perform if the median SNAP were absent?

   **Answer:** The prolonged median sensory latency with normal motor latency and abnormal ring finger comparison is consistent with **mild CTS** by most classification systems (Bland Grade 2: SCV slowed with normal DML). If the median SNAP were absent (severe CTS), the **second lumbrical-interossei motor comparison** would be the most useful study, as it remains valid even when sensory responses are unobtainable.

2. A patient has numbness in the ring and small fingers and weakness of the interossei. Ulnar motor conduction velocity across the elbow is 42 m/s. The dorsal ulnar cutaneous SNAP is normal. Where is the lesion?

   **Answer:** The ulnar motor slowing (42 m/s, below the 50 m/s threshold) is consistent with ulnar neuropathy at the elbow. However, the **normal DUC SNAP** creates a localization paradox -- in UNE, the DUC should be abnormal because the lesion is proximal to the DUC branching point. A normal DUC SNAP with ulnar motor and digit 5 sensory abnormalities is more consistent with **ulnar neuropathy at Guyon canal** (wrist). The across-elbow slowing may represent a technical artifact or incidental finding. Clinical correlation and additional studies (inching, repeat testing) are warranted.

3. A patient presents with foot drop after a prolonged surgical procedure. Needle EMG shows fibrillation potentials in the tibialis anterior, peroneus longus, and extensor hallucis longus. The tibialis posterior is normal. The short head of the biceps femoris is normal. The superficial fibular SNAP is absent. Where is the lesion?

   **Answer:** The denervation is limited to fibular nerve-innervated muscles (tibialis anterior, peroneus longus, EHL) with sparing of the tibialis posterior (tibial nerve, L5) and the short head of biceps femoris (fibular division of sciatic nerve). The normal SHBF excludes sciatic neuropathy proximal to the knee. The abnormal superficial fibular SNAP excludes L5 radiculopathy (which would have a normal SNAP by the DRG principle). The lesion is a **common fibular neuropathy at the fibular head**, consistent with positioning-related compression during surgery.
