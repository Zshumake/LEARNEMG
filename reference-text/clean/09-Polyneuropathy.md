## 9.1 Introduction: The Scope of Polyneuropathy 

Polyneuropathy represents one of the most commonly encountered diagnoses in the electrodiagnostic laboratory. The prevalence of peripheral neuropathy in the general population approaches 2-3%, rising to over 8% in individuals older than 55 years. Among patients referred for electrodiagnostic testing, suspected polyneuropathy accounts for approximately 25-30% of all studies performed.

The electrodiagnostic evaluation of polyneuropathy serves several critical functions beyond confirming or excluding the diagnosis. It characterizes the underlying pathophysiology (axonal loss versus demyelination), determines the distribution pattern (length-dependent versus non-length-dependent), identifies the fiber types involved (sensory, motor, or sensorimotor), and quantifies severity. These parameters collectively narrow the differential diagnosis from hundreds of potential etiologies to a manageable shortlist, guiding targeted laboratory testing and treatment decisions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The EDX examination is an extension of the clinical examination, not a replacement for it. Always correlate electrodiagnostic findings with the patient's history, physical examination, and clinical context. An isolated abnormal nerve conduction value without clinical correlation is insufficient for diagnosis.

## 9.2 The Seven Key Questions Framework 

A systematic approach to polyneuropathy characterization uses seven fundamental questions that every EDX study should address. This framework transforms raw electrodiagnostic data into clinically actionable information.

### Question 1: Is a Polyneuropathy Present?

The first step is confirming that the pattern of NCS abnormalities is consistent with a generalized polyneuropathy rather than a focal mononeuropathy, mononeuropathy multiplex, or normal study. A polyneuropathy diagnosis on EDX requires abnormalities in multiple nerves, typically following a symmetric, length-dependent gradient (distal greater than proximal involvement). In practice, this means abnormalities should be present in at least two nerves in one limb and ideally confirmed in additional limbs.

The AANEM recommends a minimum study battery for polyneuropathy characterization: up to 4 motor and 4 sensory nerve conduction studies distributed as 2 motor plus 2 sensory in one leg, 1 motor plus 1 sensory in the contralateral leg, and 1 motor plus 1 sensory in one arm. H-reflexes and F-waves from 2 nerves may add diagnostic value.

### Question 2: What Is the Predominant Pathophysiology -- Axonal or Demyelinating?

This is arguably the most important distinction in polyneuropathy evaluation because it immediately bifurcates the differential diagnosis into two largely non-overlapping lists. The criteria are discussed in detail in Section 9.3.

### Question 3: Which Fiber Types Are Involved?

Polyneuropathies may affect sensory fibers, motor fibers, or both. Sensory-predominant neuropathies present with reduced or absent sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) with relatively preserved compound muscle action potentials (CMAPs). Pure motor neuropathies show reduced CMAPs with normal SNAPs. Mixed sensorimotor neuropathies demonstrate abnormalities in both. Additionally, small-fiber neuropathies (affecting unmyelinated C fibers and thinly myelinated A-delta fibers) may produce entirely normal standard NCS, since conventional studies assess only large myelinated fiber function.

| Fiber Type | NCS Finding | Clinical Correlate |
|---|---|---|
| Large sensory (A-beta) | Reduced/absent SNAPs | Numbness, proprioceptive loss, sensory ataxia |
| Large motor (A-alpha) | Reduced CMAPs, neurogenic EMG | Weakness, atrophy |
| Small sensory (A-delta, C) | Normal NCS | Pain, temperature loss, autonomic dysfunction |
| Mixed large fiber | Both SNAP and CMAP abnormalities | Combined weakness and numbness |

### Question 4: What Is the Distribution?

**Length-dependent (dying-back):** The most common pattern. Distal nerves are affected first and most severely, with a stocking-glove gradient. Sural and fibular nerves show the earliest changes. This pattern is characteristic of metabolic and toxic etiologies.

**Non-length-dependent (multifocal/patchy):** Abnormalities do not follow a simple distal-to-proximal gradient. Random nerves may be affected. This pattern suggests inflammatory, vasculitic, or infiltrative etiologies. When non-length-dependent motor involvement predominates, consider multifocal motor neuropathy with conduction block (MMNCB).

**Proximal-distal (polyradiculoneuropathy):** Both proximal and distal segments are affected, with prominent proximal involvement manifested by prolonged F-wave latencies and H-reflex abnormalities. This is the hallmark of inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathies (GBS and CIDP).

### Question 5: What Is the Time Course?

- **Acute:** Days to 4 weeks (Guillain-Barre syndrome)
- **Subacute:** 4-8 weeks (toxic, nutritional, some inflammatory)
- **Chronic:** Months to years (most metabolic, hereditary, CIDP)
- **Relapsing-remitting:** CIDP, porphyria

### Question 6: How Severe Is the Neuropathy?

Severity is best assessed by CMAP and SNAP amplitude reductions, the degree of needle EMG denervation, and the presence of fibrillation potentials. A grading system can be applied:

| Severity | SNAP | CMAP | EMG Findings |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | Reduced amplitude | Normal | Minimal or no denervation |
| Moderate | Markedly reduced or absent in legs | Mildly reduced | Fibrillations in distal muscles |
| Severe | Absent in legs, reduced in arms | Reduced in legs | Widespread denervation, poor recruitment |
| Very severe | Absent all limbs | Markedly reduced/absent | Dense denervation, no voluntary MUAPs |

### Question 7: Is There Evidence of Superimposed Focal Neuropathy?

Patients with generalized polyneuropathy, particularly diabetic neuropathy, have increased susceptibility to focal entrapment neuropathies. The electrodiagnostician should evaluate for carpal tunnel syndrome, cubital tunnel syndrome, and fibular neuropathy at the fibular head when clinically indicated.

## 9.3 Practical Approach to the EDX Polyneuropathy Evaluation 

### Starting the Study: Recommended Nerve Selection

The initial nerve conduction study protocol for suspected polyneuropathy should be standardized yet flexible. Beginning with the lower extremities is logical because length-dependent neuropathies affect the longest nerves first.

**Recommended starting battery:**

| Nerve | Type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Sural (leg 1) | Sensory | Most sensitive early indicator of polyneuropathy; absent in many common neuropathies |
| Superficial fibular (leg 1) | Sensory | Complements sural; may be abnormal when sural is absent |
| Fibular/peroneal motor (leg 1) | Motor | Commonly affected in polyneuropathy; fibular head entrapment can be excluded |
| Tibial motor (leg 1) | Motor | Longer pathway; may show more advanced changes than fibular |
| Sural (leg 2) | Sensory | Bilateral comparison; confirms symmetric vs. asymmetric process |
| Fibular motor (leg 2) | Motor | Confirms bilateral involvement |
| Median sensory (arm) | Sensory | Upper extremity comparison; evaluate for concurrent CTS |
| Ulnar sensory (arm) | Sensory | Alternative upper extremity sensory nerve |
| Median motor (arm) | Motor | Assess for upper extremity motor involvement |
| H-reflex (bilateral) | Late response | May be earliest abnormality; assesses S1 pathway |

**Expanding the study based on initial findings:**

If the initial battery reveals a demyelinating pattern, expand to include:
- F-waves from at least 2 motor nerves (tibial and median or ulnar)
- Additional motor NCS to assess for conduction block (proximal stimulation sites)
- Comparison of conduction velocities across multiple nerve segments

If mononeuropathy multiplex is suspected:
- Individual nerve studies across multiple named nerves in multiple limbs
- Sensory and motor studies in each affected nerve territory

### The Role of Needle EMG in Polyneuropathy

Needle EMG adds critical information beyond what NCS alone provides:

1. **Quantifies denervation severity:** Fibrillation potential density (1+ through 4+) correlates with the degree of axonal loss
2. **Assesses acuity:** Active fibrillation potentials indicate ongoing denervation; pure chronic neurogenic changes suggest a static or slowly progressive process
3. **Confirms length-dependent pattern:** EMG abnormalities should be most severe distally (intrinsic foot muscles) and progressively less severe proximally
4. **Excludes concurrent diagnoses:** Identifies superimposed radiculopathy, motor neuron disease, or myopathy
5. **Guides prognosis:** The combination of active denervation and chronic reinnervation suggests an ongoing but partially compensated process

**Recommended EMG muscle selection for polyneuropathy:**

| Muscle | Nerve | Root | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| First dorsal interosseous (foot) | Lateral plantar | S1-S2 | Most distal; most sensitive for early neuropathy |
| Tibialis anterior | Deep fibular | L4-L5 | Mid-leg; commonly tested |
| Medial gastrocnemius | Tibial | S1-S2 | Posterior compartment comparison |
| Vastus lateralis | Femoral | L2-L4 | Proximal lower extremity; should be normal in mild PN |
| First dorsal interosseous (hand) | Ulnar | C8-T1 | Distal upper extremity; affected in severe PN |
| Lumbar paraspinals | Dorsal rami | L3-L5 | Excludes concurrent radiculopathy |

### Electrodiagnostic Severity Grading: A Practical Framework 

While no universally accepted severity grading system exists for all polyneuropathies, the following framework provides a clinically useful structure:

**Sensory NCS-based severity:**
- **Minimal:** Reduced but present sural SNAP amplitude (below normal but above 5 microV)
- **Mild:** Absent sural SNAP; present superficial fibular SNAP; present upper extremity SNAPs
- **Moderate:** Absent lower extremity SNAPs; reduced upper extremity SNAPs
- **Severe:** Absent SNAPs in all four limbs

**Motor NCS-based severity:**
- **Minimal:** Normal CMAPs with mildly slowed conduction velocities only
- **Mild:** Mildly reduced fibular CMAP amplitude (>2 mV)
- **Moderate:** Reduced fibular and tibial CMAP amplitudes; upper extremity CMAPs preserved
- **Severe:** Reduced CMAPs in all limbs; unobtainable motor responses in some nerves

**EMG-based severity assessment:**
- **No active denervation:** Chronic neurogenic changes only -- stable or very slowly progressive
- **Mild active denervation:** 1+ fibrillation potentials in distal muscles only
- **Moderate active denervation:** 2-3+ fibrillation potentials in distal muscles, extending to mid-leg muscles
- **Severe active denervation:** 3-4+ fibrillation potentials extending to proximal muscles; reduced or absent voluntary MUAP recruitment

## 9.4 Axonal Versus Demyelinating: EDX Criteria 

The distinction between axonal degeneration and segmental demyelination represents a cornerstone of electrodiagnostic practice. Each pathophysiology produces a characteristic pattern of NCS abnormalities that reflects the underlying biology.

### Axonal Polyneuropathy

In axonal degeneration, the nerve fiber (axon) undergoes Wallerian-type degeneration, typically in a length-dependent pattern. Since the axon itself is destroyed, the number of functioning nerve fibers decreases. This produces the cardinal feature of axonal loss: **reduced amplitude** of CMAPs and SNAPs.

Key criteria for axonal polyneuropathy:

- CMAP and SNAP amplitudes reduced proportionally to the degree of axonal loss
- Conduction velocity remains greater than 75% of the lower limit of normal (LLN), unless axonal loss is severe
- Distal latencies remain within 130% of the upper limit of normal (ULN)
- No conduction block or temporal dispersion
- F-wave latencies normal or mildly prolonged (proportional to distal slowing)

The conduction velocity "rule of thumb" deserves emphasis. When significant axonal loss occurs (CMAP amplitude falls below 20-30% of the lower limit of normal), the remaining fastest-conducting fibers may also be lost, producing mild conduction velocity slowing. This secondary slowing should not exceed 75% of the LLN. If conduction velocity falls below this threshold despite severe amplitude reduction, consider superimposed demyelination.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In axonal neuropathy, think "amplitude down, velocity preserved." The myelin sheath is intact on surviving axons, so conduction speed is maintained. The amplitude falls because fewer axons contribute to the compound potential.

### Demyelinating Polyneuropathy

Segmental demyelination affects the myelin sheath while initially sparing the underlying axon. Loss of myelin insulation forces nerve impulses to propagate through exposed internodal membrane (continuous conduction) rather than jumping between nodes of Ranvier (saltatory conduction). This dramatically slows conduction velocity.

Key criteria for demyelinating polyneuropathy:

- **Conduction velocity** slowed to less than 75% of the LLN (many sources use <70-80% depending on the specific criteria set applied)
- **Distal latency** prolonged beyond 130% of the ULN
- **Conduction block:** Greater than 20% drop in CMAP amplitude or area between proximal and distal stimulation sites (some criteria use >50% for definite block)
- **Temporal dispersion:** Greater than 15% increase in CMAP duration between proximal and distal stimulation sites
- **F-wave latency** prolonged beyond 130% of the ULN, or absent
- SNAP amplitudes may be preserved early (myelin loss without axonal degeneration) or reduced later (secondary axonal loss)

### Table 9.1: Axonal vs. Demyelinating EDX Features -- Summary

| Parameter | Axonal | Demyelinating |
|---|---|---|
| SNAP/CMAP amplitude | Reduced (primary finding) | Normal early; reduced with secondary axonal loss |
| Conduction velocity | Normal or mildly slow (>75% LLN) | Markedly slow (<75% LLN) |
| Distal motor latency | Normal or mildly prolonged (<130% ULN) | Prolonged (>130% ULN) |
| Conduction block | Absent | Present (>20% amplitude/area drop) |
| Temporal dispersion | Absent | Present (>15% duration increase) |
| F-wave latency | Normal or mildly prolonged | Prolonged (>130% ULN) or absent |
| Fibrillation potentials | Present (active denervation) | Absent unless secondary axonal loss |
| MUP morphology | Large, long duration (reinnervation) | Normal unless secondary axonal loss |
| Recruitment | Decreased (neurogenic pattern) | Normal unless secondary axonal loss |

### Conduction Block and Temporal Dispersion: Special Considerations

Conduction block and temporal dispersion are hallmarks of **acquired** demyelination and are not typically seen in hereditary demyelinating neuropathies. Their presence strongly suggests an immune-mediated or inflammatory etiology such as GBS, CIDP, or MMNCB.

**Conduction block** occurs when demyelination at a focal point prevents action potential propagation through that segment. Proximal stimulation produces a significantly smaller CMAP than distal stimulation, even though the axon is structurally intact distal to the block. The CMAP area is a more reliable measure than amplitude alone, since normal phase cancellation can reduce proximal amplitude by up to 20% in some nerves.

**Temporal dispersion** results from non-uniform slowing across nerve fibers. Faster fibers are slowed to different degrees, causing their action potentials to desynchronize. The resulting proximal CMAP is spread out in time (increased duration) and may be reduced in amplitude, but the total area is relatively preserved.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Conduction block without temporal dispersion indicates focal demyelination at a discrete site. Conduction block with temporal dispersion indicates diffuse demyelination over a longer segment. Both suggest acquired (inflammatory) rather than inherited demyelination.

## 9.4 Classification Systems for Polyneuropathy 

### Classification I: By Pathophysiology and Fiber Type

| Category | Subtypes | Typical Etiologies |
|---|---|---|
| Axonal sensorimotor | Length-dependent | Diabetes, alcohol, medications, uremia, B12 deficiency |
| Axonal motor > sensory | Variable distribution | Porphyria, lead poisoning, multifocal motor neuropathy |
| Axonal sensory > motor | Length-dependent | Cisplatin, pyridoxine toxicity, paraneoplastic (anti-Hu) |
| Demyelinating uniform | Hereditary | CMT1A, CMT1B, CMTX |
| Demyelinating non-uniform | Acquired inflammatory | GBS, CIDP, MMNCB |
| Mixed axonal-demyelinating | Variable | Severe CIDP, late CMT, toxic (amiodarone) |

### Classification II: By Distribution Pattern

| Distribution | Pattern Description | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Symmetric distal (length-dependent) | Stocking > glove gradient | Most metabolic and toxic neuropathies |
| Symmetric proximal-distal | Polyradiculoneuropathy | GBS, CIDP, diabetic LRPN |
| Asymmetric multifocal | Mononeuropathy multiplex | Vasculitis, sarcoidosis, HNPP, MMNCB |
| Asymmetric proximal | Plexopathy/radiculopathy pattern | Diabetic amyotrophy, radiation |
| Non-length-dependent sensory | Ganglionopathy (neuronopathy) | Sjogren, paraneoplastic, cisplatin |

> **Board Mnemonic -- DANG THERAPIST** for common causes of axonal polyneuropathy: **D**iabetes, **A**lcohol, **N**utritional (B12, B1, B6), **G**BS (axonal variants), **T**oxins, **H**ereditary, **E**ndocrine (thyroid), **R**enal (uremic), **A**myloid, **P**araproteinemic, **I**nfectious (HIV, leprosy), **S**ystemic (vasculitis, sarcoid), **T**reatment-related (medications).

## 9.5 Guillain-Barre Syndrome 

Guillain-Barre syndrome (GBS) is the prototypical acute inflammatory polyneuropathy and one of the most critical diagnoses made in the EDX laboratory. It represents a medical emergency requiring prompt recognition, as respiratory failure may develop rapidly.

### 9.5.1 Clinical Features

GBS classically presents as ascending, symmetric weakness progressing over days to a maximum of 4 weeks. Areflexia or hyporeflexia is typical. Sensory symptoms (paresthesias, pain) frequently precede weakness. Approximately two-thirds of patients report a preceding infection 1-4 weeks before symptom onset, most commonly Campylobacter jejuni gastroenteritis or upper respiratory infections.

Autonomic dysfunction occurs in up to 70% of hospitalized GBS patients and may include cardiac arrhythmias, labile blood pressure, urinary retention, and ileus. Respiratory insufficiency requiring mechanical ventilation develops in 20-30% of patients.

### 9.5.2 Electrophysiological Subtypes

GBS encompasses several electrophysiologically distinct subtypes, each reflecting different immune-mediated targets within the peripheral nerve.

**Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP)**

AIDP is the most common subtype in North America and Europe (approximately 85-90% of Western GBS cases). The immune attack targets Schwann cell myelin, producing classic demyelinating features on NCS:

- Conduction velocity slowing (<75% LLN)
- Prolonged distal motor latencies (>130% ULN)
- Conduction block and temporal dispersion
- Prolonged or absent F-wave latencies (often the earliest EDX finding)
- Prolonged or absent H-reflexes
- SNAP amplitudes initially preserved, then reduced with secondary axonal degeneration

**Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy (AMAN)**

AMAN accounts for 5-10% of GBS in Western populations but up to 30-65% in Asia and Central/South America. The immune attack targets the axolemma at the node of Ranvier, producing primary axonal degeneration of motor fibers:

- Reduced CMAP amplitudes with normal conduction velocities
- Normal SNAP amplitudes (motor only involvement)
- No conduction block or temporal dispersion (with one important exception)
- F-waves may be prolonged or absent

An important phenomenon in AMAN is **reversible conduction failure (RCF)**, in which transient conduction block or slowing mimics demyelination early in the disease course. This occurs because antibodies targeting nodal gangliosides (particularly GM1) disrupt sodium channel clustering at nodes of Ranvier, producing conduction failure without actual myelin destruction. The distinction from AIDP may require serial studies: RCF resolves rapidly (days to weeks) compared to true demyelination.

**Acute Motor and Sensory Axonal Neuropathy (AMSAN)**

AMSAN is the most severe axonal variant, affecting both motor and sensory axons:

- Reduced CMAP AND SNAP amplitudes
- Normal or near-normal conduction velocities
- No conduction block or temporal dispersion
- Poor prognosis with prolonged recovery

### 9.5.3 The Sural Sparing Pattern 

The "sural sparing pattern" is one of the most board-relevant EDX findings in GBS. In normal circumstances, the sural nerve (a distal lower extremity sensory nerve) is the most vulnerable to length-dependent processes and is typically the first sensory nerve to become abnormal. In AIDP, however, a seemingly paradoxical pattern may emerge: the sural SNAP is preserved while upper extremity sensory responses (particularly the median SNAP) are reduced or absent.

This pattern reflects the predilection of AIDP for nerve roots and proximal nerve segments, where the blood-nerve barrier is more permeable and inflammatory cell infiltration occurs more readily. The distal sural nerve is relatively spared because inflammation concentrates at sites of greater blood-nerve barrier permeability.

> **Clinical Pearl:** A normal sural SNAP with an abnormal median SNAP ("sural sparing") in a patient with acute ascending weakness is highly suggestive of AIDP. This finding has been reported in approximately 50% of AIDP patients studied within the first 1-2 weeks.

### 9.5.4 CMAP Amplitude as a Prognostic Indicator 

The distal CMAP amplitude is the single most important EDX prognostic factor in GBS. Reduced distal CMAP amplitudes indicate axonal loss (whether primary in AMAN/AMSAN or secondary to severe demyelination in AIDP) and correlate with poorer functional outcomes.

| Prognostic Factor | Favorable | Unfavorable |
|---|---|---|
| Distal CMAP amplitude | Preserved (>80% LLN) | Reduced (<20% LLN) |
| Pathophysiology | Pure demyelination | Axonal degeneration |
| Conduction block | Present (implies intact axons) | Absent with low CMAPs |
| Recovery timeline | Weeks to months | Months to years |

Patients with preserved CMAP amplitudes and conduction block (implying reversible demyelination with intact axons) generally recover well once remyelination occurs. Those with severely reduced distal CMAPs (indicating irreversible axonal loss) face prolonged recovery dependent on axonal regeneration, which proceeds at approximately 1-3 mm per day.

### 9.5.5 Timing of EDX Studies

Early electrodiagnostic studies (within the first 1-2 weeks of symptom onset) may be normal or show only subtle abnormalities such as prolonged F-wave latencies or absent H-reflexes. The most informative studies are performed 2-3 weeks after symptom onset, when NCS abnormalities have had time to evolve. Serial studies at 3-8 weeks improve electrodiagnostic subtype classification accuracy.

### 9.5.6 Anti-ganglioside Antibodies and Subtype Correlation 

Specific anti-ganglioside antibodies are associated with particular GBS subtypes and clinical presentations:

| Antibody | GBS Subtype | Clinical Association |
|---|---|---|
| Anti-GM1 IgG | AMAN | Motor predominant; Campylobacter jejuni association |
| Anti-GD1a IgG | AMAN | Motor predominant |
| Anti-GD1b IgG | Sensory variant | Sensory ataxia |
| Anti-GQ1b IgG | Miller Fisher syndrome | Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia |
| Anti-GT1a IgG | Pharyngeal-cervical-brachial variant | Bulbar weakness, arm weakness |

**Miller Fisher Syndrome** deserves special mention as a GBS variant characterized by the triad of ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, and areflexia without significant limb weakness. Anti-GQ1b antibodies are present in approximately 90% of cases. NCS may be normal or show only mild sensory abnormalities. The diagnosis is primarily clinical and serological.

### 9.5.7 CSF Findings in GBS 

The classic cerebrospinal fluid finding in GBS is **albuminocytologic dissociation**: elevated protein concentration (>45 mg/dL) with a normal cell count (<10 cells/microL). This finding reflects the breakdown of the blood-nerve barrier at nerve roots within the subarachnoid space, allowing protein to leak into the CSF without cellular infiltration.

Important caveats:
- CSF protein may be normal in the first week of illness (before blood-nerve barrier breakdown is established)
- Protein elevation increases over the first 2-4 weeks
- A significantly elevated cell count (>50 cells/microL) should prompt consideration of alternative diagnoses (HIV, Lyme disease, sarcoidosis, carcinomatous meningitis)

### 9.5.8 Treatment

Standard treatment is either intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg, 0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days) or plasma exchange (typically 5 exchanges over 7-14 days). Both are equivalent in efficacy and should be initiated early. Corticosteroids are not effective in GBS and should not be used.

**Additional management considerations:**
- Respiratory monitoring: Serial FVC measurements (intubate if FVC <20 mL/kg or declining >30% from baseline)
- Autonomic monitoring: Continuous cardiac monitoring for arrhythmias; cautious blood pressure management
- Deep vein thrombosis prophylaxis
- Pain management (often severe neuropathic pain)
- Early rehabilitation consultation
- Prognosis discussion: approximately 80% of patients achieve independent ambulation within 6 months; 5-10% have significant residual disability

### 9.5.9 Electrodiagnostic Prognostic Scoring 

Several EDX prognostic scoring systems have been developed for GBS. The Erasmus GBS Outcome Score (EGOS) incorporates clinical features, and EDX parameters add independent prognostic information:

**Favorable prognostic factors:**
- Preserved distal CMAP amplitudes (>80% LLN)
- Demyelinating pattern (AIDP) without secondary axonal loss
- Presence of conduction block (implies intact but blocked axons)
- Rapid improvement in F-wave parameters on serial studies

**Unfavorable prognostic factors:**
- Inexcitable motor nerves (absent CMAPs)
- Absent SNAPs early in the course
- Widespread fibrillation potentials on EMG (indicates axonal degeneration)
- AMSAN pattern
- Fibular CMAP amplitude <20% of LLN at 2 weeks

### Table 9.2: GBS Electrophysiological Subtypes

| Feature | AIDP | AMAN | AMSAN |
|---|---|---|---|
| Target | Schwann cell myelin | Motor axon (node of Ranvier) | Motor + sensory axons |
| Geographic | Western predominant | Asian predominant | Worldwide (rare) |
| CMAP velocity | Slow (<75% LLN) | Normal | Normal |
| CMAP amplitude | May be preserved early | Reduced | Reduced |
| SNAP | Preserved or mildly reduced | Normal | Reduced |
| Conduction block | Present | Absent (RCF possible early) | Absent |
| F-waves | Prolonged/absent | May be absent | May be absent |

## 9.6 Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyradiculoneuropathy (CIDP) 

### 9.6.1 Clinical Presentation

CIDP is the chronic counterpart of GBS, defined by progressive or relapsing weakness developing over at least 2 months (compared to GBS, which reaches nadir by 4 weeks). CIDP affects both proximal and distal muscles with sensory involvement, distinguishing it from most length-dependent polyneuropathies.

The time course distinction between GBS and CIDP is clinically important: if deterioration continues beyond 8 weeks from symptom onset, the diagnosis should be reconsidered from GBS to CIDP, as this has direct treatment implications. CIDP responds to corticosteroids, IVIg, and plasma exchange, whereas GBS does not respond to steroids.

### 9.6.2 The 2021 EAN/PNS Diagnostic Criteria 

The 2021 European Academy of Neurology/Peripheral Nerve Society (EAN/PNS) criteria represent the current international standard for CIDP diagnosis. These criteria achieve approximately 83% sensitivity and 94% specificity.

**Clinical criteria for typical CIDP:**
- Chronically progressive, stepwise, or recurrent symmetric proximal AND distal weakness
- Sensory dysfunction affecting at least 2 limbs
- Developing over at least 2 months
- Reduced or absent deep tendon reflexes in affected limbs

**CIDP variants** recognized by the 2021 criteria include:
- Distal CIDP (distal acquired demyelinating symmetric neuropathy, DADS)
- Multifocal CIDP (Lewis-Sumner syndrome / MADSAM)
- Focal CIDP
- Motor-predominant CIDP
- Sensory-predominant CIDP

**Electrodiagnostic criteria for CIDP** require demonstration of demyelination in at least 2 motor nerves. The specific parameters include:

| EDX Parameter | Threshold for Demyelination |
|---|---|
| Motor conduction velocity | <70% LLN (if amplitude >80% LLN) or <50% LLN (if amplitude <80% LLN) |
| Distal motor latency | >150% ULN (if amplitude >80% LLN) or >125% ULN (if amplitude <80% LLN) |
| F-wave latency | >130% ULN or absent |
| Conduction block | >30% amplitude reduction over a short segment |
| Temporal dispersion | >30% duration increase |

### 9.6.3 Acquired Versus Inherited Demyelination: Critical Distinction 

One of the most important tasks in evaluating a demyelinating polyneuropathy is distinguishing acquired (inflammatory) demyelination from inherited (hereditary) demyelination. This distinction has profound treatment implications: acquired demyelinating neuropathies (CIDP, GBS) are potentially treatable with immunotherapy, while hereditary demyelinating neuropathies (CMT) are not.

**Key features distinguishing acquired from inherited demyelination:**

| Feature | Acquired (CIDP) | Inherited (CMT) |
|---|---|---|
| Conduction velocity pattern | Non-uniform (variable between nerves/segments) | Uniform (similar across all nerves) |
| Conduction block | Present | Absent |
| Temporal dispersion | Present | Absent |
| Velocity reduction | Variable, often >20% difference between nerves | Consistent, typically <5 m/s difference between nerves |
| CSF protein | Elevated (>45 mg/dL in >80%) | Normal or mildly elevated |
| Disease course | Progressive or relapsing; onset any age | Slowly progressive from childhood |
| Family history | Negative | Positive (autosomal dominant most common) |
| Nerve enlargement | Patchy, asymmetric | Diffuse, symmetric |
| Response to immunotherapy | Yes | No |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most reliable EDX feature distinguishing acquired from inherited demyelination is the uniformity of conduction velocity slowing. In CMT, conduction velocities are uniformly slow across all nerves (because every Schwann cell carries the same genetic defect). In CIDP, slowing is non-uniform because inflammation affects nerves and nerve segments irregularly.

### 9.6.4 CIDP Variants and Their EDX Patterns 

**Distal Acquired Demyelinating Symmetric Neuropathy (DADS):**
DADS presents with predominantly distal symptoms and signs, often with prominent sensory ataxia and tremor. Approximately 50% of DADS patients have an associated IgM monoclonal gammopathy with anti-MAG antibodies. The EDX pattern shows distal-predominant demyelination with disproportionately prolonged distal motor latencies. Patients with anti-MAG DADS may respond poorly to standard CIDP treatments.

**Multifocal Acquired Demyelinating Sensory and Motor Neuropathy (MADSAM, Lewis-Sumner Syndrome):**
MADSAM presents with asymmetric, multifocal sensorimotor deficits involving individual named nerves, resembling mononeuropathy multiplex. Unlike MMNCB (which is pure motor), MADSAM involves both sensory and motor fibers. EDX shows demyelinating features with conduction block in individual nerves, but with concurrent sensory abnormalities in affected nerve territories. MADSAM responds to CIDP treatments (IVIg, steroids).

**Motor-Predominant CIDP:**
Presents predominantly with weakness and less prominent sensory involvement. Must be distinguished from MMNCB (which is pure motor) and motor neuron disease. EDX shows demyelinating features in motor nerves with relatively preserved sensory responses.

**Sensory-Predominant CIDP:**
Presents with numbness, paresthesias, and sensory ataxia with minimal weakness. NCS show sensory nerve demyelinating features (prolonged sensory peak latencies, reduced conduction velocities). This variant must be distinguished from sensory ganglionopathy and chronic immune sensory polyradiculopathy (CISP).

### 9.6.5 CIDP Treatment Response Monitoring 

The EDX evaluation serves as an objective measure of treatment response in CIDP. Serial NCS can document:

- Improvement in conduction velocities (remyelination)
- Resolution of conduction block
- Recovery of SNAP and CMAP amplitudes
- Improvement in F-wave parameters

A common clinical strategy involves performing NCS at baseline and then at 3-6 month intervals during treatment to objectively assess response. Failure to improve on NCS despite clinical improvement should raise concern for placebo effect or concurrent diagnosis. Conversely, NCS improvement without clinical improvement may occur if damage has resulted in irreversible axonal loss despite successful control of the inflammatory process.

### 9.6.6 CIDP Differential Diagnosis 

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Feature from CIDP |
|---|---|
| GBS | Time course <4 weeks to nadir; monophasic |
| CMT1A | Uniform slowing; no conduction block; positive family history |
| Anti-MAG neuropathy | Distal-predominant demyelination; IgM gammopathy |
| POEMS | Osteosclerotic bone lesions; elevated VEGF; multisystem features |
| MMNCB | Pure motor; no sensory involvement |
| Multifocal diabetic neuropathy | Diabetes history; asymmetric motor/sensory; does not meet CIDP NCS criteria |
| Hereditary neuropathy (CMTX1) | X-linked inheritance; intermediate velocities; males more severe |

## 9.7 Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease (CMT) 

### 9.7.1 Overview

Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease (hereditary motor and sensory neuropathy, HMSN) encompasses a genetically heterogeneous group of inherited peripheral neuropathies. CMT is the most common inherited neurological disorder, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 2,500 individuals. The electrodiagnostic classification divides CMT into demyelinating (CMT1) and axonal (CMT2) forms based on motor conduction velocity.

### 9.7.2 CMT1A and PMP-22 

CMT1A is the most common form, accounting for approximately 50-60% of all CMT cases. It results from a 1.5-megabase duplication on chromosome 17p11.2 containing the peripheral myelin protein 22 (PMP-22) gene. This duplication leads to PMP-22 overexpression, causing abnormal myelin formation and the characteristic uniform demyelinating pattern on NCS.

**EDX features of CMT1A:**

- **Uniform conduction velocity slowing:** Motor conduction velocities are characteristically in the 15-25 m/s range (compared to normal >40-50 m/s). The slowing is remarkably consistent across all nerves tested.
- **No conduction block** (distinguishing from acquired demyelination)
- **No temporal dispersion** (distinguishing from acquired demyelination)
- **SNAP amplitudes:** Reduced or absent
- **CMAP amplitudes:** May be preserved early, but decrease with secondary axonal loss over time
- **EMG:** Chronic neurogenic changes in distal muscles, reflecting secondary axonal degeneration

The classification threshold for CMT1 versus CMT2 is traditionally set at a median motor conduction velocity of 38 m/s: CMT1 patients have velocities below this threshold, while CMT2 patients have velocities above it (with intermediate forms falling near this cutoff).

> **Board Mnemonic:** PMP-22 = "**P**assed from **M**om or **P**op, chromosome **22** minus 5 (= 17)." CMT1A is autosomal dominant with a duplication on chromosome 17.

### 9.7.3 Other CMT Subtypes

| Subtype | Inheritance | Gene/Protein | NCV | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CMT1A | AD | PMP-22 duplication (chr 17) | <38 m/s (uniform) | Most common; uniform slowing |
| CMT1B | AD | MPZ (myelin protein zero) | <38 m/s | More variable severity |
| CMTX1 | X-linked | GJB1 (connexin 32) | Intermediate (25-45 m/s) | Males more severe; CNS involvement possible |
| CMT2A | AD | MFN2 (mitofusin 2) | >38 m/s | Axonal; most common CMT2 |
| CMT4 | AR | Various | <38 m/s | Severe, early onset |
| HNPP | AD | PMP-22 deletion (chr 17) | Focal slowing at entrapment sites | Recurrent pressure palsies; "tomaculae" on biopsy |

**Hereditary Neuropathy with Liability to Pressure Palsies (HNPP)** deserves special mention. HNPP results from PMP-22 deletion (the reciprocal of the CMT1A duplication). Patients present with recurrent, painless focal neuropathies at common entrapment sites (carpal tunnel, cubital tunnel, fibular head). NCS show diffusely prolonged distal latencies with focal conduction slowing at entrapment sites, and mildly reduced conduction velocities throughout.

## 9.8 Multifocal Motor Neuropathy with Conduction Block (MMNCB) Versus ALS 

### 9.8.1 Clinical Significance of the Distinction

Multifocal motor neuropathy with conduction block (MMNCB, also abbreviated MMN) is one of the most important "treatable mimics" of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). Both conditions present with progressive, asymmetric weakness and atrophy without sensory loss. However, MMNCB responds to immunotherapy (IVIg), while ALS is relentlessly progressive. Misdiagnosis of MMNCB as ALS deprives patients of effective treatment.

### 9.8.2 EDX Differentiation

| Feature | MMNCB | ALS |
|---|---|---|
| Motor conduction block | Present (defining feature) | Absent |
| Sensory NCS | Normal | Normal |
| CMAP amplitude (distal) | Normal or reduced | Reduced |
| Conduction velocity | Normal or mildly slow | Normal |
| Distribution of weakness | Follows individual nerve territories | Does not follow nerve territories |
| Fibrillation potentials | Absent or rare | Prominent, widespread |
| Fasciculation potentials | May be present | Prominent |
| UMN signs | Absent | Present (defining feature of ALS) |
| Anti-GM1 antibodies | Present in ~50% | Absent |
| Response to IVIg | Yes | No |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key EDX finding is motor conduction block outside of common entrapment sites. In MMNCB, conduction block occurs along nerve segments that are not typical sites for compression (e.g., forearm segment of the ulnar nerve rather than at the elbow). If you see motor conduction block with normal sensory responses in a patient with pure motor weakness, think MMNCB before ALS.

### 9.8.3 Defining Conduction Block in MMNCB

For the diagnosis of MMNCB, conduction block criteria are more stringent than for general demyelinating neuropathy:

- **Definite conduction block:** >50% reduction in CMAP area between proximal and distal stimulation, with <30% change in CMAP duration
- **Probable conduction block:** >30% reduction in CMAP area between proximal and distal stimulation in a segment with >50% change in duration, OR >50% area reduction in a long segment where some temporal dispersion is expected

## 9.9 Diabetic Neuropathy 

### 9.9.1 Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (DSP)

Diabetic DSP is the most common form of diabetic neuropathy and the single most common cause of polyneuropathy worldwide. It is a length-dependent, axonal-predominant sensorimotor polyneuropathy. Electrodiagnostic findings include:

**NCS findings:**
- Sural SNAP: First abnormality; reduced amplitude or absent
- Fibular (peroneal) CMAP: Reduced amplitude with disease progression
- Conduction velocities: Mildly slowed (axonal range, not reaching demyelinating thresholds)
- Upper extremity NCS: Normal early; abnormal in advanced disease
- H-reflex: Prolonged or absent (may be earliest EDX finding)

**EMG findings:**
- Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves in distal muscles (intrinsic foot muscles first)
- Chronic neurogenic MUP changes (increased amplitude and duration from reinnervation)
- Length-dependent distribution: intrinsic foot muscles affected before tibialis anterior, before proximal leg muscles

### 9.9.2 Diabetic Amyotrophy (Diabetic Lumbosacral Radiculoplexus Neuropathy) 

Diabetic amyotrophy is a distinct entity that presents with acute or subacute onset of severe, often unilateral thigh pain followed by proximal leg weakness and atrophy. It is an immune-mediated ischemic injury (microvasculitis) to the lumbosacral plexus and nerve roots. EDX findings include:

- Active denervation (fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves) in lumbosacral myotomes, particularly L2-L4 distribution
- Reduced femoral CMAP amplitude
- Reduced saphenous or lateral femoral cutaneous SNAP (if technically obtainable)
- Abnormalities may be bilateral despite clinically unilateral presentation
- Pattern does not follow a single nerve root or peripheral nerve distribution, supporting plexus-level localization

### 9.9.3 Treatment-Induced Neuropathy of Diabetes 

Treatment-induced neuropathy of diabetes (previously called "insulin neuritis") is an iatrogenic acute painful neuropathy that develops within 8 weeks of a rapid reduction in HbA1c (typically >2% drop over 3 months). It presents with severe neuropathic pain, small-fiber predominant neuropathy, and autonomic dysfunction. Standard NCS may be normal or show only mild changes, as the primary pathology involves small unmyelinated fibers.

### 9.9.4 Superimposed Entrapment Neuropathies

Diabetic patients have a 2-3-fold increased risk of focal entrapment neuropathies, particularly carpal tunnel syndrome and ulnar neuropathy at the elbow. The electrodiagnostician should actively evaluate for superimposed mononeuropathies, as these are treatable and can be distinguished from the underlying polyneuropathy by comparing nerve conduction parameters across the entrapment site to those in non-entrapped segments.

## 9.10 Critical Illness Polyneuropathy (CIP) 

Critical illness polyneuropathy develops in patients with prolonged ICU stays, sepsis, systemic inflammatory response syndrome, or multi-organ failure. It is an acute axonal sensorimotor polyneuropathy that presents as difficulty weaning from mechanical ventilation, diffuse weakness, and reduced reflexes.

**EDX findings:**

| Parameter | Finding |
|---|---|
| CMAP amplitudes | Reduced (axonal motor loss) |
| SNAP amplitudes | Reduced (axonal sensory loss) |
| Conduction velocities | Normal or mildly reduced (axonal range) |
| Distal latencies | Normal |
| F-waves | May be absent |
| EMG | Fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves in distal and proximal muscles |
| CMAP duration | Normal (distinguishes from CIM) |

The distinction between CIP and critical illness myopathy (CIM) is clinically important because CIM generally has a better prognosis. The key differentiators are discussed in Chapter 11 but bear brief mention here:

| Feature | CIP | CIM |
|---|---|---|
| SNAP amplitudes | Reduced/absent | Normal |
| CMAP duration | Normal | Prolonged (hallmark finding) |
| Direct muscle stimulation | Normal response | Reduced response |
| Prognosis | Variable | Generally better |

## 9.11 Medication-Induced Neuropathies 

Numerous medications can cause polyneuropathy. Recognition is critical because the neuropathy may be reversible upon drug discontinuation if identified early.

### Table 9.3: Medications Commonly Causing Polyneuropathy

| Medication | Neuropathy Type | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vincristine** | Axonal sensorimotor | Microtubule disruption | Dose-dependent; may be severe |
| **Cisplatin** | Sensory neuronopathy | DRG toxicity | Non-length-dependent SNAP loss; proprioceptive loss |
| **Taxanes** (paclitaxel, docetaxel) | Axonal sensory > motor | Microtubule stabilization | Painful; dose-dependent |
| **Thalidomide/lenalidomide** | Axonal sensory | Antiangiogenic | Irreversible in many cases |
| **Amiodarone** | Mixed demyelinating + axonal | Lysosomal lipid accumulation | Proximal > distal; may mimic CIDP |
| **Metronidazole** | Axonal sensory | Unknown | Length-dependent; reversible early |
| **Nitrofurantoin** | Axonal sensorimotor | Unknown | Renal failure increases risk |
| **Isoniazid** | Axonal sensory | Pyridoxine (B6) depletion | Preventable with B6 supplementation |
| **Phenytoin** | Axonal sensorimotor | Unknown | Chronic use; usually mild |
| **Statins** | Axonal sensorimotor | Uncertain | Controversial; usually mild |
| **Colchicine** | Axonal sensorimotor + myopathy | Microtubule disruption | Combined neuropathy/myopathy |
| **Bortezomib** | Axonal sensory | Proteasome inhibition | Painful; dose-limiting |
| **Linezolid** | Axonal sensory + optic | Mitochondrial toxicity | Duration-dependent; >28 days risk |
| **Pyridoxine (B6) excess** | Sensory neuronopathy | DRG toxicity | Doses >200 mg/day; non-length-dependent |

> **Board Mnemonic -- Demyelinating drug neuropathies are rare; remember "ACS":** **A**miodarone, **C**hloroquine/hydroxychloroquine, **S**uramin. Nearly all other drug-induced neuropathies are axonal.

## 9.12 HIV-Associated Neuropathies 

HIV infection is associated with several distinct neuropathic syndromes occurring at different stages of disease:

| Syndrome | HIV Stage | EDX Pattern | Pathogenesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Distal sensory polyneuropathy (DSP) | Late/advanced | Axonal sensory > motor, length-dependent | Direct HIV neurotoxicity, immune activation |
| Antiretroviral toxic neuropathy (ATN) | Any (on treatment) | Axonal sensory, identical to DSP | Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (d4T, ddI, ddC) |
| Inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (AIDP/CIDP) | Early/seroconversion or any stage | Demyelinating | Immune-mediated |
| Mononeuropathy multiplex | Various | Multiple individual nerve lesions | Vasculitis (CMV in advanced AIDS) |
| Progressive polyradiculopathy | Advanced AIDS | Lumbosacral distribution, axonal | CMV infection of nerve roots |
| Diffuse infiltrative lymphocytosis syndrome (DILS) | Any | Axonal or mixed | CD8+ T-cell infiltration of nerves |

The most common HIV neuropathy is distal sensory polyneuropathy, which is clinically indistinguishable from nucleoside-associated toxic neuropathy. The timing relative to antiretroviral initiation and the "coasting" phenomenon (worsening after drug discontinuation before eventual improvement) may help distinguish the two.

## 9.13 Paraproteinemic Neuropathies 

Paraproteinemic neuropathies are polyneuropathies associated with monoclonal gammopathies (M-proteins), which may be benign (MGUS) or malignant (multiple myeloma, Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia, amyloidosis).

### IgM Paraproteinemic Neuropathy

IgM monoclonal gammopathy is the most common paraprotein-associated neuropathy. Approximately 50% of patients with IgM MGUS neuropathy have antibodies directed against myelin-associated glycoprotein (MAG).

**Anti-MAG neuropathy EDX features:**
- Demyelinating pattern with disproportionately prolonged distal motor latencies (terminal latency index is reduced)
- Conduction velocity slowing in the intermediate range
- Typically distal-predominant, slowly progressive
- SNAP amplitudes reduced or absent

The hallmark finding is the "distal-predominant demyelination" pattern: distal motor latencies are prolonged out of proportion to the degree of proximal conduction velocity slowing. This contrasts with CIDP, where slowing tends to be more uniform across nerve segments or even proximal-predominant.

### IgG and IgA Paraproteinemic Neuropathies

These may present similarly to CIDP and indeed may represent CIDP occurring in the context of a coincidental monoclonal gammopathy, or a true paraprotein-mediated neuropathy.

### POEMS Syndrome (Osteosclerotic Myeloma) 

POEMS (Polyneuropathy, Organomegaly, Endocrinopathy, M-protein, Skin changes) is a rare but important paraneoplastic syndrome associated with lambda light chain restricted IgA or IgG monoclonal proteins. The neuropathy is characteristically demyelinating and may mimic CIDP. Key distinguishing features include osteosclerotic (rather than lytic) bone lesions, elevated VEGF levels, and the multi-systemic nature of the syndrome.

## 9.14 Additional Polyneuropathy Etiologies 

### Uremic Neuropathy

Chronic kidney disease produces a length-dependent axonal sensorimotor polyneuropathy. NCS show reduced SNAP and CMAP amplitudes with mild conduction velocity slowing. The neuropathy may improve with renal transplantation but has limited reversibility with dialysis alone.

### Alcoholic Neuropathy

Alcohol-associated neuropathy is a length-dependent axonal sensorimotor polyneuropathy that often coexists with nutritional deficiency (thiamine, folate). The contribution of direct alcohol toxicity versus nutritional deficiency remains debated. EDX findings are typical for axonal neuropathy with distal predominance.

### Vitamin B12 Deficiency Neuropathy

B12 deficiency causes a predominantly sensory neuropathy that may be axonal or have a subacute combined degeneration pattern (dorsal column involvement on MRI with myelopathy). SNAPs are reduced; CMAPs are typically preserved unless severe. The neuropathy is potentially reversible with B12 supplementation if detected early.

### Hereditary Transthyretin Amyloidosis (hATTR) 

hATTR amyloidosis causes a progressive axonal sensorimotor and autonomic polyneuropathy due to amyloid fibril deposition in peripheral nerves. The neuropathy characteristically begins with small-fiber involvement (pain and autonomic dysfunction) before progressing to large-fiber loss detectable by NCS. Treatment with tafamidis, patisiran (siRNA), or inotersen (antisense oligonucleotide) can stabilize or slow progression.

### Vasculitic Neuropathy

Vasculitis affecting the vasa nervorum produces ischemic nerve injury, typically presenting as mononeuropathy multiplex that may evolve into a confluent pattern mimicking polyneuropathy. NCS show asymmetric, non-length-dependent axonal loss affecting individual named nerves. The pattern of involvement does not respect the typical gradient of length-dependent neuropathies.

## 9.15 Approach to the Undiagnosed Polyneuropathy 

Despite thorough evaluation, approximately 20-30% of polyneuropathies remain idiopathic (cryptogenic). The approach to undiagnosed polyneuropathy should be systematic:

1. **Confirm the diagnosis and characterize the neuropathy** using the Seven Key Questions framework
2. **Directed laboratory testing** based on the EDX pattern:
   - Axonal sensorimotor: glucose/HbA1c, B12, TSH, SPEP/UPEP with immunofixation, CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, ESR/CRP
   - Demyelinating: SPEP/UPEP with immunofixation, anti-MAG antibodies, lumbar puncture (CSF protein), genetic testing for CMT
   - Sensory neuronopathy (ganglionopathy): anti-Hu, anti-amphiphysin, SSA/SSB (Sjogren), CT chest
   - Mononeuropathy multiplex: ANCA, cryoglobulins, hepatitis B/C, HIV, ESR/CRP, ANA
3. **Consider nerve biopsy** when vasculitis, amyloidosis, sarcoidosis, or other infiltrative processes are suspected
4. **Consider skin biopsy** for intraepidermal nerve fiber density when small-fiber neuropathy is suspected despite normal NCS

> **Clinical Pearl:** In clinical practice, the most common cause of "idiopathic" axonal sensorimotor polyneuropathy is prediabetes/metabolic syndrome. An oral glucose tolerance test is more sensitive than fasting glucose or HbA1c for detecting glucose intolerance in patients with otherwise unexplained neuropathy.

## 9.16 Summary: Board-High-Yield Review Points

1. The Seven Key Questions provide a systematic framework for polyneuropathy characterization: presence, pathophysiology, fiber type, distribution, time course, severity, and superimposed focal lesions.

2. Axonal neuropathies show reduced amplitudes with preserved velocities; demyelinating neuropathies show slowed velocities, prolonged distal latencies, conduction block, and temporal dispersion.

3. Conduction block and temporal dispersion indicate ACQUIRED demyelination (GBS, CIDP, MMNCB) -- not hereditary demyelination (CMT).

4. GBS subtypes: AIDP (demyelinating, Western), AMAN (motor axonal, Asian), AMSAN (motor + sensory axonal, severe). The sural sparing pattern and distal CMAP amplitude are high-yield board topics.

5. CIDP is distinguished from GBS by time course (>2 months vs. <4 weeks) and responds to steroids (GBS does not).

6. CMT1A (PMP-22 duplication, chromosome 17): uniform conduction velocity slowing, no conduction block -- the most common hereditary neuropathy.

7. MMNCB is distinguished from ALS by motor conduction block, normal sensory NCS, absence of upper motor neuron signs, and anti-GM1 antibodies.

8. Diabetic DSP is a length-dependent axonal polyneuropathy; sural SNAP is the earliest abnormality. Diabetic amyotrophy is immune-mediated lumbosacral plexopathy.

9. CIP shows reduced CMAPs AND SNAPs with normal CMAP duration; CIM shows reduced CMAPs with NORMAL SNAPs and PROLONGED CMAP duration.

10. Amiodarone is the prototypical medication causing demyelinating neuropathy; most drug-induced neuropathies are axonal.
