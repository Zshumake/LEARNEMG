## 3.1 Nerve Conduction Study Parameters 

### 3.1.1 Latency

Latency is the time interval between the application of a stimulus and the onset (or peak) of the recorded response. It is measured in milliseconds (ms).

**Types of latency:**

**Onset latency**: Measured from the stimulus artifact to the initial deflection of the response from baseline. For motor studies, this is the standard measurement and reflects the conduction time of the fastest-conducting motor fibers from the stimulation site to the recording electrode plus the time for neuromuscular junction transmission and muscle fiber activation. For sensory studies, onset latency reflects the fastest-conducting sensory fibers.

**Peak latency**: Measured from the stimulus artifact to the negative peak of the response. Peak latency is used more commonly for sensory NCS because it is more reproducible than onset latency for small-amplitude SNAPs. Peak latency is influenced by the range of conduction velocities in the nerve (temporal dispersion) as well as the fastest fibers.

**Distal motor latency (DML)**: The onset latency of the CMAP when stimulating at the most distal standard site. The DML includes conduction time through the distal nerve segment, neuromuscular junction transmission time (~0.5-1.0 ms), and the time for muscle fiber depolarization. Because of these non-neural components, conduction velocity cannot be calculated for the distal motor segment alone.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Prolonged distal motor latency is a hallmark of distal demyelination. In carpal tunnel syndrome, the median distal motor latency is prolonged because demyelination occurs at the wrist, slowing conduction through the carpal tunnel. The comparison of median vs. ulnar distal latency is a sensitive technique for detecting median neuropathy at the wrist -- a side-to-side difference greater than 1.0-1.5 ms is abnormal even if absolute values are within normal limits.

### 3.1.2 Amplitude

Amplitude reflects the number of nerve fibers or muscle fibers contributing to the response and is the primary indicator of axonal integrity.

**CMAP amplitude**: Measured from baseline to the negative peak (baseline-to-peak amplitude). Reported in millivolts (mV). Normal values range from approximately 2-20 mV depending on the nerve and muscle studied.

**SNAP amplitude**: Can be measured baseline-to-peak or peak-to-peak (the latter being more common for biphasic sensory potentials). Reported in microvolts (microV). Normal values range from approximately 6-80 microV depending on the nerve.

| Measurement | CMAP | SNAP |
|-------------|------|------|
| Unit | Millivolts (mV) | Microvolts (microV) |
| Typical range | 2-20 mV | 6-80 microV |
| Measurement method | Baseline to negative peak | Baseline-to-peak or peak-to-peak |
| Reflects | Number of muscle fibers activated | Number of sensory axons conducting |
| Reduced by | Motor axonal loss; conduction block; NMJ failure | Sensory axonal loss; postganglionic lesion |

> **Clinical Pearl:** A low CMAP amplitude has several possible causes: axonal loss, conduction block proximal to the stimulation site, neuromuscular junction failure, or technical error (submaximal stimulation, E1 off motor point). By contrast, a low SNAP amplitude almost always indicates sensory axonal loss or a postganglionic lesion (assuming adequate technique and normal temperature). The SNAP is preserved in preganglionic lesions (radiculopathy) because the DRG cell body and its peripheral axon remain intact.

### 3.1.3 Conduction Velocity

Conduction velocity (CV) is calculated as the distance between two stimulation points divided by the time difference between the corresponding response latencies:

**CV (m/s) = Distance (mm) / (Proximal Latency - Distal Latency) (ms)**

**For motor NCS**: CV is calculated between two stimulation sites (e.g., wrist and elbow for the median nerve). The distal motor latency is subtracted from the proximal latency to eliminate the non-neural components (NMJ time, muscle fiber activation time), which are common to both measurements. This gives the pure nerve conduction time over the measured segment.

**For sensory NCS**: CV can be calculated over a single segment because there is no NMJ or muscle component. CV = Distance / Onset latency (for single-segment studies).

CV primarily reflects conduction in the largest, fastest myelinated fibers. Normal CV values:
- Upper extremity: greater than or equal to 49-50 m/s
- Lower extremity: greater than or equal to 40-44 m/s

> **Board Mnemonic:** "You can't calculate distal motor velocity." This is because the distal motor latency includes NMJ transmission and muscle activation time in addition to nerve conduction time. These non-neural components cannot be subtracted from a single-segment measurement.

### 3.1.4 Duration

Duration measures the time from the initial deflection of the response to its return to baseline. It reflects the range of conduction velocities among the fibers contributing to the response (temporal dispersion).

**Negative peak duration**: Measured from the initial negative deflection to the return to baseline crossing. This is the standard duration measurement for CMAP and is used in conduction block criteria.

Increased duration indicates increased temporal dispersion -- the action potentials of individual fibers are arriving at the recording electrode over a wider time window, typically because different fibers are conducting at different velocities (as in demyelinating neuropathy).

### 3.1.5 Area

Area under the negative peak of the CMAP (or SNAP) represents the total electrical charge of the response and reflects both the number of fibers contributing and the temporal synchrony of their activation.

**Area is more accurate than amplitude for detecting conduction block** because temporal dispersion can reduce amplitude without reducing area (the response spreads out in time but maintains the same total charge). In conduction block, both amplitude AND area are reduced because some fibers fail to conduct altogether.

| Finding | Amplitude | Area | Duration | Interpretation |
|---------|-----------|------|----------|---------------|
| Normal | Normal | Normal | Normal | Normal conduction |
| Temporal dispersion | Reduced | Preserved | Increased | Differential slowing; demyelination |
| Conduction block | Reduced | Reduced | Normal or minimal increase | Fibers fail to conduct across a segment |
| Axonal loss | Reduced | Reduced | Normal | Fewer fibers contributing |

## 3.2 SNAP vs. CMAP: Key Differences 

Understanding the fundamental differences between SNAPs and CMAPs is essential for clinical interpretation:

| Feature | SNAP | CMAP |
|---------|------|------|
| Generator | Sensory axons only | Motor axons + NMJ + muscle fibers |
| Amplitude scale | Microvolts (5-80 microV) | Millivolts (2-20+ mV) |
| Waveform | Typically biphasic or triphasic | Biphasic (negative-positive) |
| Latency measurement | Onset or peak (peak preferred) | Onset (standard) |
| CV calculation | Can calculate over single segment | Requires two stimulation sites |
| Volume conduction | Minimal (small potentials) | More prominent (large potentials) |
| Effect of preganglionic lesion | NORMAL (DRG preserves peripheral axon) | May be abnormal (motor neuron affected) |
| Effect of postganglionic lesion | ABNORMAL (axonal loss distal to DRG) | May be abnormal |
| Temporal dispersion effect | More pronounced (smaller signal, greater relative effect of phase cancellation) | Less pronounced (larger signal) |
| Temperature sensitivity | Amplitude increases with cooling | Minimal amplitude change with cooling |

> **Clinical Pearl -- The Most Important Board Fact About SNAPs:** In a patient with numbness and a normal SNAP, the lesion is preganglionic (proximal to the DRG). This distinguishes radiculopathy from plexopathy or peripheral neuropathy. A patient with L5 radiculopathy will have numbness in the L5 dermatome but a normal superficial fibular SNAP, because the DRG cell body (in the foramen) and its peripheral sensory axon are intact. A patient with sciatic neuropathy or fibular neuropathy will have an abnormal SNAP because the lesion is distal to the DRG.

## 3.4 Temporal Dispersion and Phase Cancellation 

### 3.4.1 Temporal Dispersion

Temporal dispersion occurs when the individual nerve fiber action potentials within a compound response arrive at the recording electrode over an extended time window rather than simultaneously. This happens because different fibers conduct at different velocities.

In a normal nerve, temporal dispersion is minimal for short-segment studies but becomes more pronounced with increasing distance between stimulation and recording sites. This is because even small differences in individual fiber conduction velocities produce larger time separations over longer distances.

**Pathological temporal dispersion** occurs in demyelinating neuropathies where myelin damage variably affects different nerve fibers, producing a wider range of conduction velocities than normal. The hallmark of pathological temporal dispersion is:
- Increased CMAP duration (>30% increase between distal and proximal stimulation)
- Reduced CMAP amplitude (proportional to duration increase)
- Relatively preserved CMAP area (total electrical charge is maintained)
- Dispersed, polyphasic CMAP morphology

### 3.4.2 Phase Cancellation

Phase cancellation is the phenomenon in which the positive phase of one fiber's action potential overlaps with and cancels the negative phase of another fiber's action potential arriving at a slightly different time. This is a mathematical summation effect that physically reduces the recorded amplitude and area of the compound response.

Phase cancellation affects SNAPs more than CMAPs because:
1. SNAPs are much smaller (microvolts), so even small cancellation effects are proportionally larger
2. Sensory fibers have a narrower range of diameters and velocities, making phase cancellation more effective
3. The biphasic or triphasic waveform of individual sensory fiber action potentials is more susceptible to cancellation than the broader motor unit potential

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phase cancellation explains why SNAP amplitudes decrease dramatically with increasing distance between stimulation and recording sites, even in normal nerves. This is a physiological phenomenon, not pathology. For this reason, fixed standard distances should be used for SNAP recording (e.g., 14 cm for median digit 2, 14 cm for sural), and amplitude norms must be established at the specific recording distance used by the laboratory.

### 3.4.3 Clinical Significance

The distinction between temporal dispersion and conduction block has critical diagnostic implications:

| Feature | Temporal Dispersion | Conduction Block |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Amplitude change (prox vs distal) | Decreased | Decreased |
| Duration change | Increased (>30%) | Minimal (<30%) |
| Area change | Relatively preserved | Decreased (>50%) |
| CMAP morphology | Dispersed, polyphasic | Relatively preserved morphology |
| Pathology | Differential slowing (demyelination) | Failure of conduction (focal demyelination) |
| Implications | Demyelination; may be uniform or multifocal | Focal demyelination; hallmark of acquired demyelinating neuropathy |

## 3.5 Conduction Block 

### 3.5.1 Definition

Conduction block is the failure of action potential propagation across a nerve segment despite the preservation of the axon. It is a focal phenomenon caused by demyelination or other processes that prevent the action potential from traversing a specific region of the nerve. Conduction block is detected by comparing CMAP parameters from distal and proximal stimulation sites.

### 3.5.2 AANEM Consensus Criteria

The AANEM has established consensus criteria for conduction block that are high-yield board material:

**Definite Partial Conduction Block:**
- CMAP amplitude decrement: >50% between distal and proximal stimulation sites (for most nerves)
- For the tibial nerve: >60% amplitude decrement (because of normal physiological temporal dispersion in this longer nerve)
- CMAP negative peak duration increase: <30% (to exclude temporal dispersion as the cause of amplitude reduction)
- CMAP area reduction: >50%

**Probable Partial Conduction Block:**
- CMAP amplitude decrement: 40-49% (50-59% for tibial nerve)
- CMAP negative peak duration increase: <30%

**Complete Conduction Block:**
- CMAP amplitude at the proximal stimulation site is absent or nearly absent, with a normal or near-normal CMAP at the distal site

### 3.5.3 Criteria Summary Table

| Criterion | Definite CB | Probable CB |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| Amplitude drop (most nerves) | >50% | 40-49% |
| Amplitude drop (tibial nerve) | >60% | 50-59% |
| Duration increase (negative peak) | <30% | <30% |
| Area reduction | >50% | Variable |

### 3.5.4 Important Considerations

**Stimulation must be supramaximal at both sites.** Submaximal proximal stimulation will produce a falsely reduced proximal CMAP that mimics conduction block. This is a common pitfall.

**Martin-Gruber anastomosis (MGA) must be excluded.** MGA causes the median CMAP at the elbow to be larger than at the wrist -- the opposite of the expected pattern -- and can mimic conduction block on the ulnar nerve or mask conduction block on the median nerve. Always consider anomalous innervation when conduction block is suspected.

**Timing matters.** In the first 7-10 days after acute axonal injury (before Wallerian degeneration is complete), the distal nerve segment still conducts normally while the proximal segment does not conduct across the injury site. This produces a pattern that looks identical to conduction block on NCS. The distinction between acute axonal injury and true demyelinating conduction block requires follow-up studies after Wallerian degeneration has had time to occur (2-3 weeks), at which point an axonal lesion will show reduced distal CMAP amplitude, whereas true conduction block will show preserved distal CMAP.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Conduction block is the electrophysiological hallmark of acquired demyelinating neuropathies (CIDP, GBS, multifocal motor neuropathy). Its presence strongly supports an acquired rather than hereditary demyelinating process. Hereditary demyelinating neuropathies (e.g., CMT1A) show uniform slowing of conduction velocities across all nerves and segments without conduction block.

## 3.6 Antidromic vs. Orthodromic Sensory NCS 

### 3.6.1 Definitions

**Orthodromic recording**: The sensory nerve is stimulated distally (near the receptor) and recorded proximally (toward the spinal cord). This follows the physiological direction of sensory nerve impulse propagation.

**Antidromic recording**: The sensory nerve is stimulated proximally and recorded distally (near the receptor). This is opposite to the physiological direction.

### 3.6.2 Comparison

| Feature | Orthodromic | Antidromic |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Direction | Physiological (distal to proximal) | Opposite (proximal to distal) |
| SNAP amplitude | Smaller | LARGER |
| Stimulus artifact | Less interference (farther from recording) | More interference (closer to recording) |
| Volume-conducted CMAP | Not an issue | Can contaminate SNAP with motor response |
| Ease of recording | More technically challenging | Easier (larger signal) |
| Clinical utility | Used when antidromic motor contamination is a problem | Preferred for routine studies |

**Why is the antidromic SNAP larger?** When recording antidromically, the recording electrodes are placed over the digital nerves where the nerve is more superficial and the electrode is closer to the nerve. Additionally, the nerve is stimulated at a point where it is a mixed nerve with more fibers, and the recording site captures the sensory fibers at a location where the volume conductor is smaller (thin finger), producing a larger and more focused potential.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Antidromic sensory studies are preferred for routine clinical use because the larger SNAP amplitude is easier to detect and measure, particularly in patients with low-amplitude responses (neuropathy, elderly patients). However, antidromic motor volume conduction can contaminate the SNAP recording, particularly for the median and ulnar nerves. If motor contamination is suspected (the recorded potential has an unusually long duration or a late component), switching to an orthodromic technique or using digital ring electrodes can help isolate the pure sensory response.

## 3.7 Anomalous Innervation Patterns 

### 3.7.1 Martin-Gruber Anastomosis (MGA)

**Definition**: A forearm communication in which motor fibers cross from the median nerve (or its anterior interosseous branch) to the ulnar nerve. The crossover occurs in the forearm, typically in the proximal third.

**Prevalence**: 10-30% of the population based on anatomical studies; approximately 14% detected electrodiagnostically. More common unilaterally, with a right-sided predominance.

**Three Types of MGA:**

| Type | Fibers Cross From | Fibers Innervate | Prevalence (among MGA) |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Type I | Median to ulnar | Hypothenar muscles (ADM) | Most common |
| Type II | Median to ulnar | First dorsal interosseous (FDI) | Common |
| Type III | Median to ulnar | Thenar muscles (APB) | Less common |

**Electrodiagnostic Hallmarks:**

The key finding in MGA is a **paradoxical CMAP amplitude pattern** on median nerve motor studies:

- **Median CMAP at wrist < Median CMAP at elbow** (the elbow response is larger)
- This is because when stimulating at the elbow, the stimulus activates all median motor fibers, including those that will cross to the ulnar nerve in the forearm. When stimulating at the wrist, those crossover fibers have already joined the ulnar nerve and are not activated by median nerve stimulation. The elbow stimulation therefore activates more fibers innervating the thenar muscles (via both the median and the crossover fibers).

**Alternatively, the ulnar CMAP may show:**
- An initial positive deflection when recording from hypothenar muscles with median nerve stimulation at the elbow (volume-conducted from MGA fibers)
- A larger ulnar CMAP at the wrist than at the elbow in the forearm segment (the crossover fibers add to the ulnar response at the wrist)

**Diagnostic Pitfalls Created by MGA:**

1. **False conduction block on median nerve**: The larger elbow CMAP compared to wrist CMAP may be misinterpreted as proximal-to-distal conduction block
2. **False elevated ulnar CV across the elbow**: Crossover fibers adding to the CMAP at above-elbow stimulation can artificially increase the calculated ulnar CV across the elbow
3. **Confusing CTS diagnosis**: In the presence of MGA plus carpal tunnel syndrome, thenar muscles may appear relatively spared because some motor supply arrives via the ulnar nerve
4. **Missed ulnar neuropathy**: The additional fibers from MGA may compensate for ulnar axonal loss, masking the true severity

> **Clinical Pearl:** Whenever a median CMAP amplitude is higher at the elbow than at the wrist, MGA should be suspected. To confirm, stimulate the ulnar nerve at the wrist and elbow while recording from the APB (median-innervated muscle). In MGA, ulnar stimulation at the wrist will produce a response in the APB (from the crossover fibers), while ulnar stimulation above the elbow will not (or will produce a smaller response, since the crossover fibers have not yet joined the ulnar nerve at that point).

### 3.7.2 Riche-Cannieu Anastomosis (RCA)

**Definition**: A communication between the recurrent branch of the median nerve and the deep branch of the ulnar nerve within the palm (hand). This allows ulnar motor fibers to innervate muscles traditionally considered median-innervated (APB, opponens pollicis) or vice versa.

**Prevalence**: Highly variable in the literature -- 0-77% in anatomical studies, with some cadaveric studies suggesting it may be nearly universal. Electrophysiologically detected in a smaller percentage.

**Clinical Significance:**

- In patients with complete RCA, the hand may be a functional "all-ulnar hand" -- nearly all intrinsic hand muscles, including thenar muscles, receive motor innervation from the ulnar nerve
- This has major implications for carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) diagnosis and management:
  - Severe median nerve injury may not produce thenar atrophy if the ulnar nerve supplies the thenar muscles via RCA
  - CMAP recording from APB during median nerve studies may include ulnar-innervated fibers, confusing interpretation
  - The opponens pollicis (OP) is less commonly innervated by the ulnar nerve through RCA than the APB, making OP a more reliable recording target for "pure" median nerve studies

> **Clinical Pearl:** When evaluating suspected severe CTS with thenar involvement, consider recording from the opponens pollicis rather than the APB. The OP is less likely to receive ulnar innervation through Riche-Cannieu anastomosis and therefore provides a more accurate reflection of pure median motor function. If APB CMAP is preserved despite clinical evidence of severe median neuropathy, suspect ulnar contribution via RCA.

### 3.7.3 Marinacci Anastomosis

**Definition**: A communication in which motor fibers cross from the ulnar nerve to the median nerve in the forearm -- the reverse direction of MGA. This is also called the reverse Martin-Gruber anastomosis.

**Prevalence**: Much rarer than MGA.

**Electrodiagnostic finding**: Ulnar CMAP at the elbow larger than at the wrist (for ADM recording), mimicking the MGA pattern but in the opposite direction.

### 3.7.4 Accessory Deep Peroneal Nerve

**Definition**: An anomalous branch of the superficial peroneal nerve that travels behind the lateral malleolus to innervate a portion of the extensor digitorum brevis (EDB) muscle. In normal anatomy, the EDB is innervated entirely by the deep peroneal nerve.

**Prevalence**: Approximately 20-28% of the population.

**Electrodiagnostic Hallmark:**

- **Fibular (peroneal) CMAP at the ankle is SMALLER than at the fibular head**
- This is the opposite of the normal pattern (normal: distal CMAP >= proximal CMAP)
- The explanation: when stimulating at the fibular head, the stimulus activates both the deep peroneal fibers going to EDB and the accessory deep peroneal fibers (traveling via the superficial peroneal nerve behind the lateral malleolus). When stimulating at the ankle (anterior to the malleolus), only the deep peroneal fibers are activated; the accessory branch behind the lateral malleolus is not stimulated.

**Confirmation**: Stimulate behind the lateral malleolus while recording from EDB. A CMAP response confirms the presence of the accessory deep peroneal nerve. The sum of the CMAP from ankle stimulation and the CMAP from behind-the-malleolus stimulation should approximately equal the CMAP from fibular head stimulation.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The accessory deep peroneal nerve must be considered whenever the fibular CMAP is unexpectedly smaller at the ankle than at the fibular head. Failure to recognize this anomaly can lead to a false diagnosis of conduction block between the fibular head and ankle or an overestimation of axonal loss distally. Always check behind the lateral malleolus when the ankle CMAP is smaller than expected.

## 3.8 Axonal vs. Demyelinating Neuropathy Patterns 

### 3.8.1 Axonal Neuropathy Pattern

Axonal neuropathies are characterized by loss of axons themselves, with secondary loss of their myelin sheaths. The remaining axons conduct normally or nearly normally.

**NCS findings in axonal neuropathy:**
- **Reduced amplitudes** (CMAP and/or SNAP) -- this is the PRIMARY finding
- **Conduction velocities relatively preserved** (typically >75% of the lower limit of normal)
- **Distal latencies normal or mildly prolonged** (typically <130% of the upper limit of normal)
- **No conduction block**
- **No pathological temporal dispersion**
- **F-wave latencies may be mildly prolonged** (proportional to mild CV slowing)

The amplitudes are reduced because there are fewer axons contributing to the compound response. The CV is relatively preserved because the surviving axons and their myelin sheaths are intact and conduct normally.

### 3.8.2 Demyelinating Neuropathy Pattern

Demyelinating neuropathies are characterized by damage to the myelin sheath (or Schwann cells) with initial preservation of the axons. However, severe or chronic demyelination eventually leads to secondary axonal loss.

**NCS findings in demyelinating neuropathy:**
- **Prolonged distal latencies** (>130% of the upper limit of normal)
- **Slowed conduction velocities** (<75% of the lower limit of normal)
- **Conduction block** (>50% amplitude drop between distal and proximal stimulation)
- **Temporal dispersion** (>30% increase in CMAP duration)
- **Prolonged F-wave latencies** (or absent F waves)
- **Amplitudes may be preserved early**; reduced later with secondary axonal loss

### 3.8.3 Summary Comparison Table

| Parameter | Axonal | Demyelinating | Threshold |
|-----------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Amplitude | REDUCED (primary) | Normal early; reduced late | N/A |
| Conduction velocity | Normal or mildly slow | SLOW | <75% LLN = demyelinating |
| Distal latency | Normal or mildly prolonged | PROLONGED | >130% ULN = demyelinating |
| Conduction block | Absent | Present (acquired) | >50% amplitude drop |
| Temporal dispersion | Absent | Present | >30% duration increase |
| F-wave latency | Normal or mildly prolonged | PROLONGED or absent | >130% ULN = demyelinating |
| Needle EMG | Denervation; neurogenic MUAPs | May be normal early; neurogenic late | N/A |

### 3.8.4 Uniform vs. Non-Uniform Demyelination

This distinction is one of the highest-yield concepts for board examinations:

**Uniform (diffuse) demyelination** -- Hereditary demyelinating neuropathies (e.g., CMT1A):
- All nerves are similarly and symmetrically affected
- Conduction velocities are uniformly slow across all nerves and segments
- No conduction block (the demyelination is uniform, so conduction either succeeds or fails everywhere equally)
- No temporal dispersion beyond what is expected for the degree of slowing
- Very slow CVs (often <20 m/s in CMT1A)
- Absence of conduction block helps distinguish from acquired disease

**Non-uniform (multifocal) demyelination** -- Acquired demyelinating neuropathies (e.g., CIDP, GBS, MMN):
- Different nerves and segments are affected to different degrees
- Conduction velocities vary between nerves and between segments of the same nerve
- **Conduction block is present** (hallmark of acquired demyelinating neuropathy)
- Temporal dispersion is present and variable
- CVs are slow but less uniformly so than hereditary forms
- The patchy, multifocal nature reflects the immune-mediated attack on myelin at different sites

> **Board Mnemonic -- "Uniform = Hereditary; Block = Acquired":** Uniform slowing without conduction block points to hereditary demyelinating neuropathy. The presence of conduction block (with non-uniform slowing) points to acquired demyelinating neuropathy. This distinction guides the clinical workup and management -- hereditary neuropathies are managed supportively, while acquired neuropathies may respond to immunotherapy.

## 3.9 The Inching Technique 

### 3.9.1 Purpose

The inching technique (short-segment incremental study) is used to precisely localize focal nerve lesions, particularly entrapment neuropathies. It is most commonly applied to:
- Ulnar neuropathy at the elbow (to localize the site of compression along the retroepicondylar groove)
- Median neuropathy at the wrist (to localize the site of conduction slowing within the carpal tunnel)

### 3.9.2 Technique

1. Mark the skin at 1-2 cm intervals across the suspected entrapment site
2. Stimulate the nerve at each marked point, recording from the target muscle
3. Measure the latency change between each consecutive stimulation point
4. At the point of focal nerve compression, there will be an abrupt latency increase (a "step") that exceeds the expected latency change for that distance

**Normal latency change per centimeter**: approximately 0.2-0.4 ms/cm (depending on nerve and segment CV)

**Abnormal latency change**: >0.4-0.5 ms/cm (or >0.4 ms per cm in many references) at the point of entrapment indicates focal slowing

### 3.9.3 Clinical Applications

**Ulnar neuropathy at the elbow**: The inching technique can determine whether the site of compression is at the retroepicondylar groove (between the medial epicondyle and olecranon), the cubital tunnel (beneath the arcuate ligament/Osborne ligament), or the humeral-ulnar aponeurosis. This localization guides surgical decision-making.

**Median neuropathy at the wrist**: Inching across the carpal tunnel can confirm focal slowing within the tunnel and distinguish CTS from more proximal median neuropathy.

## 3.10 Comparison Studies for Entrapment Neuropathies 

### 3.10.1 Median Neuropathy at the Wrist (Carpal Tunnel Syndrome)

Several comparison techniques increase the sensitivity of NCS for CTS diagnosis:

| Comparison Study | What Is Compared | Abnormal Threshold |
|------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Median vs. ulnar sensory (digit 4) | Peak latency to digit 4 (shared innervation) | Median-ulnar difference >0.4-0.5 ms |
| Median vs. radial sensory | Peak latency at the same distance | Median-radial difference >0.5 ms |
| Median vs. ulnar motor | Distal motor latency | Median-ulnar difference >1.5-1.8 ms |
| Median vs. ulnar palmar mixed | Latency over palm-to-wrist segment | Median-ulnar difference >0.3-0.4 ms |
| Combined sensory index (CSI) | Sum of median-ulnar (digit 4) + median-ulnar (palmar) + median-radial differences | CSI >0.9-1.0 ms |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Comparison (relative) studies are more sensitive than absolute value criteria for CTS diagnosis, particularly in mild cases where absolute values may still fall within the normal range. The ring finger (digit 4) comparison is especially useful because it receives dual innervation from median and ulnar nerves, providing an internal control -- both nerves traverse the same distance, so any latency difference reflects differential slowing (i.e., median nerve slowing through the carpal tunnel).

### 3.10.2 Electrodiagnostic Grading of CTS Severity

| Grade | Findings | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Mild | Prolonged sensory latency; normal motor latency; normal SNAP amplitude | Sensory fiber demyelination only |
| Moderate | Prolonged sensory latency; prolonged motor latency; may have reduced SNAP amplitude | Motor and sensory fiber demyelination |
| Severe | Absent SNAP; prolonged motor latency or absent CMAP; fibrillation potentials in APB on EMG | Axonal loss (Wallerian degeneration) with demyelination |

## 3.11 Special Considerations in NCS Interpretation 

### 3.11.1 Age-Related Changes

Normal aging produces gradual changes in NCS parameters that must be considered during interpretation:
- SNAP amplitudes decline with age (sural SNAP may be absent in individuals >60-70 years)
- CMAP amplitudes decline modestly
- Conduction velocities decrease slightly (approximately 1-2 m/s per decade after age 40)
- H-reflex may be absent bilaterally after age 60

### 3.11.2 Height Effects

Taller individuals have longer peripheral nerves, resulting in:
- Longer absolute latencies (longer distance for impulses to travel)
- Slightly slower CVs in the lower extremities (more susceptible to length-dependent processes)
- Height-adjusted normative values are available for F-wave latencies

### 3.11.3 Technical Pitfalls

Common technical errors that can lead to misinterpretation:

1. **Submaximal stimulation**: Most common error; produces falsely low amplitudes
2. **E1 off motor point**: Produces initial CMAP positivity and reduced amplitude
3. **Co-stimulation of adjacent nerve**: Produces an unexpectedly large or polyphasic response
4. **Measurement error in distance**: Directly affects calculated CV (small errors in short segments produce large CV errors)
5. **Volume conduction**: Can contaminate SNAP recording in antidromic studies
6. **Averaging artifact**: Excessive averaging of sensory responses can include noise artifact that mimics a response

## Chapter Summary

This chapter has covered the fundamental principles and clinical applications of nerve conduction studies. The essential concepts for board preparation are:

1. **NCS parameters**: Amplitude reflects axonal integrity; CV and latency reflect myelin integrity; duration and area provide complementary information about temporal dispersion and conduction block
2. **SNAP preservation in preganglionic lesions** is the single most clinically important fact about sensory NCS
3. **Conduction block** (>50% amplitude drop with <30% duration increase) is the hallmark of acquired demyelinating neuropathy
4. **Anomalous innervations** (MGA, RCA, accessory deep peroneal nerve) must be recognized to avoid diagnostic errors
5. **Axonal vs. demyelinating patterns** and **uniform vs. non-uniform demyelination** guide the differential diagnosis of polyneuropathies
