## 11.1 Introduction: The Myopathic Electrodiagnostic Pattern [BOARD]

Myopathies are disorders of the muscle fiber itself, as opposed to neuropathies (which affect the nerve) or neuromuscular junction disorders (which affect the synapse). The electrodiagnostic evaluation of myopathy serves to confirm the presence of a myopathic process, guide muscle biopsy site selection, exclude mimicking conditions, and provide information about disease activity.

The cardinal principle of myopathic EMG is that individual motor units lose muscle fibers through necrosis, degeneration, or atrophy. Since each motor unit contains fewer functioning muscle fibers, the motor unit action potential (MUAP) becomes smaller (lower amplitude) and shorter (reduced duration). The nervous system compensates by activating more motor units earlier than expected for a given force level, producing the characteristic "early recruitment" pattern.

## 11.2 Nerve Conduction Studies in Myopathy [BOARD]

### 11.2.1 General Principles

NCS are usually normal in myopathies, and this is an important diagnostic feature. The sensory NCS should always be normal because sensory nerves and their cell bodies are unaffected by primary muscle disease. Abnormal SNAPs in a patient with suspected myopathy should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis.

**Motor NCS** are typically normal because the CMAP amplitude reflects the total number of functioning muscle fibers within the recording electrode pickup area. In most myopathies, the loss of individual muscle fibers within motor units is diffuse and partial, leaving overall muscle bulk relatively preserved early in the disease course.

### 11.2.2 Exceptions to Normal NCS

| Exception | NCS Finding | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Severe distal myopathies | Reduced CMAP amplitudes | Sufficient muscle fiber loss to reduce total fiber number under the electrode |
| Critical illness myopathy (CIM) | **Prolonged CMAP duration** (hallmark); reduced CMAP amplitude | Membrane inexcitability from sodium channel dysfunction |
| Severe end-stage myopathy | Reduced CMAP amplitudes in all muscles | Near-complete muscle fiber loss |
| Myotonic dystrophy | Low CMAP amplitudes (may be diffuse) | Combination of myopathy and membrane abnormality |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Prolonged CMAP duration is highly specific for critical illness myopathy (approaching 100% specificity when present in more than one nerve). This is one of the most important NCS findings to recognize in the ICU setting and distinguishes CIM from critical illness polyneuropathy.

### 11.2.3 AANEM Minimum Study Requirements for Myopathy [BOARD]

- Needle EMG of 2 limbs (including proximal muscles)
- 2 motor NCS and 2 sensory NCS (to exclude neuropathy and neuronopathy)
- Up to 2 repetitive nerve stimulation studies (to exclude NMJ disorder)

## 11.3 Needle EMG in Myopathy: The Classic Pattern [BOARD]

### 11.3.1 Motor Unit Action Potential (MUAP) Changes

The myopathic MUAP pattern results from loss of functioning muscle fibers within individual motor units.

**Short duration** is the single most reliable EMG feature of myopathy. Duration is measured from the initial deflection to the final return to baseline of the MUAP. In myopathy, the loss of muscle fibers at the periphery of the motor unit territory reduces the spatial and temporal extent of the electrical potential, directly shortening duration.

**Low amplitude** accompanies short duration because fewer muscle fibers contribute to the compound potential. However, amplitude is more variable than duration and can be affected by electrode position, muscle fiber hypertrophy (which may paradoxically increase amplitude in some dystrophies), and other technical factors.

**Increased polyphasia** (more than 4 phases) is common in myopathy but is a nonspecific finding also seen in neurogenic conditions, reinnervation, and normal muscle. Up to 12-15% of MUAPs in normal muscle may be polyphasic.

**Increased complexity** (increased turns within the MUAP) adds specificity when combined with short duration and low amplitude.

### Table 11.1: Myopathic vs. Neurogenic EMG Pattern

| Feature | Myopathic | Neurogenic |
|---|---|---|
| MUP duration | Short (most reliable) | Long |
| MUP amplitude | Low | High |
| Polyphasia | Increased | Increased |
| Turns | Increased | Variable |
| Recruitment | Early (full interference pattern at low force) | Decreased (rapid firing rate, few units) |
| Fibrillation potentials | Variable (present in inflammatory, dystrophies) | Present (denervation) |
| Fasciculation potentials | Rare/absent | Present in neurogenic disorders |
| Spontaneous activity | CRDs in chronic myopathies; myotonic discharges in specific conditions | Fibs, PSWs, fascs |

### 11.3.2 Recruitment Pattern [BOARD]

**Early recruitment** (also called myopathic recruitment) is the hallmark motor unit firing pattern in myopathy. Because individual motor units generate less force (fewer functioning fibers), the nervous system must activate additional motor units at a lower force threshold to achieve the desired contraction strength. The result is a full interference pattern on the EMG screen at force levels where a normal muscle would show only a partial interference pattern.

A practical way to assess recruitment is the **ratio of firing rate to number of active motor units**. Normally, a single motor unit fires at 10-15 Hz before a second unit is recruited. In myopathy, multiple units are firing at relatively low rates to generate even minimal force, producing a "full" pattern at low effort.

> **Clinical Pearl:** If a patient appears to have a full interference pattern at minimal effort, consider myopathy. If a patient has a reduced number of motor units firing at very high rates (>15-20 Hz) with minimal voluntary contraction, consider neurogenic disease. This "recruitment ratio" assessment is one of the most useful real-time clinical tools during the EMG examination.

### 11.3.3 Fibrillation Potentials in Myopathy [BOARD]

Fibrillation potentials are not exclusive to neurogenic disorders. They occur in certain myopathies and their presence carries diagnostic significance.

**Mechanism of fibrillation potentials in myopathy:** Muscle fiber necrosis or splitting can functionally disconnect segments of a muscle fiber from the endplate zone. The denervated segment becomes electrically unstable and fires spontaneously, producing fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves identical in morphology to those seen in neurogenic denervation.

**Myopathies characteristically associated with fibrillation potentials:**

| Category | Specific Conditions |
|---|---|
| Inflammatory myopathies | Dermatomyositis, polymyositis, IMNM, IBM |
| Muscular dystrophies | Duchenne, Becker, FSHD, myotonic dystrophy |
| Toxic/necrotic myopathies | Statin-induced rhabdomyolysis, necrotizing myopathy |
| Metabolic myopathies | Acid maltase deficiency (Pompe disease) |
| Critical illness myopathy | Active muscle fiber necrosis |
| Infectious myopathies | Viral myositis, trichinosis |

**Myopathies typically WITHOUT fibrillation potentials:**
- Steroid myopathy (type II fiber atrophy without necrosis)
- Endocrine myopathies (thyroid, Cushing)
- Congenital myopathies (most)
- Metabolic myopathies (McArdle, carnitine deficiency)
- Periodic paralysis (between attacks)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The presence of fibrillation potentials in a myopathy generally indicates active muscle fiber necrosis or inflammation. Fibrillations in a myopathic background help distinguish inflammatory myopathies and active dystrophies from chronic non-inflammatory myopathies. Their absence does not exclude myopathy.

## 11.4 Myotonic Discharges [BOARD]

### 11.4.1 Description

Myotonic discharges are repetitive firing of single muscle fibers characterized by **waxing and waning** of both frequency (20-100 Hz) and amplitude. This produces the classic "dive-bomber" sound on the loudspeaker. The discharges are triggered by needle insertion, voluntary contraction, or mechanical percussion.

**Mechanism:** Myotonic discharges result from instability of the muscle fiber membrane, typically due to dysfunction of chloride channels (ClC-1) or sodium channels (Nav1.4). The altered membrane properties cause the muscle fiber to fire repetitively after an initial depolarization, with progressive changes in the discharge rate producing the characteristic waxing and waning pattern.

### 11.4.2 Conditions Associated with Myotonic Discharges

| Condition | Gene/Channel | Inheritance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Myotonic dystrophy type 1 (DM1) | DMPK (CTG repeat); RNA gain-of-function affecting chloride channel | AD | Distal weakness; facial weakness; cataracts; cardiac conduction defects |
| Myotonic dystrophy type 2 (DM2) | ZNF9/CNBP (CCTG repeat) | AD | Proximal weakness; myalgia; less severe than DM1 |
| Myotonia congenita (Thomsen/Becker) | CLCN1 (chloride channel) | AD or AR | Myotonia without weakness; muscle hypertrophy |
| Paramyotonia congenita | SCN4A (sodium channel) | AD | Cold-exacerbated myotonia; paradoxical myotonia (worsens with activity) |
| Hyperkalemic periodic paralysis | SCN4A (sodium channel) | AD | Episodic weakness with elevated potassium; myotonia between attacks |
| Acid maltase deficiency (Pompe disease) | GAA gene (acid alpha-glucosidase) | AR | Paraspinal myotonic discharges; proximal weakness; respiratory failure |
| Statin-induced myotonia | (acquired) | -- | Rare; associated with myopathic symptoms during statin use |

### 11.4.3 Complex Repetitive Discharges (CRDs)

CRDs must be distinguished from myotonic discharges. CRDs fire at a **uniform** (non-waxing/waning) frequency, typically 5-100 Hz, with an abrupt onset and cessation. They are produced by ephaptic (direct) spread of action potentials between adjacent muscle fibers. CRDs are nonspecific and may be seen in chronic myopathies, chronic neuropathies, radiculopathies, and even normal muscle (iliopsoas).

| Feature | Myotonic Discharges | Complex Repetitive Discharges |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency pattern | Waxing and waning | Uniform (constant) |
| Sound | "Dive-bomber" | "Motorcycle idling" or "jackhammer" |
| Onset/offset | Gradual | Abrupt |
| Pathophysiology | Membrane instability (channelopathy) | Ephaptic transmission between fibers |
| Specificity | Suggests specific diagnoses (above list) | Nonspecific (chronic myopathy or neuropathy) |

## 11.5 Quantitative EMG Analysis in Myopathy [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 11.5.1 Manual Quantitative Analysis

While qualitative assessment (visual and auditory) of MUAPs is adequate for experienced electromyographers, quantitative analysis provides objective data, particularly valuable for research, longitudinal monitoring, and equivocal cases.

**Parameters measured quantitatively:**
- **Duration:** Most reliable; measured from initial deflection to final return to baseline. Normal values vary by muscle and age (reference tables available in standard textbooks). Duration <20% below the mean for that muscle indicates myopathic change.
- **Amplitude:** Peak-to-peak amplitude of the MUAP. Less reliable due to variability with electrode position. Low amplitude supports myopathy.
- **Phases:** Number of baseline crossings plus one. Normal MUAPs have 2-4 phases. >4 phases = polyphasic. Up to 12-15% polyphasic MUAPs are normal.
- **Turns:** Direction changes within the MUAP that do not cross the baseline. Increased turns suggest increased complexity.
- **Area-to-amplitude ratio (size index):** Helps distinguish myopathic from neurogenic patterns.

### 11.5.2 Automated MUAP Analysis

Modern EMG machines offer automated MUAP analysis algorithms (multi-MUAP analysis, decomposition-based quantitative EMG). These systems isolate individual MUAPs during low-level voluntary contraction and calculate duration, amplitude, phases, and turns for multiple motor units.

**Advantages:** Objective, reproducible, allows statistical comparison with reference values.
**Limitations:** Requires patient cooperation; complex MUAPs in myopathies may be difficult for algorithms to isolate; cannot replace clinical judgment.

### 11.5.3 Turns-Amplitude Analysis (Cloud Analysis)

This semi-quantitative technique analyzes the interference pattern during moderate force contraction by measuring the number of turns per second and the mean amplitude between turns. Normal values form a "cloud" on a turns-amplitude plot. Myopathic patterns shift the cloud toward increased turns with decreased amplitude (many small motor units firing). Neurogenic patterns shift toward decreased turns with increased amplitude (fewer but larger motor units).

## 11.6 Muscular Dystrophies [BOARD]

### 11.5.1 Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) and Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)

**Genetics and Pathophysiology:**

Both DMD and BMD result from mutations in the dystrophin gene (DMD gene) located on chromosome Xp21, the largest gene in the human genome. Dystrophin is a structural protein that anchors the intracellular cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix through the dystrophin-associated glycoprotein complex. Its absence (DMD) or reduction/truncation (BMD) leads to membrane fragility, calcium influx, and progressive muscle fiber necrosis.

- **DMD:** Frameshift or nonsense mutations producing absent dystrophin. Onset age 2-5 years; loss of ambulation by age 12; death in 20s-30s without treatment.
- **BMD:** In-frame deletions producing reduced quantity or truncated but partially functional dystrophin. Later onset; milder course; ambulation may be preserved into 20s or later.

Both are X-linked recessive, affecting almost exclusively males.

**EDX findings:**

| Feature | DMD | BMD |
|---|---|---|
| NCS | Normal early; CMAP may decrease late | Usually normal |
| MUAP duration | Short | Short (may be less prominent) |
| MUAP amplitude | Low | Low to normal |
| Recruitment | Early (myopathic) | Early (myopathic) |
| Fibrillation potentials | Present (active necrosis) | May be present |
| CRDs | May be present in chronic stage | May be present |
| CK level | Massively elevated (50-200x normal) | Moderately elevated (5-50x normal) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** In DMD/BMD, the CK level is typically massively elevated and is one of the most striking laboratory findings. CK elevations precede clinical symptoms and can be detected at birth. EMG is often not the primary diagnostic tool (genetic testing confirms diagnosis), but it helps exclude alternative diagnoses when the presentation is atypical.

### 11.5.2 Myotonic Dystrophy [BOARD]

Myotonic dystrophy is the most common adult muscular dystrophy, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 8,000. Two forms are recognized.

**Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1 (DM1, Steinert Disease):**

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Genetics | CTG trinucleotide repeat expansion in DMPK gene (chromosome 19q13.3). Normal: 5-34 repeats; affected: 50 to >2000 repeats |
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant with **anticipation** (earlier onset and greater severity in successive generations) |
| Weakness pattern | **Distal** predominant (grip weakness, foot drop); facial weakness (myopathic facies) |
| Myotonia | Prominent; grip myotonia, percussion myotonia |
| Systemic features | Cataracts (posterior subcapsular), cardiac conduction defects (first-degree AV block, bundle branch block), insulin resistance, testicular atrophy, cognitive impairment, hypersomnia |
| EMG | Myotonic discharges (prominent); myopathic MUAPs (short duration, low amplitude) |
| Congenital form | Severe neonatal form with maternal transmission; hypotonia, respiratory failure, intellectual disability |

**Myotonic Dystrophy Type 2 (DM2, PROMM -- Proximal Myotonic Myopathy):**

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Genetics | CCTG tetranucleotide repeat expansion in CNBP/ZNF9 gene (chromosome 3q21) |
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant; NO anticipation (unlike DM1) |
| Weakness pattern | **Proximal** predominant (hip flexors, neck flexors); contrast to distal pattern in DM1 |
| Myotonia | Less prominent than DM1; may be subclinical |
| Systemic features | Similar to DM1 but generally milder; cataracts, cardiac involvement less common |
| EMG | Myotonic discharges (less prominent than DM1); myopathic MUAPs |
| Key clinical clue | Myalgia and stiffness often dominate the clinical picture |

### Table 11.2: DM1 vs. DM2 Comparison

| Feature | DM1 | DM2 |
|---|---|---|
| Repeat type | CTG (trinucleotide) | CCTG (tetranucleotide) |
| Gene | DMPK (chr 19) | CNBP/ZNF9 (chr 3) |
| Weakness distribution | Distal | Proximal |
| Anticipation | Yes (prominent) | No |
| Congenital form | Yes | No |
| Myotonia severity | Prominent | Mild/subclinical |
| Pain | Less prominent | Prominent (myalgia) |
| Biopsy fiber type | Type I atrophy | Type II atrophy |
| Severity | More severe | Less severe |

### 11.5.3 Facioscapulohumeral Dystrophy (FSHD) [BOARD]

FSHD is the third most common muscular dystrophy. It has two genetic forms: FSHD1 (95% of cases) results from contraction of the D4Z4 macrosatellite repeat on chromosome 4q35, leading to de-repression of the DUX4 gene. FSHD2 involves mutations in SMCHD1 or DNMT3B with permissive D4Z4 haplotype.

**Clinical pattern:** Distinctive descending pattern of weakness -- facial muscles first (inability to whistle, inability to close eyes during sleep), then scapular stabilizers (scapular winging), then humeral muscles (deltoid, biceps), then anterior tibial (foot drop), then hip girdle.

**EDX findings:**
- EMG shows myopathic MUAPs in clinically affected muscles
- Fibrillation potentials may be present, particularly in actively degenerating muscles
- Distribution of EMG abnormalities is characteristically asymmetric
- NCS are normal
- Some FSHD patients show a mixed myopathic-neurogenic pattern in very chronic disease

---

### 11.5.4 Oculopharyngeal Muscular Dystrophy (OPMD) [COMPREHENSIVE]

OPMD is an autosomal dominant late-onset muscular dystrophy caused by a GCN trinucleotide repeat expansion in the PABPN1 gene (chromosome 14q11). Normal alleles have 6 GCN repeats; pathogenic alleles have 8-13 repeats.

**Clinical features:**
- Onset typically in the 5th-6th decade
- Progressive ptosis (often the presenting symptom) and dysphagia
- Proximal limb weakness develops later
- French-Canadian and Bukhara Jewish populations have higher prevalence

**EDX findings:**
- Myopathic MUAPs in proximal limb muscles
- May show involvement of pharyngeal muscles (if evaluated)
- NCS are normal
- CK is normal or mildly elevated

### 11.5.5 Emery-Dreifuss Muscular Dystrophy [COMPREHENSIVE]

Emery-Dreifuss dystrophy is characterized by the triad of early contractures (elbow flexors, Achilles tendons, posterior cervical muscles), slowly progressive humeroperoneal weakness (upper arm and lower leg), and cardiac conduction defects (which can be life-threatening). It can be X-linked (EMD gene, emerin deficiency) or autosomal dominant (LMNA gene, lamin A/C deficiency). Cardiac involvement mandates screening with ECG and echocardiography; pacemaker implantation may be lifesaving. EMG shows myopathic changes in affected muscles.

## 11.7 Inflammatory Myopathies: The Five-Subtype Classification [BOARD]

The inflammatory myopathies (idiopathic inflammatory myopathies, IIMs) are a group of autoimmune conditions characterized by muscle inflammation and weakness. The modern classification recognizes five distinct subtypes based on clinical features, autoantibody profiles, and pathological findings.

### 11.6.1 Dermatomyositis (DM) [BOARD]

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Pathophysiology | Complement-mediated microvasculopathy with immune complex deposition in intramuscular capillaries |
| Biopsy hallmark | **Perifascicular atrophy** (pathognomonic) |
| Skin findings | Heliotrope rash (eyelids), Gottron papules (knuckles), V-sign, shawl sign |
| Weakness | Proximal symmetric; may be mild or absent in amyopathic DM |
| CK | Elevated (5-50x normal) |
| Malignancy risk | Increased (~15-25%), especially in adults; cancer screening recommended |
| Key antibodies | Anti-Mi-2, anti-TIF1-gamma (cancer association), anti-NXP2 (cancer association), anti-MDA5 (ILD, amyopathic DM) |

### 11.6.2 Polymyositis (PM) [BOARD]

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Pathophysiology | CD8+ T cell-mediated direct muscle fiber injury |
| Biopsy hallmark | Endomysial inflammatory infiltrate invading non-necrotic fibers (MHC-I upregulation) |
| Weakness | Proximal symmetric |
| CK | Elevated (5-50x normal) |
| Important note | Diagnosis of exclusion; many previously classified PM cases are now reclassified as IMNM or antisynthetase syndrome |

### 11.6.3 Antisynthetase Syndrome (ASyS) [BOARD]

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Pathophysiology | Autoimmune syndrome associated with anti-aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase antibodies |
| Key antibodies | Anti-Jo-1 (most common; ~20-30% of IIM), anti-PL-7, anti-PL-12, anti-EJ, anti-OJ |
| Clinical triad | Myositis + interstitial lung disease (ILD) + inflammatory arthritis |
| Additional features | Mechanic's hands (cracked, roughened skin on fingers), Raynaud phenomenon, fever |
| CK | Mildly to moderately elevated |

### 11.6.4 Immune-Mediated Necrotizing Myopathy (IMNM) [BOARD]

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Pathophysiology | Macrophage-predominant infiltrate with myophagocytosis; minimal lymphocytic inflammation |
| Key antibodies | **Anti-SRP** (signal recognition particle) -- severe, rapid course; **Anti-HMGCR** -- associated with statin exposure |
| CK | Markedly elevated (20-50x normal; often highest of all IIMs) |
| Clinical | Rapid onset severe proximal weakness; may present acutely like rhabdomyolysis |
| EMG | Prominent irritability (fibs/PSWs) with myopathic MUAPs; may show more severe changes than DM/PM |
| Statin relationship | Anti-HMGCR IMNM occurs in ~2/3 of cases with statin exposure, but also occurs without statins. Disease does not resolve with statin discontinuation (requires immunotherapy) |

### 11.6.5 Sporadic Inclusion Body Myositis (sIBM) [BOARD]

IBM is discussed in detail in Section 11.7.

### Table 11.3: Inflammatory Myopathy Subtype Comparison

| Feature | DM | PM | ASyS | IMNM | IBM |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Age of onset | Any (children + adults) | Adults | Adults | Adults | >45 years |
| Weakness | Proximal | Proximal | Proximal | Proximal (severe) | Proximal LE + distal UE |
| CK | 5-50x | 5-50x | Mild-moderate | 20-50x (highest) | Mild-moderate (1-15x) |
| Skin findings | Yes | No | Mechanic's hands | No | No |
| ILD | Variable | Rare | Common | Rare | No |
| Biopsy | Perifascicular atrophy | Endomysial CD8+ T cells | Perimysial pathology | Necrosis + macrophages | Rimmed vacuoles + endomysial inflammation |
| Response to immunotherapy | Good | Variable | Variable | Requires aggressive treatment | Poor (treatment-resistant) |
| Malignancy association | Yes (15-25%) | Possible | Lower | Anti-HMGCR: low; anti-SRP: low | No |

### 11.6.6 EDX Findings in Inflammatory Myopathies [BOARD]

The EMG pattern is similar across the inflammatory subtypes (except IBM):

- **Insertional activity:** Increased (reflecting membrane instability from active inflammation/necrosis)
- **Spontaneous activity:** Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves in actively inflamed/necrotic muscles; correlate with disease activity
- **MUAPs:** Short duration, low amplitude, polyphasic (classic myopathic pattern)
- **Recruitment:** Early (full interference pattern at low force)
- **Distribution:** Proximal muscles (deltoid, biceps, iliopsoas, vastus lateralis) are most affected; EMG should sample both proximal and distal muscles, both affected and unaffected sides

**The EMG helps guide biopsy site selection:** Target a moderately affected muscle (one showing EMG abnormalities but not end-stage changes). Avoid the needle EMG site itself for biopsy, as needle insertion can produce artifact on biopsy.

## 11.7 Inclusion Body Myositis: 2024 ENMC Criteria and the Mixed EMG Pattern [BOARD]

### 11.7.1 Clinical Features

IBM is the most common inflammatory myopathy in patients over age 50 and the most common acquired myopathy in individuals over age 50 overall. It has several features that distinguish it from other inflammatory myopathies:

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Age of onset | Typically >45 years (usually >50); male predominance (~3:1) |
| Weakness distribution | **Finger flexors** (FDP, particularly FDP to index and middle fingers) AND **knee extensors** (quadriceps) -- this combination is highly characteristic |
| Asymmetry | Often asymmetric, especially early |
| Progression | Slowly progressive over years; typically more insidious than other IIMs |
| CK | Mildly elevated (typically 1-15x normal, often <1000 IU/L) |
| Dysphagia | Common (up to 60%); may be the presenting symptom |
| Response to immunotherapy | **Poor** -- characteristically resistant to corticosteroids and conventional immunosuppressants |

### 11.7.2 The 2024 ENMC Revised Diagnostic Criteria [BOARD]

The 272nd ENMC International Workshop (June 2023, published 2024) revised the previous 2013 criteria for IBM diagnosis. The updated criteria use a two-step approach:

**Step 1: Clinical presentation assessment**
- Age of onset typically >45 years
- Progressive weakness over at least 12 months
- Preferential involvement of finger flexors and/or knee extensors
- May also involve ankle dorsiflexors and hip flexors

**Step 2: Confirmatory investigations**
- Muscle biopsy (gold standard): Rimmed vacuoles, endomysial inflammation with invasion of non-necrotic fibers, protein aggregates (TDP-43, p62)
- Serological marker: Anti-cN1A (cytosolic 5'-nucleotidase 1A) antibodies -- present in approximately 33-76% of IBM patients; specificity varies
- Imaging: Muscle MRI/ultrasound showing preferential involvement of quadriceps and forearm flexor compartment

### 11.7.3 The Mixed EMG Pattern in IBM [BOARD]

IBM has a unique EMG pattern that differs from other inflammatory myopathies:

**Mixed myopathic and neurogenic MUAPs:** Both short-duration, low-amplitude (myopathic) and long-duration, high-amplitude (neurogenic) MUAPs coexist in the same muscle. This mixed pattern is seen in approximately one-third of IBM patients and, when present, is highly suggestive of the diagnosis.

**The explanation for the mixed pattern:**
- Myopathic MUAPs: Reflect ongoing muscle fiber loss within motor units (necrosis, vacuolar degeneration)
- Neurogenic MUAPs: Reflect chronic reinnervation as surviving motor neurons sprout to compensate for fiber loss, and possibly reflect a concurrent neurogenic component unique to IBM

**Other EMG features:**
- Fibrillation potentials: Present in virtually all IBM patients (reflecting active muscle fiber necrosis)
- Distribution: Proximal lower extremity (quadriceps) AND distal upper extremity (forearm finger flexors) -- matching the clinical pattern
- CRDs: May be present in chronically affected muscles

> **Clinical Pearl:** If you see a mixed myopathic-neurogenic EMG pattern in a patient over 50 with finger flexor and quadriceps weakness, IBM should be at the top of the differential. The mixed pattern is not pathognomonic (it can also be seen in some chronic neurogenic conditions) but is highly suggestive in the appropriate clinical context. The key biopsy finding is rimmed vacuoles with endomysial inflammation.

## 11.8 Limb-Girdle Muscular Dystrophies [COMPREHENSIVE]

The limb-girdle muscular dystrophies (LGMDs) are a heterogeneous group of inherited myopathies characterized by proximal limb-girdle weakness. The current nomenclature (revised 2018) uses "LGMD R" for autosomal recessive and "LGMD D" for autosomal dominant forms, followed by a number and the gene/protein name.

### Common LGMD Subtypes

| Current Name | Former Name | Gene/Protein | Inheritance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LGMD R1 calpain3 | LGMD2A | CAPN3 (calpain-3) | AR | Most common LGMD worldwide; scapular winging |
| LGMD R2 dysferlin | LGMD2B | DYSF (dysferlin) | AR | Miyoshi myopathy variant (distal); very high CK |
| LGMD R3-R6 | LGMD2C-F | Sarcoglycans (alpha-delta) | AR | Sarcoglycanopathies; DMD-like phenotype |
| LGMD R9 FKRP | LGMD2I | FKRP (fukutin-related protein) | AR | Common in Northern Europeans; cardiac involvement |
| LGMD R12 anoctamin5 | LGMD2L | ANO5 (anoctamin-5) | AR | Common in Northern Europeans; often asymmetric |
| LGMD D1 DNAJB6 | LGMD1D | DNAJB6 | AD | Late onset; distal involvement possible |

**EDX findings in LGMD:**
- Short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs in proximal muscles
- Early recruitment pattern
- Fibrillation potentials may be present (especially in dysferlinopathy, which can show prominent irritability mimicking inflammatory myopathy)
- NCS are normal
- CK levels vary by subtype (very high in dysferlinopathy, moderate in calpainopathy)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Dysferlinopathy (LGMD R2) can closely mimic polymyositis on clinical and EDX grounds -- proximal weakness with elevated CK and fibrillation potentials on EMG. A young patient with a "polymyositis" phenotype who does not respond to immunotherapy should be tested for dysferlin deficiency. Biopsy shows necrotic and regenerating fibers with minimal inflammation.

## 11.9 Metabolic Myopathies [BOARD]

### 11.8.1 McArdle Disease (Glycogen Storage Disease Type V, Myophosphorylase Deficiency) [BOARD]

McArdle disease results from deficiency of myophosphorylase, the enzyme responsible for glycogen breakdown in muscle. Patients cannot utilize muscle glycogen for energy during exercise.

**Clinical features:**
- Exercise intolerance with myalgia, contractures, and myoglobinuria (dark urine after exertion)
- "Second wind" phenomenon: improvement in exercise tolerance after 6-10 minutes of continued activity (as blood-borne glucose and free fatty acids become available for energy)
- Fixed proximal weakness may develop with age

**EDX hallmark finding -- Forearm exercise testing:**
- During the ischemic forearm exercise test (or non-ischemic variant), the patient cannot generate lactate from glycogen metabolism
- **Electrical silence during contracture:** When a McArdle patient develops a muscle contracture during forearm exercise, the EMG shows electrical silence. This distinguishes a metabolic contracture (no electrical activity, caused by failure of calcium reuptake due to ATP depletion) from a true muscle cramp (which shows high-frequency motor unit firing)

**Baseline EMG findings:**
- May be normal at rest in early disease
- Fibrillation potentials may be present in patients with fixed weakness
- Myopathic MUAPs in chronically affected muscles

### 11.8.2 Pompe Disease (Glycogen Storage Disease Type II, Acid Maltase Deficiency) [BOARD]

Pompe disease is caused by deficiency of acid alpha-glucosidase (GAA), leading to lysosomal glycogen accumulation in multiple tissues, particularly skeletal and cardiac muscle.

**Three clinical forms:**
- **Infantile-onset:** Severe; hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, hypotonia, death in first year without treatment
- **Late-onset (juvenile/adult):** Progressive proximal weakness, respiratory muscle involvement (diaphragm weakness may precede limb weakness), no cardiac involvement
- **Treatment:** Enzyme replacement therapy (alglucosidase alfa, avalglucosidase alfa) has transformed outcomes for all forms

**EDX hallmark finding:**

- **Myotonic discharges in paraspinal muscles** are a highly characteristic finding in Pompe disease and should prompt testing for GAA enzyme activity or GAA gene mutation analysis
- Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves in proximal muscles and paraspinal muscles
- Myopathic MUAPs (short duration, low amplitude)
- CRDs may be present

> **Clinical Pearl:** Myotonic discharges in the paraspinal muscles of a patient with proximal weakness and respiratory insufficiency should raise immediate suspicion for Pompe disease. This is a treatable condition (enzyme replacement therapy), making early recognition critically important.

### 11.8.3 Mitochondrial Myopathies [COMPREHENSIVE]

Mitochondrial myopathies result from dysfunction of the mitochondrial respiratory chain, caused by mutations in either mitochondrial DNA (maternally inherited) or nuclear DNA (various inheritance patterns). Examples include Kearns-Sayre syndrome, MELAS, MERRF, and chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia (CPEO).

**EDX findings:**
- Often show myopathic MUAPs, but findings may be subtle or nonspecific
- NCS may show concurrent peripheral neuropathy (mitochondrial dysfunction can affect both muscle and nerve)
- Diagnosis relies primarily on muscle biopsy (ragged red fibers, COX-negative fibers), genetic testing, and elevated serum lactate

## 11.9 Lipid Storage and Endocrine Myopathies [COMPREHENSIVE]

### Carnitine Palmitoyltransferase II (CPT-II) Deficiency

CPT-II deficiency is the most common lipid storage myopathy. It is autosomal recessive (S113L mutation most common) and presents in young adults with recurrent episodes of rhabdomyolysis triggered by prolonged exercise, fasting, cold exposure, or illness. Between episodes, CK and EMG may be completely normal. During episodes, CK is markedly elevated with fibrillation potentials in affected muscles. No fixed myopathy develops in most patients. Diagnosis is by acylcarnitine profile and genetic testing.

### Endocrine Myopathies

Endocrine disorders frequently cause myopathy:

| Endocrine Disorder | Myopathy Features | CK | EMG |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypothyroidism | Proximal weakness, myalgia, delayed relaxation | Mildly elevated (1-5x) | Often normal; may show myopathic MUAPs |
| Hyperthyroidism | Proximal weakness, weight loss | Usually normal | Myopathic MUAPs in some |
| Cushing syndrome | Proximal weakness (type II atrophy) | Normal | Often normal (type II fibers not well sampled) |
| Hyperparathyroidism | Proximal weakness, fatigue | Normal | Usually normal |
| Vitamin D deficiency | Proximal weakness, bone pain | Normal or mildly elevated | Usually normal; type II fiber atrophy on biopsy |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hypothyroidism is one of the few metabolic conditions that may elevate CK substantially. Always check TSH in any patient with unexplained myopathy and elevated CK, as thyroid myopathy is readily treatable.

## 11.10 Periodic Paralysis [BOARD]

The periodic paralyses are channelopathies characterized by episodic weakness due to shifts in serum potassium and/or membrane dysfunction.

| Feature | Hypokalemic PP | Hyperkalemic PP |
|---|---|---|
| Gene | CACNA1S (calcium channel, most common) or SCN4A (sodium channel) | SCN4A (sodium channel) |
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant | Autosomal dominant |
| Trigger | Carbohydrate-rich meals, rest after exercise, stress | Fasting, rest after exercise, potassium intake |
| K+ during attack | Low (<3.5 mEq/L) | High (>5.0 mEq/L) or normal |
| Attack duration | Hours to days | Minutes to hours |
| Myotonia | Absent | Present between attacks |
| EMG during attack | Electrically silent (non-excitable membrane) | Electrically silent during severe weakness |
| EMG between attacks | Usually normal; may show myopathic MUAPs if fixed weakness has developed | Myotonic discharges may be present |
| Fixed myopathy | May develop over time | May develop over time |

**Exercise testing (prolonged exercise protocol):**
A specialized electrodiagnostic protocol can help diagnose periodic paralysis. The CMAP amplitude is monitored during and after a prolonged exercise period. In periodic paralysis, the CMAP amplitude may decline significantly (>40%) in the post-exercise period, reflecting the membrane depolarization and inexcitability that underlies attacks.

## 11.10 Steroid Myopathy [BOARD]

Steroid myopathy (corticosteroid-induced myopathy) is the most common drug-induced myopathy and a frequent clinical concern in patients receiving chronic glucocorticoid therapy.

**Pathophysiology:** Glucocorticoids preferentially cause atrophy of type II (fast-twitch) muscle fibers through increased proteolysis and decreased protein synthesis.

**Clinical features:**
- Proximal weakness (hip flexors > shoulder girdle)
- Insidious onset over weeks to months of steroid use
- Fluorinated steroids (dexamethasone, triamcinolone) > non-fluorinated (prednisone, prednisolone)
- CK is typically **normal** (no muscle fiber necrosis)

**EDX findings:**
- **EMG may be normal** in many patients because type II fiber atrophy does not necessarily produce detectable MUAP changes on routine EMG (standard concentric needle EMG is biased toward type I fibers during low-level voluntary activation)
- When abnormal: mild myopathic MUAPs in proximal muscles
- **No fibrillation potentials** (no active necrosis)
- NCS normal

> **Clinical Pearl:** Steroid myopathy is a clinical diagnosis supported by the context (chronic steroid use, proximal weakness, normal CK). A normal EMG does not exclude the diagnosis. The diagnostic challenge arises when a patient receiving steroids for an inflammatory myopathy develops worsening weakness -- is it inadequate treatment (undertreated myositis) or steroid toxicity? Active myositis shows fibrillation potentials and elevated CK; steroid myopathy shows neither.

## 11.11 Critical Illness Myopathy (CIM) [BOARD]

### 11.11.1 Pathophysiology

CIM is an acute myopathy occurring in critically ill patients, particularly those exposed to high-dose corticosteroids and/or neuromuscular blocking agents. It is characterized by thick filament (myosin) loss on muscle biopsy and sodium channel dysfunction producing membrane inexcitability.

### 11.11.2 EDX Features

| Parameter | Finding | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| CMAP amplitude | Reduced | Reflects reduced muscle fiber excitability and necrosis |
| **CMAP duration** | **Prolonged** | **Hallmark finding; ~100% specificity if >1 nerve** |
| Conduction velocity | Normal | Myelin is not affected |
| SNAP | **Normal** | Critical differentiator from CIP |
| F-waves | Normal | Motor neurons and nerve roots are intact |
| EMG (if cooperative) | Myopathic MUAPs with early recruitment | Classic myopathic pattern |
| Fibrillation potentials | May be present | Reflects active necrosis |

### 11.11.3 CIM vs. CIP vs. CINM (Critical Illness Neuromyopathy) -- Comprehensive Comparison

| Feature | CIM | CIP | CINM |
|---|---|---|---|
| CMAP amplitude | Reduced | Reduced | Reduced |
| CMAP duration | **Prolonged** | Normal | Prolonged |
| SNAP amplitude | **Normal** | **Reduced** | Reduced |
| Direct muscle stimulation | Reduced | Normal | Variable |
| Prognosis | Better | Variable | Variable |
| Biopsy | Thick filament loss | Normal muscle; nerve shows axonal loss | Features of both |

**Direct muscle stimulation (DMS):** An advanced technique useful in uncooperative ICU patients. A needle electrode stimulates the muscle directly (bypassing the nerve). In CIM, the direct-stimulated CMAP is also reduced (confirming muscle membrane dysfunction). In CIP, the direct-stimulated CMAP is normal (confirming the problem is in the nerve, not the muscle).

## 11.12 Toxic and Drug-Induced Myopathies [BOARD]

### 11.12.1 Statin Myopathy

Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) are the most commonly prescribed medications causing myopathic symptoms. The spectrum of statin-related muscle disease ranges from myalgias without CK elevation (most common) to severe rhabdomyolysis (rare).

| Severity | CK Level | Symptoms | EDX Findings | Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Myalgia | Normal | Muscle pain, cramps | Normal | Reassurance; consider switching statin |
| Myopathy | 3-10x normal | Pain + weakness | Myopathic MUAPs possible; usually normal | Discontinue statin; monitor CK |
| Necrotizing myopathy | >10x normal | Significant weakness | Myopathic MUAPs; fibrillation potentials | Discontinue statin; may need immunotherapy if anti-HMGCR+ |
| Rhabdomyolysis | >50x normal | Severe pain, dark urine | Active myopathic changes | ICU care; IV fluids; discontinue statin |

**Important distinction:** Anti-HMGCR antibody-positive immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy (IMNM) is an autoimmune condition triggered by but not caused by statins. It does NOT resolve with statin discontinuation and requires immunosuppressive therapy. Testing for anti-HMGCR antibodies should be performed in any patient with persistent or worsening myopathy after statin withdrawal.

### 11.12.2 Other Drug-Induced Myopathies

| Medication | Type of Myopathy | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Colchicine | Vacuolar myopathy + neuropathy | Proximal weakness; combined neuromyopathy pattern on EMG |
| Chloroquine/hydroxychloroquine | Vacuolar myopathy | Slowly progressive; curvilinear bodies on biopsy |
| Zidovudine (AZT) | Mitochondrial myopathy | Ragged red fibers on biopsy; elevated CK |
| Immune checkpoint inhibitors | Inflammatory myopathy | Can cause MG, myositis, or myocarditis; may be fulminant |
| D-penicillamine | Inflammatory myopathy (can trigger PM/DM) | Also can trigger autoimmune MG |
| Fibrates | Myopathy (similar to statins) | Risk increases with statin co-administration |
| Alcohol | Acute or chronic myopathy | Acute rhabdomyolysis or chronic proximal weakness |
| Corticosteroids | Type II fiber atrophy | See Section 11.10 |

### 11.12.3 Alcohol-Related Myopathy [COMPREHENSIVE]

Alcohol affects muscle through both acute and chronic mechanisms:

**Acute alcoholic myopathy (rhabdomyolysis):**
- Binge drinking episode followed by severe muscle pain, swelling, weakness
- Markedly elevated CK (often >10,000 IU/L)
- Risk of myoglobinuric renal failure
- EMG shows fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves, myopathic MUAPs in affected muscles
- Recovery over weeks to months with abstinence

**Chronic alcoholic myopathy:**
- Insidious proximal weakness and atrophy over months to years of heavy alcohol use
- CK may be normal or mildly elevated
- Affects up to 50% of chronic alcoholics
- EMG: myopathic MUAPs in proximal muscles; fibrillation potentials variable
- Partial reversibility with abstinence and nutritional rehabilitation

## 11.13 Congenital Myopathies [COMPREHENSIVE]

Congenital myopathies are a group of genetically determined muscle disorders defined by characteristic structural abnormalities on muscle biopsy. They typically present in infancy or childhood with hypotonia and weakness.

| Type | Biopsy Finding | Gene | Inheritance | Key Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nemaline myopathy | Nemaline rods (Z-disc origin) | NEB, ACTA1, others | AR or AD | Facial weakness, respiratory insufficiency |
| Central core disease | Central cores (lack of mitochondria) | RYR1 | AD | Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility |
| Centronuclear/myotubular | Central nuclei | MTM1 (X-linked), DNM2, BIN1 | X-linked or AD/AR | X-linked form severe; AD forms milder |
| Fiber type disproportion | Type I fibers small and predominant | Multiple genes | Variable | Diagnosis of exclusion |

**EDX findings in congenital myopathies:**
- Mild myopathic MUAPs or normal EMG (EMG may be entirely normal in mild cases)
- Fibrillation potentials usually absent (no active necrosis in most subtypes)
- NCS normal
- CK is typically normal or only mildly elevated

**Central core disease and malignant hyperthermia susceptibility:** This association is critical for anesthetic safety. RYR1 mutations cause both central core disease and susceptibility to malignant hyperthermia. All patients with central core disease (and their at-risk family members) must be counseled about the risk of volatile anesthetic agents and succinylcholine. A medical alert bracelet or pre-operative communication is essential.

**Nemaline myopathy:** The most common severe congenital myopathy. Nemaline rods are composed of alpha-actinin and other Z-disc proteins. Severity ranges from severe neonatal forms (fatal respiratory failure) to mild adult-onset forms with slowly progressive proximal weakness. Respiratory insufficiency is a major concern across all subtypes.

**Centronuclear/myotubular myopathy:** The X-linked form (MTM1 mutations, myotubularin) is the most severe, presenting with profound neonatal hypotonia and respiratory failure. Autosomal dominant forms (DNM2 mutations) present later in childhood or adulthood with milder weakness and are more compatible with long-term survival.

## 11.13 The Approach to the Undiagnosed Myopathy [COMPREHENSIVE]

### Systematic EDX-Guided Evaluation

When a myopathic EMG pattern is identified, the following framework narrows the differential:

**Step 1: Assess for spontaneous activity**
- Fibrillation potentials present: Inflammatory myopathy, active dystrophy, necrotizing myopathy, Pompe disease
- Fibrillation potentials absent: Steroid myopathy, endocrine myopathy, chronic non-inflammatory conditions
- Myotonic discharges present: DM1/DM2, myotonia congenita, paramyotonia congenita, Pompe disease

**Step 2: Determine distribution**
- Proximal predominant: Most myopathies (standard pattern)
- Distal predominant: Myotonic dystrophy type 1, Miyoshi myopathy (dysferlinopathy), GNE myopathy
- Proximal LE + distal UE: IBM (highly suggestive when combined with age >50)
- Facial involvement: FSHD, myotonic dystrophy, oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy

**Step 3: Laboratory guidance based on EDX pattern**

| EDX Pattern | Suggested Testing |
|---|---|
| Myopathic with fibrillations, elevated CK (>5x) | Myositis-specific antibodies panel; ANA; ESR/CRP; anti-SRP, anti-HMGCR |
| Myopathic without fibrillations, normal CK | TSH, cortisol, vitamin D; consider endocrine myopathy |
| Myotonic discharges | Genetic testing for DM1/DM2; GAA enzyme activity (Pompe) |
| Paraspinal myotonic discharges | GAA enzyme activity (Pompe disease -- treatable) |
| Mixed myopathic-neurogenic, age >50 | Anti-cN1A antibody; biopsy for IBM |

### Muscle Biopsy Site Selection

EMG guides biopsy site selection. The ideal target is a moderately affected muscle. Critical rules:
- Do NOT biopsy end-stage muscles (fibrosis yields non-diagnostic tissue)
- Do NOT biopsy the exact muscle previously needled (needle artifact on biopsy)
- Choose the contralateral side of the EMG-tested muscle at a similar severity level
- MRI guidance with STIR sequences can identify muscles with active inflammation or characteristic fatty infiltration patterns

## 11.14 Muscle Biopsy Fiber Type Atrophy Patterns [BOARD]

Understanding fiber type atrophy patterns on muscle biopsy helps correlate with the clinical and EDX diagnosis.

### Table 11.4: Fiber Type Atrophy Patterns and Diagnostic Significance

| Biopsy Finding | Pattern | Associated Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| Type I fiber atrophy | Smaller type I (slow-twitch) fibers | Myotonic dystrophy type 1, congenital myopathies |
| Type II fiber atrophy | Smaller type II (fast-twitch) fibers | **Steroid myopathy**, disuse atrophy, cachexia, Cushing disease, DM2 |
| Grouped atrophy | Clusters of atrophic fibers of both types | Neurogenic disease (reinnervation pattern); denervation + reinnervation |
| Perifascicular atrophy | Atrophy at edges of fascicles | **Dermatomyositis** (pathognomonic) |
| Fiber type grouping | Loss of normal checkerboard pattern; groups of same fiber type together | Chronic neurogenic disease (chronic reinnervation reorganizes fiber types) |
| Scattered necrosis/regeneration | Individual fiber necrosis with adjacent regenerating fibers | Active myopathy (inflammatory, dystrophy, toxic) |
| Rimmed vacuoles | Vacuoles with basophilic rim | **Inclusion body myositis**, hereditary IBM, distal myopathies |
| Ragged red fibers | Subsarcolemmal mitochondrial accumulation (Gomori trichrome stain) | **Mitochondrial myopathy** |
| Central cores | Pale cores in center of fibers (oxidative stains) | Central core disease (RYR1 mutation) |

> **Board Mnemonic:** "**Type 2 Trouble with Steroids**" -- Type II fiber atrophy is the hallmark of steroid myopathy. "**Type 1 Trouble in Dystrophy 1**" -- Type I fiber atrophy is characteristic of myotonic dystrophy type 1.

## 11.14 Rhabdomyolysis and Myoglobinuria: EDX Considerations [BOARD]

Rhabdomyolysis is acute skeletal muscle necrosis with release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, CK, potassium, phosphate) into the bloodstream. CK levels are typically >10,000 IU/L and may exceed 100,000 IU/L. Myoglobinuria (dark "cola-colored" urine) can cause acute kidney injury.

### Common Causes

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Metabolic | McArdle disease, CPT-II deficiency, fatty acid oxidation defects |
| Drug/toxin | Statins (especially with fibrates), alcohol, cocaine, carbon monoxide, snake venom |
| Physical | Crush injury, prolonged immobilization, extreme exertion, seizures, malignant hyperthermia, NMS |
| Inflammatory | Severe dermatomyositis, IMNM (anti-SRP), viral myositis |
| Endocrine | Severe hypothyroidism, diabetic ketoacidosis |
| Genetic | Malignant hyperthermia (RYR1 mutations), king-denborough syndrome |

### EDX Findings in Rhabdomyolysis

- **Acute phase (first 1-2 weeks):** EMG may show increased insertional activity, fibrillation potentials in severely affected muscles. MUAPs may be difficult to activate due to pain and weakness. NCS are typically normal unless concurrent neuropathy is present.
- **Recovery phase (2-8 weeks):** Fibrillation potentials persist in necrotic areas. Small, polyphasic "nascent" MUAPs may appear as muscle fibers regenerate. Recruitment may be reduced initially.
- **Late phase (months):** Myopathic MUAPs if residual damage. Full recovery is typical if the underlying cause is addressed and no fixed myopathy develops.

### Malignant Hyperthermia [BOARD]

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a pharmacogenetic disorder triggered by volatile anesthetic agents (halothane, sevoflurane, desflurane) and succinylcholine. It is caused by mutations in the ryanodine receptor (RYR1) gene in approximately 70% of cases. The defective receptor releases excessive calcium from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, leading to sustained muscle contraction, hypermetabolism, rhabdomyolysis, and potentially fatal hyperthermia.

- **EDX findings:** EMG is typically normal between episodes. During an episode, the electrical activity pattern may be complex, but clinical management takes priority.
- **Diagnostic testing:** The in vitro contracture test (IVCT) using muscle biopsy is the gold standard. Genetic testing for RYR1 mutations can confirm susceptibility.
- **Treatment:** Dantrolene sodium (the specific antidote, blocks ryanodine receptor calcium release)

## 11.15 Summary: Board-High-Yield Review Points

1. **Short-duration MUAPs** are the most reliable EMG feature of myopathy. Early recruitment is the characteristic firing pattern.

2. NCS are typically normal in myopathies. Key exceptions: **prolonged CMAP duration in CIM** (most important), reduced CMAP amplitude in severe/distal myopathies.

3. Fibrillation potentials in myopathy indicate active muscle fiber necrosis (inflammatory myopathies, active dystrophies, Pompe disease). Their absence does not exclude myopathy (steroid myopathy, endocrine myopathies).

4. Myotonic discharges (waxing/waning, "dive-bomber"): DM1, DM2, myotonia congenita, paramyotonia congenita, hyperkalemic PP, **Pompe disease** (paraspinal).

5. DMD = absent dystrophin (frameshift mutation, Xp21); BMD = reduced/truncated dystrophin (in-frame deletion). Both X-linked recessive with massively elevated CK.

6. DM1 (DMPK, CTG repeat, chr 19) = **distal** weakness, anticipation. DM2 (CNBP, CCTG repeat, chr 3) = **proximal** weakness, no anticipation.

7. Inflammatory myopathies: 5 subtypes -- DM (perifascicular atrophy), PM (endomysial CD8+), ASyS (anti-Jo-1, ILD), IMNM (anti-SRP, anti-HMGCR, highest CK), IBM (rimmed vacuoles, finger flexor/quadriceps weakness, mixed EMG, treatment-resistant).

8. IBM: Mixed myopathic-neurogenic EMG pattern, age >50, finger flexors + knee extensors, anti-cN1A antibody, poor response to immunotherapy.

9. McArdle disease: **Electrical silence during contracture** on forearm exercise testing. Pompe disease: **myotonic discharges in paraspinals** + proximal weakness + respiratory failure.

10. CIM vs. CIP: CIM has normal SNAPs and prolonged CMAP duration. CIP has reduced SNAPs and normal CMAP duration. CIM generally has a better prognosis.

11. Steroid myopathy: Type II fiber atrophy, normal CK, normal or mildly myopathic EMG, no fibrillation potentials.
