# Chapter 5: Needle Electromyography

## Learning Objectives [BOARD]

Upon completing this chapter, the reader should be able to:

1. Distinguish normal insertional activity from increased and decreased insertional activity, and explain the clinical significance of each
2. Differentiate normal endplate activity (MEPPs, endplate spikes) from pathological spontaneous discharges based on morphology, firing pattern, and sound
3. Identify and classify abnormal spontaneous activity including fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves, complex repetitive discharges, myotonic discharges, fasciculation potentials, myokymic discharges, neuromyotonic discharges, and cramp potentials
4. Apply the standard grading scale (0 to 4+) for fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves
5. Analyze motor unit action potential (MUAP) parameters including duration, amplitude, phases, and stability to distinguish neuropathic from myopathic processes
6. Evaluate recruitment patterns using the Rule of Fives, recruitment frequency, and recruitment interval to identify reduced versus early recruitment
7. Interpret full and incomplete interference patterns in the context of neurogenic and myopathic disease

## 5.1 Introduction to the Needle EMG Examination [BOARD]

The needle electromyographic (EMG) examination represents the second major component of the electrodiagnostic evaluation, complementing nerve conduction studies by providing direct assessment of muscle and motor unit physiology. While nerve conduction studies evaluate the peripheral nerve in terms of conduction velocity, amplitude, and latency, the needle examination evaluates the muscle fiber, the neuromuscular junction, and the motor unit as an integrated functional entity. The needle EMG is performed by inserting a recording electrode -- most commonly a concentric or monopolar needle -- into a muscle and systematically evaluating electrical activity at rest and during voluntary contraction.

The examination is organized into four sequential phases of analysis: (1) insertional activity, assessed as the needle is advanced through muscle tissue; (2) spontaneous activity at rest, evaluated with the muscle completely relaxed; (3) motor unit action potential (MUAP) morphology, analyzed during minimal voluntary contraction; and (4) recruitment and interference pattern, observed during graded and maximal voluntary effort. Each phase provides distinct diagnostic information, and abnormalities in one phase often contextualize findings in another.

The needle EMG is indispensable in the evaluation of radiculopathy, plexopathy, peripheral neuropathy, motor neuron disease, myopathy, and neuromuscular junction disorders. Its sensitivity and specificity depend on proper technique, appropriate muscle selection, and the timing of the study relative to the onset of the pathological process. For instance, fibrillation potentials -- a hallmark of denervation -- do not appear until approximately two to three weeks after axonal injury, a fact that is critical in determining when to perform the study.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The needle EMG examination should always be interpreted in the context of nerve conduction study results. An EMG performed in isolation lacks the information needed to distinguish preganglionic from postganglionic lesions, demyelinating from axonal pathophysiology, and focal from diffuse neuropathic processes.

## 5.2 Insertional Activity [BOARD]

### 5.2.1 Normal Insertional Activity

When a needle electrode is advanced into normal muscle tissue, the mechanical disruption of muscle fibers generates a brief burst of electrical activity termed insertional activity. This activity is a direct consequence of needle movement through viable, electrically excitable muscle tissue. Normal insertional activity lasts only as long as the needle is in motion -- typically a few hundred milliseconds after each advancement -- and ceases promptly when needle movement stops. The waveforms generated are irregular, brief, and of variable morphology, arising from the mechanical depolarization of muscle fibers in direct contact with the needle tip.

Normal insertional activity serves as an important baseline assessment. Its presence confirms that the needle is positioned within electrically viable muscle tissue, and its characteristics provide immediate information about the state of the muscle membrane.

### 5.2.2 Increased Insertional Activity [BOARD]

Increased insertional activity is defined as electrical activity that persists beyond the expected duration following needle movement. The discharges continue after the needle has stopped moving, sometimes for several hundred milliseconds or longer. This finding indicates heightened muscle membrane irritability.

Increased insertional activity is one of the earliest electrodiagnostic signs of denervation, often preceding the appearance of fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves. In the acute phase of nerve injury (within the first one to two weeks), a muscle may demonstrate increased insertional activity as its only abnormality. This finding should prompt the electromyographer to reassess the muscle at a later date if the clinical suspicion for denervation remains high.

Conditions associated with increased insertional activity include:

- **Acute denervation** (early radiculopathy, peripheral nerve injury, motor neuron disease)
- **Inflammatory myopathies** (polymyositis, dermatomyositis, inclusion body myositis)
- **Muscular dystrophies** (particularly in actively degenerating muscles)
- **Metabolic myopathies** with membrane instability

> **Clinical Pearl:** Increased insertional activity is the earliest needle EMG finding after acute denervation, appearing before fibrillation potentials. When a study performed within the first two weeks of symptom onset shows only increased insertional activity in a clinically suspicious distribution, the study should be repeated in two to four weeks to assess for the development of denervation potentials.

### 5.2.3 Decreased Insertional Activity [BOARD]

Decreased or absent insertional activity occurs when needle advancement fails to generate the expected brief burst of electrical potentials. This finding indicates a reduction in the amount of electrically excitable muscle tissue at the needle tip. The most common causes include:

- **Fibrosis and scarring:** End-stage denervation with replacement of muscle fibers by fibrofatty tissue, as occurs in chronic, complete nerve injuries or advanced muscular dystrophy
- **Adipose tissue replacement:** Severe, longstanding myopathic or neurogenic atrophy
- **Edematous or necrotic tissue:** Acute rhabdomyolysis or severe compartment syndrome (early phase)
- **Needle positioned outside muscle:** Subcutaneous fat, fascia, or tendon

Decreased insertional activity carries important prognostic implications. A muscle that has undergone extensive fibrotic replacement is unlikely to recover useful function even if the nerve supply is restored, because there are insufficient viable muscle fibers to accept reinnervation.

| Insertional Activity | Duration | Significance | Common Causes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal | Brief, ceases with needle movement | Electrically viable muscle | Normal muscle |
| Increased | Persists after needle stops | Membrane irritability | Acute denervation, myopathy |
| Decreased | Minimal or absent | Loss of viable muscle fibers | Chronic denervation, fibrosis, adipose replacement |

## 5.3 Normal Spontaneous Activity [BOARD]

### 5.3.1 Electrical Silence at Rest

Outside the endplate zone, normal resting muscle is electrically silent. When the needle electrode is positioned in the body of a normal, relaxed muscle away from the motor endplate region, no spontaneous electrical activity should be recorded. This electrical silence reflects the stable resting membrane potential of healthy, innervated muscle fibers. Any spontaneous activity recorded outside the endplate zone in a relaxed patient warrants careful evaluation, as it may represent a pathological discharge.

The electromyographer must ensure that the patient is truly relaxed during the assessment of spontaneous activity. Incomplete relaxation is one of the most common sources of artifact during the needle examination, as volitional motor unit firing may be misinterpreted as spontaneous activity. Coaching the patient to relax, supporting the limb in a comfortable position, and carefully observing the audio signal for motor unit morphology are essential techniques.

### 5.3.2 Miniature Endplate Potentials (MEPPs) [BOARD]

Miniature endplate potentials arise from the spontaneous, quantal release of acetylcholine vesicles at the neuromuscular junction. Each MEPP represents the postsynaptic depolarization caused by a single quantum of acetylcholine binding to nicotinic receptors on the muscle fiber membrane in the endplate region.

**Characteristics of MEPPs (Endplate Noise):**

- **Amplitude:** Low, typically less than 100 microvolts
- **Morphology:** Monophasic, negative deflection (initial negative phase)
- **Firing pattern:** Irregular, continuous
- **Frequency:** 20 to 40 Hz
- **Sound:** A low-amplitude, continuous hissing or rushing sound, classically described as a "seashell" held to the ear

MEPPs are a normal finding when the needle tip is positioned within or near the endplate zone. They do not indicate pathology and should not be confused with fibrillation potentials. The key distinguishing features are the initial negative deflection (fibrillations have an initial positive deflection) and the highly irregular, continuous firing pattern.

### 5.3.3 Endplate Spikes [BOARD]

Endplate spikes are generated when the needle electrode mechanically irritates terminal nerve twigs or motor nerve endings in the endplate region. The needle irritation causes the nerve terminal to fire, producing a single muscle fiber action potential recorded at the endplate.

**Characteristics of Endplate Spikes:**

- **Amplitude:** 100 to 500 microvolts (larger than MEPPs)
- **Morphology:** Brief, biphasic or triphasic spike with an **initial negative deflection**
- **Firing pattern:** Highly irregular, sputtering
- **Frequency:** Up to 50 Hz, but firing is inconsistent
- **Sound:** A sharp, crackling or sputtering sound, often described as "fat frying" or "cracking eggs"

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** The critical distinction between endplate spikes and fibrillation potentials is the initial deflection. Endplate spikes begin with a **negative** (upward) deflection because the needle tip is at the generator source. Fibrillation potentials begin with a **positive** (downward) deflection because the recording electrode is distant from the depolarization origin. Additionally, endplate spikes fire irregularly, whereas fibrillation potentials fire with metronomic regularity.

| Feature | Endplate Noise (MEPPs) | Endplate Spikes | Fibrillation Potentials |
|---|---|---|---|
| Significance | Normal | Normal | Abnormal |
| Initial deflection | Negative | Negative | **Positive** |
| Amplitude | <100 uV | 100-500 uV | 20-300 uV |
| Firing pattern | Irregular, continuous | Irregular, sputtering | **Regular** |
| Sound | Seashell | Crackling/sputtering | Rain on a tin roof |
| Location | Endplate zone | Endplate zone | Throughout muscle |

## 5.4 Abnormal Spontaneous Activity [BOARD]

Abnormal spontaneous activity refers to any electrical discharge recorded from a relaxed muscle that cannot be attributed to normal endplate activity or insertional activity. These discharges arise from pathological changes in muscle fiber membrane properties, motor unit organization, or peripheral nerve excitability. The presence, type, distribution, and abundance of abnormal spontaneous activity provide critical diagnostic and prognostic information.

### 5.4.1 Fibrillation Potentials [BOARD]

Fibrillation potentials are the spontaneous action potentials of individual muscle fibers that have lost their neural input. They represent the single most important abnormal finding on needle EMG and are the electrophysiological hallmark of denervation. However, it is essential to recognize that fibrillation potentials are not exclusive to denervation -- they occur in any condition that disrupts the normal relationship between the nerve terminal and the muscle fiber membrane.

**Mechanism:** Following denervation, the muscle fiber membrane undergoes a series of biochemical and structural changes collectively termed denervation supersensitivity. Acetylcholine receptors, normally concentrated at the endplate, spread across the entire muscle fiber membrane (extrajunctional receptor upregulation). The resting membrane potential becomes unstable, and the threshold for depolarization decreases. These changes result in spontaneous, repetitive depolarization of individual muscle fibers.

**Morphology:**

- Biphasic or triphasic waveform
- **Initial positive (downward) deflection** -- the defining morphological feature
- Duration: 1 to 5 milliseconds
- Amplitude: 20 to 300 microvolts

**Firing Pattern:**

- Regular, rhythmic firing at a rate of 0.5 to 10 Hz
- Metronomic regularity distinguishes fibrillation potentials from endplate spikes
- Often described as having a "clock-like" firing pattern

**Sound:**

- Crisp, high-pitched clicking or ticking sound
- Classically described as "rain on a tin roof" or "ticking of a clock"

**Timing of Appearance After Nerve Injury:**

The time required for fibrillation potentials to appear depends on the distance between the site of nerve injury and the muscle being studied. This delay reflects the time needed for Wallerian degeneration to reach the distal nerve terminal and for denervation supersensitivity to develop in the muscle fiber membrane.

- **Paraspinal muscles:** 7 to 10 days (some studies report as early as 5 to 7 days)
- **Proximal limb muscles:** 2 to 3 weeks
- **Distal limb muscles:** 3 to 6 weeks

This temporal gradient has important implications for the timing of the electrodiagnostic study. A needle EMG performed too early after the onset of a radiculopathy may fail to demonstrate fibrillation potentials in distal limb muscles, potentially yielding a false-negative result. For optimal sensitivity, the study should be performed at least three weeks after the onset of motor symptoms.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The earliest needle EMG finding after acute denervation is **reduced recruitment**, not fibrillation potentials. Reduced recruitment appears within days because it reflects the loss of functional motor units, which is immediate after axonal injury. Fibrillation potentials require two to three weeks to develop because they depend on Wallerian degeneration and denervation supersensitivity.

### 5.4.2 Positive Sharp Waves (PSWs) [BOARD]

Positive sharp waves are the electrophysiological twin of fibrillation potentials. They arise from the same pathological process -- spontaneous depolarization of denervated muscle fibers -- but differ in waveform morphology due to the spatial relationship between the discharging fiber and the recording electrode.

**Mechanism:** A positive sharp wave is generated when the needle electrode is in direct contact with or has damaged the discharging muscle fiber. The recording electrode is positioned at or near the depolarization source, creating a characteristic waveform.

**Morphology:**

- **Initial sharp positive (downward) deflection** followed by a prolonged, slow negative phase
- Duration: 10 to 30 milliseconds (longer than fibrillation potentials due to the slow negative component)
- Amplitude: Variable, typically 50 to several hundred microvolts

**Firing Pattern and Sound:**

- Regular firing at 0.5 to 10 Hz (same as fibrillation potentials)
- Sound: A dull, bass-toned "thud" -- distinctly lower-pitched than the crisp click of a fibrillation potential

**Relationship to Fibrillation Potentials:**

Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves represent the same underlying phenomenon. The waveform difference is a consequence of tissue filtering and the geometric relationship of the spontaneously discharging fiber to the recording electrode. A single denervated fiber may produce a fibrillation potential or a positive sharp wave depending on electrode position. For this reason, they are graded together on the same scale and carry identical clinical significance.

### 5.4.3 Grading of Fibrillation Potentials and Positive Sharp Waves [BOARD]

The density and distribution of fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves are graded on a standardized 0 to 4+ scale. This grading system provides a semiquantitative estimate of the degree of denervation within the muscle. The grade is determined by systematically sampling multiple areas of the muscle (typically 10 recording positions across 2 skin insertions or 3 to 4 needle passes) and averaging the findings.

| Grade | Criteria | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | No fibrillation potentials or PSWs | No active denervation |
| 1+ | Persistent activity in at least 2 areas | Minimal denervation |
| 2+ | Moderate number of persistent potentials in 3 or more areas | Mild to moderate denervation |
| 3+ | Many potentials in all areas examined | Moderate to severe denervation |
| 4+ | Profuse activity filling the screen in all areas | Severe, widespread denervation |

The grading correlates broadly with the severity and acuity of the denervating process. A muscle with 4+ fibrillation potentials and absent motor unit recruitment has experienced near-complete denervation. However, the grade must be interpreted in context: 1+ fibrillations may be clinically significant in the appropriate setting, whereas even profuse fibrillations may be seen as a normal transitional finding during active reinnervation.

### 5.4.4 Non-Neurogenic Causes of Fibrillation Potentials [BOARD]

While fibrillation potentials are most commonly associated with denervation, they also occur in primary muscle diseases that disrupt the integrity of the muscle fiber membrane or sever the functional connection between portions of a muscle fiber and its endplate. Understanding these non-neurogenic causes is critical to avoid misdiagnosis.

**Conditions associated with fibrillation potentials in the absence of denervation:**

- **Inflammatory myopathies:** Polymyositis, dermatomyositis, and inclusion body myositis produce fibrillation potentials through segmental muscle fiber necrosis and inflammatory disruption of the muscle membrane
- **Muscular dystrophies:** Fiber necrosis, splitting, and regeneration in Duchenne, Becker, and other dystrophies
- **Toxic and necrotizing myopathies:** Drug-induced or immune-mediated necrotizing myopathies
- **Severe neuromuscular junction disorders:** Rarely, in botulism or severe myasthenia gravis, functional denervation of individual muscle fibers can produce sparse fibrillations
- **Rhabdomyolysis:** Acute muscle fiber necrosis
- **Acid maltase deficiency (Pompe disease):** Vacuolar myopathy with membrane disruption

> **Clinical Pearl:** The presence of fibrillation potentials combined with **short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs** and **early recruitment** should raise suspicion for a myopathic process rather than denervation. Fibrillation potentials alone do not prove a neurogenic etiology.

### 5.4.5 Complex Repetitive Discharges (CRDs) [BOARD]

Complex repetitive discharges are polyphasic, repetitive waveforms generated by a group of muscle fibers that discharge in a linked, sequential chain through ephaptic (direct electrical) transmission. One fiber depolarizes and, through electrical field effects, triggers the adjacent denervated fiber to depolarize, creating a circular chain of activation that repeats at a fixed, regular frequency.

**Mechanism:** CRDs require denervated, supersensitive muscle fibers in close proximity. A single "pacemaker" fiber initiates the discharge, and the signal propagates to neighboring fibers through direct electrical coupling (ephapsis) rather than through neural or synaptic mechanisms. The circuit loops back to reactivate the original pacemaker fiber, sustaining the discharge indefinitely until one fiber in the chain fails to fire.

**Morphology:**

- Polyphasic or serrated waveform with 3 to 10 spike components
- Each spike represents a single muscle fiber action potential within the chain
- Amplitude: 50 to 500 microvolts (occasionally up to 1 millivolt)
- Duration: Up to 50 milliseconds for the complete complex

**Firing Pattern:**

- **Constant, fixed frequency** -- this is the defining feature
- Frequency range: 3 to 40 Hz (most commonly 5 to 25 Hz)
- Onset and cessation are **abrupt** -- the discharge starts suddenly at full frequency and stops instantaneously when one fiber in the chain drops out
- Between discharges, the waveform morphology is identical (unlike myotonic discharges)

**Sound:**

- "Jackhammer" or "motorboat that misfires"
- Machine-like, repetitive, with a mechanical quality
- The constant frequency creates a steady rhythmic pattern that is distinctly different from the waxing-waning character of myotonic discharges

**Clinical Significance:**

CRDs are **nonspecific** for any particular disease category. They indicate chronicity -- the muscle fibers have been denervated long enough to develop the close fiber-to-fiber proximity and supersensitivity required for ephaptic transmission. CRDs are encountered in:

- Chronic radiculopathies
- Chronic peripheral neuropathies
- Slowly progressive myopathies
- Chronic inflammatory myopathies
- Spinal muscular atrophy
- Post-polio syndrome

> **Clinical Pearl:** CRDs indicate chronicity but not etiology. They are seen in both chronic neurogenic and chronic myopathic conditions. The presence of CRDs in a muscle being evaluated for acute radiculopathy suggests that the process is not purely acute -- there is likely a chronic component.

### 5.4.6 Myotonic Discharges [BOARD]

Myotonic discharges are repetitive, spontaneous discharges of individual muscle fibers characterized by their distinctive waxing and waning pattern of both amplitude and frequency. They are among the most recognizable discharges in electromyography due to their unmistakable auditory signature.

**Mechanism:** Myotonic discharges arise from abnormal muscle fiber membrane excitability, typically due to dysfunction of voltage-gated sodium or chloride channels. In chloride channel myotonia (myotonia congenita), reduced chloride conductance destabilizes the muscle membrane. In sodium channel myotonia (paramyotonia congenita, hyperkalemic periodic paralysis), abnormal sodium channel inactivation produces persistent inward currents. The result in both cases is a muscle fiber that, once depolarized, continues to fire repetitively.

**Morphology:**

- Repetitive discharge of a single muscle fiber
- Waveform may resemble a fibrillation potential or a positive sharp wave
- Each spike represents the action potential of one muscle fiber

**Firing Pattern:**

- **Waxing and waning** in both amplitude and frequency -- this is the pathognomonic feature
- Frequency: 20 to 80 Hz (higher than most other spontaneous discharges)
- Runs typically last several seconds before decrementing and ceasing
- Needle movement provokes bursts; voluntary contraction may trigger prolonged after-discharges

**Sound:**

- **"Dive bomber"** -- the classic board description
- Also described as an "accelerating and decelerating motorcycle engine" or "revving engine"
- The pitch rises and falls in parallel with the changing frequency, creating an unmistakable musical quality

**Conditions Associated with Myotonic Discharges:**

| Category | Specific Conditions |
|---|---|
| Chloride channelopathies | Myotonia congenita (Thomsen and Becker types) |
| Sodium channelopathies | Paramyotonia congenita, hyperkalemic periodic paralysis |
| Dystrophic myotonia | Myotonic dystrophy types 1 and 2 (DM1, DM2) |
| Metabolic myopathies | Acid maltase deficiency (Pompe disease) |
| Inflammatory myopathies | Polymyositis (occasionally) |
| Toxic myopathies | Statin myopathy, colchicine myopathy (rarely) |
| Hypothyroid myopathy | Occasional myotonic discharges |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Myotonic discharges on EMG do not always correlate with clinical myotonia (grip myotonia, percussion myotonia). Acid maltase deficiency and hypothyroid myopathy may produce electrical myotonia without clinical myotonia. Conversely, patients with clinical myotonia should always demonstrate myotonic discharges on EMG.

### 5.4.7 Fasciculation Potentials [BOARD]

Fasciculation potentials are the spontaneous, involuntary discharge of an entire motor unit or a portion thereof. Unlike fibrillation potentials, which arise from a single muscle fiber, fasciculation potentials reflect the activation of multiple muscle fibers belonging to the same motor unit. Fasciculations are visible through the skin as brief, irregular twitching movements.

**Mechanism:** Fasciculation potentials can originate from any point along the lower motor neuron, including the anterior horn cell, proximal nerve root, nerve trunk, terminal nerve branches, or the motor nerve terminal itself. Most benign fasciculations arise from the distal nerve terminal or terminal nerve branches. Pathological fasciculations in motor neuron disease are more likely to arise from the anterior horn cell or proximal nerve.

**Morphology:**

- The waveform resembles a motor unit action potential (MUAP) because it represents the discharge of an entire motor unit
- Simple fasciculations: Normal MUAP morphology (2 to 4 phases, normal duration and amplitude)
- Complex fasciculations: Polyphasic, long-duration, high-amplitude waveforms suggesting remodeled motor units

**Firing Pattern:**

- **Irregular** -- this is the key feature
- Firing rate: Typically semirhythmic at 0.1 to 10 Hz, but highly variable
- Unlike fibrillation potentials, there is no clock-like regularity
- Individual fasciculation potentials occur at unpredictable intervals

**Sound:**

- A "thump" or "pop" -- similar to a voluntarily activated motor unit but occurring spontaneously
- The sound characteristics depend on the size of the motor unit generating the fasciculation

**Clinical Significance -- Benign vs. Pathological:**

The mere presence of fasciculation potentials is not diagnostic of any specific condition. Fasciculations occur in numerous settings:

- **Benign fasciculation syndrome:** Fasciculations without weakness, atrophy, or other EMG abnormalities; extremely common in the general population
- **Motor neuron disease (ALS):** Fasciculations accompanied by fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves, chronic neurogenic MUAP changes, and reduced recruitment
- **Radiculopathies:** Fasciculations in a myotomal distribution
- **Peripheral neuropathies:** Associated with axonal degeneration
- **Metabolic disturbances:** Hyperthyroidism, electrolyte imbalances
- **Medication effects:** Caffeine, anticholinesterase agents

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** Fasciculation potentials alone never establish a diagnosis of ALS. The Awaji-Shima criteria accept fasciculation potentials as equivalent to fibrillation potentials only when they occur in muscles that also demonstrate chronic neurogenic MUAP changes (long-duration, high-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs). Benign fasciculations occur in muscles with entirely normal MUAP morphology and recruitment.

### 5.4.8 Myokymic Discharges [BOARD]

Myokymic discharges are spontaneous, semirhythmic bursts of motor unit potentials that fire in groups separated by periods of silence. They are distinguished from fasciculation potentials by their grouped, repetitive firing pattern and from neuromyotonic discharges by their lower intraburst frequency.

**Morphology and Firing Pattern:**

- Bursts of motor unit potentials recurring at a semirhythmic rate
- **Intraburst frequency:** 5 to 60 Hz (within each burst; most commonly 30 to 60 Hz)
- **Interburst frequency:** 0.1 to 5 Hz (rate at which bursts recur)
- Each burst typically contains 2 to 10 motor unit discharges
- The bursts are separated by silent intervals of relatively constant duration

**Sound:**

- "Marching soldiers" -- a repetitive, rhythmic grouping of sounds with regular silent intervals
- Sometimes described as sounding like groups of people walking in step

**Clinical Significance:**

Myokymic discharges carry important diagnostic implications. Their presence in specific clinical contexts strongly suggests particular diagnoses:

- **Radiation plexopathy:** Myokymic discharges are found in 50 to 70% of patients with radiation-induced brachial plexopathy and are the single most useful EMG feature distinguishing radiation from tumor plexopathy
- **Multiple sclerosis:** Facial myokymia is a classic finding
- **Brainstem glioma:** Facial myokymia
- **Guillain-Barre syndrome:** Limb myokymia
- **Chronic nerve compression:** Occasionally seen in chronic radiculopathies

### 5.4.9 Neuromyotonic Discharges [BOARD]

Neuromyotonic discharges are very high-frequency, spontaneous discharges that arise from peripheral nerve hyperexcitability. They are distinguished from other repetitive discharges primarily by their extremely high firing frequency.

**Morphology and Firing Pattern:**

- Very high frequency: **150 to 300 Hz** (some sources cite up to 300 Hz)
- Waveforms may wane in amplitude (decrement) but frequency remains relatively constant
- Duration of individual bursts: Typically several hundred milliseconds to several seconds
- Often provoked by needle movement or voluntary effort

**Sound:**

- **"Pinging"** -- a high-pitched, musical tone
- The very high frequency produces a nearly continuous, high-pitched sound
- Some describe it as a "ringing" or "whining" quality

**Clinical Significance:**

- **Isaacs syndrome (acquired neuromyotonia):** The prototypical condition; autoimmune, often associated with antibodies against voltage-gated potassium channels (CASPR2 antibodies)
- **Morvan syndrome:** Neuromyotonia with encephalopathy, insomnia, and autonomic dysfunction
- **Peripheral neuropathies:** Occasionally seen in chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy
- **Paraneoplastic syndromes:** Associated with thymoma, small cell lung cancer

> **Clinical Pearl:** The frequency of the discharge is the key to identification. Neuromyotonic discharges fire at greater than 150 Hz, myotonic discharges at 20 to 80 Hz, and CRDs at 3 to 40 Hz. When a high-frequency repetitive discharge is heard, assess the frequency and whether it waxes and wanes (myotonic) or remains constant with decrementing amplitude (neuromyotonic).

### 5.4.10 Cramp Potentials [BOARD]

Cramp potentials are involuntary, sustained discharges of motor units that correspond to the clinical phenomenon of a muscle cramp. They are the electrophysiological correlate of the painful, involuntary muscle contraction familiar to virtually all individuals.

**Morphology and Firing Pattern:**

- High-frequency discharge of multiple motor unit action potentials
- Firing rate: 20 to 150 Hz, typically 40 to 60 Hz
- Irregular firing pattern, with multiple motor units discharging asynchronously
- Onset may be abrupt or gradual, and the discharge often subsides gradually as the cramp resolves
- The discharge can often be terminated by passive stretch of the cramping muscle

**Sound:**

- A rumbling, irregular roar of overlapping motor unit activity
- Distinguished from voluntary contraction by the clinical context (the patient is not trying to contract the muscle) and by the irregular quality

**Clinical Significance:**

Cramp potentials are common and often benign. They are associated with:

- Dehydration, electrolyte disturbances (hyponatremia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia)
- Lower motor neuron diseases (ALS, radiculopathy)
- Peripheral neuropathies
- Pregnancy
- Exercise-associated muscle cramps
- Benign cramp-fasciculation syndrome

## 5.6 Motor Unit Action Potential (MUAP) Analysis [BOARD]

### 5.6.1 The Motor Unit Concept

The motor unit -- consisting of a single alpha motor neuron, its axon, all terminal branches, and all muscle fibers it innervates -- is the fundamental functional unit of the neuromuscular system. The motor unit action potential (MUAP) recorded during needle EMG represents the temporal and spatial summation of all individual muscle fiber action potentials within the recording range of the electrode that belong to the same motor unit.

The characteristics of the MUAP depend on several factors: the number of muscle fibers in the motor unit, the density of fibers near the recording electrode, the synchrony of fiber depolarization, the fiber diameter, and the distance between individual fibers and the electrode tip. Changes in any of these parameters -- as occur in neurogenic or myopathic disease -- alter the MUAP morphology in predictable ways.

### 5.6.2 MUAP Parameters [BOARD]

**Duration:**

Duration is measured from the initial departure of the waveform from baseline to its final return. It is the single most informative MUAP parameter and the most reliable indicator of motor unit territory (the spatial distribution of muscle fibers belonging to one motor unit).

- **Normal range:** 5 to 15 milliseconds (varies significantly with patient age and specific muscle)
- **Increased duration:** Indicates an enlarged motor unit territory, as occurs with collateral sprouting in chronic denervation/reinnervation. The newly sprouted terminal axon branches conduct more slowly and are distributed over a wider area, prolonging the total duration of the summated potential
- **Decreased duration:** Indicates a reduced number of muscle fibers per motor unit or reduced motor unit territory. This is the hallmark of myopathic disease, in which individual muscle fibers are lost from the motor unit through necrosis, degeneration, or splitting

**Amplitude:**

Amplitude is measured from the most negative peak to the most positive peak (peak-to-peak). It primarily reflects the density and proximity of muscle fibers closest to the recording electrode.

- **Normal range:** 200 microvolts to 3 millivolts (varies by muscle and electrode type)
- **Increased amplitude:** Seen in chronic neurogenic conditions where collateral sprouting increases the density of fibers near the electrode; also, fiber type grouping concentrates fibers of one motor unit in a small area
- **Decreased amplitude:** Characteristic of myopathies, where fewer muscle fibers per motor unit are present near the electrode tip

**Phases and Polyphasicity:**

A phase is defined as the portion of the waveform between two baseline crossings. Normal MUAPs have 2 to 4 phases. A potential with more than 4 phases is termed polyphasic.

- **Normal:** Up to 4 phases; up to 10-15% of MUAPs in a normal muscle may be polyphasic
- **Increased polyphasicity:** Indicates temporal desynchronization of the individual muscle fiber action potentials within the motor unit. This occurs in both neurogenic (from immature collateral sprouts with slow conduction) and myopathic (from variable fiber size and conduction velocity) conditions
- Polyphasicity alone is nonspecific; it must be interpreted alongside duration, amplitude, and recruitment

**Turns:**

Turns are direction changes of the waveform that do not cross the baseline. A complex MUAP may have many turns without being polyphasic. The turns:amplitude ratio is sometimes used in quantitative EMG to distinguish neuropathic from myopathic conditions.

**Stability:**

MUAP stability refers to the consistency of the waveform morphology on consecutive firings. Normal MUAPs fire with a highly reproducible waveform. Instability (moment-to-moment variation in shape, also called "jitter" and "blocking" when assessed with single-fiber EMG techniques) suggests neuromuscular junction dysfunction or immature, poorly myelinated collateral sprouts.

- **Neuromuscular junction disorders:** Marked instability with varying MUAP morphology on consecutive firings, corresponding to intermittent blocking of individual muscle fiber contributions
- **Early reinnervation:** Newly formed collateral sprouts have immature, poorly myelinated terminals that demonstrate variable conduction, producing unstable MUAPs

### 5.6.3 Neuropathic MUAP Pattern [BOARD]

In chronic denervation with reinnervation, surviving motor neurons extend collateral sprouts to reinnervate orphaned muscle fibers. This process produces characteristic MUAP changes:

- **Long duration:** The motor unit territory expands as collateral sprouts reach distant denervated fibers
- **High amplitude:** Fiber density increases near the electrode as reinnervated fibers are incorporated
- **Polyphasic:** Immature sprouts conduct asynchronously, creating temporal dispersion
- **Unstable (early):** Newly formed neuromuscular junctions are unreliable; MUAPs become stable as sprouts mature
- **Satellite potentials (linked potentials):** Late components separated from the main MUAP by a time interval, representing muscle fibers reinnervated by sprouts with slow conduction

The time course of these changes is important. In the first weeks to months after denervation, MUAPs may appear polyphasic and of normal or slightly increased duration (nascent motor units). Over months to years, as collateral sprouting matures, MUAPs progressively increase in duration and amplitude, ultimately reaching the classic "giant" MUAP morphology seen in chronic neurogenic conditions such as post-polio syndrome or longstanding radiculopathy.

### 5.6.4 Myopathic MUAP Pattern [BOARD]

In myopathic disease, muscle fibers within the motor unit are lost through necrosis, degeneration, or atrophy while the nerve supply remains intact. This produces a characteristic set of MUAP changes that are essentially the opposite of the neuropathic pattern:

- **Short duration:** Fewer fibers contribute to the MUAP, and the surviving fibers are confined to a smaller territory
- **Low amplitude:** Reduced fiber density near the electrode; atrophic fibers generate smaller action potentials
- **Polyphasic:** Variable fiber size and conduction velocity cause temporal desynchronization
- **Early recruitment:** Because each motor unit generates less force (fewer and weaker fibers), more motor units must be activated to achieve a given force level

| Parameter | Neuropathic | Myopathic | Normal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duration | Increased | Decreased | 5-15 ms |
| Amplitude | Increased | Decreased | 200 uV to 3 mV |
| Phases | Increased (polyphasic) | Increased (polyphasic) | 2-4 |
| Stability | Variable (early) | Stable (usually) | Stable |
| Recruitment | Reduced | Early | Normal |

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** Duration is the most reliable MUAP parameter for distinguishing neuropathic from myopathic processes. Amplitude depends heavily on electrode position and can be misleading. Polyphasicity is seen in both conditions and is therefore nonspecific. When in doubt, focus on duration and recruitment pattern.

## 5.7 Recruitment [BOARD]

### 5.7.1 The Physiology of Motor Unit Recruitment

Recruitment refers to the orderly activation of additional motor units as voluntary effort increases. Under normal conditions, motor units are recruited according to the Henneman size principle: smaller motor units (type I, slow-twitch) are recruited first at low force levels, and progressively larger motor units (type II, fast-twitch) are activated as greater force is demanded. Simultaneously, already-recruited motor units increase their firing rate (rate coding).

The interplay between recruitment of new units and rate coding of already-active units produces the progressively dense electrical pattern observed during graded voluntary contraction. At maximal effort, so many motor units are firing at high rates that individual MUAPs can no longer be distinguished, producing the "full interference pattern."

### 5.7.2 The Rule of Fives [BOARD]

The Rule of Fives provides a practical framework for assessing recruitment during the needle examination. Under normal circumstances, there is a predictable relationship between the number of motor units actively firing and the firing rate of the fastest motor unit:

- **1 motor unit:** Should be firing at approximately **5 Hz** before the second unit is recruited
- **2 motor units:** The fastest should be firing at approximately **10 Hz** before the third is recruited
- **3 motor units:** The fastest should be firing at approximately **15 Hz** before the fourth is recruited

In other words, each additional motor unit should be recruited at approximately 5 Hz increments of the fastest-firing unit. Deviations from this pattern indicate either reduced or early recruitment.

### 5.7.3 Recruitment Frequency [BOARD]

Recruitment frequency (or onset frequency) is the firing rate of the first motor unit at the moment the second motor unit begins to fire. It is one of the most practically useful recruitment parameters.

- **Normal recruitment frequency:** Less than 12 Hz (the first unit should not exceed approximately 10 to 12 Hz before a second unit appears)
- **Increased recruitment frequency (>12-15 Hz):** The first unit is firing at an abnormally high rate before additional units appear, indicating that fewer motor units are available for recruitment. This pattern defines **reduced recruitment** and is characteristic of **neurogenic** disease
- **Decreased recruitment frequency:** Multiple units appear at low firing rates (below 5 Hz), indicating that many motor units are activated with minimal effort. This defines **early recruitment** and is characteristic of **myopathic** disease

### 5.7.4 Recruitment Interval (Ratio) [BOARD]

The recruitment interval (or recruitment ratio) provides another quantitative measure of recruitment. It is calculated by dividing the firing rate of the fastest-firing motor unit by the total number of active motor units visible on the screen.

- **Normal recruitment ratio:** Approximately 5:1 (consistent with the Rule of Fives)
- **Increased ratio (>10:1):** Reduced recruitment -- neurogenic
- **Decreased ratio (<5:1):** Early recruitment -- myopathic

**Example calculations:**

- One motor unit firing at 20 Hz with no additional units recruited: ratio = 20/1 = 20 (severely reduced recruitment, neurogenic)
- Three motor units visible with the fastest firing at 8 Hz: ratio = 8/3 = 2.7 (early recruitment, myopathic)
- Two motor units visible with the fastest firing at 10 Hz: ratio = 10/2 = 5 (normal)

### 5.7.5 Reduced Recruitment [BOARD]

Reduced recruitment occurs when fewer motor units are available for activation, forcing the remaining units to fire at higher rates to compensate. This is the recruitment pattern of neurogenic disease.

**Key features:**

- Fewer motor units firing than expected for the degree of effort
- Individual units fire at abnormally high rates (>12-15 Hz for the first unit, >20 Hz for early units)
- The interference pattern is incomplete -- discrete MUAPs can be identified even at maximal effort
- In severe cases, a single motor unit may fire at very high rates (30-50 Hz) as the only recruiteable unit

**Causes:**

- Any process that reduces the number of functional motor units: axonal degeneration, demyelinating conduction block (which functionally removes motor units), motor neuron loss, or acute nerve injury

> **Clinical Pearl:** Reduced recruitment is the **earliest** EMG finding after acute axonal injury. It appears within hours to days because it directly reflects the loss of conducting motor axons. Fibrillation potentials require weeks to develop. Therefore, a study performed in the first few days after nerve injury should focus on recruitment analysis rather than spontaneous activity.

### 5.7.6 Early (Rapid) Recruitment [BOARD]

Early recruitment occurs when motor units are recruited at an abnormally low firing rate or when more motor units are active than expected for a given level of voluntary effort. This is the recruitment pattern of myopathic disease.

**Mechanism:** In myopathy, the number of motor units is preserved, but each unit generates less force because it contains fewer or weaker muscle fibers. To achieve a given force output, the central nervous system must recruit more motor units at lower thresholds and lower firing rates.

**Key features:**

- Many motor units active at low force levels
- Individual units fire at abnormally low rates (<5 Hz) for the force being generated
- The interference pattern fills in rapidly -- a full or nearly full interference pattern may be seen with only mild voluntary effort
- Despite the full interference pattern, the overall envelope amplitude is low (because each MUAP is small)

### 5.7.7 Interference Pattern [BOARD]

The interference pattern describes the overall electrical activity observed at maximal voluntary effort. When many motor units are firing at high rates, their potentials overlap and interfere with one another, making it impossible to identify individual MUAPs. The qualitative assessment of the interference pattern provides a final integrated assessment of recruitment.

| Pattern | Description | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Full interference | Baseline obscured, individual MUAPs indistinguishable | Normal, or early recruitment with low amplitude (myopathy) |
| Reduced (incomplete) | Individual MUAPs identifiable at maximal effort | Neurogenic: fewer units available |
| Discrete | Only 1-3 MUAPs identifiable at maximal effort | Severe neurogenic loss |
| No activity | No motor units recruiteable | Complete denervation or upper motor neuron lesion (with poor voluntary activation) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** A full interference pattern with low amplitude suggests myopathy. A reduced interference pattern with high-amplitude, long-duration MUAPs suggests chronic neurogenic disease. Always consider patient effort -- poor cooperation or pain can produce a seemingly reduced interference pattern in a neurologically normal individual. Encourage the patient and assess in the clinical context.

## 5.8 Integrating Needle EMG Findings: Diagnostic Patterns [BOARD]

The power of the needle EMG lies in the integration of all four assessment phases (insertional activity, spontaneous activity, MUAP morphology, and recruitment) to identify recognizable diagnostic patterns.

### 5.8.1 Acute Neurogenic Pattern

- Insertional activity: Increased
- Spontaneous activity: Fibrillation potentials and PSWs (if >2-3 weeks since injury)
- MUAPs: Normal morphology (reinnervation has not yet occurred) or reduced number of normal MUAPs
- Recruitment: Reduced (earliest finding)

### 5.8.2 Chronic Neurogenic Pattern

- Insertional activity: Normal or increased
- Spontaneous activity: Fibrillations may be absent (if reinnervation is complete) or persistent (if ongoing denervation)
- MUAPs: Long duration, high amplitude, polyphasic ("giant MUAPs")
- Recruitment: Reduced (though may improve with successful reinnervation)

### 5.8.3 Myopathic Pattern

- Insertional activity: May be increased (inflammatory myopathy) or decreased (end-stage fibrosis)
- Spontaneous activity: Fibrillations present in inflammatory or necrotizing myopathies; absent in many chronic myopathies
- MUAPs: Short duration, low amplitude, polyphasic
- Recruitment: Early

### 5.8.4 Neuromuscular Junction Pattern

- Insertional activity: Normal
- Spontaneous activity: Usually none (rare fibrillations in severe cases)
- MUAPs: Varying morphology on consecutive firings (instability); moment-to-moment changes in amplitude and number of phases
- Recruitment: Normal number of units but individual unit force output may be reduced

| Feature | Acute Neurogenic | Chronic Neurogenic | Myopathic | NMJ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Insertional activity | Increased | Normal/increased | Variable | Normal |
| Fibrillations/PSWs | Present (after 2-3 wk) | Variable | Present (inflammatory) | Rare |
| MUAP duration | Normal | Increased | Decreased | Normal/variable |
| MUAP amplitude | Normal | Increased | Decreased | Variable |
| Polyphasicity | Minimal | Present | Present | Variable |
| MUAP stability | Normal | Stable (mature) | Stable | Unstable |
| Recruitment | Reduced | Reduced | Early | Normal |

## 5.9 Technical Considerations and Pitfalls [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 5.9.1 Electrode Types

**Concentric needle electrodes** contain the active recording surface within a beveled cannula, with the cannula itself serving as the reference. They record from a smaller, more defined territory and produce MUAPs of shorter duration and lower amplitude compared to monopolar needles. Concentric needles are the standard for most clinical EMG laboratories.

**Monopolar needle electrodes** use a Teflon-coated needle with only the exposed tip serving as the active electrode. A separate surface electrode serves as the reference. Monopolar needles record from a larger territory, producing MUAPs of longer duration and higher amplitude. Normal reference values differ between electrode types, and the electromyographer must use normative data appropriate for the electrode being used.

### 5.9.2 Patient Factors

- **Temperature:** Cool muscles may demonstrate increased insertional activity, slightly prolonged MUAP duration, and occasional spontaneous activity that resolves with warming
- **Age:** MUAP duration increases with age due to ongoing motor unit remodeling and loss of anterior horn cells with compensatory reinnervation
- **Effort and cooperation:** The recruitment assessment is entirely dependent on voluntary effort. Pain, anxiety, and upper motor neuron lesions all limit the ability to assess recruitment accurately

### 5.9.3 Common Sources of Error

- Misidentifying endplate activity as fibrillation potentials (remember: endplate spikes have initial negative deflection and irregular firing)
- Failing to achieve complete relaxation during the assessment of spontaneous activity
- Sampling too few muscles or sampling only distal muscles in a suspected radiculopathy
- Ignoring insertional activity changes that may represent the earliest sign of denervation
- Confusing reduced recruitment due to poor effort or upper motor neuron disease with neurogenic reduced recruitment

## 5.10 AANEM Reporting Standards for Needle EMG [COMPREHENSIVE]

The American Association of Neuromuscular and Electrodiagnostic Medicine (AANEM) has established minimum reporting standards for needle EMG. A complete needle EMG report should include the following for each muscle examined:

1. **Muscle name and side** (with notation of limb, right or left)
2. **Insertional activity** (normal, increased, or decreased)
3. **Spontaneous activity** (presence or absence of specific abnormal discharges, with grading)
4. **Voluntary motor unit assessment:**
   - MUAP amplitude
   - MUAP duration
   - Number of phases
   - Recruitment pattern

Results should be presented in tabular format to allow an independent reviewer to assess the distribution of findings across myotomes and peripheral nerve territories. This distribution analysis is essential for pattern recognition and accurate localization.

## Review Questions [BOARD]

1. A patient presents with acute onset of foot drop three days ago. Needle EMG of the tibialis anterior shows no fibrillation potentials or positive sharp waves. Only one motor unit can be recruited, firing at 30 Hz. What is the most likely interpretation?

   **Answer:** The absence of fibrillation potentials is expected at three days post-injury, as fibrillations require two to three weeks to appear in distal muscles. The finding of a single motor unit firing at 30 Hz represents severely reduced recruitment, which is the earliest EMG finding after acute axonal injury. This pattern is consistent with acute axonal loss (e.g., fibular neuropathy or L5 radiculopathy).

2. A 55-year-old woman has proximal weakness and an elevated CK. Needle EMG of the deltoid shows fibrillation potentials (2+), short-duration, low-amplitude polyphasic MUAPs, and early recruitment. What is the most likely diagnosis?

   **Answer:** The combination of fibrillation potentials with short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs and early recruitment is the classic myopathic pattern with active membrane irritability. Combined with proximal weakness and elevated CK, this pattern is most consistent with an inflammatory myopathy (polymyositis or dermatomyositis). The fibrillations arise from segmental fiber necrosis, not denervation.

3. A repetitive discharge is recorded at a constant frequency of 15 Hz with abrupt onset and cessation. The waveform is polyphasic with identical morphology on each repetition. What type of discharge is this?

   **Answer:** Complex repetitive discharge (CRD). The constant, fixed frequency with abrupt onset and cessation and identical waveform morphology on each repetition are pathognomonic for CRDs. Myotonic discharges wax and wane in frequency and amplitude; neuromyotonic discharges fire at much higher frequencies (>150 Hz).
