## 13.1 Introduction: The EDX Report as a Clinical Document [BOARD]

The electrodiagnostic report is the permanent record of the EDX consultation and the primary means by which the findings of the electrodiagnostic evaluation are communicated to the referring physician, the patient's medical record, and other healthcare providers. A well-constructed report transforms raw electrical data into clinically meaningful information that guides patient management.

The EDX evaluation is not merely a laboratory test; it is a medical consultation. The electrodiagnostician is expected to evaluate the clinical problem, formulate a differential diagnosis, design and execute a tailored testing protocol, interpret the data in clinical context, and communicate findings in a clear, actionable report. This consultative model distinguishes EDX medicine from purely technical procedures.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The EDX report should be viewed as a consultation letter, not a laboratory result slip. It should demonstrate clinical reasoning, correlate findings with the patient's presentation, and provide the referring physician with information that influences management decisions.

## 13.2 AANEM Reporting Standards: Mandatory Report Sections [BOARD]

The American Association of Neuromuscular and Electrodiagnostic Medicine (AANEM) has established clear standards for the content and format of EDX reports. Every compliant report must contain four essential sections.

### 13.2.1 Section 1: Clinical Information

This section provides the clinical context necessary for understanding why the study was performed and how the results should be interpreted.

**Required elements:**
- Patient demographics (name, date of birth, date of study)
- Referring physician and referral diagnosis/clinical question
- Relevant clinical history: chief complaint, symptom onset and duration, symptom characteristics (location, quality, exacerbating/alleviating factors)
- Relevant medical history: prior EDX studies, surgical history, medications, comorbid conditions (diabetes, thyroid disease, renal disease)
- Physical examination findings: strength testing, sensory examination, reflex assessment, relevant positive and negative findings

**Rationale:** The clinical information section demonstrates that the electrodiagnostician has personally evaluated the patient and understands the clinical question being addressed. It also provides context for interpreting the EDX data -- the same NCS abnormality may carry different significance in a 25-year-old athlete versus a 75-year-old diabetic patient.

### 13.2.2 Section 2: Nerve Conduction Study Data

NCS data should be presented in a **non-narrative, preferably tabular format** that allows the reader to independently verify the data and assess the quality of the study.

**Required NCS table elements:**

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| Nerve | Name of the nerve studied |
| Side | Right (R) or Left (L) |
| Stimulation site | Specific stimulation location(s) |
| Recording site | Muscle or sensory point recorded |
| Latency (ms) | Onset latency (motor) or peak latency (sensory) |
| Amplitude | CMAP (mV) or SNAP (microV) |
| Distance (cm) | Distance between stimulation and recording sites (or between two stimulation sites) |
| Conduction velocity (m/s) | Calculated from distance and latency difference |
| Reference values | Normal values for the laboratory |

**Example NCS Table Format:**

| Nerve | Side | Stim Site | Record Site | Lat (ms) | Amp | Dist (cm) | CV (m/s) | Normal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Median Motor | R | Wrist | APB | 3.8 | 8.2 mV | 7 | -- | <4.2 ms; >4.0 mV |
| Median Motor | R | Elbow | APB | 8.0 | 7.9 mV | 23 | 54.8 | >49 m/s |
| Ulnar Sensory | R | Wrist | Digit 5 | 2.9 | 18 uV | 14 | 48.3 | <3.5 ms; >10 uV |

**Additional NCS data to include when performed:**
- F-wave latencies (minimum latency, F-wave persistence)
- H-reflex latency and amplitude
- Repetitive nerve stimulation results (decrement/increment values)
- Comparison studies (contralateral or ipsilateral for focal neuropathies)

### 13.2.3 Section 3: Needle EMG Data

Needle EMG data should also be presented in tabular format with standardized grading.

**Required EMG table elements:**

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| Muscle | Name of muscle tested (include nerve and root innervation) |
| Side | R or L |
| Insertional activity | Normal, increased, or decreased |
| Spontaneous activity: Fibrillations | Graded 0 to 4+ |
| Spontaneous activity: PSWs | Graded 0 to 4+ |
| Spontaneous activity: Fasciculations | Present or absent |
| Spontaneous activity: Other | CRDs, myotonic discharges, etc. |
| MUAP amplitude | Normal, increased, or decreased |
| MUAP duration | Normal, increased, or decreased |
| MUAP polyphasia | Normal or increased |
| Recruitment | Normal, decreased (neurogenic), or early (myopathic) |

**Standard grading scale for spontaneous activity:**

| Grade | Description | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | None | Normal |
| 1+ | Persistent activity in at least 2 areas | Mild denervation |
| 2+ | Moderate activity in 3 or more areas | Moderate denervation |
| 3+ | Abundant activity filling the screen in all areas | Severe denervation |
| 4+ | Continuous activity filling the screen; no identifiable baseline | Very severe denervation |

### 13.2.4 Section 4: Diagnostic Interpretation

The interpretation section is arguably the most important part of the report. It synthesizes the clinical and electrodiagnostic data into a coherent diagnostic impression.

**Required interpretation elements:**

1. **Summary of findings:** Brief statement of normal and abnormal results
2. **Electrophysiological diagnosis:** Specific EDX diagnosis (e.g., "findings consistent with a moderate, chronic, sensorimotor, axonal polyneuropathy, length-dependent pattern")
3. **Clinical correlation:** How the EDX findings relate to the patient's symptoms and clinical presentation
4. **Limitations:** Any factors that may have affected the reliability of the study (temperature, patient cooperation, edema, technical difficulties)
5. **Comparison with prior studies:** If previous EDX studies are available, note interval changes
6. **Recommendations:** When appropriate, suggest additional testing, follow-up, or clinical actions

> **Clinical Pearl:** The diagnostic interpretation should answer the referring physician's clinical question. If the referral asks "rule out carpal tunnel syndrome," the interpretation should specifically address whether carpal tunnel syndrome is present, absent, or indeterminate, and at what severity.

## 13.3 The Systematic EDX Consultation: From Referral to Report [BOARD]

The complete EDX consultation follows a structured sequence that ensures thorough evaluation and appropriate study selection.

### Step 1: Review the Referral

- Identify the clinical question being asked
- Review available medical records, imaging, laboratory results
- Note any contraindications to testing (anticoagulation for needle EMG, pacemaker/defibrillator considerations)

### Step 2: Take a Focused History

- Characterize the symptoms: onset, duration, progression, distribution, quality
- Identify risk factors: occupation, medical comorbidities, medications, family history
- Assess functional impact

### Step 3: Perform a Focused Physical Examination

- Motor examination: strength testing in relevant myotomes/nerve territories
- Sensory examination: light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception
- Reflex testing
- Special tests: Tinel sign, Phalen test, provocative maneuvers as indicated

### Step 4: Formulate a Differential Diagnosis

Based on the clinical evaluation, generate a ranked differential diagnosis that will guide the EDX testing protocol. The testing plan should be designed to confirm or exclude the most likely and most important diagnoses on the list.

### Step 5: Design the Testing Protocol

Select NCS and EMG tests based on the differential diagnosis, following AANEM minimum study guidelines. The protocol should be individualized for each patient and may be modified during the study based on evolving findings.

### Step 6: Perform the Study

Execute the planned protocol with attention to technical quality:
- Temperature control (limb surface temperature >32 degrees C for NCS; >34 degrees C near-nerve)
- Proper electrode placement
- Supramaximal stimulation
- Systematic muscle sampling for EMG

### Step 7: Interpret and Report

Synthesize all data into the four-section report described above.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The EDX examination should be a dynamic process. If initial NCS reveal an unexpected pattern (e.g., conduction block suggesting MMNCB when radiculopathy was the referral diagnosis), the protocol should be modified in real time to investigate the actual diagnosis. A rigid, pre-programmed protocol that cannot be adapted misses diagnoses.

## 13.4 The AANEM Model Policy [BOARD]

The AANEM Model Policy for Nerve Conduction Studies and Needle Electromyography establishes standards for who should perform EDX studies, what equipment is required, and how studies should be conducted and reported.

### 13.4.1 Qualified Practitioners

EDX evaluations should be performed by physicians trained in the diagnosis and management of neuromuscular disease. In practice, this means almost exclusively neurologists and physiatrists (physical medicine and rehabilitation physicians). Training requirements include:

- Completion of an accredited residency in neurology or PM&R
- Training in clinical and electrodiagnostic aspects of neuromuscular disease
- Ongoing competency maintenance

### 13.4.2 NCS and EMG Must Be Combined

A critical policy position is that NCS and needle EMG should be performed together as complementary components of a single evaluation. NCS alone without EMG is insufficient for most diagnoses.

**Conditions requiring needle EMG for diagnosis:**
- Radiculopathy
- Plexopathy
- Motor neuron disease
- Myopathy
- Many peripheral nerve disorders

**The AANEM explicitly states** that performing NCS without needle EMG leads to missed diagnoses and misleading results. When NCS is relied upon without EMG confirmation, important diagnostic information is lost.

### 13.4.3 Equipment Standards

- Equipment must provide assessment of all parameters of recorded signals
- Equipment designed solely for screening purposes is NOT acceptable for diagnostic studies
- Calibration and maintenance standards must be followed
- Display capabilities must allow for adequate waveform analysis

### 13.4.4 Physician Presence

The physician must be physically present during the EDX evaluation. The physician is responsible for:
- Determining the specific nerves and muscles to be tested
- Adjusting the protocol based on evolving findings
- Performing the needle EMG examination
- Interpreting all data
- Generating the report

NCS may be performed by a trained technician under the physician's direct supervision, but the physician retains responsibility for protocol design, quality control, and interpretation.

## 13.5 AANEM Choosing Wisely: Top 5 Recommendations [BOARD]

The AANEM Choosing Wisely campaign identifies five practices in electrodiagnostic medicine that are commonly performed but lack evidence of benefit, waste resources, or may cause harm.

### Recommendation 1: Do NOT Perform Needle EMG for Isolated Neck or Back Pain After Motor Vehicle Accident

**Rationale:** Isolated axial neck or back pain without limb symptoms (radiculopathy) is not a diagnosis made by EMG. Axial pain arises from muscles, ligaments, facet joints, and discs -- structures not evaluated by EDX testing. EMG is unlikely to be helpful and will not change management.

**When EMG IS indicated for neck/back pain:**
- When there are symptoms or signs suggesting radiculopathy (dermatomal pain, weakness, reflex changes, sensory deficits in a limb)
- When there is clinical concern for an alternative diagnosis (motor neuron disease, plexopathy)

### Recommendation 2: Do NOT Perform 4-Limb NCS/EMG After Trauma Without Clinical Indication for Each Limb

**Rationale:** Testing all four extremities in every trauma patient is not indicated unless there are specific symptoms or signs in each limb. Routine 4-limb testing is overutilization that increases cost and patient discomfort without improving diagnostic accuracy.

**When multi-limb testing IS appropriate:**
- Suspected polyneuropathy (need bilateral comparison)
- Suspected motor neuron disease (need multiple body regions)
- Specific symptoms or signs in each tested limb

### Recommendation 3: Do NOT Perform NCS Alone (Without EMG) for Radiculopathy Evaluation

**Rationale:** NCS cannot diagnose radiculopathy. In radiculopathy, the lesion is at the nerve root, proximal to the dorsal root ganglion for sensory fibers. Sensory NCS are typically normal because the peripheral sensory nerve and its cell body are intact. Motor NCS may be normal or show only mild CMAP amplitude reduction. Only needle EMG can demonstrate the denervation pattern (fibrillation potentials, chronic neurogenic MUP changes) in a myotomal distribution that confirms radiculopathy.

> **Clinical Pearl:** This is one of the most commonly violated Choosing Wisely recommendations. Ordering "NCS only" for a patient with suspected radiculopathy is diagnostically inadequate. If radiculopathy is on the differential, needle EMG is mandatory.

### Recommendation 4: Do NOT Order MRI of Spine/Brain for Isolated Peripheral Neuropathy

**Rationale:** When the clinical and EDX evaluation clearly demonstrates a peripheral neuropathy without signs or symptoms suggesting central nervous system involvement, MRI of the spine or brain is unnecessary and unlikely to provide diagnostically useful information. Peripheral neuropathy is diagnosed and characterized by NCS/EMG, not by imaging.

**When imaging IS indicated in neuropathy:**
- Upper motor neuron signs (suggesting myelopathy or central lesion)
- Atypical distribution suggesting structural compression
- Suspected lumbosacral plexopathy (MRI of pelvis/plexus, not spine)

### Recommendation 5: Do NOT Use Neuromuscular Ultrasound to Grade Carpal Tunnel Syndrome Severity

**Rationale:** While neuromuscular ultrasound has a growing role in diagnosing CTS (median nerve cross-sectional area at the wrist), it is not reliable for grading severity. EDX severity grading (mild = sensory only; moderate = sensory + motor latency prolongation; severe = absent SNAPs, low CMAPs, denervation) remains the standard.

### Table 13.1: AANEM Choosing Wisely Summary

| # | Recommendation | Key Point |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No EMG for isolated neck/back pain after MVA | Axial pain does not require EDX |
| 2 | No 4-limb NCS/EMG after trauma without indication per limb | Test only symptomatic limbs |
| 3 | No NCS alone for radiculopathy | Needle EMG is required for radiculopathy |
| 4 | No spine/brain MRI for isolated peripheral neuropathy | EDX characterizes neuropathy; MRI adds nothing |
| 5 | No ultrasound severity grading for CTS | EDX remains standard for severity grading |

## 13.6 Pattern Recognition in EDX: Diagnostic Frameworks [BOARD]

One of the most important skills in electrodiagnostic medicine is recognizing characteristic patterns of NCS and EMG abnormalities that point to specific diagnoses. The following framework organizes the most commonly encountered patterns.

### 13.6.1 The Five Fundamental EDX Patterns

**Pattern 1: Focal Mononeuropathy (Entrapment)**
- Focal slowing or conduction block across a specific anatomical site
- Reduced SNAP/CMAP amplitude distal to the lesion (if axonal loss)
- EMG denervation limited to muscles innervated by that nerve distal to the lesion
- Proximal muscles innervated by the same nerve are normal
- Other nerves are normal

**Pattern 2: Polyneuropathy (Length-Dependent)**
- Symmetric abnormalities, worse distally
- Legs more affected than arms
- Sural SNAP often the first abnormality
- EMG denervation in a distal-to-proximal gradient
- Multiple nerves affected in a predictable pattern

**Pattern 3: Radiculopathy**
- Normal sensory NCS (lesion proximal to DRG)
- EMG denervation in a myotomal distribution including paraspinal muscles
- Motor NCS may show reduced CMAP in severe cases
- F-waves and H-reflexes may be prolonged or absent

**Pattern 4: Motor Neuron Disease**
- Normal sensory NCS (critical finding)
- Widespread active and chronic denervation across multiple body regions
- Fibrillation potentials + large MUAPs + reduced recruitment
- No conduction block (distinguishes from MMNCB)

**Pattern 5: Myopathy**
- Normal NCS (usually)
- Short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUAPs
- Early recruitment
- Proximal muscles predominantly affected
- Fibrillation potentials present in inflammatory/necrotic myopathies

### 13.6.2 Red Flags: EDX Findings Requiring Urgent Attention [BOARD]

Certain EDX patterns require immediate communication with the referring physician:

| Finding | Possible Diagnosis | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Widespread acute denervation in multiple regions with normal SNAPs | ALS | High -- prompt neurology referral |
| Acute demyelinating pattern (GBS-like) | GBS | High -- respiratory monitoring, treatment |
| Dense acute denervation in new-onset rapid weakness | Acute axonal neuropathy, vasculitic neuropathy | High -- workup for underlying cause |
| Significant decrement on RNS in a patient with bulbar weakness | MG or LEMS | Moderate-high -- antibody testing, cancer screening (LEMS) |
| Low CMAP with increment | LEMS | High -- cancer screening (SCLC) |
| Myopathic EMG with very high CK (>10,000 IU/L) | Active necrotizing myopathy | High -- urgent rheumatology/neurology referral |

### 13.6.3 Putting It All Together: A Diagnostic Algorithm [COMPREHENSIVE]

When confronted with an EDX study, the following systematic approach ensures thorough analysis:

**Step 1: Assess sensory NCS**
- Normal SNAPs: Consider motor neuron disease, NMJ disorder, myopathy, pure motor neuropathy, radiculopathy
- Abnormal SNAPs: Consider polyneuropathy, plexopathy, ganglionopathy, sensorimotor neuropathy

**Step 2: Assess motor NCS**
- Normal CMAPs: Sensory neuropathy, mild myopathy, or normal study
- Reduced CMAPs with slow velocity: Demyelinating neuropathy
- Reduced CMAPs with normal velocity: Axonal neuropathy, myopathy (check duration), or NMJ disorder
- Conduction block present: Acquired demyelination (CIDP, MMNCB, GBS)

**Step 3: Assess EMG**
- Short-duration MUAPs + early recruitment: Myopathy
- Long-duration MUAPs + reduced recruitment: Neurogenic process
- Mixed pattern: Consider IBM, chronic myopathy with secondary neurogenic changes
- Active denervation distribution: Determines localization (myotomal = radiculopathy; nerve territory = mononeuropathy; widespread = MND or polyneuropathy)

**Step 4: Integrate NCS + EMG + clinical context**
- The final diagnosis must be internally consistent across all data
- Discrepancies between NCS and EMG warrant explanation
- Always correlate with the clinical presentation

## 13.7 Common Pitfalls in EDX Report Writing [BOARD]

### 13.6.1 Data Presentation Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Problem | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Narrative-only NCS data | Reader cannot verify values or assess quality | Always present NCS data in tabular format |
| Missing reference values | Reader cannot determine what is "abnormal" | Include laboratory reference values in tables |
| Reporting raw data without interpretation | Data without clinical context is meaningless | Always include a diagnostic interpretation section |
| Omitting normal results | Reader cannot distinguish "not tested" from "normal" | Explicitly state all normal NCS and EMG results |
| Failing to note technical limitations | Over-confident interpretation of suboptimal data | Document temperature, cooperation, edema, or other limiting factors |

### 13.6.2 Clinical Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Problem | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| "Shotgun" testing | Excessive tests increase cost and discomfort without improving accuracy | Test the minimum needed for accurate diagnosis |
| NCS without EMG | Missed radiculopathy, myopathy, MND diagnoses | Always perform both NCS and EMG |
| Failure to correlate with clinical context | EDX findings in isolation can be misleading | Include clinical information and correlate findings |
| Ignoring temperature | Cold limbs produce falsely prolonged latencies and slow velocities | Document temperature; warm if <32 degrees C |
| Not testing contralateral side | Cannot determine if abnormality is focal vs. generalized | Compare with contralateral when evaluating focal lesions |
| Overcalling borderline values | Normal variants misdiagnosed as pathology | Use clinical correlation; recognize that borderline values may be normal |
| Missing superimposed pathology | Single diagnosis mentality | Consider polyneuropathy + entrapment, or radiculopathy + neuropathy |

### 13.6.3 Interpretation Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Problem | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnosing radiculopathy by NCS alone | NCS cannot diagnose radiculopathy | Needle EMG is essential for this diagnosis |
| Diagnosing CTS based only on symptoms | Clinical diagnosis without EDX confirmation risks inaccuracy | EDX provides objective severity grading and excludes mimics |
| Attributing all findings to one diagnosis | Missing concurrent pathology | Systematically consider multiple diagnoses |
| Ignoring the clinical presentation | EMG findings without clinical context can be misinterpreted | Always correlate with history and examination |
| Failing to consider timing | EDX findings depend on disease stage (acute vs. chronic) | Factor timing of symptoms into interpretation |

## 13.7 Clinical-EDX Correlation Principles [BOARD]

### 13.7.1 The EDX Examination as an Extension of the Physical Examination

The AANEM emphasizes that the EDX evaluation is an extension of the physical examination, not an isolated laboratory test. The clinician performing EDX testing has a unique advantage: they can directly examine the patient, correlate physical findings with electrical data in real time, and modify the testing protocol based on emerging results.

### 13.7.2 Principles of Clinical-EDX Correlation

**Principle 1: Localization must be anatomically consistent**

EDX findings should localize to a single anatomical site or recognized pattern. For example, if NCS suggest a median neuropathy at the wrist, the EMG should show denervation limited to APB/opponens pollicis (median-innervated thenar muscles), not in ulnar or radial-innervated muscles.

**Principle 2: Severity should match clinical presentation**

Severe EDX abnormalities (absent SNAPs, severely reduced CMAPs, dense fibrillation potentials) should correspond to clinically significant weakness and sensory loss. A mismatch (e.g., severe EDX abnormalities with normal clinical examination) should prompt reconsideration.

**Principle 3: Time course must be physiologically plausible**

EDX findings evolve predictably after nerve injury:

| Time After Injury | Expected EDX Finding |
|---|---|
| 0-3 days | NCS may be normal; no fibrillation potentials |
| 3-5 days | NCS abnormal distal to lesion (Wallerian degeneration ongoing for motor fibers) |
| 7-10 days | Wallerian degeneration complete for sensory fibers (SNAP loss) |
| 10-14 days | Fibrillation potentials begin to appear in proximal muscles |
| 14-21 days | Fibrillation potentials in intermediate muscles |
| 21-35 days | Fibrillation potentials in distal muscles |
| Months to years | Chronic reinnervation changes (large MUPs, decreased recruitment) |

**Principle 4: Normal EDX does not exclude all pathology**

- Small-fiber neuropathy: normal NCS (tests large fibers only)
- Very early disease: EDX changes may not yet have developed
- Intermittent conditions (periodic paralysis): normal between episodes
- Mild or well-compensated lesions: within normal limits on testing
- Central nervous system pathology: EDX tests the peripheral nervous system

**Principle 5: Abnormal EDX does not always indicate disease**

- Normal variants (Martin-Gruber anastomosis, accessory peroneal nerve)
- Age-related changes (H-reflex latency increases, SNAP amplitudes decrease)
- Technical factors (cold temperature, submaximal stimulation, electrode placement)

### 13.7.3 Elements of the Diagnostic Impression

A complete diagnostic impression should address:

1. **What is the diagnosis?** (e.g., moderate carpal tunnel syndrome, right)
2. **What is the pathophysiology?** (e.g., demyelination at the wrist with secondary axonal loss)
3. **What is the severity?** (e.g., moderate based on prolonged motor latency and low SNAP amplitude)
4. **What is the acuity?** (e.g., chronic with active denervation suggesting ongoing process)
5. **What is the clinical significance?** (e.g., correlates with reported hand numbness and weakness)
6. **What is NOT present?** (e.g., no evidence of polyneuropathy, cervical radiculopathy, or ulnar neuropathy)

## 13.8 When EMG Is NOT Indicated [BOARD]

Understanding when EDX testing is inappropriate is as important as knowing when to order it.

### Table 13.2: Conditions Where EMG Is Generally Not Indicated

| Condition | Reason EDX Is Not Helpful |
|---|---|
| Isolated axial neck/back pain without limb symptoms | Axial pain generators not assessed by EDX |
| Fibromyalgia | No neuromuscular pathology; EDX will be normal |
| Central nervous system disorders (stroke, MS, SCI) | EDX tests peripheral nervous system only; will be normal unless concurrent PNS disease |
| Muscle soreness or cramps without weakness | Usually benign; EDX not informative |
| Within 1-2 weeks of acute nerve injury | Too early for Wallerian degeneration and denervation to manifest; delay testing to 3-4 weeks |
| Monitoring known neuropathy without clinical change | Repeat testing without new symptoms rarely changes management |
| "Screening" healthy asymptomatic individuals | High false-positive rate; no clinical benefit |

### Table 13.3: Conditions Where EMG IS Strongly Indicated

| Clinical Presentation | EDX Value |
|---|---|
| Suspected entrapment neuropathy (CTS, cubital tunnel) | Confirms diagnosis, grades severity, guides treatment |
| Suspected radiculopathy (with limb symptoms) | Localizes level, assesses acuity and severity |
| Progressive weakness of unknown etiology | Localizes lesion (nerve, NMJ, muscle, motor neuron) |
| Suspected motor neuron disease | Documents widespread denervation, excludes mimics |
| Suspected myopathy | Confirms myopathic pattern, guides biopsy |
| Suspected NMJ disorder | RNS/SFEMG provide diagnostic confirmation |
| Suspected polyneuropathy | Characterizes type, severity, and distribution |
| Weakness or numbness not explained by clinical evaluation | Provides objective physiological data |

## 13.9 Electrodiagnostic Quality Metrics and Benchmarks [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.9.1 Temperature Standards

Temperature management is one of the most critical quality metrics in the EDX laboratory. Cool limb temperature is the single most common cause of false-positive NCS abnormalities (falsely prolonged latencies, falsely slow conduction velocities).

**Temperature requirements:**

| Measurement Site | Minimum Temperature | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Hand/wrist surface | 32 degrees C (90 degrees F) | Surface thermometer or infrared thermometer |
| Foot/ankle surface | 30 degrees C (86 degrees F) | Surface thermometer or infrared thermometer |
| Near-nerve (deep) | 34 degrees C (93 degrees F) | Near-nerve temperature probe (research) |

**Warming methods:**
- Hot packs, heating pads, warm water immersion for 5-10 minutes
- Heat lamps (ensure consistent temperature)
- Document pre-warming temperature and post-warming temperature in the report

**Temperature-related NCS changes (approximate):**
- Conduction velocity decreases by approximately 2 m/s per 1 degree C decrease
- Distal latency increases by approximately 0.2 ms per 1 degree C decrease
- SNAP and CMAP amplitude may increase slightly with cooling (prolonged channel opening)
- F-wave latencies increase with cooling

### 13.9.2 Stimulus Artifact Management

Stimulus artifact contamination is a frequent source of measurement error, particularly for sensory NCS where the SNAP amplitude is small relative to the stimulus artifact.

**Strategies for reducing stimulus artifact:**
- Proper grounding electrode placement (between stimulating and recording electrodes)
- Maintaining clean, dry skin at electrode sites
- Reducing inter-electrode impedance
- Rotating the stimulator anode position
- Using the artifact rejection and averaging capabilities of the EMG machine
- Adjusting stimulus duration (shorter pulses produce less artifact)

### 13.9.3 Averaging Techniques

Averaging (signal averaging) improves signal-to-noise ratio for small-amplitude responses (especially SNAPs). The signal-to-noise ratio improves proportionally to the square root of the number of averaged responses: averaging 4 responses doubles the signal-to-noise ratio; averaging 16 responses quadruples it. However, excessive averaging can introduce artifact from stimulus-locked noise and should be used judiciously.

## 13.10 The EDX Evaluation in Special Clinical Situations [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.10.1 Timing of EDX After Acute Nerve Injury

Understanding the temporal evolution of EDX findings after acute nerve injury is essential for appropriate study timing and interpretation. The process of Wallerian degeneration determines when abnormalities appear.

**Wallerian Degeneration Timeline:**

| Time After Injury | Motor NCS Changes | Sensory NCS Changes | EMG Changes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-24 hours | Normal (nerve still conducting) | Normal | Normal |
| 1-3 days | Distal CMAP may begin to decrease | Normal (sensory degeneration slower) | Normal |
| 3-5 days | Distal CMAP falls significantly | SNAP beginning to decrease | Normal (too early for fibrillations) |
| 5-7 days | Distal CMAP at nadir | SNAP continuing to decrease | Normal or minimal increased insertional activity |
| 7-10 days | Established low/absent distal CMAP | SNAP at nadir or absent | Fibrillations may begin in muscles closest to injury |
| 14-21 days | Stable | Stable | Fibrillations established in proximal muscles; beginning in more distal muscles |
| 21-35 days | Stable | Stable | Fibrillations present in distal muscles; full denervation pattern established |
| Months | Slow improvement if reinnervation occurs | Slow improvement if regeneration occurs | Nascent MUAPs (small, polyphasic) if reinnervation; eventual large MUAPs |

**Optimal timing for initial EDX study:** 3-4 weeks after injury for most acute nerve injuries. This allows sufficient time for Wallerian degeneration to complete and fibrillation potentials to appear, while still detecting acute changes.

**Exception:** In acute compressive or demyelinating lesions (neurapraxia), NCS abnormalities (conduction block) are present immediately, so early testing can be informative for localization and prognostication even before denervation develops.

### 13.10.2 EDX Evaluation of the Postoperative Patient

Postoperative nerve injuries are common referrals to the EDX laboratory. Important considerations include:

- **Timing:** At least 3-4 weeks post-surgery for optimal sensitivity
- **Baseline:** Comparison with preoperative EDX (if available) is invaluable
- **Positioning injuries:** Ulnar neuropathy at the elbow, fibular neuropathy at the fibular head (from lithotomy position), brachial plexus injuries (from arm positioning)
- **Direct surgical nerve injury:** Pattern depends on the nerve involved and the completeness of the lesion
- **Tourniquet palsy:** Predominantly demyelinating injury at the tourniquet site; good prognosis

### 13.10.3 EDX in the Pediatric Population

Special considerations for pediatric EDX testing:

- **Normative values:** Nerve conduction velocities in neonates and infants are approximately 50% of adult values, reaching adult values by age 3-5 years
- **Cooperation:** Limited; sedation may be needed for needle EMG
- **Indications:** Floppy infant evaluation (SMA, congenital myopathy, congenital neuropathy), brachial plexus birth palsy, childhood neuropathies (CMT)
- **SMA diagnosis:** EDX may show fibrillation potentials and chronic neurogenic changes; genetic testing has largely replaced EDX for initial diagnosis

### 13.10.4 EDX in the ICU Setting

EDX testing in critically ill patients presents unique challenges:

- **Electrical interference:** ICU equipment generates significant 60 Hz artifact; notch filters and electrode impedance optimization are essential
- **Temperature control:** Critically ill patients are often hypothermic; cold extremities can falsely prolong latencies and slow velocities
- **Patient cooperation:** Voluntary EMG may be impossible; assessment limited to spontaneous activity and direct muscle stimulation
- **Edema:** Subcutaneous edema increases the distance between recording electrodes and nerve/muscle, reducing CMAP and SNAP amplitudes
- **CIM vs. CIP distinction:** See Chapter 11 for detailed differentiation
- **Prognosis:** EDX findings in the ICU predict long-term functional outcomes; intact SNAP amplitudes (favoring CIM over CIP) generally predict better recovery

## 13.11 Normal Variants and Technical Pitfalls That Mimic Disease [BOARD]

### 13.11.1 Martin-Gruber Anastomosis (MGA)

Martin-Gruber anastomosis is the most common peripheral nerve anomaly, present in approximately 15-30% of the population. It consists of a crossover of motor fibers from the median nerve to the ulnar nerve in the forearm.

**EDX findings that suggest MGA:**
- Higher CMAP amplitude with proximal (elbow) than distal (wrist) median stimulation when recording from APB
- Apparent "conduction block" on median motor NCS (higher amplitude proximally vs. distally) -- NOT true conduction block
- Initial positive deflection on median CMAP at APB with wrist stimulation (volume-conducted from ulnar-innervated muscles that are not activated by distal median stimulation but are activated by proximal stimulation via the anastomosis)

**Clinical significance:** MGA can be misinterpreted as median neuropathy (falsely low distal CMAP) or as conduction block. Recognizing this anomaly avoids misdiagnosis.

### 13.11.2 Accessory Deep Peroneal Nerve

An accessory branch of the deep fibular (peroneal) nerve passes behind the lateral malleolus to innervate part of the extensor digitorum brevis (EDB). Present in approximately 20-28% of individuals.

**EDX findings:**
- Higher CMAP amplitude with proximal (below fibular head) than distal (ankle) fibular nerve stimulation when recording from EDB
- Stimulation behind the lateral malleolus produces an additional CMAP component
- Can be misinterpreted as fibular neuropathy at the ankle

### 13.11.3 Volume Conduction and Co-activation Artifacts

- **Co-activation:** Nearby muscles may be inadvertently activated, contributing to the recorded CMAP. For example, stimulating the median nerve may co-activate ulnar-innervated muscles if the stimulus intensity is too high.
- **Volume conduction:** CMAPs from distant muscles may be conducted to the recording electrode, producing an initial positive deflection or a distorted waveform.

### 13.11.4 Age-Related Changes in NCS

Normal aging affects nerve conduction parameters. The electrodiagnostician must use age-appropriate reference values:

| Parameter | Change with Aging | Approximate Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| SNAP amplitudes | Decrease (~1 microV/decade after age 20) | Sural SNAP may be <5 microV or absent after age 70 |
| Conduction velocity | Decreases (~1-2 m/s per decade) | Mild slowing after age 60 |
| F-wave latency | Increases | Use height-adjusted age-corrected norms |
| H-reflex latency | Increases | May be absent unilaterally in ~25% of elderly; bilateral absence is normal variant |
| CMAP amplitude | Mildly decreases | Moderate decline after age 70 |

> **Clinical Pearl:** An absent sural SNAP in a 75-year-old patient without symptoms may be a normal age-related finding, not necessarily evidence of polyneuropathy. Clinical correlation is essential. Conversely, normal NCS in a symptomatic elderly patient does not exclude a small-fiber neuropathy.

## 13.12 Physical Examination Findings That Guide EDX Protocol Design [BOARD]

The pre-EDX physical examination is not merely a formality -- it directly shapes the testing protocol and provides the clinical context essential for interpretation.

### Key Examination Components

**Motor examination:**
- Strength testing using MRC scale (0-5) in relevant myotomes/nerve territories
- Pattern of weakness: proximal vs. distal, symmetric vs. asymmetric, upper vs. lower extremity
- Atrophy distribution: focal (nerve territory), proximal (myopathy), distal (neuropathy), segmental (radiculopathy)
- Fasciculations: location, frequency (may suggest motor neuron disease)

**Sensory examination:**
- Light touch and pinprick testing in dermatomal and peripheral nerve distributions
- Vibration (tuning fork) and proprioception testing at toes and fingers
- Positive sensory findings (paresthesias, allodynia, hyperalgesia) vs. negative findings (numbness, loss of position sense)
- Sensory level (suggests myelopathy, not peripheral neuropathy)

**Reflex examination:**
- Deep tendon reflexes: areflexia (neuropathy, NMJ), hyporeflexia (neuropathy), hyperreflexia (UMN lesion)
- Reflex pattern: diffuse areflexia (polyneuropathy), focal loss (radiculopathy), hyperreflexia below a level (myelopathy)

**Special tests:**
- Tinel sign and Phalen test (CTS)
- Elbow flexion test (cubital tunnel)
- Froment sign (ulnar neuropathy)
- Scapular winging (long thoracic or spinal accessory neuropathy; FSHD)
- Gower sign (proximal weakness, often myopathy)
- Tongue fasciculations (ALS, Kennedy disease, SMA)
- Ice pack test for ptosis (MG -- ptosis improves with cold because it slows AChE, increasing ACh availability)

### How Examination Findings Shape the EDX Protocol

| Examination Finding | EDX Protocol Modification |
|---|---|
| Wrist pain with Tinel sign | Add median comparative studies; internal comparison |
| Foot drop | Add fibular motor across fibular head; short-segment stimulation if needed |
| Proximal weakness, elevated CK | Focus EMG on proximal muscles; add RNS |
| Diffuse fasciculations | Test multiple regions; emphasize sensory NCS (to distinguish ALS from Kennedy) |
| Ptosis and diplopia | Prioritize RNS (facial/proximal muscles); consider SFEMG |
| Dermatomal pain with reflex loss | Focus EMG on corresponding myotome + paraspinals |
| Stocking-glove numbness | Standard polyneuropathy battery (bilateral legs + one arm) |

## 13.13 Billing and Documentation Considerations [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.10.1 Medical Necessity

All EDX studies must be medically necessary and documented as such. The report should clearly demonstrate:
- The clinical indication for the study
- The relationship between the ordered tests and the clinical question
- Why each nerve and muscle was tested
- How the results inform clinical decision-making

### 13.10.2 Test Appropriateness

Studies should represent the minimum necessary to answer the clinical question. Excessive testing (e.g., routine 4-limb testing for a focal problem) is considered inappropriate unless justified by specific clinical findings. The Choosing Wisely recommendations provide a framework for appropriate test selection.

### 13.10.3 Supervision Requirements

Documentation should reflect that the physician was present, supervised the NCS, performed the needle EMG, interpreted all data, and generated the report. Automated or template-generated reports that do not reflect individualized interpretation may be considered inadequate.

## 13.13 Medicolegal Considerations in EDX Reporting [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.13.1 The EDX Report as a Legal Document

EDX reports become part of the permanent medical record and may be reviewed in medicolegal proceedings, disability determinations, and workers compensation cases. The electrodiagnostician should be aware that:

- Reports should be factual, objective, and free of speculation
- Technical limitations should be clearly documented
- Normal findings should be explicitly stated (not just omitted)
- The report should reflect what was actually tested and found, not what the referral source hoped to find
- Avoid stating diagnoses with more certainty than the data supports; use qualifiers appropriately ("consistent with," "suggestive of," "no evidence of")

### 13.13.2 Workers Compensation and Disability Evaluations

In workers compensation cases, the EDX evaluation may be used to:
- Confirm the presence of a work-related nerve injury
- Assess severity and expected recovery
- Determine whether the injury is acute, chronic, or pre-existing
- Evaluate for malingering or symptom magnification (EMG provides objective, involuntary data)

**Objective vs. subjective assessment:** NCS provide entirely objective data (not influenced by patient effort). EMG recruitment analysis requires voluntary activation and can be influenced by patient effort. However, the presence or absence of fibrillation potentials and the morphology of MUAPs are involuntary measures that cannot be faked.

### 13.13.3 Documentation of Normal Findings

Documenting what is normal is as important as documenting what is abnormal. A report that states only the abnormalities creates ambiguity about whether normal structures were actually tested. Best practice is to explicitly list:
- All nerves tested and their results (normal or abnormal)
- All muscles examined by EMG and their findings
- Normal studies that were performed to exclude specific diagnoses (e.g., "normal ulnar NCS exclude concurrent cubital tunnel syndrome")

## 13.14 Diagnostic Interpretation Writing: Examples [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 13.13.1 Example Interpretation: Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

"**Summary:** This study demonstrates a right median sensory neuropathy at the wrist of moderate severity with evidence of secondary axonal loss. The right median motor nerve conduction study shows prolonged distal motor latency. Needle EMG reveals chronic neurogenic motor unit changes in the abductor pollicis brevis with no active denervation. All other sensory and motor nerve conduction studies are within normal limits. There is no electrodiagnostic evidence of polyneuropathy, cervical radiculopathy, or ulnar neuropathy.

**Electrophysiological diagnosis:** Moderate right median neuropathy at the wrist (carpal tunnel syndrome), with evidence of both demyelination (prolonged sensory peak latency) and axonal loss (reduced SNAP amplitude). Chronic but currently stable based on the absence of fibrillation potentials in thenar muscles."

### 13.13.2 Example Interpretation: Polyneuropathy

"**Summary:** This study demonstrates a length-dependent, axonal, sensorimotor polyneuropathy of moderate severity. Sensory nerve action potentials are absent bilaterally in the lower extremities and mildly reduced in the upper extremities. Compound muscle action potential amplitudes are reduced in the lower extremities with mildly reduced conduction velocities in the axonal range. Needle EMG shows active denervation (2+ fibrillation potentials) in distal lower extremity muscles with chronic neurogenic motor unit changes in a length-dependent distribution. No superimposed focal entrapment neuropathy is identified.

**Electrophysiological diagnosis:** Moderate-severity, active, length-dependent, axonal sensorimotor polyneuropathy. The presence of active denervation indicates an ongoing process. Clinical correlation and appropriate laboratory workup are recommended to determine the underlying etiology."

### 13.13.3 Example Interpretation: Normal Study

"**Summary:** All motor and sensory nerve conduction studies are within normal limits. Needle electromyography of the right upper and lower extremities reveals normal insertional activity, no spontaneous activity, and normal motor unit action potential morphology with normal recruitment in all muscles tested.

**Electrophysiological diagnosis:** Normal electrodiagnostic study. There is no evidence of peripheral neuropathy, entrapment neuropathy, radiculopathy, myopathy, or neuromuscular junction disorder affecting the tested nerves and muscles. Clinical correlation is recommended. Please note that this study evaluates only large myelinated nerve fiber function; small-fiber neuropathy cannot be excluded by normal nerve conduction studies."

The following list provides standardized language for common electrodiagnostic diagnoses:

## 13.14 Summary: Board-High-Yield Review Points

1. **Four mandatory AANEM report sections:** Clinical information, NCS data (tabular), EMG data (tabular), Diagnostic interpretation. All four are required for a complete, compliant report.

2. **Tabular data presentation** is the AANEM standard. Narrative-only descriptions of NCS/EMG values are inadequate because they prevent independent verification.

3. **NCS and EMG should always be performed together.** NCS alone cannot diagnose radiculopathy, myopathy, or motor neuron disease. This is AANEM Choosing Wisely recommendation #3.

4. **AANEM Choosing Wisely Top 5:** (1) No EMG for isolated neck/back pain after MVA, (2) No 4-limb NCS/EMG without indication per limb, (3) No NCS alone for radiculopathy, (4) No spine MRI for isolated peripheral neuropathy, (5) No ultrasound severity grading for CTS.

5. **The systematic EDX consultation** follows seven steps: referral review, focused history, physical examination, differential diagnosis, protocol design, study execution, interpretation and reporting.

6. **AANEM Model Policy:** EDX performed by neurologists/physiatrists; physician must be present; screening-only equipment is not acceptable; NCS + EMG are complementary and should not be separated.

7. **Common pitfalls:** Missing temperature control, narrative-only data, NCS without EMG for radiculopathy, shotgun testing, failing to correlate with clinical context, overcalling borderline values.

8. **EDX is NOT indicated** for: isolated axial pain, fibromyalgia, central nervous system disorders, within 1-2 weeks of acute injury (too early), and asymptomatic screening.

9. **Timing matters:** Fibrillation potentials appear 10-35 days after injury in a proximal-to-distal gradient. Testing too early misses acute denervation; the optimal timing is 3-4 weeks post-injury for most conditions.

10. **The diagnostic interpretation** should address: diagnosis, pathophysiology, severity, acuity, clinical significance, and what was NOT found. It should answer the referring physician's specific clinical question.

> **Board Mnemonic -- "CCATS" for the five elements of a complete EDX report:** **C**linical information, **C**MAP/SNAP data (NCS in tabular format), **A**ctivity and MUAPs (EMG in tabular format), **T**echnical limitations, **S**ynthesis (diagnostic interpretation with clinical correlation).

## Board-Style Practice Questions

**Q1:** A referring physician orders "NCS only" for a patient with suspected L5 radiculopathy. Why is this order insufficient, and what should be done?

**A1:** NCS alone cannot diagnose radiculopathy (AANEM Choosing Wisely Recommendation #3). In radiculopathy, the sensory nerve lesion is proximal to the dorsal root ganglion, so SNAPs are normal. Motor NCS may show only mild CMAP reduction. Only needle EMG can demonstrate the myotomal denervation pattern (fibrillation potentials, neurogenic MUP changes in L5-innervated muscles including paraspinals) that confirms radiculopathy. The study should include both NCS and needle EMG.

**Q2:** An EDX report describes "reduced sural SNAP amplitude" but does not include reference values, does not mention temperature, and presents data in narrative rather than tabular format. What deficiencies exist?

**A2:** Three AANEM reporting standard deficiencies: (1) Reference values are not provided, preventing the reader from independently verifying that the value is truly abnormal; (2) Temperature documentation is absent -- cool limb temperature could produce a falsely abnormal result; (3) Narrative data presentation prevents independent verification. AANEM recommends tabular format for all NCS and EMG data.
