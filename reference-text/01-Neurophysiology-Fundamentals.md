## 1.1 Neuron Anatomy and Structure [BOARD]

### 1.1.1 The Neuron as the Functional Unit

The neuron is the fundamental signaling cell of the nervous system. In the context of electrodiagnostic (EDX) medicine, the peripheral motor and sensory neurons are of primary interest. Each neuron consists of three principal domains: the cell body (soma), dendrites, and axon. The soma houses the nucleus and protein synthesis machinery, including rough endoplasmic reticulum (Nissl bodies) and a prominent Golgi apparatus. Dendrites extend from the soma as branching processes that receive synaptic inputs from other neurons, while the axon is a single elongated projection that conducts action potentials away from the cell body toward its target -- be that a muscle fiber, sensory receptor, or another neuron.

The axon hillock, located at the junction of the soma and axon, is the site of action potential initiation. This region contains the highest density of voltage-gated sodium channels in the neuron, giving it the lowest threshold for depolarization. From the axon hillock, the action potential propagates along the axon toward the synaptic terminal.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The dorsal root ganglion (DRG) cell body of sensory neurons lies outside the spinal cord, within the intervertebral foramen. This anatomical fact is the basis for a critical EDX distinction: sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) remain normal in preganglionic lesions (e.g., radiculopathy) because the DRG cell body and its peripheral axon remain intact, even when the proximal portion of the sensory neuron is damaged.

### 1.1.2 Axonal Transport

The axon depends on the soma for synthesis of proteins, organelles, and membrane components. These materials are delivered through axonal transport systems:

- **Anterograde fast transport** (~200-400 mm/day): Carries vesicles, mitochondria, and membrane-associated proteins from soma toward the terminal. Powered by kinesin motors moving along microtubules.
- **Anterograde slow transport** (~0.5-5 mm/day): Transports cytoskeletal proteins (neurofilaments, tubulin) and soluble enzymes. This rate limits the speed of axonal regeneration after injury.
- **Retrograde transport** (~150-200 mm/day): Returns degraded materials, endosomes, and neurotrophic factors (e.g., nerve growth factor) from the terminal to the soma. Powered by dynein motors.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The rate of slow axonal transport (~1-3 mm/day) approximates the rate of peripheral nerve regeneration after axonal injury. This principle underlies the clinical estimation that reinnervation proceeds at roughly 1 inch (25 mm) per month. Clinicians use this to predict recovery timelines after nerve repair.

### 1.1.3 Myelination

Myelin is a lipid-rich insulating sheath that wraps around axons, dramatically increasing conduction velocity while conserving metabolic energy. In the peripheral nervous system (PNS), myelin is produced by Schwann cells; in the central nervous system (CNS), oligodendrocytes serve this role.

Key differences between PNS and CNS myelination:

| Feature | PNS (Schwann Cell) | CNS (Oligodendrocyte) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Cells per axon | One Schwann cell per internode | One oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons (up to 40-50) |
| Regeneration capacity | Robust (Schwann cells guide regeneration) | Poor |
| Basement membrane | Present | Absent |
| Node of Ranvier structure | Schwann cell microvilli at nodes | Astrocytic processes at nodes |

A single Schwann cell wraps one internode of one axon in the PNS. The internode length (distance between successive nodes of Ranvier) is proportional to axon diameter, typically 100 times the axon diameter. Larger axons therefore have longer internodes and faster conduction.

Unmyelinated axons in the PNS are also associated with Schwann cells but are not individually wrapped. Instead, multiple small-diameter unmyelinated axons are embedded within invaginations of a single Schwann cell, forming Remak bundles.

## 1.2 Peripheral Nerve Connective Tissue Layers [BOARD]

### 1.2.1 Three-Layer Architecture

A peripheral nerve trunk is organized into three concentric connective tissue compartments. Understanding these layers is essential for nerve injury classification and surgical planning.

**Endoneurium** is the innermost layer, surrounding individual nerve fibers (axons with their Schwann cells). It consists of a delicate meshwork of collagen fibers, fibroblasts, and capillaries. The endoneurial fluid is similar in composition to cerebrospinal fluid, maintaining a specialized microenvironment for the axon. The endoneurium also contains the blood-nerve barrier, formed by tight junctions of endoneurial capillary endothelial cells, which protects nerve fibers from circulating toxins and immune cells.

**Perineurium** is the middle layer that bundles nerve fibers into fascicles. It is composed of concentric layers of flattened perineurial cells connected by tight junctions, forming the primary diffusion barrier of peripheral nerve. The perineurium maintains intrafascicular pressure and provides tensile strength. Its barrier function is so effective that it can maintain the endoneurial microenvironment even when the outer epineurium is disrupted.

**Epineurium** is the outermost connective tissue sheath surrounding the entire nerve trunk and the interfascicular tissue between fascicles. It is composed of loose collagenous connective tissue, adipose tissue, and the vasa nervorum (blood vessels supplying the nerve). The epineurium provides cushioning and structural support and is the layer engaged during surgical nerve repair.

> **Board Mnemonic -- "EPE" from outside to inside:** **E**pineurium (entire nerve), **P**erineurium (fascicle bundles), **E**ndoneurium (individual fibers). Think: "Every Peripheral Expert" knows the layers.

### 1.2.2 Clinical Correlation: Nerve Injury Classification

The Seddon and Sunderland classifications of nerve injury are directly tied to these connective tissue layers:

| Seddon | Sunderland | Structure Injured | Pathology | Prognosis |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Neurapraxia | Grade I | Myelin only | Focal demyelination; axon intact | Excellent; full recovery in weeks to months |
| Axonotmesis | Grade II | Axon + myelin | Wallerian degeneration distal to injury; endoneurium intact | Good; regeneration along intact endoneurial tubes |
| -- | Grade III | Axon + endoneurium | Fascicular architecture disrupted; perineurium intact | Variable; misdirected regeneration possible |
| -- | Grade IV | Axon + endoneurium + perineurium | Only epineurium intact; neuroma-in-continuity | Poor without surgical intervention |
| Neurotmesis | Grade V | Complete transection | All layers disrupted | No recovery without surgical repair |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Neurapraxia (Sunderland Grade I) is a purely demyelinating lesion that produces conduction block across the affected segment. Because the axon is intact, no Wallerian degeneration occurs, and distal nerve conduction studies remain normal. Needle EMG will not show denervation potentials. This is the mildest form of nerve injury and carries the best prognosis.

## 1.3 The Motor Unit [BOARD]

### 1.3.1 Definition and Components

The motor unit is defined as a single alpha motor neuron, its axon, the neuromuscular junctions, and all the muscle fibers innervated by that neuron. It is the smallest functional unit of motor control and the fundamental unit studied in needle electromyography (EMG).

The components of a motor unit, in sequence from central to peripheral:

1. **Alpha motor neuron cell body** -- located in the anterior horn of the spinal cord (or motor nuclei of cranial nerves for cranial muscles)
2. **Motor axon** -- exits via the ventral root, travels through the plexus and peripheral nerve
3. **Neuromuscular junctions** -- synaptic connections between axon terminals and individual muscle fibers
4. **Muscle fibers** -- all fibers innervated by one motor neuron; these are of the same histochemical type

### 1.3.2 Innervation Ratio

The innervation ratio describes the number of muscle fibers innervated by a single motor neuron. This ratio varies dramatically across muscles and reflects the precision of motor control required:

| Muscle | Approximate Innervation Ratio | Function |
|--------|------------------------------|----------|
| Extraocular muscles | 3-5 fibers per motor neuron | Extremely fine eye movements |
| Intrinsic hand muscles (first lumbrical) | ~100 fibers per motor neuron | Fine hand manipulation |
| Biceps brachii | ~750 fibers per motor neuron | Moderate force, moderate precision |
| Gastrocnemius | ~1,500-2,000 fibers per motor neuron | High force, postural stability |
| Quadriceps femoris | ~1,500-2,000 fibers per motor neuron | High force generation |

Muscles requiring fine motor control have low innervation ratios (few muscle fibers per motor neuron), while muscles generating large forces have high innervation ratios.

### 1.3.3 Motor Unit Territory

The muscle fibers belonging to a single motor unit are not clustered together but are scattered across a region of the muscle cross-section called the motor unit territory. Within this territory, fibers of the same motor unit are interdigitated with fibers of other motor units. This arrangement ensures smooth force generation as individual motor units are recruited.

> **Clinical Pearl:** After denervation and reinnervation, surviving motor neurons sprout collateral axon branches to reinnervate orphaned muscle fibers. This process clusters fibers of the same type together (fiber type grouping), increases the motor unit territory, and produces the large-amplitude, long-duration motor unit action potentials (MUAPs) seen on needle EMG in chronic neurogenic conditions.

### 1.3.4 Motor Unit Action Potential (MUAP)

The MUAP recorded during needle EMG represents the summated electrical activity of muscle fibers within the recording radius of the needle electrode that belong to a single motor unit. Standard MUAP parameters include:

- **Amplitude**: Reflects the density and proximity of muscle fibers near the electrode tip (typically 200 microV to 3 mV)
- **Duration**: Reflects the total number and synchrony of firing of muscle fibers in the motor unit (typically 5-15 ms)
- **Phases**: The number of baseline crossings plus one (normal is 2-4 phases; >4 phases = polyphasic)
- **Turns**: Directional changes without crossing baseline
- **Recruitment frequency**: The firing rate at which additional motor units are recruited

## 1.4 Nerve Fiber Classification Systems [BOARD]

### 1.4.1 Erlanger-Gasser Classification

The Erlanger-Gasser system, developed by Joseph Erlanger and Herbert Gasser (Nobel Prize, 1944), classifies nerve fibers based on conduction velocity and fiber diameter. It applies primarily to efferent (motor) and mixed nerves, though it also encompasses certain afferent fibers.

| Fiber Type | Diameter (micrometers) | Conduction Velocity (m/s) | Myelination | Function |
|------------|----------------------|--------------------------|-------------|----------|
| A-alpha | 12-20 | 70-120 | Heavy | Alpha motor neurons (skeletal muscle); proprioception (Ia, Ib) |
| A-beta | 5-12 | 30-70 | Moderate | Touch, pressure, vibration |
| A-gamma | 3-6 | 15-30 | Light | Gamma motor neurons (muscle spindle intrafusal fibers) |
| A-delta | 2-5 | 5-30 | Thin | Fast pain, temperature, crude touch |
| B | 1-3 | 3-15 | Light | Preganglionic autonomic |
| C | 0.4-1.2 | 0.5-2 | Unmyelinated | Slow pain, temperature, postganglionic autonomic |

Key principles:
- Conduction velocity is directly proportional to fiber diameter in myelinated fibers
- For myelinated fibers: CV (m/s) is approximately 6 times the fiber diameter (micrometers)
- The A-alpha fibers are the largest, fastest-conducting myelinated fibers; C fibers are the smallest and slowest
- B fibers are unique: they have no post-hyperpolarization after the action potential and are the only autonomic fibers with myelination

### 1.4.2 Lloyd-Hunt Classification

The Lloyd-Hunt system (Lloyd, 1943; Hunt, 1954) classifies afferent (sensory) fibers from muscle and skin. It uses Roman numerals and is applied specifically to sensory neurons:

| Fiber Group | Diameter (micrometers) | Conduction Velocity (m/s) | Function | Erlanger-Gasser Equivalent |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|----------|--------------------------|
| Ia | 12-20 | 70-120 | Muscle spindle primary endings (annulospiral) | A-alpha |
| Ib | 12-20 | 70-120 | Golgi tendon organs | A-alpha |
| II | 5-12 | 30-70 | Muscle spindle secondary endings (flower spray); touch, pressure | A-beta |
| III | 2-5 | 5-30 | Deep pressure, pain (muscle) | A-delta |
| IV | 0.4-1.2 | 0.5-2 | Pain, temperature (unmyelinated) | C |

> **Board Mnemonic:** "Groups go sensory, letters go motor." The Lloyd-Hunt system (Groups I-IV) is for sensory/afferent fibers; the Erlanger-Gasser system (A-alpha through C) is used for efferent/mixed nerve classification.

### 1.4.3 Integration of Both Systems

Board examinations frequently test the ability to cross-reference between these two systems. The critical correspondences are:

- **Group Ia/Ib = A-alpha**: The fastest sensory fibers serving proprioception
- **Group II = A-beta**: Medium-velocity fibers for touch and secondary spindle afferents
- **Group III = A-delta**: Thin myelinated fibers for fast pain and temperature
- **Group IV = C fibers**: Unmyelinated fibers for slow pain

> **Clinical Pearl:** Standard nerve conduction studies (NCS) primarily assess the large myelinated A-alpha and A-beta fibers. Small fiber neuropathies affecting A-delta and C fibers may produce normal NCS results, requiring alternative diagnostic approaches such as skin biopsy for intraepidermal nerve fiber density or quantitative sensory testing.

## 1.5 Action Potential Generation [BOARD]

### 1.5.1 Resting Membrane Potential

The resting membrane potential (RMP) of a typical nerve fiber ranges from approximately -70 to -90 mV (intracellular relative to extracellular). This electrical gradient is established and maintained by the unequal distribution of ions across the cell membrane and by the selective permeability of the membrane to those ions.

**Key ion concentrations (approximate):**

| Ion | Intracellular (mM) | Extracellular (mM) | Equilibrium Potential |
|-----|--------------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| K+ | 140 | 4 | -90 mV |
| Na+ | 14 | 140 | +60 mV |
| Cl- | 4 | 120 | -80 mV |
| Ca2+ | 0.0001 | 2 | +120 mV |

The RMP is determined primarily by potassium permeability at rest. The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation calculates the RMP based on the relative permeabilities and concentrations of all permeable ions. At rest, the membrane is approximately 50-100 times more permeable to K+ than to Na+, pulling the RMP close to the K+ equilibrium potential (-90 mV) but not quite reaching it because of a small resting Na+ leak.

**The Na+/K+ ATPase pump** actively maintains ionic gradients by transporting 3 Na+ ions out of the cell for every 2 K+ ions pumped in, consuming one molecule of ATP per cycle. This 3:2 electrogenic ratio contributes a small hyperpolarizing current (approximately -3 to -5 mV contribution to the RMP). More importantly, the pump prevents the gradual dissipation of ion gradients that would otherwise occur due to passive ion leaks.

### 1.5.2 Depolarization and the Action Potential

An action potential is an all-or-none electrical event triggered when the membrane potential reaches threshold (approximately -55 mV). The sequence of events:

**Phase 1 -- Depolarization to threshold:** Local currents (from a preceding action potential, synaptic input, or artificial stimulation) depolarize the membrane toward threshold. If threshold is reached, voltage-gated Na+ channels open.

**Phase 2 -- Rapid depolarization (rising phase):** Voltage-gated Na+ channels open rapidly (within 0.1 ms), producing a massive inward Na+ current. This drives the membrane potential from threshold toward the Na+ equilibrium potential (+60 mV). The membrane typically reaches approximately +30 to +40 mV before Na+ channels begin to inactivate.

**Phase 3 -- Repolarization (falling phase):** Two simultaneous events terminate the depolarization:
- Na+ channel inactivation: The inactivation gate (h-gate) closes, halting Na+ influx. This is a time-dependent process distinct from the opening of the activation gate.
- Delayed rectifier K+ channel opening: These channels open more slowly than Na+ channels and allow K+ efflux, driving the membrane potential back toward the K+ equilibrium potential.

**Phase 4 -- Hyperpolarization (undershoot):** K+ channels remain open briefly after the membrane potential returns to resting levels, causing a transient hyperpolarization (afterhyperpolarization) below the RMP. The membrane then returns to resting potential as K+ channels close and the Na+/K+ pump restores ionic balance.

### 1.5.3 Refractory Periods

The refractory periods are critical concepts for understanding nerve conduction physiology and for board examinations:

**Absolute refractory period (ARP):** During this interval, no stimulus of any intensity can generate a second action potential. The ARP corresponds to the period when Na+ channels are in the inactivated state (h-gate closed). The channel must return to the resting (closed but activatable) state before it can open again. The ARP typically lasts 0.5-1.0 ms in large myelinated fibers.

**Relative refractory period (RRP):** Following the ARP, a period during which a stimulus of greater-than-normal intensity can elicit an action potential, but it will have reduced amplitude and slower conduction velocity. During the RRP, some Na+ channels have recovered from inactivation but K+ conductance is still elevated (afterhyperpolarization). The RRP typically lasts 2-4 ms.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The refractory periods have practical implications in EDX testing. During repetitive nerve stimulation at high frequencies (e.g., 20-50 Hz), the stimulation interval may encroach upon the relative refractory period, potentially affecting CMAP amplitude. Additionally, the refractory period limits the maximum firing rate of nerve fibers, which is relevant to understanding pathological states such as neuromyotonia where nerve fibers fire at abnormally high rates.

### 1.5.4 Ion Channel Pharmacology (Board-Relevant)

| Agent | Target | Effect | Clinical Relevance |
|-------|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Local anesthetics (lidocaine) | Voltage-gated Na+ channels | Blocks Na+ influx, prevents AP | Nerve blocks; mechanism of neurapraxia from local compression |
| Tetrodotoxin (TTX) | Voltage-gated Na+ channels (external) | Blocks Na+ influx | Research tool; pufferfish poisoning |
| Tetraethylammonium (TEA) | Voltage-gated K+ channels | Blocks K+ efflux, prolongs AP | Research tool |
| 4-aminopyridine (4-AP) | Voltage-gated K+ channels | Blocks K+ efflux | Dalfampridine for MS; improves conduction in demyelinated axons |
| Ouabain/Digoxin | Na+/K+ ATPase | Inhibits pump | Cardiac glycosides; eventually dissipates ion gradients |

> **Clinical Pearl:** 4-aminopyridine (dalfampridine) is approved for improving walking speed in multiple sclerosis. It works by blocking K+ channels exposed at demyelinated segments, thereby prolonging the action potential duration and improving conduction across demyelinated internodes. This pharmacologic mechanism directly illustrates why K+ channel distribution matters at the node of Ranvier.

## 1.6 Saltatory Conduction [BOARD]

### 1.6.1 Mechanism

In unmyelinated fibers, action potentials propagate continuously along the entire axon membrane by sequential depolarization of adjacent membrane segments. This is slow and metabolically expensive because ions must be exchanged at every point.

Myelinated fibers employ saltatory conduction (from the Latin "saltare," to jump), in which the action potential appears to leap from one node of Ranvier to the next. The mechanism:

1. An action potential at one node generates local circuit currents that flow through the axoplasm beneath the myelin sheath
2. These currents spread passively (electrotonically) to the next node
3. At the next node, where voltage-gated Na+ channels are concentrated, the local current depolarizes the membrane to threshold
4. A new action potential is generated at this node
5. The process repeats at successive nodes

Because myelin prevents current leak across the internodal membrane, current flow is confined to the axoplasm and remains strong enough to depolarize the next node. The result is dramatically faster conduction (up to 150 m/s in large myelinated fibers vs. 0.5-2 m/s in unmyelinated C fibers) with far less metabolic cost (ion exchange occurs only at nodes, requiring less Na+/K+ ATPase activity).

### 1.6.2 Node of Ranvier Architecture

The node of Ranvier is a highly specialized structure approximately 1 micrometer wide, flanked by paranodal and juxtaparanodal regions:

| Region | Ion Channels | Structural Proteins | Function |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|----------|
| Node | Nav1.6 (voltage-gated Na+) | Ankyrin G, neurofascin-186 | Action potential regeneration |
| Paranode | None (myelin loops attached) | Contactin, Caspr, neurofascin-155 | Axo-glial junction; seals myelin to axon |
| Juxtaparanode | Kv1.1, Kv1.2 (voltage-gated K+) | Caspr2, TAG-1 | Repolarization; stabilizes RMP |
| Internode | Minimal ion channels | Myelin proteins (MBP, P0, PMP22) | Insulation |

The high density of Nav1.6 channels at the node (approximately 1,000-2,000 channels per square micrometer, compared to ~25 per square micrometer in unmyelinated axons) ensures reliable action potential generation. The juxtaparanodal K+ channels are normally covered by myelin and participate primarily in repolarization and setting the resting potential of the internode.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In demyelinating neuropathies, loss of myelin exposes juxtaparanodal K+ channels. The resulting K+ leak hyperpolarizes the membrane and impairs action potential propagation, contributing to conduction block. This is the rationale for 4-aminopyridine therapy. Additionally, antibodies against nodal and paranodal proteins (neurofascin-155, contactin-1, Caspr) have been identified in subsets of patients with chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP), establishing a direct link between nodal architecture disruption and acquired demyelinating disease.

### 1.6.3 Conduction Velocity Determinants

Several factors determine conduction velocity in peripheral nerves:

1. **Fiber diameter**: Larger fibers conduct faster. For myelinated fibers, CV (m/s) is approximately 6 times the fiber diameter (micrometers). For unmyelinated fibers, CV is proportional to the square root of the diameter.

2. **Myelination**: Myelin increases conduction velocity 5-50 fold compared to unmyelinated fibers of similar diameter.

3. **Internode length**: Optimal internode length is approximately 100 times the axon diameter. Shorter internodes (as seen in remyelinated fibers) result in slower conduction.

4. **Temperature**: Conduction velocity decreases approximately 1.5-2.5 m/s per degree Celsius decrease in temperature. This is due to slowed Na+ channel kinetics at lower temperatures.

5. **Node of Ranvier integrity**: Disruption of nodal Na+ channel clustering or paranodal seal integrity impairs saltatory conduction.

## 1.7 Neuromuscular Junction Physiology [BOARD]

### 1.7.1 Normal Neuromuscular Transmission

The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is the synapse between the motor nerve terminal and the skeletal muscle fiber. It is a specialized chemical synapse that converts the electrical signal of the motor nerve action potential into an electrical signal in the muscle fiber, mediated by acetylcholine (ACh).

**Sequence of events in neuromuscular transmission:**

1. **Action potential arrival**: The action potential propagates along the motor axon and reaches the presynaptic terminal
2. **Calcium influx**: Depolarization opens voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs, primarily P/Q-type) at the active zones of the presynaptic membrane. Ca2+ flows into the terminal.
3. **Vesicle fusion**: Ca2+ triggers the SNARE protein complex (synaptobrevin, syntaxin, SNAP-25) to fuse ACh-containing synaptic vesicles with the presynaptic membrane
4. **ACh release**: Each vesicle contains approximately 5,000-10,000 ACh molecules (one quantum). A single nerve action potential releases approximately 50-300 quanta (the quantal content)
5. **Receptor binding**: ACh diffuses across the synaptic cleft (~50 nm wide) and binds to nicotinic ACh receptors (nAChRs) on the postsynaptic muscle membrane (end plate)
6. **End-plate potential (EPP)**: nAChR channels open, allowing Na+ influx and K+ efflux, generating a depolarizing EPP
7. **Muscle fiber action potential**: If the EPP reaches threshold, a muscle fiber action potential is generated, propagating along the sarcolemma and into T-tubules to trigger excitation-contraction coupling
8. **ACh degradation**: Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in the synaptic cleft rapidly hydrolyzes ACh to choline and acetate, terminating transmission. Choline is recycled into the presynaptic terminal via a high-affinity choline transporter.

### 1.7.2 The Safety Factor

The safety factor is a critical concept for understanding NMJ transmission and its disorders. It is defined as the ratio of the EPP amplitude to the threshold required to generate a muscle fiber action potential.

**Normal safety factor = 3-5** (some sources cite 2-4)

This means the EPP normally exceeds the threshold by a factor of 3-5. In practical terms, even if ACh release is reduced to one-third to one-fifth of normal, the EPP still reaches threshold and transmission succeeds. This large safety margin explains why normal neuromuscular transmission is highly reliable under physiological conditions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** NMJ disorders reduce the safety factor. In myasthenia gravis (MG), antibodies destroy postsynaptic AChRs, reducing the EPP amplitude. In Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS), antibodies against presynaptic VGCCs reduce ACh quantal release. In botulism, toxin cleaves SNARE proteins, preventing vesicle fusion and ACh release. In all three disorders, the safety factor falls, and transmission fails at some NMJs -- initially at the least reliable junctions, producing fluctuating weakness.

### 1.7.3 Quantal Content and Miniature End-Plate Potentials

**Quantal content (m)**: The number of ACh quanta (vesicles) released per nerve impulse. Normal quantal content is approximately 50-300 at rest.

**Miniature end-plate potential (MEPP)**: The small depolarization (~0.5-1.0 mV) produced by spontaneous release of a single ACh vesicle at the NMJ, without nerve stimulation. MEPPs occur at a rate of approximately 1-5 per second at each junction.

The EPP amplitude equals the MEPP amplitude multiplied by the quantal content:

**EPP = MEPP x m** (where m = quantal content)

This relationship is fundamental to understanding the electrophysiology of NMJ disorders:

| Disorder | Quantal Content (m) | MEPP Amplitude | EPP | Safety Factor |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|-----|---------------|
| Normal | ~200 | Normal | Normal | 3-5 |
| Myasthenia gravis | Normal | Reduced (fewer AChR) | Reduced | Reduced |
| LEMS | Markedly reduced | Normal | Markedly reduced | Markedly reduced |
| Botulism | Markedly reduced | Normal | Markedly reduced | Markedly reduced |

### 1.7.4 Presynaptic Facilitation and Depression

During repetitive stimulation, the amount of ACh released per impulse changes:

**At low stimulation rates (2-5 Hz):**
- The immediately available store of ACh vesicles is partially depleted with each stimulus
- In normal NMJs, the safety factor compensates and transmission is maintained
- In NMJ disorders with reduced safety factor, some fibers fail to reach threshold, producing a decremental CMAP response

**At high stimulation rates (20-50 Hz) or after brief exercise:**
- Calcium accumulates in the presynaptic terminal (residual calcium hypothesis)
- Increased Ca2+ enhances vesicle fusion and increases quantal release
- This is the mechanism of post-exercise facilitation
- In LEMS, where baseline quantal content is low, this facilitation produces dramatic CMAP amplitude increment (>60-100%)

## 1.8 Skeletal Muscle Fiber Types [BOARD]

### 1.8.1 Classification System

Skeletal muscle fibers are classified by their myosin heavy chain (MHC) isoform, which determines their contractile speed and metabolic profile. The three primary types relevant to board examinations are:

| Property | Type I (Slow Oxidative) | Type IIa (Fast Oxidative-Glycolytic) | Type IIb/IIx (Fast Glycolytic) |
|----------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Alternate names | Slow-twitch, red | Fast-twitch A, intermediate | Fast-twitch B, white |
| MHC isoform | MHC I | MHC IIa | MHC IIx (human) |
| Contraction speed | Slow | Fast | Fastest |
| Fatigue resistance | High (fatigue-resistant) | Moderate | Low (fatigable) |
| Oxidative capacity | High | High | Low |
| Glycolytic capacity | Low | Moderate | High |
| Mitochondrial density | High | High | Low |
| Myoglobin content | High (red color) | Moderate | Low (pale color) |
| Capillary density | High | Moderate | Low |
| Motor neuron size | Small | Medium | Large |
| Recruitment threshold | Low | Moderate | High |
| ATPase staining (pH 9.4) | Light | Dark | Dark |
| ATPase staining (pH 4.3) | Dark | Light | Moderate |
| ATPase staining (pH 4.6) | Dark | Light | Dark |
| Example function | Posture, endurance | Walking, moderate activity | Sprinting, jumping |

> **Board Mnemonic -- "1 Slow Red Ox":** Type **1** fibers are **Slow**, **Red** (myoglobin-rich), and **Ox**idative. Conversely, Type IIb fibers are fast, white (pale), and glycolytic.

### 1.8.2 ATPase Staining (Board-Favorite)

Myofibrillar ATPase histochemistry is a classic board topic. The key principle is that ATPase staining varies with pH of the preincubation solution:

- **At pH 9.4 (alkaline):** Type II fibers stain dark; Type I fibers stain light
- **At pH 4.3 (acid):** Type I fibers stain dark; Type IIa fibers stain light; Type IIb fibers stain moderately
- **At pH 4.6 (acid):** Type I fibers stain dark; Type IIa fibers stain light; Type IIb fibers stain dark

This allows differentiation of all three fiber types on muscle biopsy. In normal muscle, fibers are arranged in a mosaic (checkerboard) pattern, with intermingling of Type I and Type II fibers.

### 1.8.3 Fiber Type and Motor Unit Correspondence

All muscle fibers within a single motor unit are of the same histochemical type. This is because the motor neuron determines the fiber type of the muscle fibers it innervates through trophic influences and patterns of neural activity.

| Motor Unit Type | Fiber Type | Motor Neuron Size | Firing Pattern | Force Output |
|----------------|------------|-------------------|----------------|-------------|
| S (Slow) | Type I | Small | Tonic, low-frequency | Low |
| FR (Fast, fatigue-Resistant) | Type IIa | Medium | Phasic, moderate | Moderate |
| FF (Fast, Fatigable) | Type IIb/IIx | Large | Phasic, high-frequency | High |

### 1.8.4 Pathological Fiber Type Changes

Understanding normal fiber type distribution helps interpret muscle biopsy findings:

- **Fiber type grouping**: After denervation and reinnervation, collateral sprouting causes clusters of fibers of the same type to appear together (loss of the normal checkerboard pattern). This is the hallmark of chronic neurogenic process.
- **Group atrophy**: In ongoing denervation, entire groups of the same fiber type atrophy together (small, angulated fibers in clusters).
- **Type II fiber atrophy**: Selective atrophy of Type II (fast) fibers is seen in disuse, corticosteroid myopathy, and upper motor neuron lesions. This is a nonspecific finding.
- **Type I fiber predominance**: Seen in some myopathies and chronic neurogenic conditions where Type I fibers are relatively spared.

> **Clinical Pearl:** On needle EMG, the distinction between neurogenic and myopathic processes is reflected in MUAP morphology. Neurogenic lesions (after reinnervation) produce large-amplitude, long-duration, polyphasic MUAPs because motor units have enlarged through collateral sprouting. Myopathic processes produce small-amplitude, short-duration, polyphasic MUAPs because individual muscle fibers within motor units are lost or dysfunctional, reducing the contribution of each motor unit.

## 1.9 The Henneman Size Principle [BOARD]

### 1.9.1 Principle Statement

The Henneman size principle, established by Elwood Henneman in 1957, states that motor neurons are recruited in order of increasing size during voluntary contraction. Smaller motor neurons have lower thresholds for activation and are recruited first, while larger motor neurons require greater excitatory drive and are recruited later.

Because motor neuron size correlates with the properties of the motor unit it controls, this recruitment order has functional significance:

**Recruitment order: S (slow) --> FR (fast, fatigue-resistant) --> FF (fast, fatigable)**

Or equivalently: Type I motor units --> Type IIa motor units --> Type IIb motor units

### 1.9.2 Physiological Basis

The size principle arises from the biophysical properties of motor neurons:

- **Smaller motor neurons** have higher input resistance (fewer ion channels, smaller membrane area). For a given synaptic current, the voltage change across the membrane is greater (by Ohm's law: V = I x R). Therefore, smaller neurons reach threshold with less excitatory input.
- **Larger motor neurons** have lower input resistance and require more excitatory drive to reach threshold.

This is an intrinsic property of the motor neuron pool and does not require specialized central nervous system circuitry for orderly recruitment -- it emerges naturally from the biophysics.

### 1.9.3 Force Gradation

The size principle provides an elegant mechanism for smooth, graded force production:

1. At low force levels, only small (S-type) motor units are active, generating small forces with high fatigue resistance -- ideal for sustained postures
2. As more force is needed, FR motor units are added, increasing force while still maintaining endurance capacity
3. At maximal effort, FF motor units are recruited, providing peak force but with rapid fatigability

Two mechanisms increase total muscle force:
- **Recruitment**: Activating additional motor units (dominant mechanism at low-to-moderate force levels)
- **Rate coding**: Increasing the firing rate of already-active motor units (dominant mechanism at moderate-to-high force levels)

The transition point from recruitment-dominated to rate-coding-dominated force increase varies by muscle. In small hand muscles, most motor units are recruited by 50% of maximum voluntary contraction (MVC), with further force increases achieved primarily through rate coding. In large limb muscles, recruitment may continue up to 80-90% MVC.

### 1.9.4 EDX Implications of the Size Principle

The size principle has direct implications for needle EMG interpretation:

1. **During voluntary activation**: The first MUAPs to appear during gentle voluntary contraction belong to small, Type I motor units. These produce relatively small-amplitude, short-duration MUAPs. As effort increases, larger motor units are recruited, and larger MUAPs appear on the screen.

2. **Recruitment analysis**: Normal recruitment follows the size principle. The recruitment ratio (firing rate of the fastest-firing unit divided by the number of active units) is normally approximately 5:1 (i.e., units fire at about 10 Hz before the next unit is recruited, and 2 units fire before the rate reaches 20 Hz, etc.).

3. **Abnormal recruitment patterns**:
   - **Reduced recruitment (neurogenic)**: Fewer motor units available; remaining units fire at higher rates to compensate. The recruitment ratio increases (e.g., a single unit firing at 20 Hz before a second unit appears).
   - **Early recruitment (myopathic)**: Motor units are weak due to loss of constituent muscle fibers. More units must be recruited at lower force levels to generate adequate force. Many small, rapidly-firing MUAPs appear simultaneously.

4. **Interference pattern**: At maximal effort, so many MUAPs overlap that individual MUAPs cannot be distinguished -- this is the full interference pattern. A reduced (incomplete) interference pattern suggests loss of motor units (neurogenic). An early full interference pattern at low force suggests myopathic weakness.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The mnemonic for distinguishing neurogenic from myopathic recruitment: **"Neurogenic = few units firing fast; Myopathic = many units firing at normal rates but at low force."** In neurogenic lesions, the firing rate of individual units increases before new units are recruited (increased recruitment ratio). In myopathic conditions, additional units are recruited at lower force levels than expected (decreased recruitment ratio), but individual firing rates are normal.

## 1.10 Wallerian Degeneration and Nerve Injury Physiology [BOARD]

### 1.10.1 Timeline of Wallerian Degeneration

After axonal injury (Sunderland Grade II or higher), the distal axon segment and its myelin sheath undergo Wallerian degeneration. The timing of this process is critically important for electrodiagnostic testing:

| Time After Injury | Event | EDX Implication |
|-------------------|-------|-----------------|
| 0-24 hours | Axon distal to lesion still conducts normally | Distal NCS remain normal |
| 1-3 days | Distal motor axon begins to degenerate | CMAP amplitude begins to fall |
| 3-5 days | Distal motor axon degeneration progresses | CMAP amplitude significantly reduced |
| 7-11 days | Motor Wallerian degeneration complete | CMAP absent or markedly reduced |
| 10-11 days | Sensory Wallerian degeneration complete | SNAP absent or markedly reduced |
| 10-14 days | Fibrillation potentials begin to appear in proximal muscles | Needle EMG becomes abnormal |
| 14-21 days | Fibrillation potentials appear in more distal muscles | Distal denervation evident on EMG |
| 3-6 weeks | Fibrillation potentials fully developed in distal muscles | Complete denervation pattern on EMG |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key clinical takeaway is that NCS performed within the first 7-10 days after an acute axonal nerve injury may show preserved distal responses, potentially leading to underestimation of injury severity. Similarly, needle EMG performed too early may not yet show fibrillation potentials. The optimal timing for a comprehensive EDX study after acute nerve injury is generally 3-4 weeks, allowing Wallerian degeneration to complete and fibrillation potentials to develop fully. An earlier study (7-10 days) can be useful for distinguishing neurapraxia from axonotmesis by comparing responses above and below the lesion.

### 1.10.2 Nerve Regeneration

Following axonal injury with an intact endoneurial tube (Sunderland Grade II), regeneration proceeds as follows:

1. Schwann cells in the distal segment proliferate and form bands of Bungner (cellular columns within the endoneurial tubes) that guide regenerating axon sprouts
2. Multiple axon sprouts emerge from the proximal stump
3. Sprouts grow at approximately 1-3 mm/day (roughly 1 inch per month)
4. Successful sprouts eventually reach their target organs
5. Remyelination occurs but with shorter internodes than the original, resulting in permanently slower conduction velocity compared to pre-injury

Nascent motor unit potentials (NMUPs) represent the earliest EMG evidence of reinnervation. These are small-amplitude, polyphasic, and long-duration potentials generated by a few muscle fibers being reinnervated by a regenerating axon sprout.

This chapter has covered the fundamental neurophysiology concepts essential for electrodiagnostic medicine and PM&R board preparation. The key themes connecting these topics are:

1. **Structure determines function**: Axon diameter determines conduction velocity; myelin enables saltatory conduction; motor unit composition determines force characteristics
2. **Ion channels are the molecular basis of electrical signaling**: Na+ channels drive depolarization, K+ channels drive repolarization, and Ca2+ channels trigger neurotransmitter release
3. **The safety factor concept** unifies understanding of normal NMJ transmission and its disorders
