## 7.1 Brachial Plexus Anatomy [BOARD]

The brachial plexus is a complex neural network formed by the ventral rami of spinal nerves C5 through T1 that provides motor and sensory innervation to the upper extremity. Understanding its anatomical organization is essential for electrodiagnostic localization, as the pattern of SNAP and CMAP abnormalities combined with needle EMG findings identifies the precise level of injury within the plexus.

### 7.1.1 Organizational Levels

The brachial plexus is organized into five sequential levels: roots, trunks, divisions, cords, and terminal branches. The mnemonic **"Robert Taylor Drinks Cold Beer"** is commonly used to remember this sequence.

**Roots (C5-T1):**

The five ventral rami (C5, C6, C7, C8, T1) emerge from the intervertebral foramina and pass between the anterior and middle scalene muscles in the posterior triangle of the neck. Two important branches arise directly from the roots before trunk formation:

- **Dorsal scapular nerve (C5):** Innervates the rhomboid major, rhomboid minor, and levator scapulae
- **Long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7):** Innervates the serratus anterior

These root-level branches are clinically significant because their involvement helps localize a lesion to the root level rather than the trunk or cord level.

**Trunks:**

- **Upper trunk:** Formed by the union of C5 and C6 ventral rami
- **Middle trunk:** Continuation of the C7 ventral ramus alone
- **Lower trunk:** Formed by the union of C8 and T1 ventral rami

The suprascapular nerve (C5, C6) arises from the upper trunk, innervating the supraspinatus and infraspinatus muscles. This is the only named branch that emerges at the trunk level.

**Divisions:**

Each trunk divides into an anterior and a posterior division. The anterior divisions supply the flexor compartments of the upper limb; the posterior divisions supply the extensor compartments. No named peripheral nerves arise at the division level, making this the most difficult anatomical level to localize electrodiagnostically.

**Cords:**

The cords are named for their relationship to the axillary artery:

- **Lateral cord:** Formed by the anterior divisions of the upper and middle trunks (C5, C6, C7). Gives rise to the lateral pectoral nerve, the musculocutaneous nerve, and the lateral contribution to the median nerve
- **Posterior cord:** Formed by the posterior divisions of all three trunks (C5-T1). Gives rise to the upper subscapular nerve, the thoracodorsal nerve, the lower subscapular nerve, the axillary nerve, and the radial nerve
- **Medial cord:** The anterior division of the lower trunk (C8, T1). Gives rise to the medial pectoral nerve, the medial brachial cutaneous nerve, the medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve (MABC), the ulnar nerve, and the medial contribution to the median nerve

**Terminal Branches:**

| Terminal Branch | Cord of Origin | Root Contribution | Key Muscles |
|---|---|---|---|
| Musculocutaneous | Lateral | C5, C6 | Biceps, brachialis, coracobrachialis |
| Median | Lateral + Medial | C6-T1 | Pronator teres, FCR, FDS, thenar (LOAF) |
| Ulnar | Medial | C8, T1 | FCU, FDP 4-5, intrinsics (interossei, hypothenar) |
| Radial | Posterior | C5-T1 | Triceps, brachioradialis, wrist/finger extensors |
| Axillary | Posterior | C5, C6 | Deltoid, teres minor |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The lateral and medial cords each contribute a "head" to the median nerve. The lateral cord provides the C6-C7 fibers (pronator teres, FCR, FDS, AIN), while the medial cord provides the C8-T1 fibers (thenar muscles via the recurrent branch). A lateral cord lesion affects the lateral head contribution; a medial cord lesion affects the medial head contribution. Only combined lateral and medial cord lesions -- or lesions of the median nerve itself -- produce complete median nerve dysfunction.

### 7.1.2 Key Sensory Nerves for Plexus Localization [BOARD]

Sensory nerve evaluation is crucial in plexopathy because SNAPs are abnormal in postganglionic plexus lesions (unlike radiculopathy, where they are preserved). Specific sensory nerves help localize the lesion within the plexus:

| Sensory Nerve | Root/Plexus Level | SNAP Recording Site |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral antebrachial cutaneous | C5, C6 / Lateral cord (via musculocutaneous) | Lateral forearm |
| Median digit 1 (thumb) | C6 / Upper trunk, lateral cord | Thumb |
| Median digit 3 (middle finger) | C7 / Middle trunk, lateral cord | Middle finger |
| Ulnar digit 5 (small finger) | C8 / Lower trunk, medial cord | Small finger |
| Medial antebrachial cutaneous (MABC) | C8, T1 / Lower trunk, medial cord | Medial forearm |
| Radial sensory | C6, C7 / Posterior cord | Anatomical snuffbox |
| Dorsal ulnar cutaneous (DUC) | C8 / Ulnar nerve (forearm) | Dorsum of hand |

## 7.2 Electrodiagnostic Approach to Brachial Plexopathy [BOARD]

### 7.2.1 Goals of Electrodiagnostic Testing

The EDX evaluation in suspected brachial plexopathy has five primary objectives:

1. **Confirm the diagnosis:** Establish that a plexus-level lesion is present
2. **Localize the lesion:** Determine which elements (trunk, cord, or combination) are involved
3. **Determine pathophysiology:** Axonal loss, demyelination (conduction block), or mixed
4. **Assess severity:** Degree of axonal loss as estimated by CMAP/SNAP amplitude reduction and extent of denervation on needle EMG
5. **Establish prognosis:** Based on pathophysiology and severity; demyelinating lesions generally recover better than axonal loss lesions

### 7.2.2 NCS Findings [BOARD]

**SNAP abnormalities -- the critical differentiator:**

Unlike radiculopathy, where SNAPs are normal (preganglionic lesion), plexopathy produces **abnormal SNAPs** because the injury is distal to the dorsal root ganglion (postganglionic). This distinction is the single most important electrodiagnostic feature separating plexopathy from radiculopathy.

The pattern of SNAP abnormalities localizes the lesion within the plexus:

- **Upper trunk lesion:** Abnormal lateral antebrachial cutaneous SNAP, abnormal median digit 1 SNAP
- **Middle trunk lesion:** Abnormal median digit 3 SNAP (isolated middle trunk lesions are rare)
- **Lower trunk lesion:** Abnormal ulnar digit 5 SNAP, abnormal MABC SNAP
- **Lateral cord lesion:** Abnormal lateral antebrachial cutaneous SNAP, abnormal median digit SNAPs (C6-C7 contribution)
- **Medial cord lesion:** Abnormal ulnar SNAP, abnormal MABC SNAP
- **Posterior cord lesion:** May have relatively preserved SNAPs if primarily motor; radial sensory SNAP may be reduced

**CMAP abnormalities:**

Axon loss at the plexus level reduces CMAP amplitudes in the distribution of the affected nerves. Side-to-side comparison with the contralateral limb is essential, as CMAP amplitude varies significantly among individuals. A reduction of greater than 50% compared to the contralateral side is considered significant.

### 7.2.3 Needle EMG Findings [BOARD]

The needle EMG in plexopathy shows denervation (fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves) in muscles innervated by the affected plexus elements. The pattern of involved muscles helps determine the anatomical level:

- **Upper trunk pattern:** Deltoid, infraspinatus, supraspinatus, biceps
- **Middle trunk pattern:** Triceps, extensor digitorum communis, pronator teres
- **Lower trunk pattern:** First dorsal interosseous, abductor digiti minimi, abductor pollicis brevis
- **Lateral cord pattern:** Biceps, pronator teres (musculocutaneous and lateral median nerve muscles)
- **Medial cord pattern:** Hand intrinsics (ulnar-innervated), thenar muscles (medial median nerve)
- **Posterior cord pattern:** Deltoid, triceps, wrist/finger extensors (axillary and radial nerve muscles)

**Paraspinal muscles are NORMAL** in plexopathy because the posterior primary rami branch from the spinal nerve proximal to the plexus. This is a critical distinguishing feature from radiculopathy, where paraspinal fibrillations are expected.

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** The three key EDX features that distinguish plexopathy from radiculopathy are: (1) **Abnormal SNAPs** in plexopathy vs. normal SNAPs in radiculopathy; (2) **Normal paraspinals** in plexopathy vs. abnormal paraspinals in radiculopathy; (3) **Distribution follows plexus anatomy** (trunk/cord pattern) rather than a single myotome.

## 7.3 Preganglionic vs. Postganglionic Lesion Differentiation [BOARD]

One of the most clinically important applications of electrodiagnostic testing in brachial plexus injuries is distinguishing preganglionic (root avulsion) from postganglionic (plexus tear or stretch) injuries. This distinction has profound surgical and prognostic implications: preganglionic avulsions cannot be repaired by direct nerve grafting and require nerve transfer procedures, whereas postganglionic injuries may be amenable to primary repair or grafting.

### 7.3.1 Preganglionic (Root Avulsion) Features [BOARD]

- **SNAP is preserved** despite complete motor and sensory loss in the limb. The DRG and peripheral sensory axon remain intact because the avulsion separates the root from the spinal cord proximal to the ganglion
- **Paraspinal fibrillations** may be present (the posterior primary ramus is torn along with the ventral ramus)
- **Denervation of root-level muscles:** Rhomboids (dorsal scapular nerve, C5) and serratus anterior (long thoracic nerve, C5-C7) may show fibrillations, indicating damage proximal to the trunk level
- **Absent SSEPs** from the affected dermatome (the central connection is severed)
- **Horner syndrome** (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) may be present with C8-T1 avulsion due to disruption of the sympathetic chain

### 7.3.2 Postganglionic (Plexus) Features

- **SNAP is absent or reduced** -- the axonal injury is distal to the DRG
- **Paraspinal muscles are normal** -- the lesion is distal to the posterior rami branching point
- **Rhomboids and serratus anterior are normal** -- these muscles are innervated proximal to the plexus
- **SSEPs may be present** if the preganglionic pathway is intact

### 7.3.3 Comparison Table

| Feature | Preganglionic (Avulsion) | Postganglionic (Plexus) |
|---|---|---|
| SNAP | **Preserved** (despite anesthesia) | **Absent/reduced** |
| Paraspinal EMG | May show fibrillations | Normal |
| Rhomboids/serratus | May show denervation | Normal |
| SSEP | Absent | May be present |
| Horner syndrome | Possible (C8-T1) | Absent |
| Surgical option | Nerve transfer | Nerve graft or repair |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The paradox of a preserved SNAP in a limb with complete anesthesia is the hallmark of preganglionic root avulsion. The patient feels nothing because the central connection is severed, but the peripheral sensory nerve continues to function normally because its cell body (DRG) and distal axon are intact.

## 7.4 Erb-Duchenne Palsy (Upper Trunk Plexopathy) [BOARD]

### 7.4.1 Anatomy and Mechanism

Erb-Duchenne palsy results from injury to the upper trunk (C5-C6) of the brachial plexus. The classic mechanism in adults is forceful downward traction on the shoulder with simultaneous lateral neck flexion to the opposite side, which stretches the upper trunk. In neonates, it is the most common birth-related brachial plexopathy, occurring during difficult deliveries (shoulder dystocia).

### 7.4.2 Clinical Features

The classic posture is the "waiter's tip" position:
- Shoulder adducted and internally rotated (loss of deltoid, infraspinatus, supraspinatus)
- Elbow extended (loss of biceps, brachialis)
- Forearm pronated (loss of supinator, brachioradialis)
- Wrist flexed (loss of wrist extensors -- variable, depending on C7 involvement)

The hand intrinsic muscles are spared because the lower trunk (C8-T1) is uninvolved.

### 7.4.3 EDX Findings

- **Abnormal SNAPs:** Lateral antebrachial cutaneous (musculocutaneous, C5-C6), median digit 1 (C6)
- **Low CMAPs:** Musculocutaneous motor, possibly axillary motor
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in deltoid, infraspinatus, supraspinatus, biceps, brachioradialis. Sparing of C8-T1 muscles (FDI, ADM, APB)
- **Normal paraspinals:** Distinguishes from C5-C6 radiculopathy

## 7.5 Klumpke Palsy (Lower Trunk Plexopathy) [BOARD]

### 7.5.1 Anatomy and Mechanism

Klumpke palsy results from injury to the lower trunk (C8-T1) of the brachial plexus. The mechanism typically involves forceful arm abduction and upward traction, as occurs in catching oneself during a fall (grabbing an overhead object) or during breech delivery in neonates (uncommon). In clinical practice, true isolated Klumpke palsy is rare compared to upper trunk injuries.

### 7.5.2 Clinical Features

- Weakness of hand intrinsic muscles (interossei, hypothenar, thenar)
- Weakness of finger flexors (FDP to digits 4-5, FPL)
- "Claw hand" deformity (MCP hyperextension, IP flexion due to intrinsic weakness)
- Sensory loss in the medial forearm and hand (C8-T1 dermatomes)
- **Horner syndrome** may be present if the T1 root is avulsed, disrupting the cervicothoracic sympathetic chain

### 7.5.3 EDX Findings

- **Abnormal SNAPs:** Ulnar digit 5 (C8), MABC (T1). The MABC is particularly important because it is the most sensitive SNAP for lower trunk/medial cord lesions
- **Low CMAPs:** Ulnar motor (recording ADM), median motor (recording APB -- T1 contribution via medial cord)
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation in FDI, ADM, APB, FDP to digits 4-5, FCU. Sparing of C5-C6 muscles (deltoid, biceps)
- **Paraspinals normal** (unless avulsion component)

## 7.6 True Neurogenic Thoracic Outlet Syndrome [BOARD]

### 7.6.1 Definition and Pathophysiology

True neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome (TN-TOS) is a well-documented but uncommon condition caused by chronic compression of the lower trunk of the brachial plexus by a cervical rib, fibrous band, or anomalous first rib. It predominantly affects the T1 nerve fibers more than C8, producing a characteristic pattern of predominantly thenar muscle wasting with relative sparing of ulnar-innervated intrinsics.

TN-TOS must be carefully distinguished from "disputed" or "nonspecific" thoracic outlet syndrome, which lacks objective neurological findings and has normal electrodiagnostic studies. TN-TOS is the only form with clearly defined, reproducible EDX abnormalities.

### 7.6.2 Characteristic EDX Pattern [BOARD]

The EDX pattern of TN-TOS is distinctive and heavily tested on boards:

- **Low median motor CMAP amplitude** (recording APB): The thenar muscles receive their primary motor innervation through T1 fibers that travel via the lower trunk and medial cord to the median nerve. This pathway is preferentially affected
- **Abnormal MABC SNAP:** The medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve (T1 primarily) is the **most sensitive** electrodiagnostic test for TN-TOS, with reported sensitivity of 95%. MABC abnormality often precedes other findings
- **Reduced ulnar SNAP** (digit 5): Affected but typically less than the MABC
- **Ulnar motor CMAP may be normal or mildly reduced:** C8 fibers to ulnar-innervated muscles may be less affected than T1 fibers to thenar muscles
- **Needle EMG:** Denervation primarily in APB; FDI and ADM may be less involved or spared

### 7.6.3 The "Median-Over-Ulnar" Pattern

The counterintuitive finding in TN-TOS is that the **median motor CMAP is more affected than the ulnar motor CMAP**, even though the lesion is in the lower trunk/medial cord territory. This occurs because:

1. T1 fibers preferentially supply the thenar muscles (via the median nerve recurrent branch)
2. C8 fibers preferentially supply the hypothenar and interosseous muscles (via the ulnar nerve)
3. The lower trunk compression in TN-TOS affects T1 more than C8

This pattern helps distinguish TN-TOS from ulnar neuropathy at the elbow (which would show ulnar motor and sensory abnormalities with a normal median motor study) and from C8-T1 radiculopathy (which would have normal SNAPs).

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** In TN-TOS, the MABC SNAP is the most sensitive test (95%), and the combination of abnormal MABC SNAP + low median motor CMAP (recording APB) with relatively preserved ulnar motor CMAP is the classic electrodiagnostic signature. Remember: "MABC and Median Motor" for TN-TOS.

## 7.7 Parsonage-Turner Syndrome (Neuralgic Amyotrophy) [BOARD]

### 7.7.1 Clinical Features

Parsonage-Turner syndrome (PTS), also known as neuralgic amyotrophy or idiopathic brachial plexitis, is an inflammatory condition of the brachial plexus characterized by the acute onset of severe pain followed by weakness and atrophy. It is the most common non-traumatic brachial plexopathy and is frequently encountered on board examinations.

**Classic presentation:**

- **Phase 1 -- Pain:** Severe, sharp, burning pain in the shoulder and upper arm, often beginning at night. Pain is typically the most severe symptom and may last days to weeks
- **Phase 2 -- Weakness:** As pain diminishes, weakness becomes apparent. The distribution is often patchy, affecting muscles in a pattern that does not conform to a single trunk, cord, or peripheral nerve
- **Phase 3 -- Recovery:** Gradual improvement over months to years, though many patients have persistent functional deficits

The patchy, multifocal distribution is a hallmark of PTS and distinguishes it from traumatic plexopathy, which typically follows a recognizable anatomical pattern. PTS may preferentially affect individual nerves arising from the plexus, such as:

- **Long thoracic nerve:** Producing scapular winging (serratus anterior weakness)
- **Suprascapular nerve:** Infraspinatus and supraspinatus weakness
- **Anterior interosseous nerve:** FPL and FDP weakness (Kiloh-Nevin pattern)
- **Axillary nerve:** Deltoid weakness

### 7.7.2 Electrodiagnostic Findings [BOARD]

PTS is primarily an **axonal** process. The EDX findings reflect patchy, multifocal motor axon loss:

**NCS:**

- Routine distal motor and sensory NCS are often **normal** because PTS is a proximal process and the distal nerve segments conduct normally
- CMAP amplitudes may be reduced in severely affected muscles
- SNAPs may be reduced if the plexus is directly involved (postganglionic)
- The lateral antebrachial cutaneous SNAP is among the most commonly affected

**Needle EMG:**

- Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves in affected muscles
- The distribution is characteristically **atypical** -- involvement may span multiple trunk and cord territories in a patchy fashion
- Complete denervation may be present in some muscles while neighboring muscles are entirely normal
- The electromyographer must examine muscles beyond the routine screen, including clinically asymptomatic muscles, to map the full extent of involvement

### 7.7.3 Distinguishing Features

| Feature | Parsonage-Turner | Traumatic Plexopathy |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Sudden severe pain, then weakness | Weakness concurrent with injury |
| Pain timeline | Pain precedes weakness | Pain and weakness simultaneous |
| Distribution | Patchy, multifocal | Follows trunk/cord anatomy |
| History | Often post-viral, post-surgical, or idiopathic | Clear trauma history |
| Bilateral involvement | Up to one-third of cases | Uncommon |
| Recovery | Good but slow (months to years) | Variable, depends on severity |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The hallmark of Parsonage-Turner syndrome is the combination of severe initial pain followed by patchy weakness that does not fit a single trunk, cord, or peripheral nerve distribution. When the needle EMG shows denervation in muscles from multiple plexus elements in a seemingly random pattern, PTS should be strongly considered.

## 7.8 Radiation Plexopathy vs. Tumor Plexopathy [BOARD]

### 7.8.1 Clinical Context

Distinguishing radiation-induced brachial plexopathy from tumor recurrence or infiltration in patients with a history of cancer and radiation therapy is one of the most important clinical applications of electrodiagnostic testing. Both conditions produce progressive weakness, sensory loss, and pain in the upper extremity, and the clinical examination alone may be insufficient to differentiate them.

### 7.8.2 Myokymic Discharges: The Key Differentiator [BOARD]

**Myokymic discharges** are the single most important EMG finding for distinguishing radiation from tumor plexopathy:

- **Radiation plexopathy:** Myokymic discharges are present in **50 to 70%** of patients (some studies report 63%)
- **Tumor plexopathy:** Myokymic discharges are present in only approximately **4%** of patients
- **Mechanism in radiation injury:** Radiation damages nerve fibers, producing segmental demyelination and abnormal membrane excitability. The damaged fibers develop ectopic excitability and ephaptic cross-talk, generating the semirhythmic grouped bursts characteristic of myokymia

### 7.8.3 Other Differentiating Features

| Feature | Radiation Plexopathy | Tumor Plexopathy |
|---|---|---|
| Myokymic discharges | Present in 50-70% | Rare (approximately 4%) |
| Pain as presenting symptom | Less common initially; may develop later | Frequently the presenting symptom |
| Onset timing | Months to years after radiation (median 1-5 years) | Variable; may be presenting feature of recurrence |
| Predominant distribution | **Upper trunk** (C5-C6) | **Lower trunk** (C8-T1) |
| Progression | Gradual, may plateau | Progressive |
| Lymphedema | Common (radiation effect on lymphatics) | Less common unless extensive disease |
| MRI findings | Diffuse enhancement, no discrete mass | Discrete mass, focal enhancement |
| Response to treatment | No effective treatment; supportive care | Possible improvement with tumor treatment |

### 7.8.4 Important Caveats

- The absence of myokymic discharges does **not exclude** radiation plexopathy -- approximately 30 to 50% of cases lack this finding
- The presence of myokymia does **not absolutely exclude** concurrent tumor infiltration -- the two conditions can coexist
- Imaging (MRI with contrast, PET-CT) should be obtained regardless of EMG findings when tumor recurrence is suspected
- The distribution preference (upper trunk for radiation, lower trunk for tumor) reflects anatomical factors: the upper plexus lies more superficially and receives a higher radiation dose in typical breast and lung cancer treatment fields

> **Clinical Pearl -- Board Favorite:** When a patient with a history of cancer and radiation therapy presents with progressive arm weakness, the presence of myokymic discharges on EMG strongly favors radiation plexopathy over tumor recurrence. However, imaging is always indicated to exclude a mass lesion, as the two conditions can coexist.

## 7.9 Nerve Root Avulsion [BOARD]

### 7.9.1 Mechanism and Clinical Significance

Nerve root avulsion occurs when extreme traction forces physically separate the spinal nerve root from the spinal cord. This is a severe form of brachial plexus injury, most commonly seen in high-energy trauma such as motorcycle accidents. The injury is preganglionic, meaning it occurs proximal to the dorsal root ganglion.

Root avulsions are surgically irreparable by direct nerve grafting because the proximal stump is no longer connected to the spinal cord. Treatment requires nerve transfer procedures, in which an expendable donor nerve (e.g., spinal accessory nerve, intercostal nerves) is connected to a distal target nerve to restore function.

### 7.9.2 EDX Signature

The electrodiagnostic hallmark of root avulsion is the paradox of **preserved SNAPs in a limb with clinical anesthesia**. This occurs because:

1. The DRG is intact (it lies in the foramen, outside the spinal canal)
2. The peripheral sensory axon remains in continuity with its cell body
3. The sensory nerve continues to conduct normally despite complete loss of sensation (the central pathway is severed)

Additional EDX findings:

- **Paraspinal fibrillations:** The posterior ramus is avulsed along with the ventral ramus
- **Denervation of proximal root-level muscles:** Rhomboids (dorsal scapular, C5), serratus anterior (long thoracic, C5-C7)
- **Absent SSEPs:** The somatosensory pathway from periphery to cortex is interrupted at the avulsion site
- **Absent motor responses in affected muscles:** Complete motor denervation
- **Myelographic findings:** Pseudomeningoceles (outpouchings of the dural sleeve at the avulsion site) are pathognomonic on imaging

### 7.9.3 Combined Injuries

In practice, many traumatic brachial plexus injuries involve a combination of preganglionic (avulsion) and postganglionic (stretch/tear) components at different root levels. For example, a patient may have C5-C6 root avulsions combined with C7-C8 postganglionic stretch injuries. Electrodiagnostic testing helps define the component injuries at each level, which is critical for surgical planning.

## 7.10 Lumbosacral Plexus Anatomy [BOARD]

### 7.10.1 Anatomical Organization

The lumbosacral plexus is divided into two major components:

**Lumbar plexus (L1-L4):**

The lumbar plexus forms within the substance of the psoas major muscle from the ventral rami of L1-L4 (with a variable contribution from T12). Its major branches include:

| Nerve | Root Origin | Key Muscles | Sensory Territory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iliohypogastric | T12, L1 | Internal oblique, transversus abdominis | Suprapubic region |
| Ilioinguinal | L1 | Internal oblique | Inguinal region, upper medial thigh |
| Genitofemoral | L1, L2 | Cremaster | Inguinal region |
| Lateral femoral cutaneous | L2, L3 | None (purely sensory) | Lateral thigh |
| Femoral | L2, L3, L4 | Iliopsoas, quadriceps, sartorius | Anterior thigh, medial leg |
| Obturator | L2, L3, L4 | Adductors (longus, brevis, magnus) | Medial thigh |

**Sacral plexus (L4-S3):**

The sacral plexus forms on the anterior surface of the piriformis muscle from the lumbosacral trunk (L4-L5) and the ventral rami of S1-S3. Its major branches include:

| Nerve | Root Origin | Key Muscles | Sensory Territory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Superior gluteal | L4, L5, S1 | Gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, TFL | None |
| Inferior gluteal | L5, S1, S2 | Gluteus maximus | None |
| Sciatic | L4-S3 | Hamstrings, all below-knee muscles | Via tibial and fibular divisions |
| Posterior femoral cutaneous | S1-S3 | None (purely sensory) | Posterior thigh |
| Pudendal | S2-S4 | Pelvic floor muscles | Perineum |

The **lumbosacral trunk** (L4-L5) is the critical connection between the lumbar and sacral plexuses. It descends over the sacral ala to join the sacral plexus and is vulnerable to injury from pelvic fractures, surgical procedures, and mass lesions.

### 7.10.2 EDX Approach to Lumbosacral Plexopathy [BOARD]

The principles of electrodiagnostic evaluation are similar to brachial plexopathy:

1. **SNAPs are abnormal** (postganglionic lesion) -- this distinguishes plexopathy from radiculopathy
2. **CMAPs are reduced** in the distribution of affected nerves
3. **Needle EMG shows denervation** in muscles that cross nerve boundaries but share plexus elements
4. **Paraspinal muscles are normal** (distinguishes from radiculopathy)

The key challenge in lumbosacral plexopathy is the frequent coexistence with other conditions (diabetic neuropathy, concurrent radiculopathy, post-surgical changes) that confound interpretation.

**Useful NCS for lumbosacral plexus evaluation:**

- Sural SNAP (S1-S2, tibial nerve territory)
- Superficial fibular SNAP (L5, fibular nerve territory)
- Saphenous SNAP (L3-L4, femoral nerve territory)
- Lateral femoral cutaneous SNAP (L2-L3, purely sensory)
- Femoral motor study (L2-L4)
- Fibular motor study (L5, S1)
- Tibial motor study (S1, S2)
- H-reflex (S1)

## 7.11 Lumbosacral Plexopathy: Common Injury Patterns [BOARD]

### 7.11.1 Upper Lumbar Plexopathy (L2-L4)

**Clinical features:** Weakness of hip flexion (iliopsoas), knee extension (quadriceps), and hip adduction (adductors). Sensory loss in the anterior thigh and medial leg. Diminished patellar reflex.

**Common causes:**

- Retroperitoneal hematoma (often in anticoagulated patients)
- Psoas abscess
- Femoral neuropathy (must be distinguished)
- Surgical injury (abdominal or pelvic procedures)

**EDX findings:**

- Abnormal saphenous SNAP (femoral nerve, L3-L4)
- Low femoral motor CMAP
- Denervation in iliopsoas, quadriceps, AND adductors (the adductor involvement, via the obturator nerve, is the key finding that distinguishes plexopathy from isolated femoral neuropathy)

### 7.11.2 Lower Sacral Plexopathy (L5-S2)

**Clinical features:** Weakness of ankle dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, knee flexion. Sensory loss in the lateral leg, foot. Diminished ankle jerk.

**Common causes:**

- Pelvic fracture
- Neoplastic infiltration
- Radiation injury
- Endometriosis
- Surgical injury (total hip arthroplasty, pelvic surgery)

### 7.11.3 Retroperitoneal Hematoma [BOARD]

Retroperitoneal hematoma is one of the most important and commonly tested causes of acute lumbar plexopathy:

- Most frequently affects the **femoral nerve and upper lumbar plexus** due to their anatomical course through and alongside the psoas muscle
- Common in patients on **anticoagulation** (warfarin, heparin, DOACs) and in patients with hemophilia
- Presents with acute groin or hip pain followed rapidly by anterior thigh weakness and sensory loss
- EDX shows acute denervation in femoral and obturator nerve distributions
- Prognosis is generally **good** if the hematoma is recognized and managed promptly (reversal of anticoagulation, possible surgical evacuation)
- CT or MRI of the retroperitoneum confirms the diagnosis

> **Clinical Pearl:** In any anticoagulated patient who develops acute hip or groin pain followed by anterior thigh weakness, retroperitoneal hematoma should be the leading diagnosis until imaging proves otherwise. This is a time-sensitive condition that may require emergent intervention.

## 7.12 Diabetic Amyotrophy (Diabetic Lumbosacral Radiculoplexus Neuropathy) [BOARD]

### 7.12.1 Clinical Features

Diabetic amyotrophy is a distinct syndrome occurring in patients with diabetes mellitus, characterized by subacute onset of severe pain followed by proximal leg weakness and atrophy. Despite its traditional name, the pathology is not limited to the proximal muscles or a single nerve root -- hence the preferred term "diabetic lumbosacral radiculoplexus neuropathy" (DLRPN).

**Classic presentation:**

- **Pain:** Severe, deep, aching pain in the hip, buttock, and anterior thigh. Pain is often the dominant initial symptom and may be debilitating
- **Weakness:** Proximal more than distal, affecting the quadriceps, iliopsoas, and hip adductors. Weakness may be asymmetric initially but often becomes bilateral over weeks to months
- **Weight loss:** Unexplained weight loss of 10 to 40 pounds is common and may raise concern for malignancy
- **Autonomic features:** Postural hypotension, impotence, and other autonomic symptoms may be present

DLRPN occurs in approximately 0.8% of all patients with diabetes and is more common in type 2 diabetes. Importantly, it can occur at any stage of diabetes severity, including in patients with well-controlled disease.

### 7.12.2 Proposed Pathophysiology

The current evidence supports an **immune-mediated microvasculitis** of the vasa nervorum (the small blood vessels supplying peripheral nerves) as the primary mechanism. Nerve biopsy studies have demonstrated:

- Ischemic nerve fiber injury with multifocal fiber loss
- Perivascular inflammatory cell infiltrates (T lymphocytes, macrophages)
- Vessel wall thickening and neovascularization
- Hemosiderin deposits indicating prior hemorrhage

This vasculitic mechanism explains the patchy, multifocal pattern of nerve involvement and distinguishes DLRPN from the length-dependent "stocking-glove" pattern of typical diabetic polyneuropathy.

### 7.12.3 EDX Findings [BOARD]

- **Concurrent distal diabetic polyneuropathy** may coexist and complicate interpretation
- **Asymmetric, proximal findings** distinguish plexus/root involvement from the symmetric distal polyneuropathy
- **SNAP abnormalities:** May be difficult to interpret in the presence of concurrent polyneuropathy, but asymmetric SNAP reductions support plexus involvement
- **Needle EMG:** Fibrillation potentials in a patchy distribution affecting the lumbosacral plexus territory; both proximal (quadriceps, iliopsoas) and distal muscles may be involved
- **Paraspinal fibrillations:** May be present, as the pathology can extend to involve nerve roots (radiculoplexus neuropathy)

### 7.12.4 Course and Prognosis

- Most patients improve over 6 to 18 months, but recovery is often incomplete
- Weight loss stabilizes and reverses as pain improves
- Immunomodulatory treatment (IV methylprednisolone, IVIG, plasma exchange) has shown some benefit in observational studies, but no large randomized trial has definitively established efficacy
- Physical therapy and pain management are the cornerstones of treatment

## 7.13 Additional Plexopathy Conditions [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 7.13.1 Neoplastic Plexopathy

Primary tumors (Pancoast tumor, lymphoma) and metastatic disease (breast, lung, cervical cancer) may directly invade or compress the brachial or lumbosacral plexus. EDX findings include progressive denervation in the plexus territory, reduced SNAP and CMAP amplitudes, and typically an absence of myokymic discharges (see Section 7.8). Lower trunk involvement is more common in neoplastic brachial plexopathy because of the proximity of the lower trunk to the lung apex and axillary lymph nodes.

### 7.13.2 Hereditary Neuralgic Amyotrophy (HNA)

HNA is an autosomal dominant condition caused by mutations in the SEPT9 gene, producing recurrent episodes of painful brachial plexus dysfunction clinically identical to idiopathic Parsonage-Turner syndrome. Features distinguishing HNA from sporadic PTS include:

- Family history of recurrent brachial plexopathy
- Recurrent episodes (often triggered by stress, infection, or surgery)
- Younger age of onset
- Dysmorphic features in some families (hypotelorism, cleft palate)

### 7.13.3 Obstetric Brachial Plexopathy

Neonatal brachial plexus injury occurs in approximately 0.5 to 3 per 1,000 live births. Risk factors include shoulder dystocia, high birth weight, breech delivery, and prolonged labor. The upper trunk (Erb) pattern predominates. Prognosis depends on severity: neurapraxic injuries recover spontaneously within weeks, while avulsion injuries require surgical intervention and carry a guarded prognosis.

## Review Questions [BOARD]

1. A 58-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer treated with mastectomy and radiation therapy 3 years ago presents with progressive right arm weakness. Needle EMG shows grouped, semirhythmic motor unit discharges in the affected muscles. What do these discharges represent, and what is the most likely diagnosis?

   **Answer:** The discharges are myokymic discharges ("marching soldiers" sound), which are found in 50 to 70% of patients with radiation-induced brachial plexopathy. Given the history of radiation and the presence of myokymia, radiation plexopathy is the most likely diagnosis. However, imaging should be obtained to exclude tumor recurrence, as both conditions can coexist.

2. A 22-year-old motorcycle accident victim has complete paralysis and anesthesia of the right arm. Electrodiagnostic testing reveals normal SNAP amplitudes in the median, ulnar, and radial sensory nerves on the right. No motor unit recruitment is present in any muscle tested. What does the combination of preserved SNAPs and complete motor paralysis indicate?

   **Answer:** Preserved SNAPs in a limb with complete anesthesia and paralysis indicate preganglionic root avulsion. The DRG and distal sensory axons remain intact (preserving the SNAP) despite complete disruption of both motor and sensory connections to the spinal cord. This has critical surgical implications: root avulsions cannot be repaired by direct nerve grafting and require nerve transfer procedures.

3. A patient presents with acute onset of hand weakness. EDX reveals a low median motor CMAP (recording APB), an abnormal MABC SNAP, a mildly reduced ulnar SNAP, and a normal ulnar motor CMAP. Needle EMG shows denervation predominantly in the APB with mild changes in the FDI. Where is the lesion?

   **Answer:** This pattern is characteristic of true neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome (TN-TOS). The low median CMAP with relatively preserved ulnar CMAP reflects the T1-predominant (over C8) nature of the compression. The abnormal MABC SNAP (the most sensitive test for TN-TOS at 95% sensitivity) localizes the lesion to the lower trunk or medial cord. The combination of low median motor + abnormal MABC is the classic TN-TOS signature.
