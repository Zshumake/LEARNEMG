## 12.1 Neuromuscular Junction Physiology [BOARD]

### 12.1.1 Normal Neuromuscular Transmission

The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is the synapse between the motor nerve terminal and the muscle fiber. Understanding the physiology of normal transmission is essential for interpreting EDX abnormalities in NMJ disorders.

**Sequence of Normal Transmission:**

1. An action potential arrives at the presynaptic motor nerve terminal
2. Voltage-gated calcium channels (primarily P/Q-type) open, allowing calcium influx
3. Calcium triggers fusion of acetylcholine (ACh)-containing vesicles with the presynaptic membrane
4. ACh is released into the synaptic cleft (quantal release: each vesicle = one quantum)
5. ACh binds to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (AChR) on the postsynaptic muscle membrane
6. AChR channel opening allows sodium influx, generating the endplate potential (EPP)
7. If the EPP exceeds the muscle fiber threshold, a muscle fiber action potential is generated
8. Acetylcholinesterase in the synaptic cleft rapidly hydrolyzes ACh, terminating transmission

### 12.1.2 The Safety Factor [BOARD]

The **safety factor** of neuromuscular transmission is the ratio of the endplate potential amplitude to the threshold required to generate a muscle fiber action potential. Under normal conditions, the EPP is approximately 2-4 times larger than the threshold. This excess -- the safety factor -- ensures that every nerve impulse reliably generates a muscle fiber action potential, even under conditions of high-frequency firing.

**Safety factor = EPP amplitude / Threshold for muscle fiber AP generation**

Normal safety factor: approximately 2-4x (i.e., the EPP exceeds threshold by a factor of 2-4)

NMJ disorders reduce the safety factor by either:
- **Presynaptic mechanisms:** Reducing ACh release (reducing EPP amplitude from the presynaptic side) -- as in LEMS and botulism
- **Postsynaptic mechanisms:** Reducing the number or function of AChR (reducing EPP amplitude from the postsynaptic side) -- as in MG

When the safety factor falls below 1.0 (EPP no longer reaches threshold), transmission failure occurs at that individual neuromuscular junction, resulting in muscle fiber "blocking."

### 12.1.3 Quantal Content and Quantal Response [BOARD]

| Term | Definition | Normal Value |
|---|---|---|
| Quantum | The amount of ACh in a single synaptic vesicle (~5,000-10,000 ACh molecules) | -- |
| Quantal content (m) | Number of quanta (vesicles) released per nerve impulse | ~50-300 quanta per impulse |
| Quantal response (q) | EPP amplitude generated by a single quantum (miniature EPP, MEPP) | ~0.5-1.0 mV |
| Endplate potential (EPP) | m x q (quantal content times quantal response) | ~50-70 mV |
| Threshold | Membrane potential needed to trigger muscle fiber AP | ~-50 to -55 mV (depolarization of ~15-20 mV from resting potential) |

**In presynaptic disorders (LEMS, botulism):** Quantal content (m) is reduced. Fewer vesicles are released per impulse, so the EPP is smaller.

**In postsynaptic disorders (MG):** Quantal response (q) is reduced. Fewer functional AChR mean each quantum generates a smaller MEPP, so the EPP is smaller.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between presynaptic and postsynaptic NMJ disorders determines whether high-frequency stimulation will produce facilitation (increment) or not. In presynaptic disorders, high-frequency stimulation increases calcium accumulation in the terminal, partially overcoming the calcium channel deficiency and producing a dramatic increment. In postsynaptic disorders, the ACh release mechanism is intact, so high-frequency stimulation does not meaningfully improve transmission.

## 12.2 The Physiology of Neuromuscular Transmission Failure [BOARD]

### 12.2.1 Normal Depletion and Recovery of the Readily Releasable Pool

Under normal physiological conditions, the presynaptic terminal maintains three functional pools of ACh-containing vesicles:

1. **Readily releasable pool (RRP):** Vesicles docked at active zones, immediately available for release upon calcium influx. Contains approximately 1,000 vesicles. This pool is depleted first during repetitive stimulation.

2. **Recycling pool:** Vesicles undergoing endocytosis and refilling with ACh. This pool replenishes the RRP during sustained activity.

3. **Reserve pool:** The largest pool of vesicles, serving as a deep reserve that is mobilized during intense or prolonged activity.

At rest, the RRP is fully stocked. With the first nerve impulse, approximately 50-300 quanta are released from the RRP. During slow-rate repetitive stimulation (2-3 Hz), the RRP undergoes progressive depletion because the replenishment rate from the recycling pool cannot keep pace with the release rate. By the 4th-5th stimulus, the RRP reaches its nadir, and quantal content per stimulus is at its lowest.

In a healthy NMJ with a safety factor of 3-4x, this physiological decline in quantal content does not produce any transmission failure because the EPP still exceeds threshold. In diseased NMJs, the reduced safety factor means this physiological depletion can push the EPP below threshold at individual junctions.

### 12.2.2 Calcium Dynamics and Facilitation

Calcium plays a dual role in neuromuscular transmission:

**Immediate effect:** Calcium entry through P/Q-type VGCCs triggers vesicle fusion and ACh release within microseconds.

**Residual calcium effect:** Some calcium remains in the terminal after each impulse, accumulating with subsequent stimuli. This residual calcium enhances vesicle mobilization and priming, partially compensating for RRP depletion. At low stimulation rates (2-3 Hz), residual calcium accumulation is modest. At high rates (20-50 Hz), substantial calcium accumulation produces marked facilitation of release.

This calcium physiology explains the differential effects of stimulation rate on presynaptic versus postsynaptic NMJ disorders.

## 12.3 Repetitive Nerve Stimulation: Principles and Protocol [BOARD]

### 12.2.1 Slow-Rate RNS (2-3 Hz)

Slow-rate RNS is the standard electrodiagnostic test for NMJ disorders. The nerve is stimulated at 2-3 Hz (one stimulus every 300-500 milliseconds), and the amplitude and area of successive CMAPs are compared.

**Mechanism of decremental response at 2-3 Hz:**

At rest, the nerve terminal contains a "readily releasable pool" (RRP) of vesicles docked at active zones, ready for immediate release. With each stimulus, some RRP vesicles are released. Between stimuli (at 2-3 Hz), there is insufficient time for the RRP to be fully replenished from the reserve pool. By the 4th or 5th stimulus, the quantal content reaches its nadir (fewest vesicles released per impulse).

In a normal NMJ, the safety factor is sufficient to tolerate this physiological decline in quantal content without any transmission failure. In NMJ disorders where the safety factor is already compromised, this further reduction in EPP amplitude causes some individual junctions to fail, producing a measurable decrement in the compound response.

**Criteria for abnormal decrement:**
- Greater than or equal to **10% decrement** in CMAP amplitude or area from the 1st to the 4th or 5th response
- The decrement should be reproducible on repeat testing

**The U-shaped (or "saddled") decrement pattern:**
In postsynaptic NMJ disorders, the decremental response typically shows maximal decrement at the 4th or 5th stimulus, followed by partial recovery (repair) of amplitude on subsequent stimuli. This partial repair occurs because calcium accumulation in the nerve terminal begins to enhance vesicle release, partially compensating for the depleted RRP. The resulting amplitude pattern forms a U-shape or saddle when plotted across 8-10 stimuli.

### 12.2.2 Post-Activation Facilitation (PAF) and Post-Activation Exhaustion (PAE) [BOARD]

The exercise protocol enhances the sensitivity of RNS testing by exploiting the physiological effects of sustained voluntary contraction.

**Protocol:**
1. Baseline RNS at 2-3 Hz (record decrement if present)
2. Maximum voluntary contraction for 10 seconds (or 60 seconds for full protocol)
3. Immediately after exercise: repeat RNS -- assess for **post-activation facilitation (PAF)**
4. At 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 minutes after exercise: repeat RNS -- assess for **post-activation exhaustion (PAE)**

**Post-activation facilitation (PAF):**
Immediately after exercise (within 10-30 seconds), calcium levels in the nerve terminal are elevated, increasing vesicle release and temporarily improving neuromuscular transmission. The decrement may transiently improve or disappear.

- In **postsynaptic disorders (MG):** Brief improvement in decrement (temporary "repair")
- In **presynaptic disorders (LEMS, botulism):** Dramatic increase in CMAP amplitude (>100% increment), reflecting the combined effect of calcium accumulation overcoming the baseline calcium channel deficiency

**Post-activation exhaustion (PAE):**
Beginning 2-5 minutes after exercise, the decrement worsens beyond the baseline level. This occurs because the nerve terminal's vesicle stores have been partially depleted by the intense exercise period, and it takes minutes for full replenishment. PAE increases the sensitivity of RNS by "unmasking" a mild NMJ defect that may have been inapparent at baseline.

> **Clinical Pearl:** If baseline RNS at rest is normal, repeat the study after 60 seconds of maximum voluntary contraction, checking at 1-minute intervals for 5 minutes. A decrement that appears only during the post-activation exhaustion window (2-5 minutes post-exercise) may reveal a mild NMJ disorder that was missed at rest.

### 12.2.3 High-Rate RNS (20-50 Hz) [BOARD]

High-rate RNS delivers stimuli at 20-50 Hz, rapidly accumulating calcium in the presynaptic terminal. This protocol is primarily useful for diagnosing presynaptic NMJ disorders.

**In presynaptic disorders (LEMS, botulism):**
- Baseline CMAP amplitude is low (reduced quantal release at rest)
- High-rate RNS produces a dramatic **incremental response** (>100%, often 200-400%)
- This increment occurs because rapid calcium accumulation overcomes the antibody-mediated calcium channel blockade, progressively increasing vesicle release with each stimulus

**In postsynaptic disorders (MG):**
- Baseline CMAP amplitude is normal
- High-rate RNS typically does not produce significant increment (the presynaptic mechanism is intact; increasing ACh release does not help when the postsynaptic receptors are blocked/destroyed)
- May produce decrement or no change

**Practical note:** High-rate RNS is extremely painful and poorly tolerated. The brief (10-second) post-exercise facilitation protocol is equivalent and much better tolerated: maximum voluntary contraction for 10 seconds, then immediately check CMAP amplitude. An increment >100% is equivalent to the high-rate RNS finding.

### 12.2.4 Muscle Selection for RNS [BOARD]

Sensitivity of RNS varies by muscle, with proximal and facial muscles generally more sensitive than distal muscles.

**Recommended testing sequence (order of sensitivity in generalized MG):**

| Muscle | Nerve Stimulated | Sensitivity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nasalis | Facial nerve | Highest (~85%) | Most sensitive for generalized MG |
| Trapezius | Spinal accessory | High (~80%) | Technically easy; minimal movement artifact |
| Orbicularis oculi | Facial nerve | High | Useful for ocular MG |
| Deltoid | Axillary nerve | Moderate | Shoulder stabilization needed |
| Abductor digiti minimi | Ulnar nerve | Lower (~50-60%) | Most technically reliable; good baseline |
| Abductor pollicis brevis | Median nerve | Lower | Commonly used initial study |

**Testing strategy:**
1. Begin with a technically reliable distal muscle (ADM or APB) to establish baseline parameters and technique
2. If distal study is negative, proceed to proximal muscles (trapezius, nasalis)
3. If proximal studies are negative despite high clinical suspicion, proceed to SFEMG

### 12.2.5 Technical Considerations [BOARD]

| Factor | Requirement | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature | Surface >32 degrees C; near-nerve >34 degrees C | Cold temperature improves NMJ safety factor, potentially masking a decrement |
| Anticholinesterase medications | Hold pyridostigmine 12-24 hours before testing | May mask decrement by increasing ACh availability |
| Electrode position | Ensure stable, reproducible electrode placement | Movement artifact is the most common cause of false-positive decrement |
| Stimulus intensity | Supramaximal stimulation | Submaximal stimulation produces apparent decrement from unstable nerve activation |
| Immobilization | Secure the tested limb | Movement between stimuli produces amplitude variability |

## 12.3 Single Fiber EMG (SFEMG) [BOARD]

### 12.3.1 Principles

SFEMG measures the time variability of neuromuscular transmission at individual neuromuscular junctions. It is the most sensitive electrodiagnostic test for NMJ disorders.

**Jitter:** The variation in the time interval between the action potentials of two muscle fibers belonging to the same motor unit recorded by the same electrode. Jitter reflects the variability in the time from nerve impulse arrival at the nerve terminal to generation of the muscle fiber action potential. This variability is overwhelmingly determined by the NMJ transmission time (particularly the EPP rise time), making jitter a direct measure of NMJ function.

**Measurement:** Jitter is quantified as the mean consecutive difference (MCD) of successive interpotential intervals (IPIs) between a pair of muscle fiber action potentials:

MCD = Mean of |IPI(n+1) - IPI(n)| across consecutive discharges

### 12.3.2 Jitter: Normal Values and Interpretation [BOARD]

Normal jitter values are age-dependent (increase with age) and muscle-specific. They have been established for both the traditional single-fiber needle electrode (SFNE) and the more widely available concentric needle electrode (CNE).

**Representative upper limits of normal jitter (CNE, voluntary activation):**

| Muscle | Age <60 | Age 60-69 | Age 70-79 | Age >80 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Orbicularis oculi | ~32 microseconds | ~35 microseconds | ~40 microseconds | ~44 microseconds |
| Frontalis | ~31 microseconds | ~33 microseconds | ~40 microseconds | ~44 microseconds |
| Extensor digitorum communis | ~33 microseconds | ~36 microseconds | ~41 microseconds | ~46 microseconds |

**Criteria for abnormal SFEMG study:**
- The study is considered abnormal if the **mean jitter** of all tested pairs is increased above the upper limit of normal for that muscle/age, OR
- If **more than 10% of individual fiber pairs** show increased jitter (e.g., more than 2 out of 20 pairs)
- **Blocking** (intermittent absence of one component of the pair) is counted as the maximum possible jitter value

### 12.3.3 Blocking [BOARD]

Blocking occurs when the jitter becomes so large that the EPP intermittently fails to reach threshold, causing the muscle fiber action potential to disappear on some discharges. On SFEMG, this appears as intermittent absence of the second potential in the pair. Blocking represents complete transmission failure at that specific NMJ and is seen when the safety factor falls below 1.0.

Blocking has diagnostic significance:
- Present in NMJ disorders when disease is moderate to severe
- Precedes clinical weakness (subclinical blocking)
- Generally begins to occur when jitter exceeds approximately 80-100 microseconds

### 12.3.4 Fiber Density [BOARD]

Fiber density is the average number of single muscle fiber action potentials recorded by the SFEMG electrode within a single motor unit. Normal fiber density varies by muscle and age (typically 1.3-1.8 for most muscles at younger ages, increasing slightly with age).

- **Increased fiber density** indicates that more muscle fibers belonging to a single motor unit are clustered within the pickup area of the electrode, reflecting collateral reinnervation (as in neurogenic conditions) or fiber splitting (as in some myopathies)
- Fiber density is increased in ALS, post-polio syndrome, and other chronic neurogenic disorders
- Fiber density is generally normal or mildly increased in NMJ disorders

### 12.3.5 Sensitivity and Specificity of SFEMG [BOARD]

| Condition | SFEMG Sensitivity | RNS Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Generalized MG | 92-99% | 75-80% |
| Ocular MG | 80-95% | 30-50% |
| LEMS | >95% | ~90% |
| Botulism | >90% | Variable |

SFEMG is the most sensitive EDX test for NMJ disorders but is **less specific** than RNS. Increased jitter can be seen in any condition affecting the NMJ, including:
- Myasthenia gravis and other NMJ disorders (primary)
- Motor neuron disease (immature NMJs from reinnervation)
- Myopathies (especially inflammatory myopathies)
- Neuropathies (terminal nerve fiber dysfunction)

Therefore, SFEMG should be interpreted in the clinical context and in conjunction with NCS, standard EMG, and RNS results.

> **Clinical Pearl:** SFEMG is recommended when clinical suspicion for a NMJ disorder is high but RNS is normal. This occurs most often in ocular MG, where RNS has limited sensitivity (30-50%) but SFEMG detects abnormalities in 80-95% of cases. However, an abnormal SFEMG is not specific for MG and must be interpreted alongside antibody testing and clinical findings.

## 12.4 Myasthenia Gravis [BOARD]

### 12.4.1 Clinical Features

MG is the prototypical postsynaptic NMJ disorder, caused by autoantibodies targeting components of the postsynaptic membrane.

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Hallmark symptom | **Fatigable weakness** -- weakness that worsens with sustained or repeated activity and improves with rest |
| Ocular symptoms | Ptosis, diplopia (in >85% at presentation; exclusively ocular in ~15% throughout disease) |
| Bulbar symptoms | Dysarthria, dysphagia, fatigable chewing |
| Limb weakness | Proximal > distal; often asymmetric early |
| Respiratory | Myasthenic crisis: respiratory failure requiring ventilation (~15-20% of patients) |
| Fluctuation | Characteristic diurnal variation (worse with activity, better with rest); day-to-day variability |
| Thymoma | Associated in ~10-15% of MG patients (thymectomy may improve outcomes) |

### 12.4.2 Antibodies in MG [BOARD]

| Antibody | Frequency | Target | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anti-AChR (binding) | 80-90% generalized; ~50-60% ocular | Nicotinic AChR at postsynaptic membrane | Highly specific (>90%); most commonly tested |
| Anti-AChR (modulating) | Subset of AChR+ | Accelerates AChR endocytosis | Complements binding antibody testing |
| Anti-AChR (blocking) | Subset of AChR+ | Blocks ACh binding site | Less commonly tested |
| Anti-MuSK | 5-10% of generalized MG (many AChR-seronegative) | Muscle-specific kinase (postsynaptic signaling) | Bulbar predominant; facial/tongue atrophy; poor response to pyridostigmine; responds to rituximab |
| Anti-LRP4 | 3-50% of double-seronegative | LRP4 (MuSK co-receptor) | Less established; variable detection across assays |
| Seronegative | ~5-10% | Unknown (likely low-affinity AChR or clustered AChR antibodies) | Diagnosis by clinical + EDX + treatment response |

### 12.4.3 MuSK-Positive MG: Special Considerations [BOARD]

MuSK-MG has distinct clinical and EDX features that deserve separate attention:

| Feature | AChR-MG | MuSK-MG |
|---|---|---|
| Predominant weakness | Ocular, then generalized | Bulbar > ocular > limb |
| Facial/tongue atrophy | Uncommon | Common |
| Respiratory crisis | Present | Frequent and severe |
| RNS pattern | Decremental in proximal/facial muscles | May be decremental in facial muscles; sometimes normal in limb muscles |
| Pyridostigmine response | Good | Poor (may worsen symptoms) |
| Treatment | AChE inhibitors + immunosuppression + thymectomy | Rituximab often effective; thymectomy not beneficial |
| SFEMG | Abnormal (standard pattern) | Abnormal (may show unusual limb distribution) |

### 12.4.4 Diagnostic Algorithm for MG [BOARD]

1. Clinical assessment: fatigable weakness + examination (ice pack test, edrophonium test historically)
2. **Serology:** AChR antibodies (binding, then modulating/blocking if negative); if AChR negative, test MuSK; if both negative, consider LRP4
3. **RNS:** Slow-rate (2-3 Hz) with post-exercise exhaustion protocol; test proximal/facial muscles
4. **SFEMG:** If RNS negative but clinical suspicion remains high
5. **CT/MRI chest:** Evaluate for thymoma (mandatory in all newly diagnosed MG patients)

### 12.4.5 EDX Findings in MG [BOARD]

| Parameter | Finding |
|---|---|
| Motor NCS (CMAP amplitude) | Usually **normal** at rest (postsynaptic defect does not reduce baseline quantal release) |
| Sensory NCS | Normal |
| Slow-rate RNS (2-3 Hz) | Decremental response >10% (4th-5th stimulus); U-shaped recovery pattern |
| Post-activation facilitation | Transient improvement in decrement immediately after 10-second exercise |
| Post-activation exhaustion | Worsening decrement at 2-5 minutes post-exercise |
| High-rate RNS | NO significant increment (presynaptic mechanism is intact) |
| SFEMG | Increased jitter; blocking in moderate-severe cases |
| Standard EMG | Usually normal; occasional moment-to-moment variability of MUAPs |

---

### 12.4.6 MG Treatment Overview [BOARD]

Understanding MG treatment is important for the electrodiagnostician because medications affect EDX findings and because the EDX evaluation may be requested to assess treatment response.

| Treatment Category | Examples | Mechanism | EDX Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors** | Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) | Inhibits ACh breakdown, increasing ACh availability at the NMJ | Must hold 12-24 hours before RNS to avoid false-negative results |
| **Corticosteroids** | Prednisone | Immunosuppression | Chronic use can cause steroid myopathy (additional EMG changes) |
| **Steroid-sparing immunosuppressants** | Azathioprine, mycophenolate, tacrolimus | Reduce autoantibody production | Minimal direct EDX effect |
| **Complement inhibitors** | Eculizumab, ravulizumab, zilucoplan | Block complement-mediated AChR destruction | New class; complement-specific MG |
| **FcRn inhibitors** | Efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab | Reduce circulating IgG (including pathogenic antibodies) | New class; rapid onset of action |
| **B-cell therapies** | Rituximab | Depletes B cells; reduces antibody production | Particularly effective for MuSK-MG |
| **IVIg / Plasma exchange** | IVIG, PLEX | Immunomodulation / antibody removal | Used for acute exacerbations and myasthenic crisis |
| **Thymectomy** | Surgical | Removes thymic tissue (site of immune dysregulation) | Beneficial in AChR-MG with or without thymoma; not beneficial in MuSK-MG |

### 12.4.7 Myasthenic Crisis [BOARD]

Myasthenic crisis is defined as worsening MG weakness requiring intubation or noninvasive ventilation. It occurs in approximately 15-20% of MG patients and can be precipitated by infection, surgery, medication changes, or new medications.

**EDX in crisis:** RNS may show more pronounced decrement during crisis, but the primary role of EDX is in initial diagnosis rather than crisis management. Serial bedside measurements of forced vital capacity (FVC) and negative inspiratory force (NIF) are more useful for monitoring respiratory function during crisis.

**Distinguishing myasthenic crisis from cholinergic crisis:** Cholinergic crisis results from excessive acetylcholinesterase inhibitor dosing and presents with increased weakness PLUS cholinergic symptoms (salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI cramping, emesis -- SLUDGE mnemonic; plus bradycardia, miosis, fasciculations). This distinction has become less clinically important with modern MG management but remains a board-tested topic.

## 12.5 Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) [BOARD]

### 12.5.1 Pathophysiology

LEMS is a presynaptic NMJ disorder caused by autoantibodies targeting **P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs)** on the presynaptic motor nerve terminal. Antibody binding reduces the number of functional calcium channels, decreasing calcium influx with each nerve impulse and consequently reducing the number of ACh vesicles released (reduced quantal content).

### 12.5.2 Clinical Features

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Clinical triad | (1) Proximal weakness (legs > arms), (2) autonomic dysfunction, (3) hyporeflexia with post-exercise facilitation |
| Weakness pattern | Proximal, symmetric; legs affected first and most severely |
| Autonomic dysfunction | Dry mouth (most common), constipation, erectile dysfunction, orthostatic hypotension |
| Reflexes | Reduced or absent at rest; **augment after brief exercise** (post-exercise facilitation) -- highly characteristic |
| Cancer association | ~60% paraneoplastic (most commonly **small cell lung cancer, SCLC**); 40% autoimmune without cancer |
| Antibodies | Anti-P/Q-type VGCC: 85-95%; anti-N-type VGCC: 30-40% |

### 12.5.3 EDX Findings in LEMS: The Key Board Topic [BOARD]

| Parameter | Finding | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| CMAP amplitude at rest | **Low** (often <2-3 mV in hand muscles) | Reduced quantal content produces small EPPs; many NMJs fail at rest |
| Sensory NCS | Normal | Sensory system unaffected |
| Conduction velocity | Normal | Nerve function intact |
| Slow-rate RNS (2-3 Hz) | **Decremental response >10%** | Similar to MG; progressive vesicle depletion with insufficient safety factor |
| High-rate RNS (20-50 Hz) or brief exercise | **Incremental response >100%** (often 200-400%) | **HALLMARK FINDING**: rapid calcium accumulation overcomes VGCC blockade, progressively increasing vesicle release |
| SFEMG | Increased jitter; blocking | NMJ transmission impaired |

**The increment explained:** In LEMS, resting calcium entry through the reduced number of VGCCs is insufficient to trigger adequate vesicle release. During high-frequency stimulation, calcium accumulates in the terminal faster than it can be cleared. This progressive calcium buildup partially restores quantal content, dramatically increasing the CMAP amplitude. The increment of >100% (doubling or more of the CMAP) is the defining EDX feature of presynaptic NMJ disorders.

> **Clinical Pearl:** When you see a low CMAP amplitude that doubles or triples after brief exercise, think LEMS. This post-exercise increment is the most recognizable and board-tested feature of LEMS. Always order a CT chest to evaluate for small cell lung cancer.

### 12.5.4 Treatment

| Treatment | Mechanism | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **3,4-diaminopyridine (3,4-DAP, amifampridine)** | Potassium channel blocker; prolongs presynaptic depolarization, increasing calcium entry | First-line symptomatic therapy |
| Pyridostigmine | Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor | Adjunctive; less effective than in MG |
| IVIg / Plasma exchange | Immunomodulation | Acute treatment or maintenance |
| Steroids, azathioprine | Immunosuppression | Chronic management |
| Cancer treatment | Treat underlying SCLC | May improve LEMS symptoms |

## 12.6 Botulism [BOARD]

### 12.6.1 Pathophysiology

Botulism is caused by Clostridium botulinum neurotoxin, which irreversibly cleaves SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin/VAMP, SNAP-25, or syntaxin, depending on the toxin serotype) required for vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane. This blocks ACh release, producing a presynaptic NMJ disorder.

### 12.6.2 Clinical Forms

| Form | Source | Onset | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Foodborne | Ingestion of preformed toxin | 12-72 hours after ingestion | Nausea, vomiting, then descending paralysis |
| Wound | Toxin production in infected wound | Variable | Often in injection drug users |
| Infant | Ingestion of C. botulinum spores (colonize gut) | Gradual | Infants <1 year; "floppy baby"; honey exposure |
| Iatrogenic | Botulinum toxin injection (therapeutic) | Days to weeks | Excessive weakness at injection site or distant spread |

### 12.6.3 Clinical Presentation

Botulism produces descending paralysis (contrast with GBS, which produces ascending paralysis):
- Cranial nerves affected first: ptosis, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia
- Descending to limbs: proximal then distal weakness
- Autonomic dysfunction: dilated pupils, dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention
- Respiratory failure may develop rapidly
- Sensation is preserved throughout

### 12.6.4 EDX Findings in Botulism [BOARD]

| Parameter | Finding |
|---|---|
| CMAP amplitude at rest | **Low** (presynaptic ACh release block) |
| Sensory NCS | Normal |
| Slow-rate RNS (2-3 Hz) | Decremental response may be present |
| High-rate RNS / post-exercise | **Incremental response** (typically 30-100%, less dramatic than LEMS) |
| SFEMG | Increased jitter and blocking |
| Fibrillation potentials | May develop after 2-3 weeks (denervation effect from prolonged transmission failure) |

**Important distinction from LEMS:** The increment in botulism is generally less dramatic than in LEMS (30-100% versus 200-400% in LEMS), because the mechanism is different. In LEMS, calcium channels are reduced but SNARE proteins are intact; increased calcium can still drive vesicle fusion. In botulism, the SNARE proteins are cleaved; even with increased calcium, the remaining intact SNARE complexes are limited.

### 12.6.5 Therapeutic Botulinum Toxin (Chemodenervation) [COMPREHENSIVE]

Botulinum toxin is widely used therapeutically for conditions including spasticity, dystonia, and hyperhidrosis. The EDX changes in muscles injected with botulinum toxin mirror those of botulism:

- Fibrillation potentials develop in the injected muscle at 2-3 weeks post-injection
- Short-duration, low-amplitude MUAPs (reflecting functional denervation of muscle fibers)
- RNS may show decremental response
- Effects are temporary, recovering over 3-6 months as new nerve terminals sprout
- Distant spread of toxin can produce systemic effects (generalized weakness, dysphagia)

### 12.6.5 LEMS Cancer Screening Protocol [BOARD]

Given the strong association between LEMS and small cell lung cancer (approximately 60% of LEMS cases), cancer screening is mandatory for all newly diagnosed LEMS patients.

**Initial screening:**
- CT chest (with contrast) at diagnosis
- PET-CT if CT chest is negative but clinical suspicion remains high
- Anti-SOX1 antibodies: associated with higher risk of underlying cancer (up to 90% of SCLC-associated LEMS vs. ~5% of autoimmune LEMS)

**Ongoing surveillance:**
- If initial cancer screening is negative, repeat CT chest every 6 months for at least 2 years
- After 2 years of negative screening, annual CT for an additional 2-4 years
- If DELTA-P score (Dutch-English LEMS Tumor Association Prediction) is low, surveillance interval may be extended

**DELTA-P scoring system for cancer prediction in LEMS:**
- Presence of each of the following scores 1 point: age >50, current/former smoker, weight loss, bulbar symptoms, erectile dysfunction (males), Karnofsky score <70
- Score >=3: high probability of cancer-associated LEMS
- Score 0-1: more likely autoimmune (primary) LEMS

> **Clinical Pearl:** The diagnosis of LEMS mandates a thorough cancer evaluation. Finding and treating an underlying SCLC can improve the neurological syndrome. Conversely, LEMS may be the presenting symptom of SCLC, and early detection of the cancer significantly impacts oncological outcomes.

## 12.7 MG vs. LEMS vs. Botulism: Comprehensive Comparison [BOARD]

### Table 12.1: The Board-Essential NMJ Comparison Table

| Feature | MG (Postsynaptic) | LEMS (Presynaptic) | Botulism (Presynaptic) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Anti-AChR/MuSK/LRP4 Ab | Anti-P/Q VGCC Ab | Clostridium toxin cleaves SNAREs |
| **Target** | Postsynaptic AChR | Presynaptic Ca2+ channels | Presynaptic SNARE proteins |
| **Weakness pattern** | Ocular > bulbar > limbs | Proximal limbs (legs > arms) | Descending (cranial > limbs) |
| **CMAP at rest** | **Normal** | **Low** | **Low** |
| **Slow-rate RNS** | Decrement >10% | Decrement >10% | Decrement may be present |
| **High-rate RNS / exercise** | No significant change | **Increment >100%** | **Increment 30-100%** |
| **SFEMG jitter** | Increased | Increased | Increased |
| **Reflexes** | Normal | Reduced (facilitate post-exercise) | Reduced |
| **Autonomic dysfunction** | Absent | **Present** (dry mouth, constipation) | **Present** (dilated pupils, dry mouth) |
| **Cancer association** | Thymoma (10-15%) | **SCLC (~60%)** | None |
| **Antibodies** | AChR (80%), MuSK (5-10%) | P/Q VGCC (85-95%) | Toxin assay |
| **First-line Rx** | Pyridostigmine | **3,4-DAP (amifampridine)** | Antitoxin |
| **Pupil involvement** | Rare | Uncommon | **Common** (dilated, unreactive) |
| **Recovery** | Chronic (manageable) | Chronic (treat cancer if present) | Weeks to months (nerve terminal regeneration) |

> **Board Mnemonic -- "Low CMAP = Presynaptic":** If the baseline CMAP amplitude is low and RNS shows an increment, the lesion is presynaptic (LEMS or botulism). If the baseline CMAP is normal and RNS shows a decrement without increment, the lesion is postsynaptic (MG). This simple principle solves most NMJ board questions.

## 12.8 Organophosphate and Nerve Agent Poisoning [COMPREHENSIVE]

### Mechanism and Clinical Context

Organophosphate compounds (including military nerve agents such as sarin, soman, VX, and agricultural insecticides such as parathion and malathion) irreversibly inhibit acetylcholinesterase at the neuromuscular junction and other cholinergic synapses. This produces excessive accumulation of acetylcholine at all cholinergic synapses, leading to sustained depolarization of the postsynaptic membrane and ultimately depolarization block. The clinical presentation includes a cholinergic toxidrome with muscarinic effects (salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI cramping, emesis -- the SLUDGE mnemonic), nicotinic effects (fasciculations, weakness, paralysis), and central nervous system effects (seizures, altered consciousness).

### EDX Findings

- **Repetitive CMAP** (double or triple CMAP) after single nerve stimulus -- similar to slow-channel CMS and endplate AChE deficiency
- RNS may show decrement at slow rates (desensitization of AChR from excessive ACh) and decrement at high rates
- Fibrillation potentials may develop after prolonged exposure (chemical denervation)

### Clinical Significance

The repetitive CMAP after single stimulation is an important finding that should raise suspicion for cholinergic excess, whether from organophosphate poisoning or from excessive cholinesterase inhibitor dosing in a MG patient.

### Treatment

- Atropine (muscarinic antagonist)
- Pralidoxime (2-PAM, reactivates cholinesterase if given early before "aging" of the phosphorylated enzyme)
- Supportive care and ventilatory support

## 12.9 Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes [BOARD]

### 12.8.1 Overview

Congenital myasthenic syndromes (CMS) are a group of inherited disorders caused by mutations affecting proteins essential for NMJ structure and function. Unlike MG, CMS are not autoimmune and do not involve antibodies. They typically present in infancy or early childhood with fatigable weakness and may be misdiagnosed as seronegative MG.

### 12.8.2 Classification by Defect Location

| Location | Specific Syndromes | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Presynaptic** | ChAT deficiency (choline acetyltransferase); vesicular ACh transporter deficiency | Episodic apnea in infancy; responds to AChE inhibitors |
| **Synaptic** | Endplate AChE deficiency (COLQ mutations) | Repetitive CMAP after single stimulus; AChE inhibitors worsen symptoms |
| **Postsynaptic** | AChR deficiency; AChR kinetic abnormalities (slow-channel, fast-channel); Rapsyn deficiency; DOK7 mutations | Most common CMS category |

### 12.8.3 High-Yield CMS Subtypes

**Slow-channel syndrome:**
- Gain-of-function mutation causing prolonged AChR channel open time
- Results in excessive cation entry (excitotoxic endplate myopathy)
- EDX: Repetitive CMAP after single nerve stimulus (pathognomonic)
- Treatment: Quinidine or fluoxetine (NOT AChE inhibitors, which worsen symptoms)

**Fast-channel syndrome:**
- Loss-of-function mutation causing shortened AChR channel open time
- Reduced EPP amplitude due to brief channel opening
- Treatment: AChE inhibitors +/- 3,4-DAP

**DOK7 (limb-girdle CMS):**
- Mutations in DOK7 (intracellular signaling protein for MuSK)
- Presents as limb-girdle weakness, may mimic limb-girdle muscular dystrophy
- AChE inhibitors may worsen symptoms; treatment: salbutamol (beta-2 agonist)
- Important diagnostic pitfall: patients with DOK7-CMS are often initially misdiagnosed as having seronegative MG and treated with pyridostigmine, which paradoxically worsens their weakness

**Rapsyn deficiency (RAPSN mutations):**
- Rapsyn is an intracellular scaffolding protein essential for clustering AChR at the postsynaptic membrane
- Presents in neonancy with hypotonia, feeding difficulties, and episodic apnea
- Arthrogryposis may be present at birth (from reduced fetal movement in utero)
- Generally responds to AChE inhibitors combined with 3,4-DAP
- The N88K mutation is the most common and is found in nearly all identified alleles of patients with European ancestry

**Endplate AChE deficiency (COLQ mutations):**
- Loss of synaptic acetylcholinesterase
- Prolonged ACh effect leads to endplate myopathy
- EDX: Repetitive CMAP after single stimulus
- AChE inhibitors are contraindicated (no enzyme to target); treatment: salbutamol

> **Clinical Pearl:** A repetitive CMAP (a second smaller CMAP following the initial CMAP after a single nerve stimulus) is a distinctive EDX finding that should raise suspicion for slow-channel CMS or endplate AChE deficiency. It can also be seen with AChE inhibitor use (pharmacological effect).

### 12.9.4 CMS Treatment Principles

Treatment of CMS differs fundamentally from autoimmune MG because the goal is to compensate for a structural deficiency rather than suppress an immune response.

| CMS Subtype | AChE Inhibitors | 3,4-DAP | Quinidine/Fluoxetine | Salbutamol | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| AChR deficiency | Beneficial | May help | No | No | Standard first-line |
| Fast-channel | Beneficial | Beneficial | No | No | Combined therapy often needed |
| Slow-channel | **CONTRAINDICATED** | No | **Beneficial** | No | Quinidine shortens channel open time |
| AChE deficiency (COLQ) | **CONTRAINDICATED** | No | No | **Beneficial** | No enzyme target for AChE inhibitors |
| DOK7 | **May worsen** | No | No | **Beneficial** | Salbutamol improves NMJ structure |
| Rapsyn deficiency | Beneficial | Beneficial | No | No | Combined therapy |
| ChAT deficiency | Beneficial | May help | No | No | Focus on preventing apneic episodes |

> **Board Mnemonic:** "**Slow and COLQ say NO to pyridostigmine**" -- Slow-channel CMS and COLQ (AChE deficiency) CMS are worsened by acetylcholinesterase inhibitors. DOK7 CMS is also potentially worsened. All three respond to salbutamol or quinidine instead.

## 12.9 SFEMG Methodology: Practical Considerations [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 12.9.1 Single-Fiber Needle Electrode vs. Concentric Needle Electrode

SFEMG was originally performed using a specialized single-fiber needle electrode (SFNE) with a small recording surface (25 micrometers) that allows selective recording of individual muscle fiber action potentials. The development of concentric needle electrode (CNE) SFEMG techniques has made the technique more accessible, as CNE needles are widely available and less expensive than SFNE.

| Feature | SFNE (Traditional) | CNE (Modern) |
|---|---|---|
| Recording surface | 25 micrometers | 150 x 580 micrometers |
| Selectivity | Higher (single fiber) | Lower (may record from >1 fiber) |
| Reference values | Established (classic literature) | Newer normative data (2016 multicenter study) |
| Availability | Limited (special order) | Widely available |
| Cost | Higher | Lower |
| Clinical validity | Gold standard | Equivalent for clinical use |

### 12.9.2 Voluntary Activation vs. Stimulated SFEMG

**Voluntary SFEMG:** The patient voluntarily activates the muscle at low effort levels while the operator identifies pairs of single-fiber action potentials belonging to the same motor unit. Jitter is measured between the pair components. This method is technically challenging and requires patient cooperation.

**Stimulated SFEMG:** Intramuscular nerve twigs are electrically stimulated at low intensity to activate individual motor axons. The recorded single-fiber action potential's jitter is measured relative to the stimulus artifact (which serves as the timing reference). Stimulated SFEMG eliminates central drive variability and can be performed in uncooperative patients.

**Key differences:**
- Stimulated SFEMG jitter values are slightly lower than voluntary values for the same muscle (less variability from central sources)
- Reference values are method-specific and must not be interchanged
- Stimulated SFEMG measures only the NMJ component of jitter; voluntary SFEMG includes a small contribution from variation in axonal conduction time

### 12.9.3 Muscle Selection for SFEMG

The choice of muscle for SFEMG depends on the clinical presentation and the likelihood of detecting an abnormality:

| Muscle | Advantages | Disadvantages | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Orbicularis oculi | Highest sensitivity for generalized and ocular MG | Patient discomfort; technically difficult | Suspected ocular MG |
| Frontalis | Good sensitivity; well-tolerated | Requires forehead relaxation | Alternative to orbicularis oculi |
| Extensor digitorum communis | Technically easiest; well-established reference values | Lower sensitivity for ocular MG | Initial study; suspected generalized MG |
| Deltoid | Proximal muscle assessment | More painful; technically challenging | When distal/facial studies are normal |

### 12.9.4 Interpreting Borderline SFEMG Results

SFEMG interpretation requires careful attention to technical factors:

- **Interpotential interval (IPI):** Published reference values are valid only for IPIs <4 milliseconds. Longer IPIs may produce falsely elevated jitter values due to increased conduction time variability.
- **Number of pairs:** At least 20 pairs should be analyzed for a reliable assessment. Fewer pairs may produce an unreliable mean jitter.
- **Proportion of abnormal pairs:** The "10% rule" (>10% of pairs abnormal) is a threshold for declaring the study abnormal.
- **Trigger stability:** The trigger potential must be stable and consistent; unstable triggers produce artifact jitter.

## 12.10 Special Topics in NMJ Electrodiagnosis [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 12.10.1 Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor-Associated MG [BOARD]

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) used in cancer therapy (anti-PD-1: nivolumab, pembrolizumab; anti-PD-L1: atezolizumab; anti-CTLA-4: ipilimumab) can trigger de novo myasthenia gravis or exacerbate pre-existing subclinical MG. This is an increasingly recognized and potentially life-threatening complication.

**Key features:**
- Onset typically within weeks to months of ICI initiation
- Can present as fulminant MG with rapid respiratory deterioration
- May co-occur with myositis (elevated CK) and/or myocarditis -- the "overlap syndrome" carries high mortality
- AChR antibodies may be positive or negative
- RNS may show decremental response
- Treatment: discontinue ICI; high-dose corticosteroids; IVIg or plasma exchange for severe cases

> **Clinical Pearl:** Acute onset of ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, or respiratory difficulty in a patient receiving immune checkpoint inhibitors should prompt immediate evaluation for MG. Co-existing myositis (elevated CK) and myocarditis (cardiac troponin, ECG changes) suggest the dangerous overlap syndrome requiring urgent multidisciplinary management.

### 12.10.2 Autoimmune Autonomic Ganglionopathy and NMJ Overlap [COMPREHENSIVE]

Autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy is caused by antibodies against ganglionic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (alpha-3 subunit). While primarily affecting autonomic ganglia (producing orthostatic hypotension, anhidrosis, gastroparesis, urinary retention), some patients have concurrent antibodies against muscle-type AChR, producing clinical overlap with MG. RNS and SFEMG findings may reflect the combined NMJ and autonomic involvement.

### 12.10.3 Drug-Induced NMJ Disorders

Several medications can impair NMJ transmission and unmask or exacerbate MG:

| Category | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Antibiotics | Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin), fluoroquinolones | Presynaptic calcium channel blockade; postsynaptic AChR blockade |
| Cardiovascular | Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, procainamide | Various mechanisms affecting NMJ transmission |
| Anesthetics | Neuromuscular blocking agents (succinylcholine, vecuronium) | Direct AChR blockade |
| Magnesium | IV magnesium sulfate | Calcium antagonism at presynaptic terminal; reduces ACh release |
| Penicillamine | D-penicillamine | Induces autoimmune MG (anti-AChR antibodies) |
| Immune checkpoint inhibitors | Nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab | Autoimmune MG (de novo or exacerbation) |

### 12.9.2 Stimulated SFEMG (Axonal Microstimulation)

Stimulated SFEMG uses electrical stimulation of intramuscular nerve twigs instead of voluntary activation to trigger single motor unit firing. This technique is useful in:
- Patients unable to voluntarily activate muscles (ICU patients, children)
- Eliminating the variability introduced by voluntary activation
- Measuring jitter more precisely (less variability from central drive irregularity)

Reference values for stimulated SFEMG differ from voluntary SFEMG and must be applied appropriately.

## 12.10 NMJ Disorders: Diagnostic Algorithm and Practical Approach [BOARD]

### The Stepwise Diagnostic Approach

When a neuromuscular junction disorder is suspected, the following algorithm optimizes diagnostic yield:

**Step 1: Clinical Assessment**
- Fatigable weakness (improves with rest, worsens with activity)
- Pattern of weakness: ocular (ptosis, diplopia), bulbar (dysarthria, dysphagia), limb (proximal)
- Autonomic symptoms (dry mouth, constipation) -- suggests LEMS
- History of cancer, recent infection, medications affecting NMJ

**Step 2: Serological Testing**
- AChR binding antibodies (first-line)
- If AChR negative: AChR modulating antibodies, then MuSK antibodies
- If both negative: LRP4 antibodies (research labs)
- If LEMS suspected: P/Q-type VGCC antibodies

**Step 3: RNS Testing**
- Begin with a distal muscle (ADM) to establish technique
- If negative: test proximal/facial muscles (trapezius, nasalis)
- Include post-exercise facilitation/exhaustion protocol
- If low baseline CMAPs: test for increment after brief exercise

**Step 4: SFEMG (if RNS negative and clinical suspicion high)**
- Orbicularis oculi (for ocular MG)
- Extensor digitorum communis (for generalized MG)
- At least 20 fiber pairs analyzed

**Step 5: Cancer Screening**
- CT chest for thymoma (all new MG patients)
- CT chest/PET-CT for SCLC (all LEMS patients)
- Annual cancer surveillance for LEMS patients (especially first 2 years)

### When RNS Is False-Negative

RNS may be normal in mild or ocular MG. Factors that increase the likelihood of a false-negative study include:

- Testing only distal muscles (lower sensitivity than proximal/facial)
- Patient on acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (hold 12-24 hours before testing)
- Cold extremity temperature (warming essential)
- Inadequate post-exercise exhaustion protocol (only tested at rest)
- Very mild or purely ocular disease

In these cases, SFEMG in the orbicularis oculi has the highest diagnostic yield and should be performed before concluding the study is normal.

### When RNS Is False-Positive

Apparent decrement on RNS may occur due to:
- Movement artifact (most common cause of false decrement)
- Submaximal stimulation (inconsistent nerve activation)
- Technical issues with electrode placement
- Concurrent neuromuscular disease (motor neuron disease, myopathy) can cause transmission instability at enlarged or immature NMJs

The decrement must be reproducible on at least 2 trials to be considered valid. Verification by testing a second muscle adds confidence to the finding.

## 12.11 Summary: Board-High-Yield Review Points

1. **Safety factor** = EPP / threshold. Normal ~2-4x. NMJ disorders reduce it. When safety factor <1, transmission fails (blocking).

2. **Quantal content** = number of vesicles released per impulse. Reduced in presynaptic disorders (LEMS, botulism). **Quantal response** = EPP per quantum. Reduced in postsynaptic disorders (MG).

3. **RNS at 2-3 Hz:** Decrement >10% (4th-5th stimulus) = abnormal. U-shaped pattern in MG. Post-activation facilitation (immediate) then exhaustion (2-5 minutes).

4. **RNS muscle selection:** Nasalis > trapezius > orbicularis oculi > distal muscles. Start with ADM/APB for technique, then proximal if negative.

5. **MG:** Normal CMAP at rest; decrement on slow RNS; no increment on high-rate RNS. Antibodies: AChR (80%), MuSK (5-10%), LRP4. Associated with thymoma.

6. **LEMS:** Low CMAP at rest; decrement on slow RNS; **increment >100% on high-rate RNS or brief exercise** (hallmark finding). Anti-P/Q VGCC antibodies. Associated with SCLC (60%).

7. **Botulism:** Low CMAP at rest; increment on high-rate RNS (30-100%, less than LEMS). Descending paralysis. Dilated pupils. SNARE protein cleavage.

8. **SFEMG:** Most sensitive test (92-99% generalized MG, 80-95% ocular MG). Measures jitter (MCD) and blocking. Less specific (abnormal in neuropathies, MND, myopathies too).

9. **Temperature matters:** Cold masks NMJ defects. Maintain surface temperature >32 degrees C. Hold pyridostigmine 12-24 hours before testing.

10. **CMS:** Inherited, not autoimmune. Repetitive CMAP on single stimulation suggests slow-channel syndrome or AChE deficiency. AChE inhibitors worsen slow-channel and COLQ CMS.

11. **"Low CMAP = Presynaptic":** The simplest rule for board questions. Low baseline CMAP + increment = presynaptic (LEMS/botulism). Normal baseline CMAP + decrement = postsynaptic (MG).

12. **ICI-associated MG:** Immune checkpoint inhibitor-triggered MG can be fulminant. The overlap syndrome of myositis + MG + myocarditis carries high mortality and requires urgent multidisciplinary care.

13. **CMS treatment pearls:** Slow-channel and COLQ CMS are worsened by pyridostigmine. DOK7 CMS is treated with salbutamol, not AChE inhibitors.

## Board-Style Practice Questions

**Q1:** A 55-year-old male smoker presents with proximal leg weakness, dry mouth, and areflexia. CMAP amplitude from ADM is 1.8 mV (normal >5 mV). After 10 seconds of maximum voluntary contraction, the CMAP amplitude increases to 5.4 mV (300% increment). What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the next most important diagnostic step?

**A1:** Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS). The low baseline CMAP with >100% increment after brief exercise is pathognomonic. Given the patient's age, smoking history, and 60% paraneoplastic association, the most important next step is CT chest to evaluate for small cell lung cancer.

**Q2:** A 30-year-old woman presents with fluctuating ptosis and diplopia for 3 months. RNS of the ADM at 2-3 Hz shows no decrement. AChR antibodies are negative. What should be done next?

**A2:** Perform RNS of a proximal or facial muscle (nasalis, trapezius) with post-exercise exhaustion protocol, as distal muscles have lower sensitivity for ocular MG. If still negative, perform SFEMG of the orbicularis oculi, which has 80-95% sensitivity for ocular MG. Also test MuSK antibodies.
