## 4.1 F Waves [BOARD]

### 4.1.1 Physiology: Not a Reflex

The F wave is a late motor response that appears after the CMAP (M wave) on motor nerve conduction studies. Despite its "late response" classification alongside the H reflex, the F wave is fundamentally NOT a reflex. It does not involve a sensory afferent limb or a synaptic relay.

**Mechanism of F-wave generation:**

1. A supramaximal stimulus is applied to a peripheral motor nerve
2. The action potential propagates in two directions simultaneously:
   - **Orthodromically** (distally) toward the muscle, producing the direct M wave (CMAP)
   - **Antidromically** (proximally) toward the spinal cord, traveling up the motor axon to the anterior horn cell
3. The antidromic impulse reaches the anterior horn cell body
4. A small percentage of anterior horn cells (approximately 1-5%) "backfire" -- they are re-excited by the antidromic impulse and generate a new orthodromic action potential
5. This orthodromic impulse travels back down the same motor axon to the muscle, producing a small, late muscle response: the F wave

The F wave is named for "foot" because it was first recorded from foot muscles by Magladery and McDougal in 1950. The name is often mistakenly thought to stand for "late" (F for "following" the M wave).

> **Board Mnemonic -- "F = Firing back, not a reflex":** The F wave is a recurrent discharge (backfiring) of the anterior horn cell, NOT a reflex arc. There is no sensory limb, no synapse, and no afferent component. This distinguishes it from the H reflex, which IS a true monosynaptic reflex.

### 4.1.2 Characteristics That Distinguish F Waves from Reflexes

| Feature | F Wave | H Reflex |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Mechanism | Antidromic motor neuron backfire | Monosynaptic Ia reflex arc |
| Afferent limb | NONE (motor axon only) | Ia sensory afferents |
| Synapse | NONE (backfire at cell body) | ONE synapse (Ia to alpha motor neuron) |
| Stimulation required | SUPRAMAXIMAL | SUBMAXIMAL |
| Morphology | VARIABLE (different MNs each stimulus) | CONSTANT (same pathway each time) |
| Latency | VARIABLE | CONSTANT |
| Amplitude | Small (~1-5% of CMAP) | Can be large (up to CMAP size) |
| Persistence | Variable (not every stimulus produces one) | Consistent (present with each stimulus at appropriate intensity) |
| Behavior at supramaximal intensity | PRESENT | ABSENT (collision with antidromic motor volley) |

### 4.1.3 Why Supramaximal Stimulation?

F waves require supramaximal stimulation for several reasons:

1. **All motor axons must be activated** to ensure the antidromic volley reaches all anterior horn cells. If stimulation is submaximal, only some motor neurons are antidromically activated, reducing the probability of backfiring.
2. **To distinguish from the H reflex**: At submaximal intensity, the H reflex may be present and could be confused with the F wave. At supramaximal intensity, the H reflex disappears (see Section 4.2), leaving only the F wave.
3. **To ensure reproducibility**: Supramaximal stimulation provides consistent activation of the motor neuron pool.

### 4.1.4 F-Wave Parameters

The electromyographer records 10-20 consecutive F waves per nerve (minimum 10 for reliable minimal latency determination). The following parameters are measured:

**Minimal F-wave latency**: The shortest latency among all F waves recorded. This reflects the conduction time of the fastest motor fibers from the stimulation site to the spinal cord and back, plus a brief turnaround time at the anterior horn cell (approximately 1 ms). It is the most commonly used clinical parameter.

**Mean F-wave latency**: The average latency of all recorded F waves. Less commonly reported but potentially more stable than minimal latency.

**Maximal F-wave latency**: The longest latency among all F waves. Reflects the slowest motor fibers in the nerve.

**Chronodispersion**: The difference between the maximal and minimal F-wave latencies. It reflects the range of conduction velocities among motor fibers.

**F-wave persistence**: The percentage of stimuli that produce an F-wave response:

**Persistence (%) = (Number of F waves / Number of stimuli) x 100**

Normal persistence is generally >50-80% depending on the nerve. Decreased persistence suggests loss of motor neurons or impaired motor neuron excitability.

**F ratio**: Calculated to compare proximal and distal conduction:

**F ratio = (F latency - M latency - 1) / (2 x M latency)**

Where the "1" represents the turnaround time at the anterior horn cell. The F ratio estimates the ratio of proximal-to-distal conduction time.

### 4.1.5 Estimation Formula for F-Wave Latency

A commonly used estimation for expected F-wave latency:

**Expected F latency (ms) = (Distance from stimulation site to spine x 2) / CV + M latency + 1 ms**

A simpler height-based estimation:

**F latency (ms) approximately = 0.5 x height (cm) + constant**

Where the constant varies by nerve (approximately 9-10 for tibial nerve, 7-8 for ulnar nerve).

### 4.1.6 Normal F-Wave Latency Values

| Nerve | Normal Minimal F Latency | Recording Muscle |
|-------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Median (wrist) | 25-32 ms | APB |
| Ulnar (wrist) | 25-32 ms | ADM |
| Tibial (ankle) | 45-56 ms | AH |
| Fibular (ankle) | 45-56 ms | EDB |

Values are highly dependent on arm/leg length (height). Side-to-side differences of >2 ms are generally considered abnormal.

### 4.1.7 Clinical Applications of F Waves

**Polyneuropathy:**
- Prolonged minimal F-wave latency is the most frequent F-wave abnormality across all polyneuropathy types
- In Guillain-Barre syndrome (GBS): F-wave abnormalities (prolonged latency, decreased persistence, or absent F waves) may be the earliest electrodiagnostic finding, appearing before distal NCS become abnormal. This is because GBS frequently affects nerve roots and proximal nerve segments first.
- In chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP): Prolonged F-wave latencies, increased chronodispersion, and decreased persistence are common findings
- F-wave latency can be prolonged disproportionately to distal CV slowing, suggesting preferential proximal demyelination

**Radiculopathy:**
- Overall diagnostic yield is LOW (approximately 10-20% sensitivity)
- F waves assess the entire nerve from stimulation site to spinal cord and back, and cannot localize to a specific root level because multiple nerve roots contribute motor fibers to each peripheral nerve
- A prolonged or absent F wave in the setting of an otherwise normal motor NCS may suggest proximal pathology but is nonspecific
- Most useful when combined with needle EMG findings

**Motor neuron disease (ALS):**
- Increased chronodispersion (reflects the variable loss of motor neurons)
- Decreased persistence (reflects loss of the motor neuron pool)
- Repeater F waves: The same F-wave morphology appears repeatedly because the reduced motor neuron pool means the same few neurons backfire consistently. This is a characteristic finding in ALS.

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Repeater" F waves -- F waves with identical morphology appearing on consecutive stimuli -- are a hallmark of motor neuron disease. In a normal nerve, each F wave is generated by a different random subset of motor neurons, producing variable morphology. When the motor neuron pool is depleted (as in ALS), the same neurons backfire repeatedly, producing stereotyped F waves. The presence of multiple repeater F waves should raise concern for motor neuron disease.

## 4.2 H Reflex [BOARD]

### 4.2.1 Physiology: The Electrical Analog of the Stretch Reflex

The H reflex is named for Paul Hoffmann, who first described it in 1910. It is the electrical equivalent of the monosynaptic muscle stretch reflex (tendon tap reflex) and involves a complete reflex arc:

**Afferent limb**: Group Ia sensory fibers from muscle spindle primary endings (the largest, lowest-threshold sensory fibers in the nerve)

**Central relay**: Monosynaptic connection between Ia afferents and alpha motor neurons in the spinal cord

**Efferent limb**: Alpha motor neurons, traveling via the ventral root to the muscle

**Complete H-reflex pathway (tibial nerve):**
1. Submaximal stimulus activates Ia afferents in the tibial nerve (posterior tibial nerve in the popliteal fossa)
2. Ia afferent impulses travel proximally to the spinal cord (via the tibial nerve, sciatic nerve, lumbosacral plexus, S1 dorsal root)
3. Ia afferents synapse directly on alpha motor neurons in the S1 anterior horn
4. Alpha motor neurons fire orthodromically, traveling back through the S1 ventral root, lumbosacral plexus, sciatic nerve, tibial nerve to the soleus muscle
5. The soleus muscle contracts, producing the H reflex CMAP

### 4.2.2 H-Reflex Behavior with Increasing Stimulus Intensity

This is one of the most frequently tested board topics regarding the H reflex. The behavior is a classic sequence:

**Step 1 -- Very low stimulus intensity:**
- Ia sensory fibers are activated (they have the lowest threshold due to their large diameter)
- The H reflex appears as a late response
- No M wave is present (stimulus is below motor threshold)

**Step 2 -- Low-to-moderate intensity:**
- More Ia fibers are activated; H reflex amplitude INCREASES
- Motor fibers begin to be activated; a small M wave appears
- Both H reflex and M wave are present

**Step 3 -- Moderate intensity:**
- H reflex reaches MAXIMUM amplitude
- M wave continues to grow
- Both responses are present

**Step 4 -- High intensity (approaching supramaximal):**
- H reflex amplitude begins to DECREASE
- M wave approaches maximum
- The explanation: as motor fibers are increasingly activated, the antidromic motor volley traveling toward the spinal cord COLLIDES with the orthodromic H-reflex motor volley coming from the spinal cord. These two impulses traveling in opposite directions on the same axon cancel each other. This "collision" eliminates the H reflex response.

**Step 5 -- Supramaximal intensity:**
- H reflex DISAPPEARS completely (all motor fibers are antidromically activated, blocking all returning H-reflex impulses)
- M wave is maximal
- F wave appears (backfiring of anterior horn cells)

> **Board Mnemonic -- "H goes High then Hides":** The H reflex amplitude first increases, then decreases and disappears as stimulus intensity rises to supramaximal. At supramaximal intensity, only the M wave and F wave remain. The H reflex requires SUBMAXIMAL stimulation; the F wave requires SUPRAMAXIMAL stimulation.

### 4.2.3 Standard H-Reflex Recording: Tibial Nerve

The tibial H reflex (soleus H reflex) is the only H reflex routinely recorded in adult clinical practice:

**Technique:**
- Patient prone with ankle at neutral
- Stimulate tibial nerve in the popliteal fossa
- Use long-duration stimulus (0.5-1.0 ms) to preferentially activate large Ia afferents
- E1 (active) over the soleus muscle (midline posterior calf, midway between popliteal crease and calcaneus)
- E2 (reference) over the Achilles tendon
- Slowly increase stimulus intensity until maximum H-reflex amplitude is obtained

**Normal values:**
- Latency: approximately 28-35 ms (dependent on height and leg length)
- Side-to-side latency difference: <1.0-1.5 ms (abnormal if difference exceeds this threshold)
- H/M ratio: approximately 0.5-0.7 (ratio of maximum H amplitude to maximum M amplitude)

### 4.2.4 Clinical Applications

**S1 Radiculopathy:**
- The tibial H reflex is the MOST USEFUL electrodiagnostic test for S1 radiculopathy
- Sensitivity: approximately 50% overall; some studies report higher (up to 80% when acute)
- An absent or prolonged UNILATERAL tibial H reflex strongly suggests S1 nerve root dysfunction
- Side-to-side latency comparison is more clinically useful than absolute values
- A prolonged H reflex latency indicates slowed conduction somewhere along the reflex arc (S1 root, sciatic nerve, tibial nerve)
- An absent H reflex unilaterally, in the appropriate clinical context, is highly suggestive of S1 radiculopathy

**Polyneuropathy:**
- H reflexes are prolonged or absent bilaterally in polyneuropathies (especially demyelinating types)
- Bilateral absence of H reflexes can be the earliest finding in GBS
- In diabetic polyneuropathy, H reflex may be prolonged or absent before distal NCS become abnormal

**Age-Related Changes:**
- H reflexes may be absent bilaterally in normal individuals over age 60
- Bilateral absence in elderly individuals is a nonspecific finding and should not be overinterpreted
- Unilateral absence or asymmetric prolongation remains clinically significant at any age

> **Clinical Pearl:** The H reflex tests the S1 reflex arc -- it is the electrodiagnostic equivalent of the ankle jerk. When a patient presents with clinical features suggesting S1 radiculopathy (sciatica, absent ankle jerk, S1 dermatomal numbness), an absent or prolonged unilateral H reflex provides objective electrophysiological confirmation. It is the most sensitive NCS-based test for S1 radiculopathy and should be performed in all patients with suspected lumbosacral radiculopathy. Remember that bilateral absence in the elderly is common and does not indicate pathology.

### 4.2.5 Upper Extremity H Reflexes

While not routinely performed, upper extremity H reflexes can provide useful information:

**Flexor carpi radialis (FCR) H reflex:**
- Tests the C6-C7 reflex arc via the median nerve
- Afferent: median nerve Ia fibers; Efferent: median nerve motor to FCR
- Stimulate median nerve at elbow; record from FCR
- Sensitivity for cervical radiculopathy: approximately 72%; specificity approximately 85%
- Not widely used due to technical difficulty and lack of standardized normative data

**Other upper extremity H reflexes** (triceps/C7, extensor carpi radialis/C6) have been described in research settings but are not part of routine clinical practice.

## 4.3 The A Wave (Axon Reflex) [BOARD]

### 4.3.1 Definition and Mechanism

The A wave is a late response that appears between the M wave and the F wave. It is generated by an axon reflex -- antidromic conduction along a motor axon reaches a branch point (typically a collateral sprout), and the impulse is diverted down the collateral branch to produce a small muscle response.

### 4.3.2 Characteristics

| Feature | A Wave |
|---------|--------|
| Latency | Between M wave and F wave (shorter than F wave) |
| Morphology | CONSTANT (same shape and latency with each stimulus) |
| Amplitude | Small |
| Mechanism | Axon reflex via collateral sprout |
| Significance | Indicates collateral sprouting (chronic neurogenic process) |
| Stimulus required | May appear at submaximal or supramaximal |

### 4.3.3 Clinical Significance

The A wave is a marker of collateral motor axon sprouting, which occurs in chronic neurogenic processes (chronic denervation with reinnervation). Its presence indicates:

- Prior motor axon loss with compensatory collateral sprouting
- Chronic neuropathy or chronic radiculopathy
- Motor neuron disease (where ongoing denervation and reinnervation coexist)

**Key distinction from F waves**: A waves have CONSTANT morphology and latency (the same branch point is activated each time), whereas F waves have VARIABLE morphology and latency (different motor neurons backfire each time).

> **Clinical Pearl:** When a late response with constant morphology appears on motor NCS, it is likely an A wave, not an F wave. A waves indicate collateral sprouting and chronic neurogenic disease. Occasional A waves may be seen in normal individuals, but persistent, prominent A waves are pathological. Do not confuse A waves with F waves -- the key distinguishing feature is the constant vs. variable morphology.

## 4.4 Blink Reflex [BOARD]

### 4.4.1 Anatomy and Physiology

The blink reflex is the electrophysiological analog of the corneal reflex. It tests the trigeminal-facial nerve reflex arc and its central brainstem connections. Stimulation of the supraorbital nerve (a branch of the ophthalmic division, V1, of the trigeminal nerve, CN V) produces two distinct responses recorded from the orbicularis oculi muscle (innervated by the facial nerve, CN VII).

**Afferent pathway**: Supraorbital nerve (V1) --> trigeminal ganglion --> brainstem trigeminal nuclei

**Central pathways**:
- R1 pathway: Principal (main) sensory nucleus of V (in the PONS) --> ipsilateral facial nucleus (short, oligosynaptic pathway)
- R2 pathway: Spinal nucleus and tract of V (descending in the LATERAL MEDULLA) --> reticular formation --> BILATERAL facial nuclei (long, polysynaptic pathway)

**Efferent pathway**: Facial nerve (CN VII) motor fibers --> orbicularis oculi muscle

### 4.4.2 R1 and R2 Components

| Component | Normal Latency | Side Recorded | Pathway | Synapse Count | Clinical Correlate |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| R1 | 10-13 ms | Ipsilateral ONLY | Oligosynaptic via pons (principal sensory nucleus of V --> ipsilateral facial nucleus) | 2-3 synapses | Pontine function |
| R2 (ipsilateral) | 25-40 ms | Ipsilateral | Polysynaptic via lateral medulla (spinal nucleus of V --> reticular formation --> bilateral facial nuclei) | Multiple | Medullary function |
| R2 (contralateral) | 25-40 ms | Contralateral | Same pathway as ipsilateral R2, but reaches contralateral facial nucleus | Multiple | Medullary function |

**Recording setup:**
- Stimulate supraorbital nerve on one side
- Record from orbicularis oculi bilaterally (E1 inferior to each eye, E2 lateral to each eye)
- Repeat stimulation on the opposite side
- This produces four recordings per side of stimulation: ipsilateral R1, ipsilateral R2, contralateral R2 (from each side)

### 4.4.3 Five Classic Patterns of Blink Reflex Abnormality

The interpretation of blink reflex abnormalities is a high-yield board topic. The five classic patterns localize the lesion based on which components are affected and which side is stimulated:

**Pattern 1: Trigeminal Nerve (Afferent) Lesion** [BOARD]

| Stimulate | R1 (ipsi) | R2 (ipsi) | R2 (contra) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Affected side | PROLONGED | PROLONGED | PROLONGED |
| Normal side | Normal | Normal | Normal |

Interpretation: When the affected (V1-lesioned) side is stimulated, ALL responses are delayed because the afferent signal is delayed entering the brainstem. When the normal side is stimulated, all responses are normal because the afferent input is intact.

**Pattern 2: Facial Nerve (Efferent) Lesion** [BOARD]

| Stimulate | R1 (ipsi) | R2 (ipsi) | R2 (contra) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Either side | PROLONGED on affected facial nerve side | PROLONGED on affected side | Normal on unaffected side |

Interpretation: Regardless of which side is stimulated, responses recorded from the side of the facial nerve lesion are delayed. Responses recorded from the normal side are always normal. The afferent pathway is intact; the efferent output on the affected side is delayed.

More specifically:
- Stimulate affected side: Ipsilateral R1 prolonged, ipsilateral R2 prolonged, contralateral R2 normal
- Stimulate normal side: Ipsilateral R1 normal, ipsilateral R2 normal, contralateral R2 prolonged (because the contralateral R2 is recorded from the affected facial nerve side)

**Pattern 3: Pontine Lesion (Ipsilateral)** [BOARD]

| Stimulate | R1 (ipsi) | R2 (ipsi) | R2 (contra) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Affected side | PROLONGED | Normal or mildly prolonged | Normal |
| Normal side | Normal | Normal | Normal |

Interpretation: The R1 pathway traverses the pons (principal sensory nucleus of V to ipsilateral facial nucleus). A pontine lesion disrupts this short pathway, delaying R1 when the affected side is stimulated. The R2 pathway descends to the medulla and may be spared by a pontine lesion.

**Pattern 4: Lateral Medullary Lesion (e.g., Wallenberg Syndrome)** [BOARD]

| Stimulate | R1 (ipsi) | R2 (ipsi) | R2 (contra) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Affected side | Normal | PROLONGED | PROLONGED |
| Normal side | Normal | Normal | PROLONGED (contralateral to stimulated side = ipsilateral to lesion) |

Interpretation: The R1 pathway (in the pons) is spared. The R2 pathway descends through the lateral medulla (spinal nucleus of V and reticular formation). A lateral medullary lesion on one side disrupts the R2 pathway from that side. When the affected side is stimulated, both ipsilateral and contralateral R2 are prolonged (because the afferent R2 signal must pass through the affected medulla). When the normal side is stimulated, the ipsilateral R2 is normal, but the contralateral R2 (which crosses to the affected side) is prolonged because the efferent limb passes through the affected facial nucleus region.

**Pattern 5: Combined Trigeminal and Facial Nerve Lesion (e.g., CPA tumor)** [BOARD]

| Stimulate | R1 (ipsi) | R2 (ipsi) | R2 (contra) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Affected side | PROLONGED (both afferent and efferent) | PROLONGED | PROLONGED |
| Normal side | Normal | Normal | PROLONGED (recorded from affected side) |

Interpretation: Cerebellopontine angle (CPA) tumors (e.g., acoustic neuroma/vestibular schwannoma) can compress both CN V and CN VII, producing a combined afferent and efferent lesion on the same side.

> **Board Mnemonic for Blink Reflex Patterns:** "TRI-ALL, FACIAL-ONE, PONS-R1, MEDULLA-R2":
> - **TRI**geminal lesion: **ALL** responses prolonged when affected side stimulated
> - **FACIAL** lesion: Only responses on the **ONE** affected facial nerve side are prolonged
> - **PONS** lesion: **R1** prolonged (R1 pathway goes through the pons)
> - **MEDULLA** lesion: **R2** prolonged bilaterally when affected side stimulated (R2 pathway goes through medulla)

### 4.4.4 Clinical Indications for Blink Reflex

- **Bell palsy**: Assessing the severity and prognosis of facial nerve injury. R1 latency can help predict recovery.
- **Acoustic neuroma / CPA tumors**: May show combined CN V and CN VII pattern
- **Brainstem lesions**: Stroke (Wallenberg syndrome), multiple sclerosis, tumors
- **Trigeminal neuralgia**: Assessing trigeminal nerve function
- **Guillain-Barre syndrome**: Bilateral R1 prolongation (cranial nerve involvement)
- **Hemifacial spasm**: Abnormal lateral spread responses (R1 recorded from contralateral side during affected-side stimulation, indicating ephaptic transmission)

## 4.5 Direct Facial Nerve Study [BOARD]

### 4.5.1 Technique

The direct facial nerve study stimulates the facial nerve (CN VII) at its exit from the stylomastoid foramen (just anterior to the mastoid process) and records from the orbicularis oculi or nasalis muscle.

**Parameters measured:**
- Distal motor latency (normal: approximately 3.0-4.0 ms for orbicularis oculi; approximately 3.0-3.5 ms for nasalis)
- CMAP amplitude (compared side-to-side)

### 4.5.2 Clinical Applications

**Bell palsy (idiopathic facial nerve palsy):**
- CMAP amplitude compared between affected and unaffected sides
- Side-to-side amplitude ratio predicts prognosis:
  - Amplitude >10% of unaffected side (by day 10-14): Good prognosis for recovery
  - Amplitude <10% of unaffected side: Poor prognosis, may benefit from surgical decompression
- Studies should be performed 10-14 days after onset to allow Wallerian degeneration to occur (earlier studies may overestimate the CMAP amplitude if axonal degeneration is incomplete)

**Limitation**: The direct facial nerve study only evaluates the DISTAL segment of the facial nerve (from the stylomastoid foramen to the muscle). It cannot assess the intracranial or intratemporal segments. The blink reflex is needed to evaluate the proximal facial nerve.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In Bell palsy, the combination of the blink reflex and direct facial nerve study provides complementary information. The blink reflex assesses the entire nerve including the proximal intratemporal segment (where the lesion usually occurs), while the direct study quantifies the degree of distal axonal loss. A prolonged blink reflex R1 with a preserved direct CMAP suggests demyelinating (neurapraxic) injury with good prognosis. A prolonged R1 with a markedly reduced direct CMAP suggests significant axonal loss with a more guarded prognosis.

## 4.6 Phrenic Nerve Study [BOARD]

### 4.6.1 Anatomy

The phrenic nerve arises from the C3, C4, and C5 nerve roots (primarily C4) and is the sole motor innervation to the diaphragm. It descends through the thorax along the lateral aspect of the pericardium to reach the diaphragm.

> **Board Mnemonic:** "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive."

### 4.6.2 Technique

**Stimulation**: Percutaneous stimulation of the phrenic nerve at the posterior border of the sternocleidomastoid muscle at the level of the cricoid cartilage

**Recording**: Surface electrode placement:
- E1 (active): Xiphoid process or 8th intercostal space at the anterior axillary line
- E2 (reference): 16 cm from E1, on the chest wall

**Normal values (approximate):**
- Distal motor latency: 6.0-8.0 ms (varies with body habitus)
- CMAP amplitude: >0.5 mV (varies widely; side-to-side comparison is more useful)
- Side-to-side latency difference: <0.5-1.0 ms
- Side-to-side amplitude difference: <50%

### 4.6.3 Clinical Applications

- **Diaphragmatic paralysis**: Unilateral or bilateral; may be idiopathic or secondary to surgery, tumor, neuropathy
- **Post-cardiac surgery**: Phrenic nerve injury is a recognized complication (hypothermic injury from ice slush, surgical stretching)
- **Critical illness polyneuropathy/myopathy**: Bilateral phrenic nerve involvement contributes to failure to wean from mechanical ventilation
- **Neuralgic amyotrophy (Parsonage-Turner syndrome)**: Phrenic nerve involvement can cause unexplained dyspnea
- **C3-C5 radiculopathy or myelopathy**: Phrenic nerve dysfunction can indicate high cervical pathology

### 4.6.4 Limitations

- Technical challenges: Difficulty achieving consistent supramaximal stimulation in obese patients or those with short necks
- Co-stimulation of the brachial plexus can produce confounding EMG signals
- Amplitude measurements are highly variable and influenced by lung volume, body habitus, and electrode placement
- Needle EMG of the diaphragm provides complementary information but requires specialized training and carries risk of pneumothorax

## 4.7 Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (SSEPs) [BOARD]

### 4.7.1 Principles

Somatosensory evoked potentials are electrical responses generated along the somatosensory pathway in response to repetitive peripheral nerve stimulation. They assess the integrity of the dorsal column-medial lemniscal pathway from the peripheral nerve through the spinal cord, brainstem, and thalamus to the somatosensory cortex.

Because individual SSEP responses are extremely small (often <1 microV) and buried in background EEG activity and noise, signal averaging of hundreds to thousands of responses is required to extract the SSEP signal from the noise.

### 4.7.2 Upper Extremity SSEP: Median Nerve Stimulation

**Stimulation**: Median nerve at the wrist (or ulnar nerve as alternative)

**Recording sites and generator potentials:**

| Waveform | Recording Site | Generator | Anatomical Level |
|----------|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| N9 | Erb's point (supraclavicular fossa) | Brachial plexus | Peripheral |
| N13 | Posterior cervical spine (C5 or C7 spinous process) | Posterior horn / dorsal column nuclei at cervical cord | Spinal cord |
| P14 | Scalp (Fz) referenced to noncephalic | Medial lemniscus (caudal brainstem) | Brainstem |
| N18 | Scalp (Fz) referenced to noncephalic | Brainstem / thalamus | Subcortical |
| N20 | Contralateral scalp (C3'/C4', 2 cm posterior to C3/C4) | Primary somatosensory cortex (area 3b, postcentral gyrus) | Cortical |

The naming convention uses N for negative and P for positive deflections, with the number indicating the approximate latency in milliseconds.

### 4.7.3 Lower Extremity SSEP: Posterior Tibial Nerve Stimulation

**Stimulation**: Posterior tibial nerve at the medial ankle

**Recording sites and generator potentials:**

| Waveform | Recording Site | Generator | Anatomical Level |
|----------|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| N22 | Lumbar spine (L1 or T12 spinous process) | Lumbar spinal cord / cauda equina | Spinal cord |
| P37/P40 | Vertex (Cz) or slightly ipsilateral to stimulated side | Primary somatosensory cortex (medial surface, foot representation) | Cortical |

Note: The cortical response from lower extremity stimulation is recorded at or near the vertex because the foot somatosensory representation is located on the medial surface of the hemisphere (interhemispheric fissure), facing upward. The P37 (or P40 in some nomenclatures) is a positive potential because of the orientation of the cortical dipole.

### 4.7.4 SSEP Recording Parameters

| Parameter | Cortical SSEP | Subcortical/Spinal SSEP |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| Low-frequency (high-pass) filter | 1-30 Hz | 30-100 Hz |
| High-frequency (low-pass) filter | 250-1,000 Hz | 1,000-3,000 Hz |
| Stimulation rate | 2-5 Hz (clinical); may be higher intraoperatively | Same |
| Averaging | 500-2,000 trials (cortical) | 500-2,000 trials |
| Stimulator | Constant current, cathode proximal | Same |

### 4.7.5 Clinical Applications (Non-Operative)

**Multiple sclerosis**: Central demyelination prolongs cortical SSEP latencies (particularly N20 and P37). SSEPs can detect clinically silent lesions in the sensory pathways, supporting the diagnosis of MS with dissemination in space.

**Cervical spondylotic myelopathy**: Prolonged N13-N20 interpeak latency (central conduction time) indicates cervical cord compression affecting the dorsal columns.

**Spinal cord injury**: Absence of cortical SSEPs below the level of injury; preserved SSEPs above. Useful for prognostication and monitoring during surgical stabilization.

**Brainstem lesions**: Abnormalities in P14 and N18 with preserved N9 and N13 indicate brainstem pathology.

**Brachial plexopathy**: Absent N9 with preserved cortical responses may indicate preganglionic lesion (nerve root avulsion) vs. postganglionic plexus injury.

**Coma and brain death**: Bilateral absence of cortical SSEPs (N20) with preserved N13 strongly suggests poor neurological outcome. SSEPs are among the most reliable prognostic tools in comatose patients after cardiac arrest.

### 4.7.6 Intraoperative Monitoring (IOM)

SSEPs are widely used for intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring during surgeries that place the spinal cord or sensory pathways at risk:

- Spinal deformity correction (scoliosis surgery)
- Spinal tumor resection
- Cervical spine decompression
- Thoracoabdominal aortic surgery (monitoring spinal cord perfusion)
- Brain surgery near the somatosensory cortex (cortical mapping using phase reversal of N20 to identify the central sulcus)

**Alarm Criteria (ASNM 2024):**

The commonly used (though not universally standardized) alarm thresholds are:

- **50% decrease in amplitude** of a reproducible SSEP component
- **10% increase in latency** (approximately 2-3 ms prolongation)

When either criterion is met, the surgical and monitoring teams are alerted, and a systematic evaluation is initiated (check anesthetic depth, blood pressure, temperature, electrode integrity, and surgical maneuver).

> **Clinical Pearl:** SSEP monitoring assesses the dorsal column (sensory) pathways but does NOT directly monitor the corticospinal (motor) tracts, which travel in the anterolateral spinal cord. A patient can suffer isolated motor injury (e.g., from anterior spinal artery ischemia) with preserved SSEPs. For this reason, motor evoked potential (MEP) monitoring is often used in conjunction with SSEPs during high-risk spinal surgeries to provide comprehensive monitoring of both sensory and motor pathways.

## 4.8 Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS) [BOARD]

### 4.8.1 Physiology of NMJ Transmission During Repetitive Stimulation

To understand RNS findings, one must first understand the physiology of ACh vesicle pools at the neuromuscular junction:

**Immediately available store (IAS)**: The pool of ACh vesicles docked at active zones, ready for immediate release. This pool is partially depleted with each nerve impulse.

**Mobilization store**: A larger reserve pool of vesicles that replenishes the IAS. Mobilization takes several seconds.

During low-rate repetitive stimulation (2-3 Hz), the IAS is progressively depleted over the first 4-5 stimuli. In a normal NMJ with an adequate safety factor, this depletion does not cause transmission failure because the EPP still exceeds threshold. However, in NMJ disorders with a reduced safety factor, the progressive depletion of the IAS causes some end plates to fail, producing a decremental CMAP response.

### 4.8.2 Technique

**Standard protocol:**
1. Record CMAP from target muscle (typically distal hand muscle for generalized MG, or proximal muscle like trapezius for higher sensitivity)
2. Apply a train of 6-10 stimuli at 2-3 Hz (low-rate)
3. Compare the amplitude of the 1st and 4th (or 5th) CMAP
4. Exercise the muscle briefly (10-30 seconds of maximal voluntary contraction)
5. Repeat stimulation trains at intervals: immediately post-exercise, and then at 1, 2, 3, and 4 minutes post-exercise

**Post-exercise sequence:**
- **Immediately post-exercise (0-30 sec)**: Post-exercise facilitation -- ACh release is transiently increased due to calcium accumulation in the presynaptic terminal. Decrement may improve or resolve.
- **2-4 minutes post-exercise**: Post-exercise exhaustion -- the IAS has been further depleted by the exercise, and mobilization cannot fully compensate. Decrement reaches its MAXIMUM (most severe). This is the most sensitive time to detect a decremental response.

### 4.8.3 Myasthenia Gravis (Postsynaptic Disorder)

**Pathophysiology**: Antibodies against postsynaptic AChRs (85% of cases) or MuSK (5-8%) reduce the number of functional receptors, decreasing MEPP amplitude and reducing the safety factor.

**RNS findings:**
- **Baseline CMAP amplitude**: NORMAL (all muscle fibers are fully activated by supramaximal stimulation because the safety factor, though reduced, is still sufficient at baseline in most fibers)
- **Low-rate RNS (2-3 Hz)**: DECREMENTAL response of 10% or greater from 1st to 4th/5th CMAP
- Decrement is typically maximal at the 4th or 5th stimulus
- **Post-exercise facilitation**: Brief exercise may temporarily improve the decrement (increased ACh release temporarily restores the safety factor)
- **Post-exercise exhaustion (2-4 minutes)**: Decrement WORSENS (most sensitive time to detect abnormality)
- **High-rate RNS**: Not routinely performed in MG; may show mild facilitation but not the dramatic increment seen in LEMS

**Sensitivity:**
- Generalized MG: approximately 75% sensitivity when testing proximal muscles (trapezius, nasalis, orbicularis oculi)
- Ocular MG: approximately 50% sensitivity
- Distal muscles (ADM): Lower sensitivity (~50%)
- Single-fiber EMG (jitter): >95% sensitivity (most sensitive test for NMJ disorders)

### 4.8.4 Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (Presynaptic Disorder)

**Pathophysiology**: Antibodies against presynaptic P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs) reduce calcium influx into the nerve terminal, severely decreasing quantal ACh release. The postsynaptic receptors are intact.

**RNS findings:**
- **Baseline CMAP amplitude**: LOW (the reduced ACh release at rest fails to activate some muscle fibers)
- **Low-rate RNS (2-3 Hz)**: DECREMENTAL response (similar to MG, because the already-reduced IAS is further depleted)
- **High-rate RNS (20-50 Hz) or post-exercise**: INCREMENTAL response
  - Diagnostic hallmark: greater than or equal to 60% CMAP amplitude increment (some use 100% as the most specific criterion)
  - Mechanism: Rapid stimulation causes calcium accumulation in the presynaptic terminal despite the reduced number of VGCCs. The increased intracellular calcium progressively enhances vesicle fusion and ACh release, increasing the EPP above threshold at more end plates
  - The increment can be dramatic -- sometimes 200-400% increase from baseline

**Association**: LEMS is a paraneoplastic syndrome in approximately 50-60% of cases, most commonly associated with small cell lung cancer. All patients diagnosed with LEMS should be screened for malignancy.

### 4.8.5 MG vs. LEMS: Comprehensive Comparison

| Feature | Myasthenia Gravis | Lambert-Eaton |
|---------|------------------|---------------|
| NMJ defect | Postsynaptic (AChR or MuSK) | Presynaptic (VGCC) |
| Antibody | Anti-AChR (85%); Anti-MuSK (5-8%) | Anti-P/Q VGCC |
| Baseline CMAP | NORMAL | LOW |
| Low-rate decrement | Present (>=10%) | Present |
| High-rate increment | Absent or minimal | PRESENT (>=60-100%) |
| Post-exercise facilitation | Mild | DRAMATIC |
| Post-exercise exhaustion | Present (peak at 2-4 min) | Present but less prominent |
| Clinical weakness | Ocular, bulbar, proximal limb; fatigable | Proximal limb, autonomic (dry mouth, constipation) |
| Reflexes | Normal | DEPRESSED (may improve post-exercise) |
| Malignancy association | Thymoma (10-15%) | Small cell lung cancer (50-60%) |
| Most sensitive test | SFEMG (jitter) | Low baseline CMAP + increment |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The triad that defines LEMS on electrodiagnostic testing: (1) Low baseline CMAP amplitudes, (2) decremental response at low-rate RNS, and (3) incremental response (>=60%) at high-rate RNS or post-exercise. This triad distinguishes LEMS from both MG and axonal neuropathy. Remember that MG has a normal baseline CMAP, while LEMS has a low baseline CMAP -- this is because in LEMS, the presynaptic release is so impaired that some muscle fibers fail to activate even at rest.

### 4.8.6 Botulism

**Pathophysiology**: Botulinum toxin cleaves SNARE complex proteins (synaptobrevin, syntaxin, or SNAP-25 depending on toxin serotype), preventing vesicle fusion and ACh release. This is a presynaptic disorder, like LEMS.

**RNS findings:**
- Low baseline CMAP amplitude (similar to LEMS)
- Decremental response at low-rate RNS
- Incremental response at high-rate RNS (though less consistent than LEMS)
- Unlike LEMS, botulism typically presents acutely and may show fibrillation potentials on needle EMG in severely affected muscles

## 4.9 Single-Fiber EMG (SFEMG): Key Concepts [BOARD]

### 4.9.1 Jitter

Jitter is the beat-to-beat variation in the time interval between two single muscle fiber action potentials belonging to the same motor unit. It reflects the variability of neuromuscular transmission at individual end plates.

**Normal jitter**: approximately 20-45 microseconds (varies by muscle and technique -- voluntary activation vs. stimulated)

**Increased jitter**: >55-60 microseconds (depending on the muscle) indicates NMJ transmission instability. This is the most sensitive indicator of NMJ dysfunction.

**Blocking**: When jitter is so severe that the second potential intermittently fails to appear, this is called blocking. It represents complete failure of NMJ transmission at that end plate on some impulses.

### 4.9.2 Clinical Significance

- **Most sensitive test for NMJ disorders**: Sensitivity >95% for generalized MG; approximately 85-90% for ocular MG
- More sensitive than RNS (which has ~75% sensitivity for generalized MG)
- Abnormal jitter is NOT specific for MG -- it can be seen in any condition that affects NMJ transmission, including reinnervation (chronic neurogenic diseases), Lambert-Eaton syndrome, and botulism
- Fiber density (number of single fiber potentials per motor unit in the uptake area) is increased in neurogenic conditions due to collateral sprouting

> **Clinical Pearl:** While SFEMG is the most sensitive test for NMJ disorders, it is not the most specific. Increased jitter can be seen in any condition with immature or unstable NMJ transmission, including chronic denervation with reinnervation (the newly formed NMJs are less reliable). Therefore, SFEMG results must always be interpreted in the clinical context. A normal SFEMG study in a clinically suspected muscle effectively excludes MG in that muscle.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter has covered the late responses and special electrophysiological studies essential for PM&R board preparation. The unifying themes are:

1. **F waves** assess the entire motor pathway from peripheral nerve to anterior horn cell and back; they are NOT reflexes and require supramaximal stimulation. Their primary clinical utility is in polyneuropathies and GBS.
2. **H reflexes** are true monosynaptic reflexes testing specific reflex arcs. The tibial H reflex is the best electrodiagnostic test for S1 radiculopathy. H reflexes disappear at supramaximal intensity.
3. **The blink reflex** evaluates the trigeminal-facial nerve brainstem circuit and can localize lesions to CN V (afferent), CN VII (efferent), the pons (R1), or the lateral medulla (R2).
4. **SSEPs** assess the dorsal column-medial lemniscal pathway from periphery to cortex and are essential for intraoperative monitoring and evaluation of central nervous system disorders.
5. **RNS** differentiates presynaptic (LEMS, botulism) from postsynaptic (MG) NMJ disorders based on baseline CMAP amplitude and the pattern of decrement/increment.
