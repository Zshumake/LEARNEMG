The Localization Mindset

Every peripheral nerve case is the same puzzle: where exactly is the lesion?

  • Use SNAP integrity to separate pre-ganglionic from post-ganglionic lesions
  • Use paraspinal EMG to confirm or exclude root-level involvement
  • Use the pattern of weak muscles (and the spared ones) to find the most distal common ancestor in the nerve tree
  • Use one "key muscle" or "key SNAP" per case to lock in the diagnosis

Five cases. Same toolkit. Different answers.

Parsonage-Turner Syndrome

Also called: Neuralgic Amyotrophy, Acute Brachial Neuritis, Idiopathic Brachial Plexopathy

Key Features That Confirm It

  • Pain-weakness temporal sequence
  • Patchy, multi-nerve pattern (not following any single trunk/cord)
  • Normal paraspinals (post-ganglionic)
  • Predilection for shoulder girdle nerves: Long thoracic, Suprascapular, Axillary, Musculocutaneous, AIN
  • 30% of cases are bilateral
  • 70-80% recover by 2-3 years
★
Parsonage-Turner Pearl 1

The Pain-Weakness Sequence

The hallmark of Parsonage-Turner is severe pain that PRECEDES the weakness.

Pain comes first (days to weeks), then weakness develops as the pain resolves.

If a patient has pain AND weakness simultaneously, think compression. If pain came first then weakness followed -- think Parsonage-Turner.

★
Parsonage-Turner Pearl 2

Why It Wasn't Radiculopathy

The weakness involves muscles from three different peripheral nerves:

Axillary Nerve

Deltoid (C5-C6)

Suprascapular Nerve

Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus (C5-C6)

Musculocutaneous

Biceps, Brachialis (C5-C6)

Long Thoracic

Serratus Anterior (C5-C7)

A C5 root would affect ALL these. But the pattern is patchy -- some nerves spared, others severely affected. That's PTS.

★
Parsonage-Turner Pearl 3

The LABC SNAP Tells the Story

The Lateral Antebrachial Cutaneous SNAP is the sensory branch of the musculocutaneous nerve.

In PTS affecting the musculocutaneous nerve, the LABC SNAP is abnormal. This confirms the lesion is post-ganglionic -- the sensory axons have undergone Wallerian degeneration distal to the dorsal root ganglion.

If this were a C6 root lesion, the LABC would be NORMAL because the DRG is spared.

★
Parsonage-Turner Pearl 4

Normal Paraspinals = Post-Ganglionic

The posterior primary ramus branches off the nerve root almost immediately after it exits the spinal cord. If the lesion is at the root level, paraspinals will show denervation.

In PTS, paraspinals are typically normal (though they CAN be abnormal in 10-20% of cases). Combined with abnormal SNAPs, this localizes the lesion to the plexus or peripheral nerve level -- not the root.

Right Lower Trunk Brachial Plexopathy

(Klumpke's Palsy)

Localization Logic

  • BOTH median + ulnar territories affected -> proximal to peripheral nerves
  • EIP (radial nerve, C8) affected -> spans nerve boundaries -> root or lower trunk
  • Abnormal MAC SNAP -> POST-ganglionic -> NOT a root lesion
  • Normal paraspinals -> confirms not at root level
  • No Horner's -> confirms not T1 root avulsion
  • Proximal muscles (biceps, deltoid) spared -> NOT a pan-plexopathy
  • Conclusion: Lower trunk lesion, post-ganglionic
★
Klumpke's Pearl 1

Thenar AND Hypothenar Atrophy

The thenar muscles are MEDIAN-innervated. The hypothenar muscles are ULNAR-innervated. If BOTH are weak, you've crossed peripheral nerve boundaries -- the lesion must be PROXIMAL to where median and ulnar separate.

This puts the lesion at the lower trunk (C8-T1), the medial cord, or the C8-T1 roots. Now you need to figure out WHICH of those three.

★
Klumpke's Pearl 2

The MAC SNAP Is the Money Test

The Medial Antebrachial Cutaneous nerve is the sensory branch of the medial cord. It carries C8-T1 sensory information from the medial forearm.

Why It Matters

  • If the lesion is at the C8 or T1 ROOT -- pre-ganglionic -- the MAC SNAP is NORMAL (DRG intact)
  • If the lesion is at the LOWER TRUNK or MEDIAL CORD -- post-ganglionic -- the MAC SNAP is ABNORMAL (Wallerian degeneration distal to DRG)

An abnormal MAC SNAP is the single best test to distinguish lower trunk plexopathy from C8 radiculopathy.

★
Klumpke's Pearl 3

No Horner's Syndrome

Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) requires disruption of the sympathetic chain at the T1 root level.

Its absence here tells us this is NOT a T1 root avulsion. It's a post-ganglionic lesion at the lower trunk level.

If you see Horner's in a "Klumpke-pattern" presentation, that's actually a root avulsion, not a true Klumpke's.

Traumatic C5-T1 Root Avulsions

The Pathognomonic Pattern

  • Complete clinical anesthesia + paralysis (C5-T1)
  • SNAPs preserved (pre-ganglionic)
  • CMAPs absent
  • Paraspinal denervation (root-level)
  • Horner's syndrome (T1 sympathetic disruption)
  • MRI: pseudomeningoceles at the affected levels

Prognosis: very poor. Surgical nerve transfer is the only intervention.

★
Root Avulsion Pearl 1

Horner's Syndrome = T1 Avulsion

The sympathetic fibers to the head and face exit the spinal cord at T1 and travel through the sympathetic chain to the superior cervical ganglion.

If you avulse the T1 root, you disrupt these sympathetic fibers BEFORE they leave the spinal canal. The result: ipsilateral Horner's syndrome.

Ptosis

Drooping eyelid (Mueller's muscle)

Miosis

Constricted pupil

Anhidrosis

No sweating on that side of face

Significance

VERY poor prognosis -- root is gone

★
Root Avulsion Pearl 2

Preserved SNAPs in an Anesthetic Limb

This pattern -- SNAPs preserved despite complete clinical anesthesia -- is PATHOGNOMONIC for pre-ganglionic root avulsion.

The Anatomy

The dorsal root ganglion (DRG) sits OUTSIDE the spinal canal, in the neural foramen. Sensory cell bodies live in the DRG, with two axons:

  • One axon goes centrally (DRG -> spinal cord)
  • One axon goes peripherally (DRG -> skin)

A root avulsion happens BETWEEN the DRG and the spinal cord. The peripheral axon is still attached to its cell body in the DRG -- so it does NOT undergo Wallerian degeneration. The patient has no sensation centrally, but the peripheral nerve still works.

★
Root Avulsion Pearl 3

Paraspinal Denervation Confirms Root-Level

The posterior primary ramus branches off the spinal nerve immediately after it exits the foramen, BEFORE the nerve forms the brachial plexus.

If paraspinals show denervation, the lesion must be at or proximal to the root level. This rules out plexopathy and localizes to the spinal nerve or root.

In root avulsion: paraspinals abnormal + SNAPs preserved + Horner's = the complete pathognomonic triad.

Femoral Neuropathy from Iliacus / Retroperitoneal Hematoma

Localization Logic

  • Femoral nerve dysfunction (quad weak, saphenous SNAP absent)
  • ILIOPSOAS WEAK -> lesion is proximal to inguinal ligament -> in the iliac fossa
  • Adductors normal -> NOT a plexopathy, NOT an L3-L4 radic
  • Paraspinals normal -> NOT a radiculopathy
  • Anticoagulation history -> hematoma is the etiology
  • CT confirms: 8x6 cm hematoma in iliacus and retroperitoneum

Treatment: reverse anticoagulation, supportive care, sometimes surgical decompression.

★
Retroperitoneal Hematoma Pearl 1

The Anticoagulation Red Flag

Acute femoral neuropathy + anticoagulation = retroperitoneal/iliacus hematoma until proven otherwise.

This is a TIME-SENSITIVE diagnosis. The hematoma compresses the femoral nerve in the iliac fossa. Delay in recognition can lead to permanent quadriceps weakness.

Other causes of femoral neuropathy: post-pelvic surgery (compression by retractors), lithotomy position (prolonged hip flexion), psoas abscess, diabetes (femoral neuropathy variant of diabetic amyotrophy).

★
Retroperitoneal Hematoma Pearl 2

Iliopsoas Weakness Localizes the Lesion

The femoral nerve gives off branches to the iliopsoas WITHIN the iliac fossa, BEFORE passing under the inguinal ligament.

Two Possible Lesions, One Differentiator

At the Inguinal Ligament

Quadriceps weak
Iliopsoas SPARED
Cause: post-surgical, compression, hernia repair

In the Iliac Fossa (Retroperitoneum)

Quadriceps weak
Iliopsoas ALSO weak
Cause: hematoma, abscess, tumor

Weak iliopsoas = lesion is PROXIMAL = something in the retroperitoneum. Order a CT.

★
Retroperitoneal Hematoma Pearl 3

The Saphenous SNAP

The saphenous nerve is the sensory branch of the femoral nerve. An absent saphenous SNAP confirms a post-ganglionic femoral nerve lesion.

If this were an L3-L4 radiculopathy (pre-ganglionic), the saphenous SNAP would be NORMAL because the DRG is intact.

Always compare to the contralateral side -- the saphenous SNAP can be tricky to obtain even in healthy patients.

Sciatic Nerve Injury at the Hip

Post-Total Hip Arthroplasty

Localization Logic

  • Both peroneal AND tibial divisions affected -> NOT a pure peroneal lesion
  • Hamstrings weak (including BFSH) -> lesion proximal to mid-thigh
  • GLUTEALS SPARED -> NOT a plexopathy or root lesion
  • Paraspinals normal -> NOT a radiculopathy
  • Femoral nerve normal -> isolated sciatic injury
  • Conclusion: Sciatic nerve injury at the hip level

Prognosis: 6-18 months for partial recovery. Many patients have permanent residual foot drop.

★
Sciatic Injury Pearl 1

Sciatic Injury After Hip Surgery

Sciatic nerve injury occurs in 0.5-2% of total hip arthroplasties. It is one of the most feared complications.

Mechanisms

  • Direct surgical trauma (rare with modern techniques)
  • Retractor compression
  • Limb lengthening (stretch injury) -- common with revision surgery
  • Hematoma compression in the post-operative period
  • Thermal injury from cement
★
Sciatic Injury Pearl 2

Gluteal Sparing Localizes the Lesion

The superior and inferior gluteal nerves arise from the sacral plexus PROXIMAL to where the sciatic nerve forms and exits the pelvis.

If the gluteal muscles are normal, the lesion CANNOT be at the plexus or root level. It must be at or below the hip -- exactly where the sciatic nerve is at risk during a hip arthroplasty.

Gluteus Maximus weak?

NO -> Lesion below the inferior gluteal nerve takeoff. Sciatic at hip or below.

Gluteus Maximus weak + foot drop?

YES -> Sacral plexopathy or L5-S1 radiculopathy. NOT a sciatic injury.

★
Sciatic Injury Pearl 3

Why the Peroneal Division Is More Vulnerable

Even in a complete sciatic injury, the peroneal division is often MORE severely affected than the tibial division.

The Anatomical Reasons

  • Fewer fascicles -> less protective architecture
  • More connective tissue around fascicles -> more susceptible to compression
  • Positioned more lateral and posterior in the sciatic trunk -> closer to surgical instruments
  • Tethered at the fibular head -> less mobility under stretch

A patient may present looking like a "common fibular palsy" but actually have a partial sciatic injury. The tipoff: hamstring weakness or any tibial-distribution involvement.

★
Sciatic Injury Pearl 4

The Biceps Femoris Short Head Test

The short head of the biceps femoris is the ONLY hamstring innervated by the peroneal division of the sciatic nerve.

If BFSH is denervated, the lesion is at or proximal to the mid-thigh where the peroneal division branches off to supply it.

Why It Matters

  • Common fibular palsy at fibular head: BFSH is NORMAL
  • Sciatic injury at the hip: BFSH is ABNORMAL
  • This single muscle distinguishes the two diagnoses

Putting It All Together

Three brachial plexus presentations -- one anatomical principle ties them together.

SNAP integrity is the single most important test for distinguishing root avulsion from plexopathy.

Five Take-Home Points

  • Pain BEFORE weakness + patchy multi-nerve pattern = Parsonage-Turner. Don't miss this.
  • The MAC SNAP is your money test for lower trunk plexopathy vs C8 radiculopathy.
  • Preserved SNAPs in an anesthetic limb is pathognomonic for pre-ganglionic root avulsion. Always check paraspinals to confirm.
  • Iliopsoas weakness in femoral neuropathy localizes the lesion proximal to the inguinal ligament -- think retroperitoneal hematoma in anticoagulated patients.
  • Gluteal sparing in foot drop + hamstring weakness means sciatic injury at the hip, not plexopathy or radiculopathy. Always test BFSH.
