## CASE 1: Severe Shoulder Pain Followed by Weakness
**35M painter, no trauma, viral illness 3 weeks ago**

**DIAGNOSIS: Parsonage-Turner Syndrome (Neuralgic Amyotrophy)**

History clues:
- Pain PRECEDED weakness by 10 days (pathognomonic sequence)
- No trauma (rules out plexopathy from injury)
- Recent viral illness = classic trigger (~50% have preceding infection)

Exam findings to emphasize:
- Deltoid 2/5, Infraspinatus 2/5, Biceps 3/5 -- THREE different nerves (axillary, suprascapular, musculocutaneous)
- Hand 5/5 -- distal sparing
- Patchy numbness lateral shoulder only (axillary nerve sensory patch)
- Negative Spurling's -- no radiculopathy

NCS key findings:
- Median, Ulnar, Radial SNAPs: NORMAL
- LABC SNAP: 5 uV (ABNORMAL) -- post-ganglionic, musculocutaneous nerve involved
- Axillary motor: 1.5 mV (LOW)
- Musculocutaneous motor: 2.8 mV (LOW)

EMG key findings:
- Deltoid 3+ fibs, Infraspinatus 3+ fibs, Biceps 2+ fibs, Serratus Anterior 2+ fibs
- Pronator Teres: NORMAL (the patchy clue -- median C6-C7 muscle spared)
- APB, FDI: NORMAL (hand spared)
- C5-C6 Paraspinals: NORMAL (post-ganglionic = not a root lesion)

Teaching points after reveal:
1. Pain-weakness sequence is the hallmark
2. Pattern is PATCHY (multiple individual nerves, not following a trunk/cord)
3. LABC SNAP abnormal = post-ganglionic
4. Normal paraspinals = not root level (though 10-20% CAN have abnormal paraspinals)
5. 30% bilateral, 70-80% recover by 2-3 years
6. AIN variant exists (thumb/index DIP flexion weakness)

## CASE 2: Hand Weakness After a Fall
**24M bike messenger, grabbed tree branch, upward traction**

**DIAGNOSIS: Right Lower Trunk Brachial Plexopathy (Klumpke's Palsy)**

History clues:
- Mechanism: upward traction on arm (classic for lower trunk)
- Immediate axillary pain + medial arm numbness
- Progressive clawing at 4 weeks

Exam findings to emphasize:
- BOTH thenar AND hypothenar atrophy (crosses median/ulnar boundary = proximal lesion)
- Claw hand (MCP hyperextension, IP flexion digits 4-5)
- APB 2/5, FDI 2/5, ADM 2/5, FDP 3/5, FCU 4/5
- Triceps 5/5, Deltoid 5/5 (PROXIMAL SPARING)
- Sensory: medial arm, forearm, ulnar hand
- NO HORNER'S SYNDROME (= not T1 root avulsion)

NCS key findings:
- Median sensory (index): NORMAL 45 uV (C6-C7 territory spared)
- Ulnar sensory: 5 uV (ABNORMAL)
- **MAC SNAP: 4 uV (ABNORMAL) -- THE MONEY TEST**
- Radial sensory: NORMAL
- Median motor (APB): 3.5 mV (low)
- Ulnar motor (ADM): 2.5 mV (low)

EMG key findings:
- APB 2+ fibs, FDI 2+ fibs, ADM 2+ fibs (C8-T1 via median + ulnar)
- EIP 1+ fibs (C7-C8 via radial -- crosses nerve boundaries)
- FCU 1+ fibs
- Biceps: NORMAL, Deltoid: NORMAL (upper trunk spared)
- C8-T1 Paraspinals: NORMAL (post-ganglionic)

Teaching points after reveal:
1. Both thenar + hypothenar = lesion proximal to where median/ulnar separate
2. MAC SNAP is THE test: abnormal = post-ganglionic (plexus), normal = pre-ganglionic (root)
3. No Horner's = NOT T1 root avulsion (Horner's = sympathetic chain at T1)
4. Normal paraspinals confirms post-ganglionic

## CASE 3: Flail Arm After Motorcycle Accident
**62M construction worker, high-energy MVA, 6 months out**

**DIAGNOSIS: Traumatic C5-T1 Root Avulsions (Pan-Plexopathy)**

History clues:
- High-energy motorcycle accident + clavicle fracture
- Complete arm paralysis + numbness (C5-T1)
- 6 months out = chronic (plenty of time for Wallerian degeneration)

Exam findings to emphasize:
- Entire left arm atrophic, 0-1/5 throughout
- **HORNER'S SYNDROME: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis** (T1 involvement)
- Winged scapula (long thoracic = C5-C7 roots)
- All reflexes absent
- Sensation decreased throughout C5-T1

NCS key findings -- THE PATHOGNOMONIC PATTERN:
- **Median SNAP: 28 uV (NORMAL)**
- **Ulnar SNAP: 22 uV (NORMAL)**
- **Radial SNAP: 25 uV (NORMAL)**
- All three SNAPs PRESERVED despite COMPLETE ANESTHESIA = PRE-GANGLIONIC
- All motor responses: ABSENT (Median, Ulnar, Radial, Musculocutaneous)

"How can the patient have no sensation but normal SNAPs?"
- The DRG sits OUTSIDE the spinal canal
- Root avulsion = tear BETWEEN DRG and spinal cord
- Peripheral sensory axon still connected to its cell body in DRG
- So the peripheral nerve still works -- it just can't talk to the brain

EMG key findings:
- EVERY muscle C5-T1: 3+ fibs, no MUAPs (deltoid, biceps, infraspinatus, triceps, EIP, APB, FDI)
- **Cervical Paraspinals C5-T1: 3+ fibs** (confirms ROOT-LEVEL lesion)
- Paraspinals abnormal because posterior primary ramus branches off AT the root level

Teaching points after reveal:
1. Preserved SNAPs + anesthetic limb = PATHOGNOMONIC for root avulsion
2. Horner's = T1 root avulsion (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis)
3. Paraspinal denervation = root level (posterior ramus branches before plexus)
4. The triad: preserved SNAPs + abnormal paraspinals + Horner's = root avulsion
5. Prognosis: very poor. Surgical nerve transfer only option.
6. Wait 2-3 weeks post-injury for EDX (Wallerian degeneration timing)

## CASE 4: Sudden Groin Pain and Buckling Knee
**68F on warfarin, INR 4.2, coughing episode**

**DIAGNOSIS: Femoral Neuropathy from Iliacus/Retroperitoneal Hematoma**

History clues:
- On warfarin with supratherapeutic INR (4.2)
- Sudden onset after coughing (Valsalva = increased abdominal pressure)
- Hemoglobin drop 12.5 -> 9.8 (internal bleeding)
- Hip held in flexion (psoas sign)
- TIME-SENSITIVE -- can lead to permanent quad weakness if not caught

Exam findings to emphasize:
- Hip Flexion (Iliopsoas): 2/5 -- **THIS IS THE KEY**
- Knee Extension (Quad): 1/5
- Hip Adduction: 5/5 NORMAL (obturator nerve spared = not L3-L4 radic)
- Ankle muscles: 5/5 NORMAL
- Patellar reflex: ABSENT
- Anterior thigh + medial leg (saphenous) numbness

Why iliopsoas matters:
- Femoral nerve branches to iliopsoas arise WITHIN the iliac fossa
- These branches leave the nerve BEFORE it passes under the inguinal ligament
- If iliopsoas is weak: lesion is PROXIMAL to inguinal ligament (retroperitoneum)
- If iliopsoas is spared: lesion is AT inguinal ligament (post-surgical, retractor)

NCS key findings:
- Sural, Superficial Fibular: NORMAL (sciatic territory fine)
- Saphenous SNAP: ABSENT on right (post-ganglionic femoral lesion)
- Femoral motor: ABSENT on right, 6.5 mV on left

EMG key findings:
- **Iliopsoas: 2+ fibs, severely reduced** (localizes PROXIMAL to inguinal ligament)
- Vastus Lat/Med/Rectus Femoris: 3+ fibs, no MUAPs
- Adductor Longus (Obturator): NORMAL (rules out L2-L4 radic and plexopathy)
- Gluteus Medius: NORMAL (rules out plexopathy)
- Lumbar Paraspinals: NORMAL (rules out radiculopathy)

Teaching points after reveal:
1. Anticoagulation + acute femoral neuropathy = hematoma until proven otherwise
2. Iliopsoas weakness = lesion proximal to inguinal ligament
3. Adductor sparing = not radiculopathy (obturator shares L2-L4 roots)
4. Absent saphenous SNAP = post-ganglionic
5. Treatment: reverse anticoagulation, CT scan, possible surgical decompression

## CASE 5: Foot Drop and Hamstring Weakness After Surgery
**67F, 6 weeks post-total hip arthroplasty**

**DIAGNOSIS: Sciatic Nerve Injury at the Hip (Post-THA)**

History clues:
- Total hip arthroplasty 6 weeks ago
- Difficult anatomy, longer-than-expected surgery
- Awoke with foot drop AND knee flexion weakness
- 6 weeks of PT with minimal improvement

Exam findings to emphasize:
- **Hip Abduction (Glut Med): 5/5 NORMAL** -- KEY FINDING
- **Hip Extension (Glut Max): 5/5 NORMAL** -- KEY FINDING
- Knee Flexion (Hamstrings): 2/5
- Ankle Dorsiflexion: 0/5, Plantarflexion: 1/5, Eversion: 0/5
- Achilles absent, Patellar NORMAL
- Sensation lost: foot, lateral leg, posterior calf
- Sensation PRESERVED: anteromedial thigh (saphenous = femoral nerve intact)

Why gluteal sparing matters:
- Superior gluteal nerve (glut med) and inferior gluteal nerve (glut max) branch from sacral plexus PROXIMAL to sciatic nerve formation
- If glutes are normal: lesion is AT or BELOW the hip (sciatic level)
- If glutes are weak: lesion is at plexus or root level
- This single finding rules out plexopathy and radiculopathy

NCS key findings:
- Sural SNAP: ABSENT (normal 15 uV on left)
- Superficial Fibular SNAP: ABSENT (normal 12 uV on left)
- Saphenous SNAP: NORMAL (femoral nerve territory intact)
- Fibular motor: ABSENT or 0.4 mV
- Tibial motor: 1.2 mV (severely reduced)
- H-reflex: ABSENT on right

EMG key findings:
- TA, EHL, Peroneus Longus: 3+ fibs, NO MUAPs (peroneal division = more severe)
- Gastrocnemius, Tibialis Posterior: 2+ fibs, severely reduced (tibial division = less severe)
- **Biceps Femoris SHORT HEAD: 2+ fibs** (peroneal division in thigh -- confirms sciatic level)
- BF Long Head, Semimembranosus: 1+ fibs (tibial division in thigh)
- **Gluteus Maximus: NORMAL** (inferior gluteal = plexus level = spared)
- **Gluteus Medius: NORMAL** (superior gluteal = spared)
- Vastus Lateralis: NORMAL (femoral = spared)
- Lumbar Paraspinals: NORMAL (not radiculopathy)

Teaching points after reveal:
1. Sciatic injury = 0.5-2% of THAs (retractor, stretch, hematoma, cement)
2. Peroneal division MORE vulnerable (fewer fascicles, tethered at fibular head)
3. Gluteal sparing = NOT plexopathy or root
4. BFSH is the KEY muscle: only hamstring from peroneal division. If abnormal = sciatic level, not common fibular at knee
5. Common pitfall: "foot drop post-op" gets misdiagnosed as peroneal palsy. Always check hamstrings + glutes.
6. Prognosis: 6-18 months partial recovery, many have permanent foot drop
